*Working extracts from the personal consultation records of [[Ostal Mrev]]. Cases organized by problem category. The following are from the section on environmental parameter drift — sigil arrays installed to specification that subsequently fail as conditions change.*

---

**Case 7 in category: Load-bearing array, southern warehouse district, [[Valdenmoor]].**

Referred by original installation practitioner. The array was installed to specification five years prior. Specification was based on standard stone-and-timber construction with seasonal temperature variation within documented range. The building was subsequently modified — upper floor added, within the load specification's stated margin. The array began showing stress indicators eighteen months after the modification. Standard remediation did not resolve it.

What the original practitioner missed, and what the standard remediation process assumes can be corrected: the upper floor modification did not change the total load symmetrically. The stairwell placement pulled distribution toward the northern wall. The array was calibrated for a specific distribution pattern, not a specific total weight. Adding weight within the margin would have been fine. The distribution shift was not within the margin.

Diagnostic process: three site visits. First to document current stress indicator pattern. Second to map original installation geometry against current load distribution. Third to verify analysis against what the indicators were actually showing.

Correction: partial reinstallation of three of eleven array nodes, adjusted for actual current distribution. The remaining eight nodes were correctly placed for the new conditions. Original practitioner could have done this work if the diagnostic framework existed — the casting is within [[Control Tier|T3]] range. The diagnostic framework does not exist in standard training.

Note for category: Environmental parameter drift cases are almost always the same story. The installation was correct. The conditions changed. The array was not designed to accommodate the change because no one asked that question when the change was being made. The recurring intervention point is not after installation. It is at modification planning. I have presented this at two guild sessions. It has not yet produced a change in the modification review process.

**Case 12 in category: Structural reinforcement failure, commercial building, mid-city district.**

Unusual case. The array was not installed to specification — it was installed to what the practitioner believed was specification, and the belief was wrong. Not negligence in the usual sense. The practitioner had a plausible reading of an ambiguously written specification document.

The interest is the failure mode. Most installation errors produce immediate or near-immediate indicators. This one held for three years and then degraded in the specific way that an array holds until the load exceeds the error tolerance and then does not hold. The three years of apparent function made the practitioner certain the installation had been correct.

Diagnostic process revealed the spec ambiguity within the first site visit. The conversation with the practitioner after took longer. People who have done good work for years and believe an installation is correct require different handling than people who know something went wrong.

Note: Specification language clarity is a documentation problem as much as a casting problem. Ambiguous specifications produce this case. Clear specifications produce Case 7. Neither can be fully prevented. Both can be made more or less frequent depending on whether the [[Trade Guilds|guild]] network takes documentation seriously at the specification-writing stage, not just the installation stage.
