# Council Review Board

The **Council Review Board** is [[The Council of Auralis]]'s internal dispute resolution and appeals body. It exists to handle cases where institutions contest compliance findings, where directives from different council bodies contradict each other, or where operational realities make formal compliance structurally impossible without council-level resource reallocation.

The Board does not overturn council policy. It decides what happens when policy collides with itself.

## Mandate

The Review Board's charter grants it authority to:
- **Adjudicate appeals** from institutions that dispute [[Council Compliance Directorate]] findings.
- **Resolve directive conflicts** when orders from [[Council Orders Registry]] create incompatible requirements for the same institution.
- **Recommend exceptions** to standing policy when compliance would create worse outcomes than non-compliance.
- **Refer systemic failures** to the full council when patterns indicate resource gaps or structural design problems.

The Board operates as a buffer between the council's high-level decisions and the operational chaos those decisions sometimes create. It is the institution that admits — quietly, in closed hearings — that the system does not always work as designed.

## How the Board Functions

The Review Board is composed of five permanent members appointed by the council for three-year terms:
- [[Helva Drost]] (Board Chair) — former district administrator from [[Hedun]], thirty-two years in governance roles.
- [[Soren Velk]] — ex-guild auditor, specialist in budget constraint analysis.
- [[Lenna Torv]] — expedition logistics coordinator, fifteen years at [[Emberfall]].
- [[Rett Massen]] — legal specialist in administrative procedure and institutional authority boundaries.
- [[Kess Varn]] — mid-tier caster with technical expertise in [[Flux]]-dependent infrastructure systems.

Cases come to the Board through formal petition. An institution files a review request, submits documentation of the disputed issue, and the Board schedules a hearing. Most cases are resolved in closed session without the full council's involvement. High-impact cases — where the Board's decision would affect multiple institutions or set precedent for future disputes — are elevated to council review with the Board's recommendation attached.

## Types of Cases

### Compliance Appeals

An institution contests a [[Council Compliance Directorate]] finding, arguing that:
- The original directive was ambiguous or technically unachievable.
- Conditions changed between the order's issuance and the inspection, making compliance impossible.
- The Directorate misinterpreted the evidence or applied the wrong compliance standard.

The Board reviews the original order, the inspection findings, and the institution's counter-documentation. It can uphold the finding (institution remains non-compliant), modify the finding (conditional compliance with adjusted corrective timeline), or overturn the finding (compliance determination was incorrect).

Most appeals result in modified findings. The Board rarely overturns Directorate conclusions outright — doing so would undermine the Directorate's authority — but it often softens timelines or accepts institutional explanations that the Directorate rejected.

### Directive Conflicts

Two council orders create incompatible requirements for the same institution:
- [[Basin Grain Allocation Board]] is ordered to increase district reserves by 10% while simultaneously reducing storage facility operating budgets by 8%.
- [[Emberfall Expedition Registry]] is required to accelerate permit approvals while also implementing more rigorous safety review protocols.

The Board cannot rewrite council orders, but it can issue **implementation prioritization rulings** that tell institutions which directive takes precedence when both cannot be satisfied. These rulings are technically advisory. In practice, they provide institutional cover: if compliance with Directive A forces non-compliance with Directive B, the Board's prioritization prevents the institution from being penalized for the gap.

### Exception Requests

An institution asks the Board to grant a formal exception to standing policy because compliance would create unacceptable risk or operational failure:
- [[Crestward Intake Bureau]] requests an exception to mandatory background verification timelines during a seasonal hiring surge.
- [[Solhaven Elevation Works Office]] asks for temporary suspension of routine maintenance schedules to focus on emergency infrastructure repair.

The Board can grant time-limited exceptions with conditions. Exception grants are logged and reported to the council quarterly. Too many exceptions from the same institution signals a systemic problem — either the policy is poorly designed or the institution is chronically under-resourced.

## What the Board Actually Does

On paper, the Review Board resolves disputes. In practice, it manages the gap between what the council wants and what [[Auralis]]'s operational institutions can deliver.

[[Helva Drost]] has chaired the Board for six years. She has seen the same patterns repeat:
- The council sets ambitious goals without proportional resource increases.
- Institutions stretch to comply, defer maintenance, cut corners on non-inspected processes.
- Compliance failures accumulate until someone files a Board appeal.
- The Board grants exceptions or softens timelines, temporarily relieving pressure.
- The council sets new ambitious goals. The cycle continues.

Helva writes an annual report summarizing Board case trends. The report consistently notes that **exception frequency is increasing**, **directive conflicts are accelerating**, and **the gap between council intent and institutional capacity is widening**. The council receives these reports. Policy does not adjust.

## Institutional Relationships

The Review Board operates in a deeply uncomfortable position:
- The [[Council Compliance Directorate]] sees the Board as too lenient — willing to excuse institutional failures that should result in stronger enforcement.
- Operational institutions see the Board as too cautious — unwilling to challenge the council's structural over-commitment of resources.
- The [[Council Orders Registry]] sees the Board as a cleanup mechanism for poorly-drafted orders, which is both accurate and irritating.

[[Vessa Rolt]] (Orders Registry Coordinator) and [[Helva Drost]] (Review Board Chair) meet monthly. Vessa provides advance warning when orders are likely to generate Review Board cases. Helva provides feedback on which types of order language create the most disputes. The collaboration is useful. It does not fix the underlying problem: the council wants more than the system can sustainably deliver.

## What the Board Knows But Cannot Say

The Review Board's case logs contain a record of every time the system bent rather than broke:
- 143 compliance appeals over three years (67% resulted in modified findings).
- 89 directive conflict cases (most resolved through prioritization rulings that effectively suspended one of the two orders).
- 201 exception requests (granted at a 74% rate, with average exception duration extending beyond the "temporary" framing used in approvals).

The Board's existence is proof that the council's governance model does not work as smoothly as council chambers suggest. Helva Drost knows this. She also knows that the Board's function — preventing small failures from cascading into system-wide collapse — is the only reason the model still operates at all.

She writes careful reports. She grants careful exceptions. She schedules the next hearing.
