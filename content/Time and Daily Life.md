**Time and Daily Life** describes how [[Solumora]]'s inhabitants measure time, coordinate activities, structure their days, and mark historical eras across the supercontinent. Time measurement is not neutral infrastructure — access to accurate timekeeping stratifies by class and wealth, creating practical advantages for those who can afford mechanical clocks and disadvantages for those relying on shadow estimation and meal rhythms. This is not melodramatic injustice but ordinary inequality: precise time costs money, and missing an appointment because "around noon" turns out to be 90 minutes off has different consequences depending on whether you are scheduling communal planting or negotiating a 10,000 [[Watts]] commercial contract.

## The Calendar System

**[[The Solumoran Calendar]]** is the unified timekeeping framework used across both [[Auralis]] and [[Terravelle]]. The system is not arbitrary cultural convention — accurate shared timekeeping prevents trade failures, missed meetings, and logistical disasters when coordination spans hundreds of miles and weeks of travel time. The calendar emerged from pre-[[The First Contact|Contact]] astronomical observations that both kingdoms independently developed and later discovered were functionally identical, which enabled immediate integration of time-based systems once Contact began.

### Structure

The calendar divides Solumora's **365-day orbital cycle** into **12 months of 30 days each** (aligned with the 30-day lunar cycle) plus **5 transition days** called Turndays at year-end.

**Month names** follow seasonal and agricultural patterns: **Colden** (mid-winter), **Thawmark** (late winter), **Firstgreen** (early spring), **Plantmoon** (mid-spring), **Brightward** (late spring), **Highdry** (early summer), **Harvestfirst** (mid-summer), **Peaklite** (late summer), **Harvestfall** (early autumn), **Coolward** (mid-autumn), **Darkmoon** (late autumn), **Frostfast** (early winter).

**Weeks** consist of 6 days: **Firstday** (work week begins), **Midweek**, **Thirdday**, **Longday** (heaviest work traditionally), **Marketday** (trade coordination, guild meetings), **Restday** (traditional rest, [[Ascendant Path]] observances, family time, minimal work).

Week structure is more strongly observed in [[Terravelle]] (Marketday and Restday culturally enforced) than [[Auralis]] (looser, work continues when necessary).

**Years** are counted from the **Foundation Era** origin point — an arbitrary administrative decision made during Contact negotiations as compromise between the kingdoms' previous incompatible dating systems. **Current year: 623 FE** (Foundation Era).

### Seasonal Patterns

Solumora's **equatorial desert** creates distinct climate zones with very different seasonal experiences:

**[[Equatorial Desert|Desert Zakros]] (Equatorial Region):**
- Consistent high heat year-round (40-50°C / 104-122°F daily highs)
- Minimal temperature variation seasonally
- Dry and wet periods replace traditional seasons (moisture availability cycles)
- Crossing windows determined more by [[Rift Incursions|rift activity patterns]] than seasonal weather

**[[Terravelle]] and [[Auralis]] (Temperate Zones):**
- Moderate seasonal variation
- [[Terravelle]] northern latitude: Colder winters (river ice, snow common, Darmarkarian winters harsh), moderate summers
- [[Auralis]] southern latitude: Milder winters (occasional frost, minimal snow), warm summers
- Spring: Planting season [[Terravelle]] ([[Maren Valley]] agricultural cycle begins Firstgreen through Plantmoon), rainfall increasing
- Summer: Warmest period, lowest rainfall, peak maritime activity, first harvests
- Autumn: Temperature declining, main harvest [[Terravelle]], winter preparation, maritime activity reduces
- Winter: Coldest period, stored food consumption, reduced external activity

**[[Wolfpoint]] (Northern Extremes):**
- Harsh winters with extreme cold, heavy snow, maritime water freezing (Colden through Firstgreen)
- Settlement isolation increases, focus on stored supplies
- Brief intense summers (Harvestfirst through Coolward) with rapid snow melt, hunting season peak ([[Mountain Game Collective]]), expedition/supply compression
- Transitional periods extremely short (spring thaw and autumn freeze-up measured in weeks)

**Maritime patterns:** Storm seasons affect [[Outer Coast Packet Line]] and coastal fishing operations. Spring storms (Thawmark through Plantmoon) highest frequency, summer (Highdry through Peaklite) lowest frequency (preferred shipping season), autumn/winter moderate storm activity. [[Maritime Storm Coordination]] operates year-round but most critical during transition periods.

### Historical Eras

**Foundation Era (1 FE - Present):**
Current era, origin point established retroactively during Contact negotiations as neutral compromise. Neither kingdom claims the era commemorates their specific history. Current year **623 FE**.

**Key historical dates (Foundation Era):**
- **423 FE** — [[The First Contact]] (first successful crossing [[Auralis]] expedition)
- **430-480 FE** — Early Contact period (trade establishment, cross-border relations develop)
- **492 FE, 540 FE, 580 FE** — [[The Three Near-Wars]] (border tensions nearly escalate to open conflict)
- **590-610 FE** — Stable period (trade flourishes, cross-border cooperation peaks)
- **610-623 FE** — [[The Fraying Peace]] (current tensions escalating, war preparation increasing)

**Pre-Foundation (Relative Dating):**
For events before Foundation Era, relative notation used: **"Years Before Foundation" (BF)** — [[The Isolation Period]] began ~400 BF, [[The Ancient Civilization]] collapse estimated 1000+ BF (extremely imprecise beyond 500 BF due to conflicting chronicles, lost records, measurement errors).

Both kingdoms used different systems historically ([[Terravelle]]: "Years Since Unity", [[Auralis]]: "Ascendant Count" via [[Ascendant Path]]) but these were abandoned formally during Contact negotiations. Historical records translated to FE retroactively, though conversion errors known to exist.

## Timekeeping Methods

**[[Timekeeping Technology]]** varies dramatically by class, wealth, and settlement type. Access to accurate timekeeping creates practical advantages and disadvantages across commercial, agricultural, maritime, and social coordination.

### Astronomical Methods (Accessible)

**Sundials:** Most common method. Public sundials in urban market squares, temple courtyards, guild halls. Middle-class households own fixed sundials (5-15 Watts), merchants carry portable versions with latitude adjustment (25-50 Watts). Accuracy ±15-30 minutes under clear skies. Useless during cloudy weather, nighttime, or near dawn/dusk.

**Lunar Phase Tracking:** Free observation method. 30-day lunar cycle visible across Solumora, month boundaries marked by new moon. [[Ascendant Path]] temples and [[Trade Guilds]] maintain formal lunar calendars. Agricultural planning, maritime tidal prediction, religious observances all rely on lunar tracking.

**Star Position Navigation:** Requires knowledge/training. [[Outer Coast Packet Line]] sailors, [[Desert Zakros]] expedition guides, rural travelers use star navigation routinely for nighttime timing (accuracy ±30-60 minutes). Urban populations rarely learn detailed star positions.

### Mechanical Methods (Expensive)

**Water Clocks:** Controlled drip mechanisms mark hours. Common in [[Ascendant Path]] temples, [[Trade Guilds]] halls, government offices, wealthy households. Cost 150-300 Watts (quality installation), accuracy ±10-15 minutes per hour. Require daily refilling, periodic cleaning, recalibration against astronomical observations. Some settlements have public water clocks visible to passersby.

**Mechanical Clocks:** Spring-driven or weight-driven gear mechanisms. Extremely rare, cost 800-2000 Watts (quality clock). Accuracy ±5-10 minutes per day, require weekly adjustment and expert clockmaker maintenance (50-100 Watts annually). Urban elite, major institutions, government offices only. Notable examples: [[Solhaven]] [[Crestward District]] public clock (audible bell chimes each hour), [[Valdenmoor]] Guild Council Chamber clock.

**Personal Watches:** Miniature mechanical timepieces worn on chain/pocket. Vanishingly rare, cost 2000-5000 Watts (extreme luxury item). Accuracy ±10-20 minutes per day, require daily adjustment and frequent expert repair (100-200 Watts per repair, 1-2 repairs yearly). Only wealthiest merchants, high-tier [[Flux Users]], senior officials, aristocratic families own watches. Extreme status symbol signaling wealth comparable to country estate ownership.

### [[Flux]]-Assisted Methods (Specialized)

**Flux-Oscillation Counters:** [[Control Tier|T3-T4]] practitioner creates stable oscillating flux field, counts oscillations for extreme precision (accuracy ±1-5 seconds per hour). Cost 50-100 Watts per hour (practitioner time). Laboratory and specialized applications only (experimental [[Flux Medicine|medical treatments]], alchemical processes, expedition planning, astronomical observations). Requires continuous practitioner attention, not practical for civilian daily use.

**Time-Binding [[Sigils]]:** [[Uncommon Grimoire]] technique (T2-T3 practitioners). Sigil activates after specified duration (cost 10-30 Watts per sigil). Accuracy ±5-15 minutes for durations under 24 hours, degrades for longer durations. Single-use, consumed upon activation. Middle-class occasional use (expensive dinner party, critical business meeting), upper-class routine use (appointment management, household staff coordination).

**Flux-Preserved Time Markers:** T3+ practitioner creates flux field degrading at known rate, field collapse indicates time elapsed. Cost 20-40 Watts per marker, accuracy ±20-40 minutes for 6-12 hour durations. Specialized technique used by expedition teams ([[Desert Zakros]] camp timing), remote work coordination (logging, mining, field surveys), military patrol timing. Single-use, expensive, vulnerable to environmental flux interference.

### Informal Methods (Universal)

**Shadow Length Estimation:** Free observation, anyone working outdoors learns this. Accuracy ±30-60 minutes for experienced individuals. Primary working-class timekeeping method. Requires sun visibility, not usable at night, difficult in northern latitudes ([[Wolfpoint]] extreme shadow angles).

**Work Rhythms and Meal Times:** Regular daily activities structure time implicitly. Meals (breakfast after sunrise, midday meal around noon, evening meal at sunset), work shifts, [[Tavern Culture and Social Drinking|tavern hours]], market activity, religious observances. Accuracy ±30-90 minutes. Working-class meal times highly regular (dictated by work schedules), middle/upper-class more flexible.

**Natural Indicators:** Environmental cues indicate approximate time (accuracy ±1-2 hours). Bird calls (dawn chorus, evening calls), temperature (coolest pre-dawn, warmest mid-afternoon), tidal cycles (coastal settlements), light quality (morning sharp shadows, afternoon warmth, evening golden light). Rural populations and outdoor workers rely heavily, urban populations less practiced.

## Social Stratification and Time

### Working-Class Time Experience

Primary methods: Shadow estimation, public sundials when available, work rhythms, natural indicators.

Precision level: "Morning", "around noon", "before dark" adequate for agricultural labor, communal work coordination, household routines.

Limitations: Cannot reliably coordinate activities requiring 30-minute precision. Disadvantaged in contract timing disputes ("did you deliver before noon?" becomes subjective without shared clock). Economic penalty for late arrival to time-sensitive day labor hiring.

Timekeeping budget: Essentially zero (public infrastructure and free observation only).

### Middle-Class Time Experience

Primary methods: Household sundials, portable sundials for travel, guild-maintained institutional water clocks, public mechanical clocks (urban), printed lunar calendars, occasional time-bind sigils.

Precision level: ±15-30 minutes adequate for business appointments, merchant negotiations, guild meeting coordination.

Limitations: Cannot afford personal mechanical watch (cost prohibitive). Cannot routinely use flux-assisted timing (reserved for critical needs only). Precision suffers during cloudy weather (sundials useless, must rely on cruder methods).

Timekeeping budget: ~5-10 Watts annually after initial sundial purchase (25-50 Watts one-time, lasts decades; lunar calendar 3-5 Watts annually; occasional time-bind sigils 30-60 Watts).

### Upper-Class Time Experience

Primary methods: Private household mechanical clocks, personal watches (minority even among wealthy), private water clocks (backup), routine time-bind sigils, hired practitioners for flux-oscillation timing (specialized).

Precision level: ±5-10 minutes daily (mechanical clocks with weekly adjustment). Personal watches enable portable precision and ostentatious wealth display.

Advantages: Never late to important meetings. Can coordinate time-sensitive business with hour-level precision (both parties have mechanical clocks). Social signaling (visible watch announces wealth).

Timekeeping budget: 300-800 Watts annually (excludes initial clock/watch purchase: mechanical clock 800-2000 Watts, personal watch 2000-5000 Watts; includes maintenance, time-bind sigils, occasional flux-assisted timing).

## Institutional Time Infrastructure

**[[Ascendant Path]] Temples ([[Auralis]]):**
Maintain water clocks and mechanical clocks (largest temples only). Announce month boundaries and significant astronomical events. Provide public time reference (sundials in courtyards, visible clocks, audible bells). Funding via temple tithes and [[Council Orders|Council]] grants (declining current period).

**[[Trade Guilds]] ([[Terravelle]]):**
Maintain water clocks in guild halls (shift coordination, meeting schedules). Distribute printed lunar calendars (annual guild service, 3-5 Watts). Coordinate market timing (opening/closing bells, auction schedules). Funding via guild dues and commercial transaction fees (pressured current period but more stable than temple funding).

**Government Offices (Both Kingdoms):**
Mechanical clocks and water clocks for record-keeping precision. Official calendar maintenance via astronomical observations. Public clock installations (urban settlements, high-visibility locations). Funding via tax revenue (stable but increasingly diverted to military).

## Calendar Use in Practice

### Agriculture

Calendar timing critical for [[Terravelle]] [[Maren Valley]] agricultural cycle:
- **Firstgreen through Plantmoon:** Planting preparation and execution (planting too early risks frost damage, too late reduces yield)
- **Brightward through Harvestfirst:** Crop growth, maintenance, irrigation
- **Harvestfall through Coolward:** Main harvest, processing, storage
- **Darkmoon through Thawmark:** Winter storage, equipment repair, planning

Farmers track calendar precisely — timing errors directly reduce yield and income.

### Maritime Operations

**[[Outer Coast Packet Line]]** schedule depends on seasonal storm patterns:
- **Highdry through Coolward:** Peak season (lower storm frequency, maximum trips)
- **Colden through Thawmark:** Reduced frequency (winter storms, ice hazards northern routes)
- **Thawmark/Plantmoon:** Storm season (dangerous, shipping reduced but not stopped)

Ships schedule departures around known storm seasons, cargo delivery dates contractually specified. Calendar accuracy essential for maritime commerce.

### Expedition Planning

**[[Desert Zakros]]** crossings less calendar-deterministic than maritime operations — [[Rift Incursions|rift patterns]] vary unpredictably. Historically **Thawmark through Brightward** shows slightly lower rift activity (better crossing window), **Harvestfall through Colden** shows increased rift frequency (worse window). Expedition departures planned months in advance (supply staging, guide coordination, equipment prep). Missing departure window delays expedition 30-60 days (waits for next guide availability, supply refresh).

### Legal and Commercial

Contract specifications require precise calendar notation:
- Delivery dates: "15 Harvestfall 623 FE" (precise, legally binding)
- Payment terms: "30 days from contract signing" (duration rather than absolute date)
- Lease agreements: "renewable annually 1 Colden" (predictable coordination)

Missed dates trigger penalties, defaults, [[Law and Order|legal disputes]]. Both kingdoms enforce time-stamped contracts.

### Personal and Social

Life events recorded by day/month/year: Births ([[Birth Runes|birth rune]] inscription includes date), marriages often scheduled Brightward or Peaklite (traditional, pleasant weather), deaths recorded precisely (legal, property transfer, family records).

**[[Festivals and Public Celebrations]]** scheduling: Major festivals have fixed calendar dates (kingdom celebrations, religious equinoxes, lunar events), agricultural festivals float (depend on actual harvest completion, roughly Harvestfall).

## Contemporary Timekeeping Crisis

### Practitioner Requisitions

T3-T4 practitioners increasingly conscripted for military applications (flux-oscillation timing for artillery, troop coordination). Civilian access to flux-assisted timekeeping collapsing (no practitioners available, costs skyrocketing). Time-bind sigil inscriptions declining (T2-T3 practitioners leave civilian practice for military work).

**Examples:** Alchemical laboratories closing (cannot obtain precise timing for temperature-critical processes), wealthy households hoarding existing time-bind sigils (replacement uncertain), medical treatments delayed (flux-assisted timing unreliable without practitioners).

### Clockmaker Conscription

Skilled clockmakers drafted for military work (timing mechanisms, artillery synchronization). Civilian mechanical clock maintenance deteriorating (fewer experts available, wait times months). Personal watches increasingly unrepairable (clockmakers prioritize military contracts). New clock construction essentially ceased (all skilled labor diverted).

**Examples:** [[Solhaven]] [[Crestward District]] public clock recently stopped (three-week delay finding clockmaker for repair), wealthy merchant's personal watch broken (no clockmaker available, watch useless paperweight), water clocks more common than mechanical (lower maintenance skill requirements, less military relevance).

### Institutional Funding Collapse

[[Ascendant Path]] temples receive less funding (public clock maintenance declining). [[Trade Guilds]] under pressure (maintaining guild clocks but cutting public services). Government-funded public clocks deteriorating (funds diverted to war preparation).

**Examples:** Smaller settlements lose public time infrastructure (sundials remain but mechanical clocks silent), lunar calendar printing reduced (fewer middle-class households can purchase), astronomical observation coordination declining (less cross-border cooperation).

### Social Consequences

**Coordination Degrading:** Business appointments less reliable (parties arrive at different times, disputes increase), [[Outer Coast Packet Line]] schedules less predictable (port coordination relies on shared clocks, accuracy declining), guild shift changes messier (water clock disruptions cause disputes over hours worked).

**Class Inequality Widening:** Middle-class loses access to institutional clock infrastructure (forced to cruder timekeeping methods), upper-class hoards personal watches and mechanical clocks (maintenance expensive but still obtainable with connections), working-class unaffected directly (already using crude methods, no infrastructure to lose).

**Economic Impact:** Contract disputes increase (contested delivery times, timing disagreements), productivity declines (shift coordination harder, labor market efficiency drops), maritime operations riskier ([[Maritime Storm Coordination]] requires shared time reference, coordination deteriorating).
