[[Greyveil]] is two cities occupying the same space. The first is a resource extraction town built on what the northern terrain produces — ore from the rock formations that rise through the settled belt's edge, timber from the last substantial forest stands before the treeline thins, the steady harvesting work that anchors any community to a place over generations. The second is a waypoint city, organized around the movement of people and goods through the last significant settlement before the map runs out. Most residents of Greyveil understand both cities and move between them depending on the season and what work is available.

The combination produces a particular character. Greyveil is self-sufficient in a way that [[Valdenmoor]] is not — the resource economy gives it reasons to exist independent of any road — but it is also far more oriented toward the transient than a purely extractive settlement would be. The result is a city that is rooted and permeable at the same time.

## Work

The extraction economy employs a substantial portion of Greyveil's permanent population. Mining operations in the rocky formations east and north of the city have run for generations. Timber operations in the western forest stands supply lumber both locally and for trade south. These are not prestigious industries in the Terravellian sense — guild certification exists but the work is physically demanding enough that demonstrated competence speaks louder than any credential. A miner who has worked the northern shafts for fifteen years does not need a guild endorsement to be trusted with the difficult work.

The trade economy layered on top of this is service-oriented. Inns, outfitters, repair services, the establishments that make a good living off travelers who have spent weeks on the northern road and need everything restocked. A merchant family running a waypoint inn in Greyveil occupies a comfortable position — the demand is consistent, the margins are reasonable, and the clientele is varied enough to make the work interesting. A craftsperson who can repair equipment that has failed in northern conditions, or who supply specialized gear that travelers realize they need before going further, does not struggle for customers.

[[Flux Users|Flux users]] in Greyveil tend toward practical applications — preservation work for the extraction industries, structural reinforcement in the mines, weather assessment before long northern runs. High-tier casting is rare and somewhat more notable here than in [[Valdenmoor]], where such things are at least familiar. A T5 in Greyveil is an event.

## The Permanent Population

The people who were born in Greyveil and intend to stay know something that most visitors do not register: that the city is more stable than it looks. The transit character of the trade economy makes it feel impermanent, full of people who are passing through. But beneath the flow of travelers, the mining and timber families have been here for generations. They own property. They know each other's children. They have the particular confidence of people who understand that when the travelers are gone, the city is still theirs.

This creates a mild but consistent social distinction between the extraction workers and the service economy. A family that runs mining operations does not dislike the inn families — the trade is good for everyone — but they understand themselves as the reason the city is here, and the service families as people who arrived later to take advantage of that reason. The distinction is rarely stated directly. It shapes who sits with whom at community events.

Children in Greyveil grow up understanding both economies. A miner's child knows that the northern road brings interesting things. An innkeeper's child knows that the rock and the timber will still be there when the interesting things have moved on. Most end up working in one economy or the other, with the occasional person who genuinely bridges both.

## The North's Presence

The northern road means that Greyveil absorbs everything that comes down from beyond the settled belt. Traders who have been to [[Wolfpoint]] and back. People who went north for various reasons and returned changed in ways they are not entirely able to explain. Occasional people who came from further north than most travelers go and are not entirely forthcoming about what that was like.

Greyveil has been processing the north for several generations. Most long-term residents have a working sense of [[Wolfpoint]] — what it is, who lives there, why people go, what they tend to bring back. This knowledge is practical and accurate in outline, vague in detail, and held without much mystification. The north is the north. People go. Some come back. The ones who come back buy supplies and occasionally have things to say.

There is a small, informal community of people who have spent time in Wolfpoint and returned with practical knowledge of [[Hypertext]]. These individuals are not a guild or an organization — they have no formal structure and no shared agenda. They are simply people who know something that most Terravellians do not, living in the city where that knowledge is least remarkable. They tend to recognize each other. They do not make a point of announcing themselves.

## What Greyveil Does to People

A person raised in Greyveil develops a specific relationship to impermanence. The flow of travelers through the city means that unusual is normal — unusual goods, unusual backgrounds, unusual reasons for being on the northern road. This produces an adaptability and a certain difficulty being surprised that makes Greyveil residents slightly alien to people from more settled cities. A Valdenmoor merchant visiting for the first time sometimes finds themselves disconcerted by how unbothered the locals are about things that would cause significant comment further south.

The extraction economy, meanwhile, produces a different trait — the directness of people whose work is physical and whose mistakes have immediate consequences. A miner who misjudges a shaft or a timber worker who misreads a felling does not get a second chance to think about it. This creates a population that values clear communication and is less interested in the social circumlocutions that a more institutional city develops.

The combination is notable. Greyveil residents tend to be direct without being aggressive, open to the unusual without being credulous, rooted without being provincial. They have learned, generation by generation, that the world is larger than their city and that the city is more permanent than the world gives it credit for.
