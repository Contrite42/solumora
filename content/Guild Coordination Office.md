# Guild Coordination Office

The **Guild Coordination Office** operates in [[Valdenmoor]], [[Ashford]], and [[Greyveil]] as the institutional mechanism that converts guild consensus decisions into executable directives. When a city's Guild Council reaches agreement on infrastructure priorities, regulatory shifts, or resource allocation, the Coordination Office translates that agreement into specific instructions for guild administrators, contractors, and operational bodies.

The Office does not make policy. It makes policy actionable.

## What the Office Does

Each city's Guild Coordination Office handles:
- **Directive translation**: Converting Guild Council decisions into detailed implementation plans (budgets, timelines, responsible parties, compliance metrics).
- **Cross-guild coordination**: Ensuring directives affecting multiple guilds are sequenced appropriately and don't create conflicting requirements.
- **Documentation maintenance**: Tracking active directives, completed work, pending issues, and coordination gaps requiring Guild Council attention.
- **Implementation monitoring**: Following execution progress, identifying bottlenecks, escalating problems that cannot be resolved at operational level.

The Office sits between guild leadership and guild practitioners — leadership decides what should happen, practitioners execute the work, and the Coordination Office ensures the connection between intent and execution functions smoothly.

## How It Works

### Directive Development

Guild Council decisions are often strategic rather than operational: "increase dock maintenance capacity," "improve scrivener authentication turnaround," "coordinate with Covenant for rural supply continuity." These statements need operational detail before anyone can act on them.

The Coordination Office drafts **implementation directives** specifying:
- **Who** executes the work (which guilds, which subdivisions, which individual coordinators).
- **What** gets delivered (infrastructure thresholds, service timelines, resource allocations, quality standards).
- **When** outcomes are due (phased schedules, milestone checkpoints, seasonal coordination).
- **How** success is measured (inspection criteria, reporting requirements, escalation triggers if problems arise).

Draft directives circulate to affected guilds for technical review. Guild specialists identify resource gaps, timeline conflicts, or ambiguities that would prevent execution. The Office revises based on feedback, balances competing priorities, and issues final directives when all affected guilds confirm feasibility.

### Cross-Guild Sequencing

Many Guild Council decisions affect multiple guilds whose operations must coordinate. Dock infrastructure upgrades impact both Maritime Guild operations and Trade Guild cargo handling. Scrivener authentication protocol changes affect grimoire merchants, research institutions, and customs operations.

The Coordination Office maintains a **sequencing log** tracking dependencies:
- Maritime Guild dock upgrades scheduled spring before fishing fleet returns.
- Trade Guild cargo rerouting begins after upgrades complete, not simultaneously.
- Customs training on new authentication protocols scheduled after Scrivener Guild finalizes standards, not before.

When sequencing conflicts arise — two guilds need the same resource simultaneously, or one guild's timeline delays another's work — the Office escalates to Guild Council for priority determination. Most conflicts resolve through negotiation before reaching Council level because guild coordinators prefer operational solutions to political decisions.

### Documentation and Tracking

The Coordination Office maintains three core records:
1. **Active Directives Log**: All current implementation directives, responsible parties, deadlines, progress status.
2. **Completion Registry**: Finished directives with outcomes documented, used for future planning and accountability.
3. **Coordination Gap List**: Problems identified during implementation that cannot be resolved operationally and require Guild Council attention.

These records are **accessible to guild leadership** but not public. Guild coordinators check the Active Directives Log to understand current priorities. Guild Council members review the Coordination Gap List before sessions to identify issues requiring decisions.

[[Tessa Vorn]] coordinates Valdenmoor's office, [[Renn Kolst]] runs Ashford's operation, and [[Miren Dalt]] manages Greyveil's smaller office. None has authority over guild operations — their function is coordination, not command. Their authority derives from guild consensus that coordination serves everyone's interests better than independent action.

## Valdenmoor Operations

[[Valdenmoor]]'s Coordination Office handles the largest volume of guild directives due to the city's size and economic complexity. Tessa Vorn's office includes:
- **7 permanent coordinators** covering major guild domains (maritime, overland trade, scrivener, thermal/structural, agricultural).
- **3 documentation clerks** maintaining directive logs, tracking completion, preparing Guild Council reports.
- **2 technical specialists** reviewing complex implementation plans for resource conflicts and timeline feasibility.

The office operates from a three-story building adjacent to Valdenmoor's Guild Council chambers. Coordinators attend Guild Council sessions to document decisions in real-time, then draft directives within **3-5 days** of Council votes. Urgent directives (emergency infrastructure repairs, crisis response coordination) are drafted same-day.

Valdenmoor processes approximately **180 directives annually** ranging from routine maintenance schedules to major infrastructure projects. Most directives (65%) complete within expected timelines. Delayed directives typically reflect resource shortages, weather disruptions, or discovery of unexpected complications during execution rather than coordination failures.

### Relationship with Guild Council

The Coordination Office reports directly to Valdenmoor's Guild Council and serves at Council discretion. Tessa Vorn attends Council sessions, presents coordination gap reports, and answers questions about implementation progress. She does not vote on Council decisions and cannot override guild leadership.

The relationship is functionally cooperative: Council needs the Office to translate decisions into execution, the Office needs Council authority to compel guild coordination. When the Office identifies implementation problems, Council decides whether to resource the solution or accept the gap.

Tension occurs when Guild Council decisions are politically satisfying but operationally impossible. Tessa documents that approximately **15% of directives** issued by Council require subsequent clarification or revision because initial decisions lacked technical feasibility assessment. The Office raises these concerns before finalization when possible, but Council sometimes votes before staff review completes.

## Ashford Operations

[[Ashford]]'s Coordination Office operates in a politically complex environment due to the city's border position and cross-kingdom trade concentration. [[Renn Kolst]]'s office includes:
- **5 permanent coordinators** (fewer than Valdenmoor due to smaller city size).
- **4 border-specific staff** handling coordination between guild directives and Crown customs operations.
- **2 documentation clerks** managing directive logs and Crown reporting requirements.

Ashford's office coordinates not just between guilds but also between guild operations and [[Eddan Voss]]'s limited Crown authority in external affairs. When Guild Council decisions affect [[Border Trade]], customs procedures, or cross-kingdom relations, Renn coordinates with [[Crown-Guild Liaison Office]] in [[Wolfpoint]] to ensure guild actions align with Crown policy without Crown micromanaging guild operations.

This creates additional complexity: directives affecting border trade must satisfy both guild operational needs and Crown diplomatic requirements. Renn's office drafts implementation plans, circulates them to affected guilds for technical review, sends them to Wolfpoint for external affairs assessment, revises based on Crown feedback, and issues final directives only after all parties confirm feasibility.

The process is slower than Valdenmoor's purely guild-internal coordination, but the alternative — guild decisions creating unintended diplomatic incidents or Crown decisions disrupting guild operations — serves no one. Renn coordinates **140 directives annually** with average development time of **5-8 days** (longer than Valdenmoor's 3-5 days due to Crown coordination requirements).

## Greyveil Operations

[[Greyveil]]'s Coordination Office is the smallest, reflecting the city's scale and agricultural focus. [[Miren Dalt]]'s office includes:
- **3 permanent coordinators** covering agricultural, overland trade, and civic domains.
- **2 documentation clerks** managing logs and Guild Council reporting.

Greyveil processes approximately **90 directives annually**, primarily focused on Agricultural Guild operations, seasonal resource allocation, and [[Covenant of Measure]] coordination for rural supply continuity. Miren's office handles substantial Covenant liaison work that Valdenmoor and Ashford offices do not encounter at similar scale because Greyveil's rural hinterland depends more heavily on Covenant practitioners for governance functions.

Coordination between guild directives and Covenant operations requires cultural translation: guilds operate through certification, contracts, and documented standards; the Covenant operates through community consensus, personal obligation, and informal networks. Miren's office translates between these models without forcing either institution to adopt the other's methods.

Greyveil's smaller scale creates faster coordination: average directive development time is **2-4 days** because fewer guilds are involved and consensus-building happens through personal relationships between guild coordinators who have worked together for years.

## Differences from Auralis Registry

[[Terravelle]]'s Coordination Offices serve similar functions to Auralis's [[Council Orders Registry]] but through fundamentally different institutional structures:

**Auralis Registry** is centralized. One institution in [[Solhaven]] translates all council decisions for the entire southern kingdom. [[Vessa Rolt]] signs every order, making her the single person institutions blame when directives fail.

**Terravelle Coordination Offices** are distributed. Three independent offices translate decisions for their respective cities. No central authority coordinates between them. Consistency emerges through shared guild standards and professional networks between coordinators, not hierarchical command.

**Auralis directives** are binding orders issued under council authority. Institutions must comply or face [[Council Compliance Directorate]] enforcement.

**Terravelle directives** are coordination plans issued under guild consensus. Guilds execute because they agreed to the decision, not because external authority compels compliance. The Coordination Office lacks enforcement power — it schedules, sequences, and documents, but cannot force guilds to act.

**Auralis Registry** absorbs friction between council ambition and institutional capacity. When execution fails, the Registry is structurally positioned to receive blame from both directions.

**Terravelle Coordination Offices** surface friction through coordination gap reporting. When guild decisions are operationally unrealistic, the Office documents the problem and returns it to Guild Council for resolution. Coordinators are facilitators, not administrators bearing responsibility for impossible execution.

## Resource Constraints and Failure Modes

Coordination Office effectiveness depends on staffing levels remaining proportional to directive volume. When Guild Council decision velocity increases without coordinator capacity expansion, backlogs develop.

[[Tessa Vorn]] documents that Valdenmoor directive volume increased **28% over 4 years** while coordinator headcount remained constant, producing longer development timelines. Average directive drafting time increased from 3 days to 5 days. Urgent same-day directives still meet targets, but routine coordination now takes longer.

Failure modes include:
- **Sequencing conflicts** not identified until mid-execution when two guilds discover they need the same resource simultaneously, requiring emergency re-coordination.
- **Documentation drift** where Active Directives Log falls behind actual work progress, reducing coordination visibility and creating duplicate efforts.
- **Technical review gaps** when complex directives are issued without adequate specialist assessment, producing implementation failures after launch rather than correction before finalization.
- **Cross-city coordination breakdown** when Valdenmoor, Ashford, and Greyveil offices pursue inconsistent priorities without mechanism for reconciliation.

The third failure mode is most concerning to Tessa. Guild Council can vote rapidly when political consensus exists, but technical feasibility assessment requires time. When Council wants visible action faster than staff review completes, directives sometimes issue before coordinators confirm whether execution is actually possible. These directives fail predictably, requiring subsequent revision or quiet abandonment.

## Wartime Pressures

Current conditions stress Coordination Offices in ways peacetime operations do not encounter:

**Increased directive velocity**: Guild Councils meeting more frequently as wartime priorities shift, producing more directives in shorter periods without additional coordinator capacity.

**Resource competition**: Multiple directives competing for scarce practitioners, materials, and infrastructure capacity simultaneously, creating sequencing conflicts that require extensive negotiation to resolve.

**Cross-kingdom coordination complexity**: Ashford office handling growing volume of directives affecting border trade and customs operations, requiring Crown coordination that slows development timelines.

**Precedent gaps**: Peacetime coordination precedent doesn't account for wartime disruptions. Coordinators drafting implementation plans for contexts where standard sequencing, resource assumptions, and timeline expectations no longer apply reliably.

**Documentation overload**: Coordination Gap Lists growing as more directives encounter implementation problems, requiring Guild Council attention at higher frequency than Council can effectively process.

Renn Kolst projects that Ashford directive volume will increase **another 15-20%** over the next year if current border tensions persist, requiring additional coordinator hires or acceptance of longer development timelines and reduced technical review thoroughness.

The offices coordinate informally — Tessa, Renn, and Miren meet quarterly to share coordination practices, discuss common problems, and maintain consistency where desirable. The coordination is professional rather than institutional: no Terravelle-wide Coordination authority exists to compel alignment, cooperation happens because coordinators share interest in maintaining guild system effectiveness.
