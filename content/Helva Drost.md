# Helva Drost

**Helva Drost** is the Chair of [[Council Review Board]], the institutional body that handles disputes, appeals, and exceptions when [[The Council of Auralis]]'s governance machinery collides with operational reality. She is fifty-seven years old, [[Control Tier|Tier 4]], and has spent thirty-two years in [[Auralis]] governance — twenty-four as a district administrator in [[Hedun]], eight running the Review Board.

She knows the system is over-committed. She manages the gap so it bends instead of breaking.

## What the Board Does

The Review Board exists to resolve problems the council does not want to see:
- Institutions that cannot comply with directives because the directives are impossible.
- Two council orders that contradict each other, forcing institutions to choose which failure they prefer.
- Compliance findings from [[Council Compliance Directorate]] that institutions believe are unfair or based on incomplete context.

Helva chairs the five-member Board through closed hearings, reviews the evidence, and issues rulings that either uphold the system's formal requirements or grant exceptions that admit the system is not working as designed. Most cases end in exceptions. The council does not discuss what this means.

## The Institutional Position

Helva's job is officially **dispute resolution**. Her actual job is **preventing small failures from becoming system-wide collapse**.

When [[Basin Grain Allocation Board]] cannot meet reserve targets because council budget cuts eliminated their warehouse capacity, the Board does not force compliance. It grants a time-limited exception, documents the resource gap, and reports the exception to the council quarterly. The council reads the report. Policy does not adjust. The exception becomes permanent through repeated renewals.

When [[Emberfall Expedition Registry]] is ordered to accelerate permit approvals while simultaneously implementing stricter safety reviews, the Board issues a **prioritization ruling** that tells the Registry which directive takes precedence. The ruling is technically advisory. In practice, it provides legal cover: if compliance with Order A forces failure on Order B, the Board's prioritization prevents penalties.

Helva has been running these interventions for six years. She is very good at preventing disasters. She is not preventing the underlying cause: the council wants more than [[Auralis]] can sustainably deliver.

## Case Patterns

The Review Board hears three main types of cases:

### Compliance Appeals
Institutions dispute [[Council Compliance Directorate]] findings, arguing the original directive was ambiguous or conditions changed after the order was issued. Helva's team reviews the original order from [[Council Orders Registry]], the inspection findings from [[Torn Hess]]'s team, and the institution's counter-documentation.

Most appeals result in **modified findings**. Helva rarely overturns Directorate conclusions outright — doing so would undermine the Directorate's credibility. But she often softens timelines, accepts institutional explanations Torn rejected, or reclassifies "material non-compliance" as "conditional compliance with extended corrective window."

Torn considers this too lenient. Helva considers it the only reason institutions still attempt compliance at all.

### Directive Conflicts
Two council orders create incompatible requirements. The Board cannot rewrite council policy, but it can tell institutions which directive to prioritize when both cannot be satisfied.

Example: [[Solhaven Elevation Works Office]] is required to complete emergency infrastructure repairs (council priority after a recent stability incident) while maintaining routine inspection schedules (standing directive from three years ago). The Board rules that emergency repairs take precedence, temporarily suspending inspection requirements.

The suspension is officially "time-limited." Helva has learned that "time-limited" means "until the next crisis forces another exception."

### Exception Requests
Institutions ask for formal waivers from standing policy because compliance would create unacceptable operational risk. Most exceptions are granted. Helva's criteria:
- Is the institution demonstrating good-faith effort, or are they seeking exceptions to avoid work?
- Is the exception request proportional to the constraint, or is it a structural capacity failure the council needs to see?
- Will granting the exception prevent a larger disaster, or just delay accountability?

The third question is the hardest. Sometimes delaying accountability is the right call — it buys time for resource reallocation or leadership replacement. Sometimes it allows dysfunctional institutions to continue failing without consequence.

Helva makes these calls. She does not always know if she made the right choice until months later.

## Annual Reports

Every year, Helva writes a summary report for [[The Council of Auralis]] documenting Review Board case trends. The reports are careful, precise, and written in neutral language that does not assign blame.

Key findings (repeated across multiple years):
- **Exception frequency is increasing.** Three years ago, the Board handled 47 exception requests. Last year: 89.
- **Directive conflicts are accelerating.** The council is issuing orders faster than institutions can integrate them, creating policy collisions the Board must resolve.
- **The gap between council intent and institutional capacity is widening.** More institutions are requesting exceptions not because they are failing to try, but because the directives are structurally impossible given current resources.

The council reads these reports. Factions cite whichever findings support their priorities. Policy does not adjust to address the underlying resource constraints. Helva writes the next annual report. The trends continue.

## Relationships

Helva operates in the uncomfortable middle:
- [[Torn Hess]] at [[Council Compliance Directorate]] sees the Board as too lenient, willing to excuse failures that should result in stronger enforcement.
- Operational institutions see the Board as too cautious, unwilling to challenge the council's structural over-commitment.
- [[Vessa Rolt]] at [[Council Orders Registry]] is the closest thing Helva has to a collaborator — they meet monthly to discuss which order structures create the most disputes. The feedback loop helps at the margins. It does not fix the systemic problem.

Helva respects Torn's commitment to neutral measurement. She also thinks rigid enforcement without operational context accelerates institutional collapse. They meet twice a year to discuss boundary cases. The meetings are professional. They agree on very little.

Her relationship with Vessa is more productive. Vessa provides advance warning when orders are likely to generate Review Board cases. Helva provides feedback on which directive language creates the most confusion. Both are trying to make an over-committed system survivable. Both know they are fighting symptoms, not causes.

## What She Knows But Cannot Say

Helva's case logs contain six years of documented system strain:
- 143 compliance appeals (67% resulted in modified findings, meaning the Directorate's initial determinations were too harsh given operational context).
- 89 directive conflict cases (resolved through prioritization rulings that effectively suspended one of the two council orders).
- 201 exception requests (granted at 74% rate, with most "temporary" exceptions renewed indefinitely).

The Review Board's existence is proof that [[The Council of Auralis]]'s governance model does not work smoothly. The Board's caseload acceleration is proof that the model is degrading.

Helva has never written a report stating the obvious: **the system is bending, and if resource gaps are not addressed, it will break.** She does not write this because it exceeds her mandate. The Board resolves disputes. Strategic sustainability analysis is someone else's job.

She writes careful reports. She grants careful exceptions. She schedules the next hearing. The gap widens.

## Why She Stays

Helva could retire. She has the pension, the fatigue, and the institutional knowledge to write a memoir that would embarrass several council members.

She stays because the alternative is worse. If someone less experienced runs the Review Board, exceptions will be granted too freely (institutional dysfunction gets rewarded) or too rarely (operational institutions collapse under impossible requirements). The Board is the only mechanism preventing the council's ambition from destroying the institutions required to execute it.

She is exhausted. She is also the only thing standing between "dysfunctional but survivable" and "systemic failure."

She reviews the next case. She drafts the ruling. She signs her name.
