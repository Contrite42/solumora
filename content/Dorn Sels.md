# Dorn Sels

**Dorn Sels** is professional wilderness naturalist and creature specialist based in [[Auralis]], holding institutional appointment with combined [[University of Emberfall|Emberfall research establishment]] and [[Council of Auralis]] practical ecology division. He is [[Control Tier|T4]], age 51, with 28 years documented field experience across desert, temperate, and maritime ecosystem zones.

## Professional History

Dorn's pathway to naturalist specialization emerged through non-traditional trajectory. He began career as [[Flux Users|Scholar]] trainee in [[Emberfall]] academic system (19 years old, motivated by family connection to academic networks rather than exceptional Flux capacity). After 8 years theoretical Flux study, he recognized his actual interest centered on understanding creature biochemistry and population dynamics rather than advancing theoretical spell architecture. Institutional constraints — [[Scholar Track]] advancement required commitment to higher Flux theoretical work, not practical biology — prompted his transition away from formal Flux education.

He spent following 12 years as independent naturalist: working with [[Mountain Game Collective]] population-assessment contracts, conducting creature documentation projects for [[Auralis]] institutional bodies, and building reputation as specialist capable of detailed field observation and pattern recognition across multiple ecosystem types.

At age 39, he secured formal institutional appointment combining [[Emberfall research]] position (half-time documentation and publication of creature biology findings) with [[Council of Auralis]] practical role (assessment of creature population health, evaluation of predator-control operations effectiveness, prediction of ecological impact from institutional decisions). This dual-appointment permits both research-publication work and practical policy application.

## Research Focus

Dorn's primary research interest centers on Flux-creature ecology interface: how creatures with measurable Flux sensitivity respond to changing Flux conditions, how creature populations relate to rift-activity patterns, and how human Flux-intensive activities affect creature ecosystem stability.

His most significant published works:

- **"Ripplecat Territory Density Correlation with Localized Flux Installations"** (4-part study, published 615-618 FE) documenting how ripplecat population density declined measurably in river zones receiving new permanent Flux-intensive defensive systems while density increased in nearby non-developed zones. Study provided first empirical evidence that ripplecat demonstrate measurable Flux-sensitivity severe enough to alter distribution patterns.

- **"Boneshell Crab Congregation Timing: Environmental vs. Institutional Variables"** (2-part analysis, published 620-621 FE) correlating boneshell congregation timing to whale-migration cycles (environmental) versus seasonal military deployments requiring maritime transport (institutional variables). Documented unexpected finding that congregation start-dates have shifted 3-5 weeks earlier in recent years correlating with increased military maritime activity disrupting normal oceanic current patterns.

- **"Predator Pack Social Organization: Communication Methods and Population Stability"** (ongoing, no publication yet, conducted in collaboration with [[Mountain Game Collective]] control operations specialists) documenting how predator-pack territorial communication functions, how pack-elimination operations disrupt social cohesion in surviving predator populations in adjacent territories, and predicting ecological cascades following predator-control interventions.

## Field Work Methodology

Dorn's field methodology reflects combination of disciplined observation technique and practical resource minimization. His typical field documentation process:

1. **Territory Selection:** Identifying specific ecosystem zone requiring study (often supplied by [[Council of Auralis]] or [[Mountain Game Collective]] requesting assessment). Selection considers ecosystem accessibility, population variance visibility, and contemporary human activity patterns.

2. **Baseline Establishment:** 4-8 weeks systematically surveying territory to establish baseline population estimates, distribution patterns, activity cycles. This stage requires extensive field time, minimal equipment, and detailed notation/mapping work.

3. **Variant Observation:** 8-20 weeks observing responses to environmental changes (seasonal transitions, human activity changes, institutional decisions generating ecosystem impact). Detailed field logs documenting creature behavior, population shifts, novel behaviors.

4. **Analysis and Publication:** 4-12 weeks conducting statistical analysis, correlating observations with institutional/environmental records, writing documentation for [[Emberfall]] scholarly publication and [[Council of Auralis]] policy briefing.

This cycle typically occurs 2-3 times annually depending on research question complexity and funding availability.

## Contemporary Role

Current role centers on assessing ecological impact from war preparation pressure. Dorn's assigned projects (official and informal) include:

- **Military Territory Requisition Ecology:** Assessing what creature population disruption results from military claiming hunting territories for encampment/training use. Initial findings suggest predator species (prowlpack, bloodshrike, ripplecat) suffer population disruption from increased human activity, requiring 3-6 month recovery periods when military presence ceases. Scavenger species (boneshell crab, certain bird populations) seem relatively unaffected.

- **Flux Installation Impact:** Documentation of how [[control Tier|T3+]] defensive infrastructure (permanent Flux field generators) affects creature populations. Comprehensive study already in progress but accelerated given military desire for baseline ecology understanding before deploying field-scale Flux systems in new regions.

- **Unsanctioned Harvesting Assessment:** Investigating whether wartime commodity-price surges on creature products (bloodshrike feathers, boneshell meat, ripplecat fur) are driving unsustainable harvest pressure. Preliminary data suggests 40-50% increase in harvest attempts by non-professional harvesters, with corresponding 25-30% increase in failed hunts and associated ecosystem disruption from wasted effort.

## Limitations and Institutional Tension

Dorn's work maintains substantial credibility within both academic and policy institutions, but faces persistent constraints:

**Funding Instability:** Research budgets have declined 35-40% over past 4 years as military pressure absorbs institutional resources. Current budget barely sustains 1-2 major field projects annually where 4-5 would be consistent with prior pace.

**Policy Application Gap:** Dorn's ecological assessments frequently identify problems (unsustainable harvest rates, territory disruption patterns, population decline indicators) but policy institutions prove reluctant to implement restrictive measures (harvest licensing, territorial protection designations, population preservation mandates) when these policies conflict with military resourcing needs or commercial economic interests.

**Personal Toll:** 28 years unrelenting field work in often-hostile ecosystems has produced cumulative physical wear. Dorn documents joint damage from repetitive carrying loads through rough terrain, respiratory issues from sustained exposure to high-Flux density zones, and vision degradation from extended work in desert glare conditions. He expects 8-10 more years maximum sustainable field work capacity before physical limitations force transition to purely institutional/supervisory roles.

## Interpersonal Standing

Dorn maintains reputation as rigorous, professional, and fundamentally honest in assessment. Colleagues respect his field methodology rigor and collaborative willingness to work with institutions holding different interests than pure academic knowledge acquisition. He does not advertise strong political positions on ecological issues, maintaining professional pragmatism regarding institutional constraints.

Creature-exploitation interests (fishing operators, hunting communities, military command) view him amb ambivalently. His research frequently identifies unsustainable practices, creating institutional pressure on exploitative sectors, but his recommendations remain moderate — he does not advocate extreme preservation stance, instead proposing sustainable-yield management frameworks. This generates respect from practical-minded harvesters even when they disagree with specific recommendations.
