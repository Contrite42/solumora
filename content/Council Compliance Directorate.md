# Council Compliance Directorate

The **Council Compliance Directorate** is [[The Council of Auralis]]'s enforcement and inspection arm. It verifies that council orders issued through [[Council Orders Registry]] are actually executed: that budgets reach their intended targets, that infrastructure projects meet their specifications, that regulatory shifts are implemented by the institutions required to follow them.

The Directorate does not make policy. It measures whether policy happened.

## Mandate and Scope

The Directorate's formal mandate is "verification of institutional compliance with council-issued directives across all operational domains within [[Auralis]]." In practice, this means:

- **Scheduled inspections** of district-level infrastructure, guild facilities, and expedition staging operations.
- **Audit reviews** of financial flows between the council treasury and operational institutions.
- **Compliance reports** documenting gaps between ordered outcomes and delivered results.
- **Escalation authority** to flag non-compliance for council review when institutions fail to meet directive requirements.

The Directorate operates independently of the institutions it inspects. It reports directly to the full council, not to individual factions, though factions interpret its findings through their own priorities.

## What Compliance Actually Means

"Compliance" is a technical term that hides constant negotiation. An order might require [[Basin Grain Allocation Board]] to maintain a 90-day reserve across all districts. The Directorate's inspection finds an 87-day reserve in [[Hedun]] and 92 days in [[Solhaven]].

Is this compliant? 

The aggregate meets the threshold. Individual districts do not. The Directorate must decide whether to report this as a pass with notes, a conditional compliance requiring corrective action, or a material failure demanding council intervention. [[Torn Hess]], the Directorate's senior compliance officer, makes these calls regularly. His decisions shape which problems the council sees and which problems stay buried in technical footnotes.

## Inspection Process

Directorate inspections follow a standard cycle:
1. **Notice period**: Institutions receive two weeks' advance warning of scheduled inspections (unscheduled inspections are rare and indicate prior compliance failures).
2. **Site review**: Inspectors assess physical infrastructure, interview operational staff, and request documentation of outcomes.
3. **Compliance assessment**: Inspectors compare observed conditions against the requirements specified in the original council order.
4. **Draft report**: Findings are documented with compliance ratings (full compliance, conditional compliance, material non-compliance).
5. **Institutional response window**: The inspected institution has five days to dispute findings or provide additional context.
6. **Final report**: The Directorate issues a binding compliance determination that enters the official record.

Most inspections result in conditional compliance — problems are noted, corrective timelines are set, but no immediate escalation occurs. This preserves institutional stability while documenting that the Directorate is doing its job.

## The Enforcement Gap

The Directorate identifies non-compliance. It does not fix non-compliance.

When an institution fails inspection, the Directorate can:
- Issue a formal non-compliance notice (public record, visible to the council).
- Recommend corrective actions with binding timelines.
- Escalate to the council for resource reallocation or leadership replacement.

What it cannot do:
- Directly allocate additional funding to fix capacity gaps.
- Override institutional leadership decisions in real-time operations.
- Compel other council bodies to prioritize the non-compliant institution's needs.

This creates a recurring pattern: the Directorate documents failures, institutions explain why the failures were inevitable given their constraints, and the council decides whether to resource the fix or accept the gap. Often, they accept the gap.

## Staff and Operations

The Directorate operates from a dedicated office building in [[Solhaven]], near the [[Council Orders Registry]] but institutionally separate. It employs:
- Twenty-three field inspectors (rotating assignments across [[Auralis]]'s districts and key operational sites).
- Six compliance analysts who translate inspection data into trend reports.
- Three senior officers ([[Torn Hess]], [[Mella Korss]], Dren Valt) who handle high-stakes cases and council-facing reporting.

Field inspectors are drawn from former guild administrators, ex-military logistics officers, and mid-tier casters with technical expertise in the domains they inspect. They are trained to document accurately, not to solve problems. The Directorate's credibility depends on neutral observation.

## Institutional Relationships

The Directorate is not popular.

Operational institutions see inspections as adversarial by default — external judgment imposed by people who do not face the daily constraints of making council orders work with inadequate resources. [[Emberfall Expedition Registry]] has a standing complaint that Directorate inspectors do not understand desert risk timelines. [[Basin Grain Allocation Board]] argues that compliance metrics ignore seasonal variability that makes 90-day reserves structurally impossible in certain quarters.

The [[Council Orders Registry]] has a complicated relationship with the Directorate: when orders are poorly designed and institutions fail to comply, both bodies can claim the other should have prevented the problem. Vessa Rolt and Torn Hess meet quarterly to negotiate whose responsibility specific failures represent. These meetings are professionally civil and accomplish very little.

## What the Directorate Knows

The Directorate sees patterns the council does not discuss:
- Expedition permit approval timelines from [[Emberfall Expedition Registry]] have degraded 18% over three years while expedition quotas increased 27%.
- Infrastructure maintenance cycles in [[Solhaven Elevation Works Office]] are now reactive rather than preventive — systems are repaired after failure, not before.
- [[Basin Grain Allocation Board]]'s reserve calculations have been "conditionally compliant" for nine consecutive quarters, meaning the system is perpetually one bad harvest away from critical shortage.

Torn Hess includes these patterns in annual summary reports. The council reads them. Policy does not shift to address the underlying resource gaps. The Directorate continues documenting the slow drift toward structural failure.
