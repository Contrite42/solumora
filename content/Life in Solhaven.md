[[Solhaven]] is the most ambitious city in [[Solumora]] and one of the most uncomfortable to live in if you are not already comfortable. Its famous vertical architecture is also its social architecture. The harbor district at the base is where the city's labor happens. The Crestward at the top is where the city's decisions happen. The terraces between are a long, granular negotiation between these two facts, and most Solhaven residents spend their lives somewhere in the middle of it without ever resolving which direction they are actually moving.

## The Harbor District

The harbor is where the city starts and where most of its population lives. Roughly a third of Solhaven's million residents work within three streets of the waterfront — dockworkers, fish traders, warehouse staff, boat maintenance crews, rope-makers, sail-repairers, the entire support structure of a port that processes a significant fraction of [[Auralis]]'s maritime trade. The buildings here are dense and low, cut off from the upper city's light by the terraces above, and they have the particular quality of places that have been repaired too many times to have any original material left.

A dockworker in the harbor district earns a wage that covers rent, food, and not much else. The boarding houses that line the streets inland from the waterfront are full of single workers who came to the city from somewhere smaller with more optimism than plan. Some of them stay and build something. Most of them cycle through.

[[Control Tier]] in the harbor district is low by city standards — the work does not attract high-tier casters, and the [[Auralis]] soft hierarchy has pushed lower-tier workers toward the bottom of the terrace system over generations. T0 is common here. T2 is average. A T4 who ends up in the harbor district is usually either passing through, has made a significant mistake, or is hiding from something.

The [[Ascendant Path]] operates a small temple at the harbor's northern end. It is the least prestigious temple in the city and the most practically useful one — it offers a basic infirmary, a children's tier-assessment service, and a weekly food distribution that the Luminants do not advertise in their official publications but have been running continuously for forty years. The people who work the harbor know the temple less for its theology than for these services.

## The Middle Terraces

The terraces between harbor and Crestward are not one thing. They are the city's working middle — administrative scribes, guild masters, tier-certified Formulists, mid-tier merchants, the management layer of every institution that runs through Solhaven. Housing here is better: private rooms, some buildings with structural [[Sigils]] that keep them dry, a few streets that have never smelled like the harbor even on wet days.

The [[Control Tier|tier]] distribution widens here. T2 through T4 is the normal range. Higher tiers are seen with enough frequency to be unremarkable. The [[Ascendant Path]]'s middle terrace temples are the functional ones — formal ceremony, tier celebration events, the social occasions that mark a family's progress through the city's hierarchy.

The middle terraces are where Solhaven's ambition is most visible and most frustrated simultaneously. Everyone here is above the harbor but below the Crestward. Most of them are aware of both distances. The families who have been on the same terrace for two generations tend to be settled and practical about it. The ones who arrived from somewhere lower and managed to climb tend to be vigilant in ways that the settled families find slightly exhausting.

A T5 assessment in a middle-terrace family is a significant event. The Ascendant Path registers it. The guild halls notice. The family receives visits. The child's trajectory changes, and whether the child is consulted about this is highly variable.

## The Crestward

The Crestward operates on a different logic than the rest of the city. It is not insulated from the harbor — the food comes up, the labor comes up, the information about what is happening in the kingdom comes up — but the daily texture is entirely different. Residents of the upper Crestward do not interact with the harbor district directly. They interact with intermediaries who interact with intermediaries.

The residences are large, quiet, and designed to be impressive to other people who live in large, quiet, impressive residences. The social events are the work. At a significant Crestward gathering, the conversations in the corners are doing more political labor than most people will accomplish in a year of formal council sessions, and everyone present understands this.

[[Fennick]] finds the Crestward gatherings specifically interesting. The tier density is high enough that the conversations are fast and the stakes are real, and the combination of genuine intelligence, genuine ambition, and genuine social performance produces an atmosphere he has found nowhere else on the continent. He has attended three such gatherings in the past decade. On each occasion, at least two other people in the room were not human. None of them are aware of each other.

## What Solhaven Does to People

A child who grows up in the harbor district and is assessed at T0 or T1 will understand their position in the city clearly by the time they are twelve. The information is not delivered harshly. It does not need to be. The information is delivered by the city's entire visible structure — who lives where, who works what, who gets recognized at the temple and who gets a meal — and it lands with the particular weight of things that have never been said aloud and therefore cannot be argued with.

A child who grows up in the middle terraces and is assessed at T4 or T5 will understand their position differently — as something to develop, to deploy, to translate into motion upward. The ambition is not considered unseemly here. It is considered appropriate. The Ascendant Path's theology confirms it. The city's architecture makes it visible. The gap between where they are and where they could be is framed as a distance worth crossing, and the question is only how fast.

The children who do not fit either framework — the T5 assessed in the harbor district, the T0 born into a middle-terrace family — carry something heavier than ambition or acceptance. They carry the sense that the city's system has made an error about them specifically, and this is correct, and the city will not adjust.
