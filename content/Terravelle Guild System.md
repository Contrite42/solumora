In [[Terravelle]], [[Flux Users|Flux]] education operates through guild-structured apprenticeship rather than institutional schooling. Knowledge passes directly from practicing masters to working apprentices, embedded in live professional contexts where mistakes carry real consequences.

## Structure

[[Terravelle]]'s guild system divides Flux instruction across specialized trade guilds rather than centralized academies. Each guild controls training and certification within its domain. The **Scribe Guild** teaches [[Grimoires|grimoire]] reproduction. The **Thermal Guild** teaches heat and cold management for industrial and domestic applications. The **Warden Guild** teaches security, detection, and barrier work. The **Maritime Guild** teaches navigation, weather reading, and ship-focused [[Sigils|sigil]] work. The **Agricultural Guild** teaches crop protection, pest deterrence, and soil chemistry.

There is no universal curriculum. What you learn depends on which guild accepts you, and guilds accept based on demonstrated interest, family connection, or direct recommendation from existing members. A prospective apprentice does not choose their education path by filling out an application form. They approach a guild through personal introduction, demonstrate willingness to work, and prove they will not waste a master's time.

Apprenticeship is not classroom instruction. It is labor. An apprentice to a Scribe Guild master spends the first year copying notation under supervision, preparing ink, and maintaining tools. An apprentice to a Thermal Guild master works on job sites, carries equipment, observes installations, and performs preparatory work while the master handles actual casting. Learning happens through observation, correction, and gradual delegation of responsibility as competence is demonstrated.

## Admission

Guilds do not conduct formal entrance exams. Prospective apprentices are evaluated through work samples and personal introduction. A young person hoping to join the Scribe Guild might present a carefully copied page of notation to demonstrate precision. Someone seeking Thermal Guild training might approach a working Formulist on a job site, offer to assist without pay, and prove through repeated presence that they are serious.

Family lineage matters. A child of a guild member has automatic consideration. A nephew or cousin of a working master is more likely to be accepted than a stranger. This is not nepotism in the sense of unearned advancement — family members still have to demonstrate competence — but it is gatekeeping. Guilds prefer to train people whose families already operate within their professional networks because those families understand the standards, risks, and expectations.

Strangers can enter guilds, but the path is harder. They need a sponsor — someone already in the guild willing to vouch for them and accept responsibility if the apprentice fails or misbehaves. Sponsorship is not given lightly. A master who sponsors a poor apprentice damages their own reputation within the guild.

[[Control Tier]] is assessed informally during early apprenticeship. Masters watch how much Flux an apprentice can handle safely and assign work accordingly. A [[Control Tier|T0]] apprentice in the Scribe Guild is not rejected — copying does not require personal casting ability. A T0 apprentice in the Thermal Guild will be steered toward preparatory and logistics work rather than direct casting roles. A [[Control Tier|T3]] apprentice with strong control will be advanced faster and given more responsibility.

Apprenticeship is not paid in [[Watts]]. Apprentices receive room, board, and eventual guild membership. The master benefits from the apprentice's labor. The apprentice benefits from the master's knowledge and professional network. Money enters the arrangement only after the apprentice progresses to journeyman status and begins taking independent contracts.

## Teaching Methods

Instruction happens on the job. A Scribe Guild apprentice learns notation by copying thousands of pages under a master's correction. A Thermal Guild apprentice learns thermal regulation by watching a master install heating sigils in residential buildings, then gradually assisting, then eventually handling simpler installations under supervision.

Mistakes are corrected immediately and bluntly. If an apprentice copies a [[Sigils|sigil]] incorrectly, the master does not schedule a review meeting — they stop the apprentice, point out the error, explain why it fails, and require the apprentice to redo it correctly before moving forward. If an apprentice misfires a spell on a job site, the master intervenes, assesses what went wrong, decides whether the apprentice was careless or under-prepared, and adjusts supervision accordingly.

There are no formal examinations. Competence is demonstrated through successful execution of real work. An apprentice advances when their master judges them ready, and that judgment is based on observed performance across months or years, not a single test.

**Advancement Stages:**

- **Apprentice** (2-7 years): Works under direct supervision. Does not take independent contracts. Learns through observation, correction, and gradually increasing responsibility.
- **Journeyman** (5-10 years): Can take independent low-tier contracts but remains affiliated with the guild and their original master. Builds personal reputation and client base.
- **Master** (10+ years minimum): Fully independent practitioner with the standing to train their own apprentices. Guild members recognize them as competent to represent the guild's standards.

The timeline is not fixed. A talented apprentice with strong [[Control Tier]] might reach journeyman status in three years. A slower learner might take ten. Masters do not graduate apprentices based on time served. They graduate apprentices when the apprentice can perform guild-standard work without supervision.

## Guild Standards

Each guild maintains internal quality standards, and those standards are enforced through reputation rather than regulation. A master who graduates an incompetent journeyman damages their own standing. A journeyman who takes contracts beyond their ability and fails publicly reflects poorly on their master and their guild.

Guilds do not issue certifications in the [[Auralis]] sense. A [[Terravelle]] Formulist does not carry a document proving they passed standardized tests. They carry the reputation of their master, their guild affiliation, and the referrals of clients they have worked for successfully. That reputation structure works because [[Terravelle]]'s professional networks are smaller and more interconnected than [[Auralis]]'s urban labor markets.

When a Terravelle guild member misbehaves — takes contracts they cannot handle, produces substandard work, injures someone through negligence — the guild handles correction internally. Minor failures result in reduced standing and fewer referrals. Serious failures result in expulsion, which effectively ends a practitioner's career within that trade. Someone expelled from the Thermal Guild cannot simply move to another city and start over — guild networks communicate, and expelled members are known.

## Cultural Perspective

[[Terravelle]] views guild apprenticeship as superior to [[Auralis]]'s institutional model, though the reasons given vary depending on who is speaking. Conservative voices argue that apprenticeship preserves traditional knowledge and prevents dangerous experimentation. Practical voices argue that learning on live job sites produces more adaptable practitioners than classroom instruction. Family-oriented voices argue that guild structure keeps professional knowledge within trusted lineages rather than distributing it to strangers through tuition payments.

All three perspectives share an underlying distrust of [[Auralis]]'s meritocratic approach. [[Terravelle]] does not believe that anyone willing to pay tuition should have access to Flux education. Guilds gate access deliberately, and that gatekeeping is considered a feature, not a flaw. Flux is powerful, dangerous, and socially consequential. Teaching it to people who lack family grounding, professional sponsorship, or demonstrated commitment is viewed as reckless.

[[Auralis]] views Terravelle's guild system as exclusionary, nepotistic, and economically inefficient. Aldrane Academy can train 800 students per year across multiple tracks. Terravelle's entire guild system produces perhaps 200-300 new practitioners annually because apprenticeship does not scale. From an Auralis perspective, that bottleneck limits [[Terravelle]]'s economic development and concentrates power in entrenched family networks.

Both assessments are correct within their own value frameworks.

## Limitations

Guild apprenticeship produces practitioners who are highly adaptable within their specific trade but often lack broad theoretical knowledge. A Thermal Guild master can solve novel heating problems through improvisation and experience, but they may not understand the underlying Flux theory well enough to explain why their solution works. A Scribe Guild master can copy any notation accurately but may struggle to design new [[Sigils|sigils]] because they were never taught sigil theory — only notation reproduction.

The system also reinforces geographic and social immobility. A young person born in a [[Wolfpoint]] fishing family has access to Maritime Guild apprenticeships through family networks. That same person has almost no access to Scribe Guild or Scholar networks unless they relocate to [[Solhaven]] or [[Valdenmoor]] equivalent settlements and spend years building new connections. Talent alone does not override geography and family context in [[Terravelle]]'s guild structure.

Finally, the system is vulnerable to poor masters. In [[Auralis]], a bad instructor at [[Aldrane Academy]] affects one cohort of students until they are replaced. In [[Terravelle]], a bad master affects every apprentice they train across decades, and because guild standards are enforced through reputation rather than oversight, a master's incompetence may not be recognized until multiple apprentices fail publicly.

## Comparison with Auralis

[[Auralis]] produces more practitioners. [[Terravelle]] produces practitioners who are more embedded in professional networks and better at improvisation. [[Auralis]] practitioners are trained to execute documented procedures reliably. [[Terravelle]] practitioners are trained to solve problems that do not have documented procedures.

Neither system is objectively superior. They optimize for different goals: [[Auralis]] optimizes for scale, standardization, and meritocratic access. [[Terravelle]] optimizes for quality control, professional continuity, and gatekept expertise.

When practitioners from both systems work together — as occasionally happens in cross-border trade, desert crossing expeditions, or joint [[Flux]] research — the differences are immediately visible. The Auralis practitioner has better theoretical grounding and follows safety protocols rigorously. The Terravelle practitioner adapts faster to unexpected conditions and knows how to improvise without supervision. Both recognize the other's strengths. Both notice the other's weaknesses.
