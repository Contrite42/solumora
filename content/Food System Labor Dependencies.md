**Food System Labor Dependencies** documents the T0-T2 workers who perform every stage of food production, processing, transport, storage, and retail. Without this labor layer, food systems collapse. Most food workers earn subsistence wages (3-15 Watts daily), lack job security, and experience seasonal unemployment. They are the infrastructure that makes ordinary survival possible; they are also the most vulnerable to disruption.

## Production Labor

Food systems depend on agricultural workers, fishers, and herders.

### Agricultural Workers

The [[Maren Valley]] produces [[Terravelle]]'s primary grain supply through work by 20,000-30,000 seasonal and permanent agricultural workers.

**Seasonal workers (spring-autumn):** Hired for planting (Firstgreen-Plantmoon), maintenance (Brightward-Harvestfirst), and harvest (Harvestfirst-Coolward). Earn 10-20 Watts daily during season, zero during off-season. Income totals ~500-800 Watts annually for 5-6 months work. Most depend on additional winter work (urban labor, trade work, craft work) to earn 6 months of subsistence.

**Permanent workers:** Farm field hands, irrigation specialists, maintenance crews. Earn 8-12 Watts daily year-round, with seasonal surges to 15-18 during peak labor periods. Total ~2500-3500 Watts annually. Slightly better security than seasonal (year-round employment) but still below comfortable living.

**Harvest labor bottleneck:** Historic harvest yields depend on speed of work (grain must be cut before rainfall damages standing crop, threshed before spoilage). Labor shortage translates to crop loss. Documented losses (poor harvest due to insufficient labor) run 5-15% of yield in low-labor years, typically during conscription periods when military needs claim working-age men.

### Fishing Labor

Coastal fishing employs 1000-2000 active fishers and 2000-3000 processing workers supporting maritime-based food production.

**Fisher earnings:** Highly variable (weather-dependent). Good season (summer): 15-25 Watts daily. Poor season (spring, autumn): 5-10 Watts daily. Winter conditions force many fishers into alternative work (repair, craft, urban labor) earning 3-8 Watts. Average annual 800-1200 Watts, concentrated in summer months.

**Processing workers:** Clean, preserve, salt, dry fish for distribution. Earn 8-12 Watts daily, working year-round in processing that begins immediately on catch processing (fish spoils in hours without processing). Processing labor bottleneck: fish quantity exceeds processing capacity 2-4 weeks in summer; excess fish lost to spoilage, reducing total available supply. Capital investment in additional processing facilities (buildings, equipment, salt acquisition) is limiting factor.

**Risk profile:** Fishing involves casualty risk (drowning, storms, equipment accidents). Injury rates run 4-8 per 100 fishers annually. High-risk periods (spring, autumn storms) see casualty clusters. Injured fishers often cannot return to work but have no compensation system — injury means loss of income and often permanent income reduction.

### Herding and Dairy Labor

Mountain herding (goat, sheep) and lowland dairy operations employ 500-1000 workers producing milk, cheese, and meat.

**Herder wages:** 5-10 Watts daily, highly seasonal (calving spring through summer surge to 12-15 Watts; winter maintenance 3-5 Watts). Total 600-800 Watts annually. Safety risk includes predators (known annual casualty rate 1-2 per 100 herders).

**Dairy workers:** Milk processing, cheese making, cream separation. Earn 7-12 Watts daily, working year-round (dairy production requires daily processing). Skills matter: experienced cheese makers earn 15-20 Watts; trainees 5-8. 

## Processing and Preservation Labor

The transformation of raw material (grain to flour to bread; fish to preserved and salted product; vegetables to preserved forms) requires large labor forces.

### Grain Processing

Milling, baking, and grain handling.

**Millers and mill workers:** Operate watermills (Maren River powered) or horse/hand mills (smaller settlements). Earn 10-15 Watts daily, working year-round at variable intensity (post-harvest surge as grain processed). 

**Bakers:** Small bakeries (neighborhood level) employ 1-3 bakers plus apprentices/helpers. Baker earnings 15-25 Watts daily (skill-dependent); helper earnings 5-8 Watts. Baking is dawn-start work (bread baked before morning sale). Physical strain high (standing, kneading heavy dough, heat exposure).

**Bottleneck:** Available milling capacity limits how much flour the system can produce. Historic grain surpluses have sometimes exceeded milling capacity, leaving grain unprocessed. Capital intensive (watermills very expensive to build/maintain).

### Fish and Meat Processing

Salting, smoking, drying for preservation.

**Preservation workers:** Earn 8-12 Watts daily doing physically taxing, unpleasant work (salt chemical residue, smoke inhalation, heat from smoking). Work is seasonal intensity (peaks during harvest, drops to 3-4 Watts daily maintenance work in winter). Occupational health issues common: respiratory problems from smoke exposure, chemical burns from salt, heat stress.

**Supervision:** Preservation operations (salting, smoking) require experience to execute correctly. Supervisors earn 18-25 Watts daily.

### Produce Preservation

Drying, salting, fermenting vegetables for winter storage.

**Preserverworkers:** Typically women and children earning 3-8 Watts daily doing detailed work (selecting vegetables, preparing, arranging for drying). Seasonal surge (autumn Harvestfall-Coolward) to 10-15 Watts as all vegetables must be preserved before spoilage.

## Transport and Storage Labor

Food moves from production areas to storage and distribution points.

### Transport Workers

Wagon drivers, porter teams, dock workers moving food along supply chains.

**Wagoners (land transport):** Earn 12-18 Watts daily during active season (spring-autumn when roads passable), 0-5 Watts during winter (minimal carting). Seasonal employment, total ~600-900 Watts annually. High-skilled wagoners (long-distance trade routes) earn 20-30 Watts.

**River boatmen:** Transport grain down [[Maren River]] to [[Ashford]] for export. Earn 10-15 Watts daily during navigation season (Plantmoon-Coolward, ~7 months), 0 during ice freeze. Total ~700-1000 Watts annually. Dangerous work (drowning risk, current accidents); estimated 2-4% annual casualty rate during peak season.

**Port dock workers:** Load/unload ships, move cargo in warehouses. Earn 8-12 Watts daily, working year-round at variable intensity (high during shipping seasons, low during rough weather/ice). Many are casual labor, hired day-by-day, subject to weather and supply demand variation.

### Storage and Warehouse Labor

Granary keepers, storage facility managers, warehouse workers.

**Specialized storage workers (grain):** Granary keepers maintain [[Basin Grain Allocation Board]] warehouses and merchant-house grain stores. Earn 12-18 Watts daily managing inventory, preventing spoilage, verifying stock. Positions are somewhat more secure than casual labor (ongoing need for storage maintenance).

**Warehouse workers:** Load, move, organize stored goods. Earn 8-12 Watts daily, working year-round at relatively stable intensity. Compared to dock work, more predictable schedule and consistent employment.

**Risk:** Grain dust inhalation (causes respiratory disease over time), injuries from falls/crushing, exposure to rodents/insects maintaining stored grain.

## Retail and Market Labor

Markets and food retail require workers at every management level.

### Market Handlers and Salesworkers

Transport goods from wholesale suppliers to market stalls, arrange displays, work as market vendors.

**Handler wages:** 6-10 Watts daily, working set hours during market operation. Seasonally variable (peak during harvest crunch, reduced during winter). Total ~1200-1600 Watts annually from market work alone, often supplemented with other seasonal work.

**Vendors (independent):** Small-scale merchants, farmers, and processors who directly retail fish, vegetables, preserved goods. Income highly variable (depends on sales, which depends on competition and customer demand). Good day might earn 15-25 Watts; poor day 2-5 Watts. Average vendor takes home ~400-800 Watts annually from market sales alone.

### Tavernkeepers and Food Service Workers

Taverns employ cooks, servers, kitchen helpers, maintenance workers.

**Kitchen workers:** Chefs and experienced cooks earn 15-25 Watts daily. Kitchen helpers (learning positions or unskilled) earn 5-10 Watts daily. Taverns operate 6-7 days weekly, providing more consistent employment than seasonal food labor. Positions have higher stability than casual market labor.

**Servers and attendants:** Earn 8-12 Watts daily in tips and wages, working evenings and nights. Opportunity for income supplement (tips, informal second employment). Higher social mobility than production labor (can learn business operations, gain management experience).

### Quality Control and Inspection

Grain inspectors, weighers, food safety assayers.

**Official weighers:** Licensed by guilds, position offers 15-25 Watts daily work plus fees for verification services. More stable than casual labor; requires training to achieve certification.

**Inspectors:** Assess quality, prevent fraud, verify weights. Earnings 15-30 Watts daily depending on certification level and experience.

## Labor Disruption Cascades

Food systems break when labor becomes unavailable.

### Conscription and Labor Loss

War mobilization conscripts working-age men into military service. Estimated military conscription claims 5-10% of able-bodied working men during wartime mobilization. Impact on food labor:

**Harvest labor loss:** Peak harvest (Harvestfirst-Coolward) coinciding with military mobilization can result in 15-30% reduction in available harvest labor. Grain left in fields (cannot harvest quickly enough with reduced workforce) rots. Estimated loss runs 5-10% of potential harvest in moderate conscription scenarios, up to 20-30% in heavy conscription years without exception policies.

**Transport labor depletion:** Best wagoners and boatmen conscripted. Remaining transport capacity drops 20-40%; shipping delays increase 10-20 days. Food spoils in transit or warehouses due to transport delays.

**Result:** Net 10-25% reduction in food reaching civilian populations during war years, even before accounting for military requisition quotas.

### Plague and Illness

Disease outbreak among food workers creates production gaps. Preserved fish spoilage if processors cannot process catch before spoilage (24-48 hour window). Documented plague outbreak in [[Solhaven]] (autumn 618 FE) temporarily disabled 30-40% of dock workers; spoilage losses estimated 800-1200 pounds of fish (~5-10% of month's supply) during 3-week outbreak peak.

### Weather and Seasonal Injury

Cold injury (frostbite from winter work), heat stress (summer processing work), and physical strain create occupational disability. Annual disability rate for food workers estimated 3-5% (workers temporarily unable to work due to injury or illness). Peak injury periods (high-intensity seasons) correlate with labor shortages in processing and transport.

## Labor Income and Household Survival

Most food workers occupy the economically vulnerable portion of settled populations.

An agricultural worker earning 500-800 Watts annually from farming, supplemented by 200-400 Watts from winter work (urban labor, craft), totals 700-1200 Watts annually. At [[Solhaven]] baseline costs (bread 1.2 Watts per pound, protein 8-20 Watts per pound, shelter 2-3 Watts weekly rent, minimal healthcare access), a family of 4 needs 1600-1800 Watts annually minimum. Agricultural worker household faces chronic shortfall — they survive through debt, extended family support, institutional relief programs, or permanent underconsumption.

Food workers are thus highly vulnerable to income disruption. A lost season of work translates directly to household malnutrition. This is why seasonal employment patterns show malnutrition correlations: harsh winters create food worker unemployment and corresponding malnutrition periods in working-class populations.
