# Banking Institutions

[[Solumora]]'s financial system is structured around institutional banking that operates differently in each kingdom but serves analogous functions: maintaining ledger accounts, facilitating inter-merchant transactions, providing currency authentication, enabling credit mechanisms, and managing institutional reserves. Banking institutions are the operational backbone of the economy, representing the only mechanisms through which large-scale financial transfers function reliably. Recent war preparation has created substantial institutional strain on both banking systems simultaneously.

## Auralis Banking Model — Council Integration

[[The Council of Auralis|Auralis]] operates a centralized banking structure oriented around [[Emberfall]] as the primary institutional hub. The Council of Auralis Banking Authority maintains exclusive licensing for major banking operations and operates subsidiary institutional banking services through the [[Advancement Corps]] network.

**Authority Structure** — The Banking Authority is a formal division within Council administrative structure, reporting to the Council Treasury Ministry. The Authority operates through:

- **Central Bank** (located in [[Emberfall]], functions as institutional reserve custodian and inter-bank settlement mechanism) maintains vault reserves estimated at 2-4 million Watts in Flux-marked currency and equivalent Flux-capacity deposits
- **Regional Banks** (four major locations: [[Solhaven]], [[Halveth]], [[Emberforth]], [[Thel Reach]]) maintain local ledger accounts, handle merchant transactions, authenticate currency, and process institutional loans
- **Advancement Corps Banking Network** (distributed across institutional installations throughout [[Auralis]]) provides patronage-integrated services for institutional practitioners and creates informal banking layer for those within Council networks

**Deposit and Account Management** — Merchants and institutions maintain accounts at Regional Banks, with balances tracked through ledger systems and verified through periodic authentication. Accounts earn no interest — balance is simply maintained as asset storage and transaction facilitation. Account maintenance requires fees: 5-15W annually for merchant accounts, 50-200W annually for large institutional accounts (typically 100,000W+ balances).

**Credit Mechanisms** — Regional Banks provide commercial loans at rates of 4-6% annually under standard conditions, with rates adjusted based on borrower credit history and collateral offered. Military-related ventures receive preferential 2-3% rates through formal Council collaboration. Civilian merchant leverage for major capital expenses (building warehouses, acquiring exclusive supplier agreements, transitioning family businesses to institutional scale) typically costs 6-10% annually due to lower institutional priority. Individual small-business loans (for craftspeople, emerging merchants) cost 10-15% annually due to high administration overhead and default risk.

**Institutional Lending Bias** — Contemporary pressure heavily favors military-related lending. Institutional borrowing for military equipment production, supply logistics, and force mobilization operates at preferential rates and receives expedited approval. Civilian lending approval timelines have extended from 2-4 weeks to 6-12 weeks in affected regions, effectively rationing available capital toward military priorities.

**Institutional Constraints on Auralis Banking** — The Council exercises substantial control over banking operations through:
- **Policy Direction** — Council can issue mandatory lending requirements, restrict certain market sectors from accessing credit, or freeze institutional accounts suspected of undermining Council interests
- **Practitioner Conscription** — Skilled practitioners (particularly T3-T4) in Regional Banks are increasingly conscripted for military administrative or security roles, reducing banking capacity and increasing operational strain
- **Currency Supply Management** — Council exercises authority to increase or restrict currency in circulation through printing of Flux-marked notes, creating inflation pressure and currency instability
- **Surveillance Capacity** — Council practitioners maintaining institutional relationships can theoretically monitor large Watt transfers through Flux-detection of currency signatures, creating structural transparency for Council-favored institutions while others operate under implicit surveillance

## Terravelle Banking Model — Guild Federation Approach

[[Terravelle]] operates a decentralized banking structure with no single central authority. Instead, independent banking houses operate in major settlements ([[Valdenmoor]], [[Ashford]], [[Drest]]) with loose coordination through the [[Trade Guilds|Guild Federation]]. This distributed model creates resilience against institutional failure but also creates fragmentation and coordination challenges.

**Banking House Structure** — Each major settlement operates one or more independent banking houses (Valdenmoor supports 3-4 major houses, Ashford supports 2-3, smaller settlements typically 1 house) that are privately capitalized and operated. Banking houses typically have 50-150W in operational capital and maintain vault reserves through customer deposits and reinvested earnings.

**Ledger Networks** — Banking houses maintain merchant accounts and process transactions through physical correspondence (carried by [[Courier Guilds|courier services]]) and periodic settlement through inter-house agreements. Account holders maintain relationships with specific banking houses and authorize transfers through signed documentation verified by house authenticators. Inter-house transfers require additional verification (charged at 1-3% of transfer value) and can take 2-4 weeks to settle.

**Credit Mechanisms** — Independent banking houses provide commercial loans at rates of 6-8% annually under standard conditions, with rates adjusted based on borrower reputation and collateral. Unlike the Auralis institutional model, Terravelle banking houses compete with each other for merchant accounts, creating pressure to maintain reasonable rates and acceptable terms. However, accessibility to capital varies geographically — rural settlements have limited banking access and may pay 10-15% rates due to higher individual banking house overhead per customer.

**Institutional Absence of Centralized Bias** — Terravelle banking lacks Council-equivalent centralized control mechanisms, which means no preferential military lending rates. Military production ventures access capital at standard commercial rates (6-8%), creating cost equality between military and civilian lending. However, this equality creates friction: merchant houses financing military supply contracts sometimes experience institutional pressure (particularly in [[Ashford]], which maintains supply relationships with [[Auralis]] military forces) regarding contract terms and delivery prioritization.

**Guild Coordination Function** — The [[Trade Guilds|Guild Federation]] provides inter-house dispute resolution and standard-setting for authentication and credential verification. Guild maintains authentication standards that all member banking houses must meet, ensuring currency verification and ledger account validity function consistently across different banking houses. Guild also maintains a mutual-assistance fund (capitalized through annual fees) that provides emergency liquidity support if a major banking house experiences temporary capital crises.

**Institutional Constraints on Terravelle Banking** — Unlike Auralis, Terravelle banking is not centrally controlled, but faces pressures through different mechanisms:
- **Guild Enforcement** — The Guild can suspend banking house licenses if institutions violate authentication standards or engage in predatory lending practices. This self-regulatory approach is less powerful than Council enforcement but creates reputational/operational consequences
- **Practitioner Conscription Pressure** — Military mobilization in [[Ashford]] creates pressure for banking house practitioners to support military operations, reducing banking capacity, though less systematically than in Auralis
- **Capital Scarcity** — War preparation funding pressures reduce merchant capital availability across kingdom, indirectly constraining banking houses' ability to offer loans by reducing available merchant deposits and institutional capital bases
- **Settlement Vulnerability** — Physical correspondence-based settlement system creates vulnerability if courier services are disrupted by military operations or security degradation in borderlands

## Comparative Economic Position

**Security and Stability** — Auralis institutions provide greater central security (larger reserves, centralized risk management) but operate under Council control. Terravelle institutions are more fragmented (higher institutional failure risk) but operate with greater independence. Merchants concerned about institutional instability increasingly diversify across multiple institutions and maintain holdings in both kingdoms to reduce single-institution risk.

**Accessibility** — Auralis central banking provides universal access (all Regional Banks offer consistent service standards). Terravelle decentralized structure creates geographic variation: urban merchants in major settlements have multiple banking house options, while rural Terravelle practitioners have limited access and face higher costs. Urban-rural inequality in financial service access is more pronounced in Terravelle than Auralis.

**Credit Availability** — Auralis institutional lending favors military priorities, constraining civilian capital. Terravelle lending treats military and civilian ventures equally at cost, but overall capital availability is lower due to lower merchant class wealth and smaller institutional reserves. Merchants operating across both kingdoms increasingly locate capital sources in Terravelle (where rates are equal) and use Auralis institutions primarily for authentication and settlement functions.

**Innovation Capacity** — Auralis centralized structure can implement policy changes rapidly (Council-mandated lending priorities, currency adjustments) but these can also be restrictive. Terravelle decentralized structure creates competition-driven innovation: individual banking houses sometimes introduce service innovations (credit guarantees, merchant-specific ledger formats, new authentication methods) to attract accounts. However, innovation diffuses slowly across the federation unless Guild-mandated.

## Lending and Credit Market

The credit market operates as critical infrastructure for merchant activities, though contemporary war preparation has created substantial stress:

**Collateral Requirements** — Loans require collateral to reduce lender risk. Typical collateral: physical assets (buildings, inventory, equipment), future revenue (harvest guarantees, contract security), or personal Flux capacity guarantees (T3+ practitioners offering Flux-based security). Collateral requirements increase during periods of institutional uncertainty: pre-war baseline collateral ratios were 1.3-1.5x loan value; contemporary wartime ratios are 2.0-2.5x loan value in Auralis, 1.5-2.0x in Terravelle.

**Commercial Lending** — Merchant houses, traders, and production operations access capital through commercial loans (50-500W typical range, larger operations can access 1000W+). Commercial lending approval depends on credit history, collateral, and intended use. Military-related ventures in Auralis receive expedited approval and preferential rates. Civilian ventures face longer approval timelines and higher rates.

**Personal Lending** — Individuals access capital through personal loans (10-100W typical, used for travel, equipment purchase, business startup) at higher rates (10-15% annually) due to higher administrative burden and default risk. Personal lending is increasingly difficult to access in Auralis due to institutional prioritization of commercial/military lending.

**Debt Liability and Default** — Loan defaults are tracked by banking institutions, with defaulters recorded in institutional ledgers. Defaulted debtors face restrictions: inability to access future institutional credit, pressure from debt-collection authorities, potential asset seizure if collateral-backed loans default. Institutions generally exercise collection authority more aggressively during war preparation (higher institutional pressure to recover defaults improves financial positions).

**Interest Rate Variation** — Interest rates vary based on borrower tier, collateral, intended use, and contemporary institutional conditions:
- Military lending (Auralis): 2-3% annual
- Commercial lending (Auralis): 6-10% annual
- Personal lending (Auralis): 10-18% annual
- Commercial lending (Terravelle): 6-8% annual
- Personal lending (Terravelle): 12-16% annual

## Institutional Vulnerabilities and War Preparation Pressure

Contemporary military preparation creates multiple financial system vulnerabilities:

**Capital Drain** — Military equipment production, force mobilization, and supply operations consume institutional reserves and merchant capital rapidly. Institutional lending to military ventures is high-volume and long-term (contracts often span 6-24 months), which ties up capital that would otherwise be available for civilian ventures.

**Inflation Pressure** — Increased currency in circulation from military financing creates persistent inflation (estimated 2-3% annually). This erodes deposit values and reduces purchasing power for individuals and merchants holding Watts. Institutions respond by raising lending rates, which further reduces civilian capital accessibility.

**Practitioner Scarcity** — Authentication practitioners are increasingly conscripted for military roles, reducing banking institution capacity. Remaining practitioners face increased workload and stress, with service quality degradation and verification delays as likely outcomes in stressed institutions.

**Settlement Disruption Risk** — If military operations disrupt courier services or create security barriers that prevent inter-institution settlement, the distributed financial system (particularly Terravelle's correspondence-based model) could experience cascade failures. Auralis centralized model is more resilient to settlement disruption but more vulnerable to single-point failure if central bank operations are disrupted.

**Institutional Failure Risk** — Smaller banking houses, particularly in Terravelle, face potential insolvency if military operations reduce merchant deposits (through reduced merchant profitability) and simultaneously increase loan defaults (through merchant operational strain). Insurance-equivalent mechanisms do not exist in either kingdom, so banking house failure would directly impact merchant accounts and create secondary effects through lost access to credit.

## Expedition-Risk Finance Integration

Banking institutions are increasingly entangled with high-risk expedition insurance markets.

- [[Crossing Risk Underwriters]] rely on bank settlement rails for premium pooling and payout release.
- [[Death Ledger Claim Adjudication]] delays now affect liquidity timing and reserve planning.
- [[Expedition Insurance Fraud Patterns]] raise compliance overhead and force tighter verification controls.
- [[Survivor Benefit Contracts]] create recurring payout obligations that can stabilize or destabilize district household economies depending on adjudication performance.

This integration means expedition casualty dynamics are no longer isolated to adventure sectors; they now feed directly into broader credit and trust conditions.
