Rent in [[Solhaven]] is not one market. It is a stepped system by terrace elevation, service access, and landlord screening standards.

## The Terrace Ladder

The lower harbor edge offers the cheapest beds but highest turnover, shared facilities, and flood-season maintenance failures. Mid-terrace rooms cost significantly more for better drainage, more reliable water access, and proximity to stable employers. Upper terraces price beyond ordinary labor incomes and require sponsorship, family standing, or long-form credential history.

Rent pressure is intensified by migration from smaller settlements and by households attempting to move one terrace upward after any earnings improvement. The result is a constant upward bid for limited mid-terrace space.

## Fee Stack and Penalties

The listed monthly rent is rarely the true cost. Households face layered charges:

- late fees that compound weekly,
- utility surcharges in high-demand months,
- "repair assessments" after storm or water-line interruption,
- deposit replacement when prior references are missing.

Low-tier labor households often spend 45 to 60 percent of income on housing when wage flows are stable. Under shift collapse, this ratio can exceed 70 percent temporarily, forcing food substitution and debt.

## Landlord Screening and Status

Landlords favor tenants with predictable roster work, guild references, and documented payment history. Households tied to volatile hiring pools are routed toward poorer stock even when they can match base rent at signing.

This is a core mechanism by which labor instability becomes spatial inequality: unstable shifts do not only lower income, they reduce housing choice, which then increases exposure to service disruptions and health risk.

## Adaptation Behaviors

Common survival responses include subletting bed-hours, rotating family sleep schedules for multi-shift workers, and temporary relocation to kin households during rent spikes. These practices keep people housed but increase domestic strain and conflict frequency.
