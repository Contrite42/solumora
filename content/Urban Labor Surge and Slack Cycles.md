# Urban Labor Surge and Slack Cycles

Urban employment in [[Solumora]] cities is not steady work. It is seasonal labor compressed into intense periods interrupted by weeks or months of minimal income and high unemployment. Understanding urban seasonal labor is understanding how most ordinary people survive economically.

## Seasonal Employment Pattern

Urban labor demand follows agricultural and trade cycles rather than independent urban rhythms.

### Spring Surge (Firstgreen through Brightward)

**Duration:** 6-8 weeks of maximum employment availability

**Drivers:**
- **Agricultural preparation:** Equipment provisioning (blacksmiths, tool makers), seed processing, fertilizer production all ramp up simultaneously
- **Trade preparation:** Merchants hire additional warehouse staff, porters, inventory management workers preparing for expedition season
- **Expedition demand:** Temporary labor for guide support, porter work, supply pack assembly, animal handling
- **Craft acceleration:** Textile production, metalwork, shipbuilding all prepare for export deadlines

**Employment patterns:**
- Unskilled labor: 15-25 Watts daily (double off-season rate)
- Semi-skilled: 20-35 Watts daily
- Skilled craftspeople work overtime (6-day weeks, extended hours)

**Who Works:**
- Day laborers (primary income population seasonally employed)
- Craft apprentices working extended hours
- Older workers past prime physical capacity (spring is when older people can still find work)
- Adult women (culturally available for temporary labor that excludes permanent guild positions)

### Summer Maintenance (Brightward through Harvestfirst)

**Duration:** 8-10 weeks of moderate employment

**Drivers:**
- **Expedition support:** Ongoing through-season support for active expeditions (supply chain continues, messaging, logistics)
- **Production continuation:** Craftspeople working standard schedules fulfilling spring contracts
- **Maintenance:** Buildings, roads, port facilities requiring upkeep (delayed from winter, performed before autumn rush)
- **Transportation:** River and maritime shipping moves produce and goods continuously

**Employment patterns:**
- Unskilled labor: 8-15 Watts daily (below full employment but better than winter)
- Skilled labor: Steady work with reduced overtime

**Structural change:**
Some workers hired spring transition to summer relocation (travel to seasonal harvest work). Urban unemployment rises slightly mid-summer as workers transition out.

### Autumn Crunch (Harvestfall through Coolward)

**Duration:** 4-6 weeks of maximum emergency intensity

**Drivers:**
- **Grain processing:** Massive grain influx from completed harvests requires milling, drying, storage
- **Food preservation:** Salt procurement, preservation container construction, food storage coordination
- **Expedition returns:** Equipment maintenance, personnel recovery, logistical support for expedition completions
- **Trade movement:** Autumn shipping requirements (grain moving to buyers, winter goods positioning)

**Employment patterns:**
- Unskilled labor: 12-20 Watts daily (intense, often forced to accept minimal accommodation conditions and extended hours)
- Processing facility work: 10-16 hour workdays common, wage premium for accepting harsh conditions

**Conditions are brutal:**
- Midnight shifts processing grain in mill heat exhaustion
- Food preservation work (salt handling, preservation container construction) with minimal safety
- Sanitation problems from rushed conditions creating disease risk
- Exploitative conditions accepted because employment is temporary and alternatives are starvation

### Winter Austerity (Colden through Thawmark)

**Duration:** 8-12 weeks of extreme unemployment

**Employment patterns:**
- Unskilled labor: 3-8 Watts daily if work exists (refuse collection, water hauling, cleaning)
- Skilled labor: Limited to urgent repair work or commissioned projects
- Most workers unemployed or underemployed

**Survival strategies:**
- **Relief work:** Some cities operate "poor work" programs (roads, walls, public benefit projects) paying subsistence 2-5 Watts daily
- **Charity:** Religious institutions provide minimal support (food, shelter)
- **Debt:** Borrowing from summer wages, expected repayment from spring employment
- **Family support:** Dependent on household having employed members with income to share

## Economic Stratification in Seasonal Labor

### Skilled Craftspeople

Craft guild members have status protecting them from worst seasonal volatility:

- Guild provides work-sharing arrangements (craftspeople work reduced individual hours rather than layoffs)
- Year-round commitment means guild negotiates wage stability (reduced income vs. unemployment)
- Guild membership provides some winter relief assistance

**But:** Guild protection depends on guild prosperity. War conscription removing craftspeople, military material priority disrupting normal craft demand, and economic disruption weakening guild treasuries reduce cushion.

### Unskilled Day Laborers

Unskilled laborers experience full seasonal volatility:

- **Peak work:** Spring surge pays 15-25 Watts daily
- **Slack:** Winter unemployment or 3-5 Watts daily "poor work"
- **Annual income:** 2,000-3,500 Watts in typical years, assuming 80-120 workdays annually

This income pools the survival. A household depending on single day-laborer income experiences crisis:

- Spring income surge allows repaying winter debt, building minimal reserves
- Autumn income provides foundation for winter, but 3-4 month winter income drought exceeds single surge's saved amount
- Winter undersupply forces new debt or reduces family consumption

Families manage through:
- **Household diversification:** Multiple income-earners (adult woman does laundry or textile work, adolescent does odd jobs, elderly does craft if skills exist)
- **Debt rollover:** Borrowing each spring assuming repayment from next year (this creates permanent debt burden if income fails any single year)
- **Subsistence transition:** Winter consumption shifts to cheaper/lower-nutrition food, reduced discretionary spending, using charity where available

### Merchant Class

Merchant operations stabilize by managing seasonal cash flow:

- Summer and autumn income surge concentrated, carefully allocated for winter operations
- Inventory management minimizes capital tied up in dead stock
- Merchant house maintains capital reserves averaging 3-6 months operating expense (craftspeople and poor have zero reserves)

**But:** War inventory requisitions, increased insurance costs, and military shipping priority reduce merchant profitability. Marginal merchant houses vulnerable to single bad season.

## Geographic Variation

### [[Solhaven]]

Major trade hub experiences moderate seasonal variation:
- Spring surge: 40-50% employment increase
- Summer/autumn: Sustained 80-85% base employment (better than rural equivalents because diversified economy)
- Winter: 60-70% employment available (worst-case unemployment not as severe as smaller cities)

### [[Valdenmoor]]

River-focused economy creates intense spring surge, moderate autumn crunch:
- Spring: 60-70% employment surge (timber + trade)
- Summer: Employment drops (river commerce transitions normal levels)
- Autumn: 40-50% employment surge (grain processing, merchant activity)
- Winter: Minimal employment, severe undersupply

### Smaller settlements

Settlements like [[Thel Reach]], [[Seln Watch]] experience extreme volatility:
- Spring/autumn: 2-3x base employment as expedition staging dominates
- Summer: 60-70% normal employment
- Winter: 20-30% normal employment (core necessary work only)

Survival in small settlements requires acceptance of extreme seasonal bottleneck: income must carry 8 months from 4 months work.

## Temporal Stress and Risk

The seasonal bottleneck creates permanent economic stress:

**Debt**
Seasonal workers continuously maintain debt. Borrowing each winter against spring work creates rolling debt burden. Interest accumulation (5-10% annually on typical debt) means each debt cycle increases principal, creating perpetual trap.

**Health**
Intense seasonal work produces injury and exhaustion cascade:
- Spring/autumn surge work creates physical damage (tendon injuries, back strain, heat exhaustion)
- Winter rest allows some recovery but incomplete (chronic injuries accumulate)
- Winter malnutrition from reduced income weakens immunity, extends recovery from illness

Occupational injuries concentrate in seasonal surge work. Workers with cumulative injuries eventually cannot handle peak-season intensity, triggering unemployment during their highest-earning period.

**Social dislocation**
Seasonal work separates people from their normal communities:
- Harvest labor takes workers to distant farms (affecting family stability, social bonds)
- Expedition work removes guides and porters for weeks
- Mining and logging seasonal work concentrates in isolated work camps

Long-term seasonal migration creates family instability and reduces community cohesion.

## Contemporary Pressure

**Military conscription** directly disrupts seasonal employment:

- Spring recruitment removes day laborers and young craft workers who form seasonal labor supply
- Summer conscription removes skilled workers (masons, carpenters, metalworkers) at peak operational demand
- Autumn grain processing understaffed because harvest workers drafted

Result: seasonal labor supply reduced 20-30%, wages rise (benefiting remaining workers), but employment intensity becomes unsustainable (workers forced into mandatory overtime conditions).

**Material scarcity** shifts seasonal employment patterns:

- Military priority allocation reduces merchant trade volume, eliminating spring trade surge component
- Metal shortage reduces blacksmith employment
- Timber military requisition reduces logging employment

Complex effect: some seasonal workers benefit from war (military supply demand creates new peak), others devastated (trade-dependent workers lose employment).

## Practical Implication

For inhabitants depending on seasonal work, the calendar governs survival more directly than most realize:

- Spring labor income determines winter survival capacity
- Autumn employment decision (accept dangerous conditions for premium, or wait for milder spring work) shapes year's financial trajectory
- Single season failure (weather disruption preventing spring, illness preventing autumn work) creates cascading debt crisis

The seasonal rhythm is not theoretical abstraction — it is the heartbeat of whether an ordinary family eats adequately, remains solvent, or faces crisis.
