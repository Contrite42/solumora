# Mining Operations

Mining operations extract metal ores, Flux-reactive minerals, and specialty stone from underground and surface deposits. The sector spans from individual prospectors and small family operations to large institutionally-backed sites employing 50-100+ workers across multiple shifts. Contemporary war pressure is rapidly consolidating operations toward institutional control.

## Extraction Methods

**Surface mining** occurs where geological formations expose ore or mineral deposits directly, or where weathered overlying rock requires removal before ore extraction. Surface mines have lower capital requirements but produce lower-grade ore and create visible environmental disturbance. Typical operational life: 3-10 years depending on deposit grade and size.

**Underground mining** accesses deeper deposits through shaft development and horizontal tunneling. Requires significant infrastructure investment (shaft construction, support beaming, ventilation systems) but reaches higher-grade deposits. Operational complexity increases risk: collapses, flooding, toxic gas accumulation. Typical operational life: 10-30 years if geological stability permits.

**Placer mining** (small-scale) searches riverbeds and sediment deposits for loose mineral particles. Requires minimal equipment and training but offers small daily yields. Practiced by independent prospectors and marginal operators competing in commodity markets where scale prevents profitability.

## Extracted Materials

**Iron and base metals (copper, tin, lead)** — Primary production output. Distributed to metalworking operations, institutional construction, and military equipment production. Prices relatively stable; high-volume commodity with competitive markets.

**Flux-reactive minerals** (Voidstone, Aetherlite, Resonant Crystals) — Premium materials required for high-tier Flux applications, grimoire production, and military Flux infrastructure. Rare availability and institutional monopolization creates persistent shortage; pricing 5-10x base commodity rates. Military demand for Flux-reactive materials is primary driver of extraction acceleration.

**Specialty stone** (construction grade, decorative, Flux-preparation surfaces) — Supportive market with steady demand. Used in settlement construction, institutional operations, and specialized craftswork. Pricing stable; margins lower than metalwork applications.

## Mine Sites

**Major institutional mining** (Auralis Council or merchant-house backed) operate at significant scale (30-80 workers, infrastructure investment of 5,000+ Watts) with professional management, safety equipment (where capacity permits), and systematic ore processing. These sites achieve 8-15 tons annual output and command preferential access to military purchasing.

Primary example: [[Keln Ridge Mines]] ([[Auralis]]'s largest institutional mining complex, 200-250 workers) managed by [[Voss Keld|T3 director]] under direct [[The Council of Auralis|Council]] oversight. Operations target 750 tons iron ore + 150kg Flux-reactive minerals annually, operating above sustainable capacity with documented safety degradation. Shaft operations managed by mid-tier supervisors like [[Lorn Kess|T2 foreman]] coordinating day-to-day extraction under resource-constrained conditions.

**Medium commercial mining** (independent merchants or guild-affiliated operations) operate at 10-30 worker scale with moderate capital investment and inconsistent safety standards. Output typically 2-5 tons annually. Increasingly pressured by institutional consolidation.

**Small independent mining** (family operations or prospector teams) employ 1-6 workers on marginal deposits, achieve <1 ton annual output, face immediate underselling pressure from larger operations. Most independent mining is economically marginal and practiced out of necessity rather than profitability.

## Labor Conditions

**Field miners** (T0-T2 practitioners) perform physical extraction — swinging picks, hauling ore, breaking stone, installing temporary support structures. Working conditions are hazardous: poorly ventilated shafts create respiratory risk, unstable rock creates collapse risk, inadequate lighting creates injury risk.

**Support workers** manage equipment maintenance, ore transport, site cleaning, and perimeter security. Often less skilled than field miners, with correspondingly lower compensation.

**Shift coordinators** (T2-T3 practitioners) manage daily work allocation, safety enforcement, ore sorting/grading, and production reporting. These practitioners are increasingly diverted toward military operations.

**Wages** vary by site and commodity type. Field workers earn 10-20 Watts weekly at major sites (with deductions for food/shelter often provided on-site), 5-15 Watts at smaller sites, <5 Watts at marginal operations. Hazard pay (additional 20-30% compensation for particularly dangerous work) is accepted practice at established sites but inconsistently enforced.

Work is typically 6 days weekly, 8-10 hours daily. Injury rates are high: broken bones from accidents, silicosis from ore dust inhalation, infections from work-related cuts, and temporary disablement from tunnel collapses are standard occupational risks. Fatal accidents are less common but occur regularly enough that mining families maintain psychological expectation of catastrophic loss.

## Contemporary Pressure and Acceleration

**Military demand for Flux-reactive minerals** is driving aggressive expansion into marginal and previously uneconomical deposits. Sites with low-grade Flux-reactive material that would normally be rejected due to processing costs are now actively mined to meet military requisition quotas.

**Institutional consolidation** is systematically absorbing independent and medium-scale operations through preferential access to mechanized equipment (Flux-enhanced extraction), military purchasing agreements, and capital deployment that undercuts independent operators. Medium-scale operators report 30-40% margin compression in past 18 months.

**Labor conscription and voluntary departure** are creating severe staffing shortages. Institutional military conscription is directly requisitioning experienced miners. Simultaneously, mining-dependent settlement populations are showing increased "voluntary" departure as families relocate seeking lower-risk employment and better wages.

**Environmental degradation** is visible in mining-dependent regions. Abandoned tailings piles are creating run-off contamination, deforested hillsides surround mining sites (fuel wood for mine operations), and water sources downstream of major mining operations show sediment and chemical contamination.

**Sustainability boundary crossing** — Multiple mining regions show signs of deposit depletion or unprofitable ore grades. Operations are expanding into deeper shafts (higher cost, higher risk), exploring in marginal geological zones (lower yields), or redirecting toward marginal-grade deposits that would normally be rejected. This extension of operational boundaries is technically possible but economically and environmentally unsustainable beyond 3-5 year horizon.

Mining is transitioning from established labor sector to forced-production system optimized for short-term military material supply rather than sustainable resource extraction.
