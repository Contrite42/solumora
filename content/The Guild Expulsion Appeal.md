**The Guild Expulsion Appeal** was a [[Terravelle]] legal case establishing that formal courts have no authority to override guild internal discipline decisions. The ruling reinforced guild autonomy and clarified the boundary between [[Terravelle Guild Mediation|guild mediation]] and formal justice systems.

## The Background

Trev Osven was a journeyman in Terravelle's Warden Guild (security and detection specialists) operating in [[Solhaven]]. He was accused by multiple clients of providing inadequate barrier maintenance, causing security breaches that resulted in theft losses. Clients brought complaints to guild elders.

Guild mediation panel investigated. They interviewed clients, examined Trev's work records, inspected several barrier installations, and concluded that Trev's work was consistently substandard. Barriers were correctly constructed but inadequately anchored, causing premature failure under sustained load. This indicated incomplete training rather than deliberate misconduct, but clients had suffered real losses.

**Mediation Resolution:**
- Trev must compensate clients 18,000 [[Watts]] for documented theft losses
- Trev suspended from independent contracting for 12 months
- Must complete supervised retraining under senior Warden Guild master
- Can resume independent work after demonstrating competence

Trev refused. He argued that guild mediation was unfair — panel included his direct competitor, client testimony was not cross-examined rigorously, and he was not provided opportunity to bring external expert witnesses to contest technical findings.

Guild elders gave Trev 30 days to accept resolution or face expulsion. Trev did not comply. Guild formally expelled him.

## The Court Case

Trev filed civil case in Terravelle formal court, arguing:

1. Guild mediation lacked due process protections — no formal evidence rules, no right to legal representation, no transcript for appeal
2. Competitor presence on elder panel created conflict of interest that mediation did not address
3. Guild expulsion was excessive punishment for first offense that elders themselves characterized as training deficiency rather than misconduct
4. Expulsion destroyed his livelihood without providing fair adjudication
5. Courts should review guild discipline decisions to ensure basic fairness standards are met

Warden Guild countered:

1. Guild mediation is voluntary association matter, not subject to court review
2. Trev accepted guild membership knowing mediation was binding dispute resolution process
3. Mediation panel composition followed guild internal procedures — competitor recusal is not required unless personal animosity exists
4. Expulsion was not for technical deficiency — it was for refusal to accept mediation outcome
5. Courts reviewing guild decisions would undermine guild autonomy and make mediation unworkable

## The Ruling

Judge Korven Dalt ruled in favor of Warden Guild:

Guilds are voluntary professional associations. Members join knowing that guild membership includes accepting guild discipline procedures. When guild procedures follow guild internal rules and do not violate fundamental public policy (no violence, no coercion, no prohibited discrimination), courts have no basis to override guild decisions.

**Key Findings:**

1. **Voluntary Association:** Trev was not compelled to join Warden Guild. He voluntarily joined, accepted membership benefits, and agreed to guild mediation as binding dispute resolution. Courts cannot rewrite voluntary agreements because one party dislikes the outcome.

2. **No Due Process Right:** Due process protections apply to government action, not private association decisions. Guilds are not government institutions. They do not have constitutional obligation to provide formal trial procedures.

3. **Internal Procedures Followed:** Guild mediation followed Warden Guild documented procedures. Panel was properly constituted under guild rules. Trev was informed of accusations, given opportunity to present his account, and received resolution in writing. This meets guild procedural standards, which courts will not second-guess.

4. **Expulsion for Non-Compliance:** Guild expelled Trev not for technical deficiency but for refusing to accept mediation outcome. This is legitimate enforcement of guild membership obligations. If courts prevented guilds from expelling members who refuse mediation, guild discipline would be unenforceable.

5. **Alternative Remedies Available:** Trev can pursue independent work outside guild structures, relocate to areas with weak guild presence, or enter different profession. Guild expulsion does not violate legal rights because guild membership is not legal entitlement — it is professional privilege conditioned on meeting guild standards.

**Verdict:** Case dismissed. Guild expulsion stands.

## The Impact

Ruling established clear precedent: [[Terravelle]] courts will not intervene in guild internal discipline unless fundamental rights are violated or guild procedures involve illegal conduct.

**What courts WILL review:**
- Physical violence or threats
- Discrimination based on prohibited categories (if Terravelle establishes such categories legislatively)
- Guild discipline imposed without following guild's own documented procedures
- Fraud or corruption in mediation process (bribery, falsified evidence)

**What courts WILL NOT review:**
- Fairness of mediation outcomes
- Composition of elder panels (unless guild rules require recusal and guild violated its own rules)
- Adequacy of evidence considered
- Severity of discipline imposed
- Whether guild procedures provide formal due process protections

The ruling reinforced [[Terravelle]]'s cultural preference for distributed authority. Guilds self-govern, and formal legal system intervenes only when self-governance breaks down into illegal conduct. This preserves guild autonomy but also means guild members have limited recourse when they believe mediation was unfair.

## Practical Consequences

**For Guild Members:**
- Mediation outcomes are effectively final — courts will not reverse them
- Refusing mediation is career-ending — expulsion guaranteed, no court protection
- Guild procedures are what guild documents say they are — not what members wish they were
- Dispute prevention is better strategy than dispute litigation

**For Guilds:**
- Autonomy confirmed but with it responsibility to maintain fair procedures
- Bad faith mediation (corruption, bias, procedural abuse) risks legislative intervention even if courts won't act
- Guild reputation enforcement becomes more important — courts won't fix guild discipline failures
- Documentation of procedures matters — courts will enforce guild's own rules even if courts won't impose external standards

**For Terravelle Legal System:**
- Clear boundary established between guild jurisdiction and court jurisdiction
- Reduces court caseload — guild disputes handled internally
- Accepts that justice within guild communities may not meet formal procedural standards
- Reinforces cultural expectation that professional communities self-govern

## Political Context

The ruling pleased guild leadership across [[Terravelle]] — autonomy confirmed and court interference prevented. It frustrated some legal reformers who argue that guild mediation lacks accountability and that expelled members should have meaningful appeal rights.

No legislative action has been proposed to override the precedent. [[Terravelle]] political culture generally supports guild autonomy, and most citizens view formal courts as backup systems for cases guilds cannot handle, not as oversight mechanisms for guild decisions.

## Trev's Outcome

After losing his court case, Trev relocated to a remote settlement with minimal Warden Guild presence. He works independently providing basic detection services to local clients who do not verify guild standing. His income is perhaps 40% of what he earned in Solhaven, and his professional reputation remains damaged. He occasionally argues publicly that guild system is unjust, but few practitioners sympathize — most view his case as what happens when you refuse mediation and then lose in court.

## Comparative Perspective

[[Auralis Civic Courts]] view The Guild Expulsion Appeal as evidence that [[Terravelle Reputation Justice|Terravelle's reputation system]] operates without legal safeguards. [[Auralis]] practitioners expelled from professional associations can appeal to courts and sometimes win reversals. Terravelle practitioners have no such recourse.

[[Terravelle]] views the case as correctly preserving guild autonomy against legal system overreach. If courts could review every guild expulsion, mediation would become preliminary step before real litigation instead of binding resolution. That would destroy the speed and community-embedded advantage that makes guild mediation superior to formal courts.

Both perspectives are internally consistent. The case highlights fundamental difference: [[Auralis]] trusts institutional legal systems and distrusts private associations with unreviewable power. [[Terravelle]] trusts professional communities and distrusts formal courts imposing external standards on voluntary associations.
