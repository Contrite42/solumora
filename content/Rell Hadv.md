**Rell Hadv** is fifty-eight years old and has been guild master at a mid-sized certification practice in [[Valdenmoor]] for eighteen years. [[Control Tier|T4]]. He certified at twenty-two, moved through journeyman in four years, built a steady practice in structural sigil application, and was offered the master position at forty. He took it because it seemed like the right kind of work at the right point in his life, and eighteen years later he still thinks this was correct.

The guild master's work is administrative as much as technical: managing the certification process for apprentices coming through the practice, evaluating journeyman applications, maintaining the documentation standards that the guild network holds practitioners accountable to. Rell is good at this in the specific way of someone who finds process legible rather than constraining — he understands what the documentation is for and therefore knows when it is and is not doing its job.

He currently has a T5 apprentice, fourteen years old, in the first year of formal study. The certification system accommodates high-tier candidates straightforwardly at most stages, but a T5 apprentice working under a T4 master creates a situation the system's writers did not specifically address. The tier does not determine whose assessment is authoritative in a training context — that is determined by certification level, which Rell holds and the apprentice does not. But the guild's implicit assumption that a senior certified practitioner sits at the ceiling of what they can teach is being tested in ways Rell finds interesting rather than threatening.

He has started a documentation file for the specific procedural questions the case raises. He expects other masters will encounter this situation more often as the population's tier distribution plays out over time. He is not certain the guild's current procedural language handles it cleanly, and he would rather build the precedent carefully than have it built carelessly by whoever comes next.

He intends to retire in approximately ten years, which means the apprentice will be certified well before he steps down. He finds it likely that the former apprentice will be running the practice within twenty years of his retirement. He does not find this unsettling. That is, he thinks, what training is for.
