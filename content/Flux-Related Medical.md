Flux-related medical cases are the point where ordinary injury care overlaps with system-level uncertainty.

A practitioner can treat fever, infection, fracture, and blood loss with known methods. Flux-specific harm introduces diagnostic ambiguity: symptoms can mimic ordinary disease while the underlying mechanism is pathway disruption, casting residue, or anomaly exposure.

## Case Categories

Operationally, practitioners sort Flux-related cases into four categories:

1. **Overextension injury:** collapse after sustained casting output.
2. **Pathway irritation:** unstable response after repeated mid-tier work.
3. **Anomaly exposure:** post-incident symptoms near rift activity.
4. **Residual contamination:** persistent dysfunction after failed sigil interactions.

These categories overlap, and misclassification is common at lower tiers.

## Diagnostic Ladder

Most districts use a staged ladder:

- T1 screen for immediate life threats and obvious trauma;
- T2 assessment for pattern irregularity and treatment response;
- T3+ evaluation when symptoms do not match expected recovery path.

The dangerous interval is between T2 suspicion and T3 intake. Patients who cannot travel or pay often stay in repeated partial-treatment cycles that reduce symptoms without resolving causes.

## Common Presentations

Recurring symptoms include:

- tremor and intermittent motor loss after casting episodes;
- sensory distortion (light, heat, or sound processing failures);
- cyclical pain with no visible tissue injury;
- severe fatigue out of proportion to exertion;
- abrupt drops in casting capacity and cognitive stability.

Without Flux-capable diagnostics, these cases are frequently misread as anxiety, exhaustion, or noncompliance.

## Treatment Patterns

Care usually combines standard medicine and Flux-specific intervention.

- stabilize sleep, hydration, and infection risk first;
- reduce casting load to prevent escalation;
- apply limited pathway support where safe;
- monitor for relapse during staged re-entry to work.

The best predictor of recovery is not initial symptom severity. It is how quickly a patient reaches a practitioner who recognizes the pattern.

## Occupational Exposure

High-risk professions include:

- refinery and workshop crews around active sigil infrastructure;
- expedition medics and guides near rift corridors;
- enforcement and response teams handling Flux incidents;
- practitioners themselves, especially those running repeated interventions without adequate recovery.

The profession most likely to underreport injuries is medical practice itself. Practitioners delay care to maintain patient load, then present later with harder-to-treat damage.

## Cost and Access

Flux-related care is expensive because diagnosis is scarce.

- first specialist intake: 12-30 [[Watts]];
- multi-visit stabilization cycles: 25-80 Watts;
- deep pathway interventions: often beyond working-class affordability.

Guild sponsorship and temple funds cover some cases, usually where loss of a worker would disrupt valuable operations. Unaffiliated households absorb risk directly.

## Failure Modes

System failures repeat across both kingdoms:

- delayed referral from low-tier clinics;
- partial treatment that suppresses symptoms and masks progression;
- transport loss between rural edge and city specialists;
- social disbelief when symptoms are intermittent or invisible.

Most preventable severe outcomes begin with a mild case that was not escalated quickly enough.
