# Keln Moss

**Keln Moss** is a [[Control Tier|T0]] mine laborer working primarily in iron-ore extraction operations near [[Ashford]], with occasional contract work in secondary mineral mining during peak seasons. He is 34 years old, has worked mining for 12 years since early childhood apprenticeship with his father, and represents typical low-tier extraction workforce experiencing contemporary military pressure.

## Appearance

Keln is a man in his mid-thirties with the heavily muscled build and permanent fatigue of someone who does hard physical labor six days a week. He has the characteristic appearance of a mine worker — skin perpetually stained with rock dust and ore residue that no amount of washing fully removes, calloused hands thick from years of tool work, scarred knuckles from rock breaking and ore transport. His hair is dark and kept short to minimize dust accumulation.

He dresses in heavy mining work clothes — reinforced canvas trousers blackened with ore dust, thick wool shirts layered for temperature regulation underground, heavy boots with steel reinforcement. His clothing is patched, worn, and permanently discolored from rock dust. His forearms show the developed musculature of someone who swings picks and carries ore loads daily, and his posture shows the beginning of the characteristic forward stoop that long-term miners develop from working in low-ceiling shafts.

His face is weathered and prematurely aged from underground work in poor air quality, with deep lines around his eyes and mouth. He has the slightly hollow look of someone who works hard but doesn't get quite enough quality food. His hands shake slightly when at rest — a common tremor among miners from repeated tool vibration exposure. His expression carries the permanent wariness of someone doing dangerous work with inadequate safety provisions.

## Background and Work

Keln came to mining after his father secured him apprenticeship at age 12 with a medium-scale iron operation. No alternative career paths were anticipated — mining work provided family income sufficient to avoid conscription pressure into military service. He progressed from simple rock-breaking work to ore transport to occasional shaft-support installation as years of experience accumulated.

His tier ceiling at T0 means he will never access Flux-based extraction operations requiring T1+ capacity. Advancement is toward supervisor roles requiring experience depth rather than tier capacity, but supervisor positions are limited and increasingly diverted toward military operations. Most of Keln's peer-group progression has stalled: experienced miners who could expect supervisor advancement are now conscripted for military operations.

## Current Circumstances

Keln works in a medium-scale iron operation employing approximately 20 workers across two shifts. He earns 12 Watts weekly for 6-day weeks, with food and minimal shelter provided on-site. Recent conscription of the operation's shift coordinator (T2) has created workflow disruption — the replacement supervisor is less experienced, resulting in inefficient crew management and reduced safety enforcement.

His family situation is precarious. His wife and two children live in [[Ashford]] proper while he works contract at the mine site. Wages are adequate for subsistence but insufficient for material accumulation or emergency reserves. Recent informational indications suggest military conscription quotas are expanding, creating personal anxiety about whether mining exemption claims will survive next enrollment review.

Conscription of experienced miners from competing operations has paradoxically improved his job security in short term (experienced-worker scarcity makes current operation reluctant to lose him) while worsening long-term prospects (conscription will eventually reach his operation; military is systematically recruiting extraction-sector workers for specialized labor brigades).

## Perspective

Keln describes mining as "necessary work performed by people without better options." He is aware that his T0 tier ceiling prevents advancement within mining operations and military service. His survival strategy is maintaining current employment long enough to accumulate minimal resources, then attempting transition to lower-risk work before conscription reaches conscription-avoiding mining roles.

Direct military experience stories from conscripted peers create anxiety about warfare reality — three cousins conscripted into expanding military operations have not returned communication. Their assumed circumstance (dead, injured, or trapped in active operations) drives Keln's preference for avoiding military service despite its higher wages and apparent prestige.

He possesses no illusions about mining ethics or sustainability. He recognizes that current extraction acceleration is unsustainable but feels powerless to influence institutional decisions operating at scale above individual worker agency.

## What Keln Knows

- Mining operations are accelerating production to unsustainable levels
- Conscription is removing experienced workers faster than replacements can be trained
- Safety standards are declining as operations prioritize output over worker protection
- Military demand for specialized metals is driving unprofitable mining of marginal deposits
- His operation will likely face conscription within 12-18 months
