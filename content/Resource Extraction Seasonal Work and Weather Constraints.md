# Resource Extraction Seasonal Work and Weather Constraints

Mining and logging operations operate on rigid seasonal constraints defined by weather rather than market demand. Unlike trade or agriculture which can adapt timing, extraction operations are bound by when terrain is passable and when work conditions allow.

## Mining Operations Calendar

**Stenn Ridge** (characteristic [[Terravelle]] hill mining operation) operates on weather-determined schedule:

### Operating Season (Plantmoon through Coolward)

**Duration:** Approximately 6-7 months of active mining

**Early Season (Plantmoon through Brightward):** Access roads transition from impassable winter mud to navigable. Equipment is moved into position. Claims are reestablished (mapping seasonal boundary shifts). Initial ore extraction targets highest-grade deposits closest to extraction points.

**Peak Season (Highdry through Harvestfirst):** Maximum ore extraction. Weather is dry, roads are optimal condition. Mining crews work 6-day weeks. Processing facility operates at full capacity.

**Late Season (Harvestfall through Coolward):** Harvest labor competition pulls workers into agriculture. Mining workforce thins unless premium wages offered. Extraction accelerates before season close (extract as much as possible before weather window closes). Transport of accumulated ore to smelters must complete before autumn roads degrade.

**Bottleneck:** Mine produces ore continuously through operating season but output must move to smelters/processors before weather closes transport. Road degradation in late autumn creates cascading bottleneck where accumulated ore awaits transport as weather window closes. A mine with poor transport coordination experiences end-of-season backlog (ore sitting awaiting spring transport).

### Shutdown Season (Colden through Plantmoon)

**Duration:** 5-6 months minimal activity

**Conditions:** 
- Mining: Shallow mines close entirely (frozen ground, snow makes access impossible)
- Deep mines: Operations continue winter at reduced capacity (seasonal workers depart, skeleton crew maintains shafts)
- Transport: Overland routes impassable (mud/water making roads unusable)
- Processing: Winter processing of accumulated ore stockpiles happens at facility (equipment doesn't shut down but operates on stored raw material)

**Personnel:** Core year-round staff (25-35 people) maintain facilities. Seasonal workers (60-100 people) depart. Spring recruitment begins February identifying next season's workers.

**Equipment Maintenance:** Winter is maintenance season. Equipment that ran continuously April-October undergoes repair, replacement of worn parts, clearing of accumulated sediment/blockages.

## Logging Operations Calendar

**Seasonal Felling Constraints:**

### Winter Felling (Colden through Thawmark)

**Advantage:** Deep snow supports sledge transport. Logs can be moved on frozen snow/ice surfaces more easily than wheeled transport on mud.

**Disadvantage:** Cold makes wood more brittle (higher breakage rate during felling/movement). Worker conditions harsh. Equipment becomes brittle (metal freezes, becomes more likely to snap under load).

**Pattern:** Some operations shift felling to winter taking advantage of

 snow sledge transport, accepting higher material loss and harder conditions.

### Spring to Autumn Felling (Firstgreen through Coolward)

**Advantage:** Weather tolerance. Frozen equipment hazard absent. Worker conditions manageable.

**Disadvantage:** Spring mud season creates equipment movement difficulty. Logs must be sledge-moved to processing area before spring thaw, or log-driving on rivers during spring flood (dangerous, requires specialized crews).

**Pattern:** Most operations concentrate felling spring through summer, maximizing worker comfort and equipment reliability. Autumn completion attempts to extract remaining accessible timber before winter.

### Transport Constraints

Log movement depends absolutely on weather:

**Water Transport:** Spring flood enables massive volume movement. Rivers that are impassable autumn-winter become superhighways April-May. Log-driving crews work continuous shifts moving winter/spring-accumulated logs downriver before water levels drop.

**Overland Transport (Sledge):** Snow eliminates mud bottleneck. Winter sledging on frozen ground moves logs more easily than summer wagon movement. But extreme cold reduces wood flexibility and increases equipment stress.

**Seasonal Bottleneck:** Maximum timber is felled, maximum timber is ready for transport, but transport capacity (river flood season, snow sledge capacity) creates chokepoint where accumulated timber exceeds movement capacity. Timber logistics is constant optimization balancing harvest rate against transport capacity.

## Coastal Fishing Seasonal Reality

Fishing operations follow weather and creature migration patterns rather than administrative calendars.

### Spring Dangerous Season (Thawmark through Plantmoon)

Storm intensity high, winds unpredictable, water temperature lethal (cold-water shock kills swimmers). Fishing fleet activity drops 50% because risk exceeds financial return.

**But:** Spring is spawning season for some fish species. Fishing grounds are productive. Risk-tolerant fishing crews and crews needing income take elevated risk. Wages spike (compensating for danger). 

Insurance costs rise: ships operating spring fishing grounds accept higher loss probability, raising insurance premiums. Some fishing operations cease spring activity entirely, concentrating on safer seasons.

### Summer Harvest (Highdry through Peaklite)

Optimal fishing season. Weather cooperative, creature populations stable, fish movement predictable. Fishing fleets fully deployed. Catch volumes highest.

Equipment maintenance happens slowly (cannot afford downtime during peak productive season). Equipment stress accumulates (overworked nets, hull repairs deferred, sails patched rather than replaced).

### Autumn Transition (Harvestfall through Coolward)

Autumn storms begin (cyclone season emerging, winds shifting patterns). Fishing activity remains high because summer accumulated catch requires processing/preservation before winter. Fishing crews work despite rising weather risk.

End-of-season rush creates equipment failure risk: boats operating at capacity with accumulated wear, weather deteriorating, urgency driving unsafe decisions.

### Winter Isolation or Selective Activity (Colden through Thawmark)

Northern fishing operations shut down (ice hazard). Southern fishing (like [[Marn Cove]]) continues at reduced capacity — good crews fish winter storms, poor-quality crews sit idle.

Equipment intensive maintenance happens. Net repair, hull caulking, sail mending — all the delayed maintenance from peak season happens here.

## Extraction Labor Patterns

### Seasonal Worker Movement

Peak-season extraction work (mining, logging, quarrying) recruits temporary labor November-December for spring through autumn deployment. Recruitment targets:

- Agricultural workers with winter unemployment
- Urban day laborers seeking consistent peak-season employment
- Adolescents (cheaper labor, acceptable risk exposure in extraction culture)

**Pattern:** Labor recruitment concentrates in winter (4-6 weeks), creating temporary urban employment surge during otherwise slow season. Workers accept recruitment with assumption they will:

- Earn 20-25 Watts daily peak season (vs. 5-8 Watts winter alternatives)
- Be housed on extraction site (barracks conditions, often inadequate)
- Work 6-day weeks with minimal rest

### Injury Patterns

Extraction work injuries concentrate in peak season:

- Logging accidents: Falls from trees, run-over incidents, log-jam breaks
- Mining accidents: Shaft collapses, equipment failures, Flux-blast accidents in special zones
- Processing facility: Equipment-related injuries, chemical burns (metal smelting), repetitive strain

Seasonal intensity creates emergency medicine surges where local healers (if available) or traveling medical practitioner networks encounter casualties faster than treatment capacity.

Injuries in autumn create medical debt that winter unemployment cannot repay, creating debt cycles.

## Water-Level and Road Condition Constraints

### Spring Flood Advantage and Risk

Rivers flood April-May (temperate zones), enabling 10-20x normal transport volume. Logging operations gamble on completing log drives before water levels normalize (May-June). Flooding earlier than expected accelerates schedules; flooding later than expected creates cascading delays.

### Summer Mud and Drought

Early summer experiences transition from flooding to normal flow. Roads remain muddy June-July (slow travel, equipment wear, heavy transport bottleneck). Late summer enters low-water conditions — rivers drop, road conditions normalize, transport becomes routine.

### Autumn Weather Unpredictability

Storm systems create sudden flooding (roads impassable for days) or drought-like conditions (river navigation difficult). Expedited autumn transport (grain, final ore movement, late timber shipments) must navigate unpredictable weather.

### Winter Complete Shutdown

Terrain impassable. Most transport ceases. Operations that must move goods (emergency priority, military supplies) use sledge/snow conditions and accept extreme costs.

## Profitability and Seasonal Economics

### Calendar-Driven Revenue

Extraction operations must complete revenue-generating work in operating season. Shutdown season is investment and maintenance:

- Salaries for core crew: 30-50 Watts daily × 25-35 people × 150 days winter = 112,500-262,500 Watts minimum
- Equipment maintenance: 20,000-50,000 Watts
- Facility upkeep: 10,000-20,000 Watts

Winter operations cost 150,000-350,000 Watts with zero revenue (unless processing accumulated ore generates modest cash). Summer operations must generate sufficient revenue to cover winter costs plus profit.

Margin pressure is severe: poor summer (weather disruption, equipment failure, transport bottleneck) can wipe out annual profit.

### Labor Cost Competition

Extraction work competes with other seasonal labor (harvest, urban rush, maritime work) for workers. Wage pressure increases when multiple industries need workers simultaneously.

Autumn creates extreme competition: harvest labor competes with logging autumn completion, urban processing needs labor, fishing preparation requires crews. Extraction operations must choose: pay premium wages losing profit margin, or accept reduced staffing completing work below optimal capacity.

## Vulnerability to Climate and War Disruption

**Climate disruptions** create operational crises:

- Early spring thaw eliminates winter sledge advantage
- Late spring flooding extends mud season
- Early autumn freeze closes transport before scheduled completion
- Extreme winter (cold enough to make equipment-breaking common) damages capital equipment

**War disruption** adds complications:

- Military conscription removes experienced workers exactly when seasonal labor needed
- Material requisitions reduce equipment availability for extraction
- Transport priority (military shipments take precedent) delays extraction product movement
- Uncertainty (route safety, merchant reliability) forces strategic decisions about season-long supply

The result: extraction operations operating at reduced efficiency during war, with compressed seasonal windows, vulnerable to any additional disruption.
