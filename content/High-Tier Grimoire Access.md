[[Rare Grimoire|Rare]], [[Legendary Grimoire|Legendary]], and above grimoires do not move through the same channels as [[Common Grimoire|Common]] or [[Uncommon Grimoire|Uncommon]] texts. The economics are different, the distribution is different, and the regulatory environment around them is substantive rather than nominal.

## Why High-Tier Grimoires Are Different

The exponential Flux cost structure means that high-tier spells have a naturally limited market. A T5 practitioner exists at a frequency in the population that makes the total number of potential buyers for a Rare grimoire small. This limits the commercial production incentive — volume economics do not apply — and shifts the market toward high-margin individual transactions rather than the mass distribution that defines the Common tier.

The physical quality requirements are also different. A [[Hearthlight]] copied onto a household tile with minor imprecision is annoying but still mostly functional. A Rare-tier sigil with minor imprecision is a misfire risk with consequences proportional to the spell's power. The market enforces quality standards for high-tier copies not through regulation but through the fact that a buyer who can afford a Rare-tier text can also afford to verify its quality before purchasing, and will.

## How Rare Texts Actually Move

In [[Terravelle]], Rare and above texts are held in guild libraries and city institutional archives. Private Rare holdings exist among high-tier practitioners, senior guild masters, and old families with long practitioner lineages. Copies from these sources reach new owners through three channels: formal sale at prices that mark a meaningful financial event for the buyer, inheritance, and institutional loan against a significant deposit.

In [[Auralis]], the [[The Council of Auralis|Council]] and [[The Advancement Corps]] hold most of the institutional Rare library. Access is governed by the Scholar network: a practitioner with sufficient standing can request access to copy a specific spell, and the request is reviewed against the institution's assessment of purpose and reliability. The criteria are not published. Practitioners who have been granted access understand, through experience, what kind of request is likely to succeed.

## The Patronage Layer

Above the formal institutional channel, a significant portion of high-tier grimoire access in both kingdoms operates through patronage. A senior high-tier practitioner who holds originals is approached by a junior practitioner or researcher who wants access. The arrangement is negotiated privately: copying rights in exchange for service, research collaboration, political support, or direct payment. The resulting copy is not guild-certified in Terravelle and not Scholar-vouched in Auralis — it is a private arrangement between two parties, and its value rests entirely on the reputation of the patron who authorized it.

[[Drest]] holds this kind of access, through the [[The Culmination Faction|Culmination Faction]]'s institutional relationships, to ruins-survey spell sets that are not in circulation through any public channel. The practitioners who work with him on expedition work receive access to these texts as part of the working arrangement. They understand that the access ends with the relationship.

## Legendary and Above

[[Legendary Grimoire|Legendary]]-tier texts — T7 and T8 spells — are held almost exclusively by institutions. In Terravelle, the [[Valdenmoor]] guild archive holds three authenticated Legendary masters. In Auralis, the Advancement Corps holds a small Legendary collection that is not publicly inventoried. Individual practitioners at T7 or above who have developed original Legendary-tier work may hold personal copies; these are known to the institutions and quietly tracked.

[[Mythic Grimoire|Mythic]] and [[Pale Grimoire|Pale]] tier materials are documentation only, not functional copies. The Watt costs for T9 spells are in ranges that no practitioner in either kingdom's history has sustainably accessed. The texts exist as records of what was possible, held in the most restricted sections of institutional archives, and are reviewed by researchers with specific scholarly purposes rather than practitioners expecting to cast from them.
