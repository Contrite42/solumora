# Terravelle Civil Disputes

Civil disputes in [[Terravelle]]—contract breaches, property boundaries, inheritance conflicts, debt collection—are resolved through guild mediation for guild-affiliated parties and formal courts for non-affiliated or cross-party disputes. Unlike criminal matters where reputation and neighborhood resolution dominate, civil disputes involve monetary stakes sufficient to justify institutional involvement and legally-binding remedies.

## Types of Civil Disputes

**Contract Disputes.** Buyer and seller disagree about goods received, services rendered, or payment obligation. A merchant contracts for 100 bolts of fabric; 80 arrive. Merchant refuses payment for shortfall. Seller claims buyer agreed to reduced delivery due to supply constraints. Dispute involves interpreting contract terms and assessing performance.

**Boundary and Property Disputes.** Neighbors disagree about property boundaries, building encroachment, or usage rights. One household builds wall encroaching 2 feet on neighbor's property. Neighbor demands wall removal. Builder claims property line was established based on verbal agreement now reinterpreted differently.

**Inheritance Conflicts.** Family members dispute distribution of deceased's estate. Will allocates house to eldest son; widow claims widow's dower (standard entitlement to portion of estate). Widow and son negotiate but cannot reach settlement.

**Debt Collection.** Creditor demands repayment of personal loan, business debt, or wage advance. Debtor claims debt was forgiven verbally, or repayment obligation was contingent on conditions unmet. Creditor sues for repayment.

**Rental Disputes.** Landlord and tenant disagree about rent owed, property damage responsibility, or lease termination. Tenant moves out without notice; landlord claims abandoned lease obligating tenant to pay through lease end-date. Tenant claims lease was terminated by landlord's instruction.

**Employment Disputes.** Employer and worker disagree about wages owed, job description changes, or termination legitimacy. Employer cuts worker's wage claiming productivity decline; worker claims wage reduction was not authorized and demands full pay. Employer claims worker was fired for theft; worker claims firing was retaliatory.

## Guild Mediation Process

Guild-affiliated parties (those belonging to Trade Guilds, Scrivener Guilds, or specialty guilds) resolve contracts within their guild structure:

**Informal Guild Panel.** When dispute arises, either party can request guild mediation. Guild coordinator summons both parties. A 3-5 person panel (guild officers, neutral guild members, sometimes external arbitrators) hears brief case presentation from both sides. Panel reviews contract or relevant documentation, asks clarifying questions, and issues non-binding preliminary assessment. Many disputes settle at this stage when preliminary assessment clarifies likely formal outcome.

**Formal Guild Arbitration.** If informal panel doesn't resolve dispute, parties request formal arbitration. Arbitration panel holds hearing (1-3 hours depending on complexity), examines evidence and testimony, and issues binding decision. Decision is documented in guild precedent log. If losing party refuses to comply, guild enforcement mechanisms activate—certification restrictions, commercial isolation, eventual guild expulsion.

**Appeal to Guild Council.** Major disputes can appeal arbitration decision to Guild Council (highest guild authority), which reviews whether arbitration panel correctly applied guild standards. Appeal is limited—Council doesn't revisit facts, only examines whether procedure was fair and precedent correctly applied. Council decisions are final within guild structure.

**Cost and Timeline.** Guild mediation is inexpensive (filing fee 5-8 Watts for informal panel, 10-20 Watts for formal arbitration) and rapid (informal panel 3-5 days, formal arbitration 1-3 weeks from request to decision). Binding nature of formal arbitration means losing party must comply or face guild sanctions.

## Formal Court Process

Non-guild-affiliated parties, or cross-party disputes where one party is non-affiliated, are resolved through formal courts:

**Court Filing.** Plaintiff files complaint with court clerk, paying filing fee (8-15 Watts depending on claim amount). Complaint briefly describes dispute and relief requested. Clerk records filing and notifies defendant.

**Defendant Response.** Defendant receives notice and has 2-4 weeks to file response. Response admits or denies complaint allegations, raises legal defenses, and can counterclaim against plaintiff for relief.

**Pre-Trial Examination.** Judge (or magistrate for minor disputes) reviews complaint and response, assesses whether dispute involves genuine disagreement of material fact or is settled legal question. If settled law, judge can dismiss case or rule in plaintiff's favor without trial. If factual dispute, case proceeds to trial.

**Trial.** Both parties present evidence and testimony before judge. Parties can hire legal representatives (typically an educated scribe or retired guild officer with legal knowledge; trained legal professions don't exist in Terravelle). Judge assesses credibility, weighs evidence, and determines outcome. Trial duration varies from 1-2 hours for simple contract disputes to multiple sessions for complex property disputes.

**Judgment.** Judge issues written judgment stating who prevails and what remedy is awarded (monetary damages, specific performance, injunction, etc.). Judgment is recorded in court registry and becomes binding legal authority.

**Appeal.** Losing party can appeal to higher court within 30 days if they dispute legal reasoning or claim procedural error. Appeals are expensive (20-50 Watts in appeal fees) and lengthy (2-4 months process), and appellate judges rarely overturn trial decisions, making appeals rare for disputes under 100 Watts.

**Enforcement.** If defendant refuses to comply with judgment, plaintiff can file execution petition requesting court enforcement. Court officers attempt collection (seizing property, garnishing wages if defendant is currently employed). Enforcement is slow and uncertain; many judgments are never fully collected.

## Practical Dispute Resolution Patterns

Civil disputes follow predictable resolution pathways based on party status and dispute value:

**Guild-Affiliated Both Parties.** Disputes resolve through guild arbitration 80-90% of time. Parties prefer arbitration because cost is low, timeline is rapid, and informal preliminary panel often clarifies expected outcome encouraging settlement. Arbitration maintains guild relationships because arbitrators emphasize restoring commercial cooperation. Formal courts are rare (reserved for enforcement of prior arbitration decisions).

**Guild-Affiliated + Non-Affiliated.** Disputes resolve through guild arbitration if non-affiliated party consents (choosing guild justice over formal courts). Guild arbitration remains cheaper and faster than courts. If non-affiliated party refuses arbitration, dispute goes to formal court. Guild-affiliated party is disadvantaged because formal courts apply different standards (formal law vs. guild precedent) and arbitrators may not understand guild operational context. Courts can be weaponized by non-guild parties to create unwanted litigation costs.

**Both Non-Affiliated.** Disputes likely resolve informally through neighborhood mediation or direct negotiation. If informal resolution fails, formal court is only option. Courts are expensive and slow for both parties; many disputes settle before trial because litigation costs exceed dispute value. Example: dispute over 15 Watts debt involving 20 Watt litigation costs incentivizes settlement at 10 Watts (half of claimed debt) because this costs both parties less than formal trial.

**High-Value Disputes.** For disputes exceeding 100+ Watts, formal court is more likely to be used by both guild and non-guild parties. Arbitration has liability limits (arbitrators are not fully liable for incorrect decisions; courts have greater accountability). Parties are willing to absorb court costs for large-value disputes where outcome significance exceeds litigation expense. Example: 200-Watt contract dispute justifies 30 Watt court costs because outcome matters sufficiently. 15 Watt contract dispute cannot justify equivalent court costs.

## Enforcement and Non-Compliance

Judgment enforcement is the most uncertain phase of dispute resolution. Courts issue judgments but possess limited power to compel compliance:

**Wage Garnishment.** Winning party can petition court for wage garnishment, directing defendant's employer to withhold percentage of wages toward judgment. If defendant becomes unemployed, garnishment ceases and judgment is effectively uncollected.

**Property Seizure.** Court officers can seize property and auction it to satisfy judgment. Auctioned property often sells for 30-50% of fair value because buyers know property is distressed sale. Judgment for 50 Watts might result in property seizure only raising 30 Watts.

**Debtor's Prison.** Historical practice where debtors were imprisoned until debts paid. Modern Terravelle constrains imprisonment (debt is civil matter, not criminal), but severe contempt of court (deliberate non-compliance with judgment despite ability to comply) can trigger brief imprisonment (typically 5-15 days) pressuring compliance. Perpetual poverty prevents this mechanism functioning; if defendant is genuinely unable to pay, imprisonment produces no revenue.

**Social/Guild Enforcement.** If defendant is guild-affiliated, winning plaintiff can petition guild to enforce judgment. Guild enforcement is often more effective than court enforcement because guild sanctions (certification suspension, member expulsion) are severe business penalties. Non-guild defendants face only court remedies.

**Judgment Escape.** Defendants sometimes flee jurisdiction to avoid compliance burden. Judgment becomes uncollected. If defendant later returns, winning party can refile and attempt enforcement, but enforcement costs and defendant's continued evasion often make pursuit uneconomical.

## Comparison with Auralis Formal Courts

Terravelle courts operate with less formality, lower cost, and slower timelines than [[Auralis Civic Courts]]:

| Factor | Terravelle | Auralis |
|--------|-----------|---------|
| Judicial Training | Community reputation, some legal education | Formal legal training, government appointment |
| Evidence Standards | Common sense assessment, witness credibility judgment | Strict evidence rules, admissibility determinations |
| Precedent | Loose consultation of prior cases | Strict precedent binding |
| Procedure | Flexible, judge-directed | Rigorous, rule-bound |
| Cost | 10-30 Watts filing + representation | 30-80 Watts filing + legal representation |
| Timeline | 2-6 weeks trial | 3-6 months pre-trial + trial |
| Appeal | Rare due to cost/unlikelihood | Common for significant cases |
| Enforcement | Weak, depends on defendant cooperation | Stronger institutional capacity |

Terravelle advantages: cheaper, faster, more accessible to poor parties. Auralis advantages: greater consistency, stronger enforcement, clearer precedent.

## Limitations and Inequities

Civil dispute resolution in Terravelle favors parties with:
- Guild affiliation (mediation access)
- Wealth (can afford court costs and legal representation)
- Local reputation (judges trust established community members)
- Documented contracts (written evidence weighs heavily)

Poor non-guild-affiliated parties face:
- Expensive formal courts as only option
- Legal representation difficult to afford
- Judge skepticism toward poor parties (less community standing)
- Weak enforcement remedies (no property to seize, no employment to garnish)

Systematic inequity means many poor-party disputes are effectively unresolved—winning judgments that cannot be enforced because defendant has no attachable assets.
