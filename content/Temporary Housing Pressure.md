# Temporary Housing Pressure

**Temporary Housing Pressure** describes the recurrent mismatch between short-term shelter demand and available beds in labor districts, crossing towns, and clinic-adjacent neighborhoods.

It is not only a homelessness indicator. It is a system-load indicator that rises when wage volatility, care delays, and ration shifts overlap.

## Demand Drivers

Temporary housing demand increases under five recurring conditions:

- delayed wage settlement in casual labor markets
- displacement from overcrowded multi-family rentals
- return from failed crossings and interrupted convoy work
- family consolidation after medical shocks in [[Clinical Care Systems]]
- seasonal migration into district work zones

Most spikes are not caused by one crisis event. They are caused by many small household failures converging in the same week.

## Supply Structure

Short-cycle shelter supply is distributed across:

- low-cost boarding houses
- guild-affiliated dormitory beds
- temple emergency cots
- contract beds reserved through [[Queue Management Offices]]

This supply is elastic only within limits. Once reserve beds are consumed, households cycle through informal arrangements that are less stable and less visible to administrators.

## Operational Behaviors Under Strain

When pressure rises, shelter managers and district offices adopt survival policies:

- shorter stay limits
- stricter proof-of-work requirements
- dependency-priority lanes for children and elders
- rotation lists coordinated through [[District Continuity Coordinators]]

These rules preserve turnover but increase administrative burden on households already under stress.

## Links to Food and Care

Housing instability amplifies both [[Ration Substitution Practices]] and [[Care Triage Workflows]].

Households that move repeatedly lose storage continuity, meal regularity, and follow-up appointment adherence. That creates predictable downstream load in clinics and queue systems.

## Early Warning Signals

Operators track pressure through practical indicators:

- rising same-day denial rates at district beds
- increased repeat-use by the same households within 30 days
- longer queue rollover chains in shelter-adjacent offices

These indicators usually precede visible public disorder by weeks.

## Why It Matters

Temporary housing pressure is where "functional" systems reveal thin margins.

If shelter churn stays high, other continuity systems absorb the cost: food distribution becomes less efficient, care follow-up falls, and labor reliability drops. The system keeps running, but each subsystem runs hotter.
