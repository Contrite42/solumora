Stenn Ridge is an iron-mining settlement in the northern uplands, sixty miles northeast of [[Greyveil]]. Population approximately 400, distributed across three work camps and a central processing site. It supplies raw ore to Greyveil's smelters through seasonal overland freight during the dry months.

## The Operation

The iron seams at Stenn Ridge run shallow and horizontal through layered sedimentary rock. Mining here is not deep-shaft work — it is surface extraction, where crews cut benches into the hillside and follow the iron layers as they trace through the ridge face. The work advances slowly, methodically, and stops entirely when winter weather makes the access roads impassable.

Most miners are [[Control Tier|Tier 0]] or [[Control Tier|Tier 1]]. The work is manual: hammer, chisel, lever, and patience. [[Flux]] use concentrates around two problems: structural support for the work face and controlled fracturing of rock layers that are too consolidated to break with hand tools.

Toren Marsk, T2, manages structural work for the eastern camps. He casts Rockbind sequences to stabilize overhangs while crews work beneath them, and maintains support pillars in sections where the bench cuts have created unstable zones. His work is precise, low-cost, and critical — a collapse at Stenn Ridge means lost production time and injured miners, both of which the settlement cannot afford.

## The Season

Stenn Ridge operates on a seasonal rhythm dictated by road conditions. Mining runs from late spring through mid-autumn. Processing and ore transport happen during the dry summer months when the overland route to Greyveil is passable for heavy freight wagons. Winter shuts the operation down entirely — access roads become mud channels, and the settlement population drops to a skeleton crew that maintains equipment and plans next season's cuts.

This year's mining season started three weeks late. Spring rain extended further into the dry transition than usual, which delayed road access and compressed the operational window. Kess Aldren, who coordinates transport logistics between Stenn Ridge and Greyveil, estimates they lost 15% of expected ore volume before they started cutting.

The shortfall has already propagated. Greyveil's smelters expected consistent ore delivery through late autumn. Kess has rescheduled the freight runs to concentrate volume into a shorter window, but transport capacity is fixed and the roads will close on the same calendar regardless of how much ore remains unprocessed.

## The Economics

Stenn Ridge operates on thin margins. The ore quality is adequate — not premium, not poor — and the settlement's economic viability depends on consistent seasonal volume delivered at predictable cost. A 15% volume loss does not destroy the operation, but it eliminates the buffer that allows the settlement to absorb other disruptions: equipment breakdown, frost arriving early, or market price fluctuations in Greyveil.

The miners know this. They are not increasing work hours or extending the season beyond safe operating conditions. What they are doing is recording the shortfall carefully and watching the autumn weather closely. If next season's spring transition runs long again, the settlement will have to decide whether the operation remains viable or whether Stenn Ridge becomes one of the many extracted-out sites that dot the northern uplands.

## Daily Life

Stenn Ridge is not a town. It is three work camps connected by service roads and a central processing site where ore is sorted, graded, and prepared for transport. Most miners live in camp housing during the operational season and return to their home communities during winter shutdown. Families rarely live at the ridge year-round — there is no school, no permanent market, and no infrastructure for children or dependents who are not part of the mining operation.

The exception is the core crew: equipment specialists, transport coordinators, and the handful of practitioners who maintain structural and operational casting. They stay through winter, maintain the site, and form the continuous institutional memory that keeps Stenn Ridge functioning across seasonal breaks.

Toren Marsk is one of them. He has spent eleven winters at Stenn Ridge. He knows which sections of the work face are stable, which are marginal, and which should not be cut without additional support. When miners return each spring, they rely on his judgment to resume work safely.
