**Auralis Civic Courts** are the formal legal institutions handling criminal and civil disputes in [[Auralis]]. Operating through professional judges, documented procedures, and codified law, the courts provide standardized justice across Auralis's urban and settled regions. They are designed for complex cases, commercial disputes, and crimes requiring institutional authority to resolve.

## Structure

The court system operates at three levels:

**District Courts** handle routine criminal cases (theft, assault, fraud) and civil disputes (contracts, property, debt) in [[Auralis]]'s major settlements. Each district court employs 3-8 judges depending on population. Judges hear cases individually, not in panels. District court rulings can be appealed to Regional Courts.

**Regional Courts** hear appeals from District Courts and handle serious crimes (murder, large-scale fraud, high-tier Flux crimes). Each regional court covers multiple districts and employs 8-15 judges. Regional court rulings can be appealed to the High Court in limited circumstances (procedural error, precedent conflict, constitutional questions).

**High Court** sits in [[Solhaven]]'s [[The Crestward|Crestward]] district with seven justices appointed by [[The Council of Auralis]]. It hears constitutional cases, cross-kingdom disputes, and appeals from Regional Courts when significant precedent questions arise. High Court rulings establish binding precedent across all Auralis courts.

## Judges

Civic Court judges are professional appointments, not elected positions. Candidates typically have:
- Legal training through [[Aldrane Academy]]'s administrative law program or equivalent private instruction
- 5-10 years experience as court clerks, legal representatives, or civic administrators
- [[Control Tier|T2-T4]] capacity (not required but common, as many cases involve Flux evidence assessment)
- Council nomination and confirmation

Judges serve indefinite terms subject to conduct review every five years. Removal requires Council vote following documented incompetence, corruption, or abuse of authority. Most judges serve 15-30 years before retiring.

Judicial appointments reflect Auralis's institutional philosophy: law should be consistent, precedent-based, and administered by trained professionals rather than community consensus. Judges are expected to apply documented law regardless of personal opinion or public pressure.

## Trial Process

Criminal trials follow standardized procedure:

**Investigation Phase:** [[Auralis Enforcement Bureau]] investigates reported crimes, gathers evidence, and brings charges when evidence supports prosecution. Defendants are informed of charges and given time to prepare defense.

**Pre-Trial:** Judge reviews evidence admissibility, hears motions from both sides, and sets trial parameters. Heavy evidence disputes are resolved here rather than during trial.

**Trial:** Prosecution presents evidence and witness testimony. Defense cross-examines and presents counter-evidence. Judges assess credibility, weigh evidence, and determine guilt. There are no juries — judges render verdicts individually.

**Sentencing:** If convicted, judges impose sentences based on standardized guidelines with discretion for aggravating or mitigating factors. Defendants can appeal within 30 days.

Civil trials follow similar procedure but use "preponderance of evidence" standard rather than "beyond reasonable doubt." Judges determine liability and impose remedies (financial compensation, contract enforcement, injunctions).

## Flux Crime Challenges

The courts struggle with [[Flux]]-enabled criminal activity because evidence standards designed for physical crimes do not adapt well to effects that can be concealed, manipulated, or destroyed through spellwork.

**Detection Limitations:** Most judges cannot personally detect high-tier Flux manipulation. Courts must rely on expert testimony from contracted [[Flux Users|practitioners]], creating dependency on witnesses whose credibility defendants can challenge.

**Evidence Destruction:** Competent practitioners can destroy evidence chemically, thermally, or through sigil-based concealment. Courts must infer guilt from evidence *absence* when the defendant had capability and motive to destroy it. [[The Halveth Evidence Manipulation Case]] established this inference as legally valid, but the precedent remains controversial.

**Witness Reliability:** Courts struggle to verify whether witnesses have been Mind-influenced. Standard procedure requires T3+ detection assessment for any witness testifying against a T4+ defendant, but detection is not perfectly reliable. Defense attorneys routinely challenge witness credibility in high-tier cases.

**Tier Asymmetry:** Judges cannot personally understand what high-tier practitioners can do. A T3 judge hearing a case against a T6 defendant must accept expert testimony about capabilities beyond their direct experience. This creates credibility assessment challenges that lower-tier cases do not face.

Despite these limitations, Auralis Civic Courts prosecute Flux crimes more effectively than [[Terravelle Guild Mediation|Terravelle's guild system]] because institutional resources, specialist investigators, and precedent documentation enable systematic evidence gathering and consistent sentencing.

## Sentencing Guidelines

Auralis law specifies sentencing ranges for different crime categories:

- **Minor Theft (under 5,000 [[Watts]]):** Restitution plus 50-500 Watts fine
- **Major Theft (5,000-50,000 Watts):** Restitution plus 6-18 months civic labor
- **Violent Assault (non-lethal):** 1-5 years imprisonment
- **Murder:** 10-25 years imprisonment or execution in aggravated cases
- **Flux Crime (evidence destruction, Mind manipulation, remote harm):** Enhanced sentencing +50% to +200% depending on tier and harm caused
- **Repeat Offenses:** Escalating sentences, eventual permanent exile from Auralis territory

Judges have discretion within these ranges. Mitigating factors (first offense, cooperation with investigation, victim compensation) reduce sentences. Aggravating factors (high-tier abuse, victim vulnerability, premeditation) increase sentences.

## Corruption and Fairness

Auralis Civic Courts are not immune to corruption. Judges appointed through Council patronage sometimes favor Council interests in politically sensitive cases. Wealthy defendants hire better legal representation and receive more favorable outcomes than poor defendants facing similar charges.

However, institutional oversight limits overt corruption. Judges face conduct reviews. Rulings are documented and appealable. Public trial records enable reputational accountability even when formal discipline does not occur.

The system is imperfectly fair but more consistent than pure community justice. Defendants know what law applies, what evidence is required for conviction, and what sentences they face if found guilty. That predictability is Auralis's institutional advantage over Terravelle's adaptive but variable guild mediation.

## Relationship with Enforcement Bureau

Courts depend on [[Auralis Enforcement Bureau]] for investigation, evidence gathering, and arrest. Bureau limitations constrain what courts can prosecute. If the Bureau lacks resources to investigate a case, courts never see it. If Bureau investigators cannot gather sufficient evidence (often true in high-tier Flux crimes), courts cannot convict regardless of actual guilt.

This dependency creates frustration. Judges know many criminals escape prosecution not because courts would fail to convict but because Bureau cannot gather evidence. Some judges advocate for expanded Bureau funding and specialist hiring. Others argue courts should accept their role as final decision-makers on cases that reach them, rather than attempting to expand institutional scope.

## Public Perception

Common Auralis citizens view Civic Courts as distant but legitimate. Few people interact with courts directly, but the system's existence provides reassurance that formal recourse exists if wronged. Wealthy citizens view courts as navigable institutions where competent legal representation produces favorable outcomes. Poor citizens view courts as intimidating and expensive, often avoiding formal legal action even when wronged.

High-tier practitioners view courts with mild disdain. They recognize that most judges cannot fully assess their capabilities and that evidence standards favor defendants when evidence can be destroyed. High-tier criminals prosecuted by Civic Courts are either unusually incompetent or victims of political prosecution rather than genuine evidence-based conviction.

## Notable Judges

**[[Cordan Vess]]** is the most prominent Flux crime specialist among sitting judges, with 18 years on Regional Court hearing cases involving T3+ defendants and establishing important evidence precedents.

**Selt Varren** is a High Court justice known for strict precedent adherence and resistance to expanding judicial interpretation beyond documented law.

**Nessa Trell** is a District Court judge in [[Solhaven]]'s [[Harbor District]] with reputation for pragmatic sentencing focused on victim restitution rather than punitive imprisonment.

## Limitations

Auralis Civic Courts cannot:
- Prosecute crimes outside Auralis jurisdiction (including [[Equatorial Desert|Desert Zakros]] interior)
- Compel [[Terravelle]] witnesses or defendants to appear
- Detect all Flux-based evidence manipulation
- Afford equal legal representation to all defendants regardless of wealth
- Process all reported crimes (volume exceeds institutional capacity)
- Prevent political interference in high-profile cases

The system functions within these constraints. It is not perfect justice. It is institutional justice — consistent, documented, precedent-based, and limited by the resources and capabilities available to professional administrators operating under formal mandate.
