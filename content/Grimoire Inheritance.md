When a practitioner dies, their grimoire library is among the more economically significant items in their estate — and among the more complicated to settle. The complications arise from the layered nature of grimoire ownership: the physical object, the copying rights, the originator declarations, and the institutional relationships that gave certain copies their value.

## Physical Inheritance

The physical grimoire passes to heirs through normal estate process in both kingdoms. In [[Terravelle]] this is typically documented through the inheritance record system that the [[Trade Guilds]] maintain for practitioners with certified libraries. In [[Auralis]] it flows through family arrangements with the Scholar network sometimes acting as administrator for practitioners whose libraries have institutional significance.

A practitioner's personal working grimoire — the annotated, modified, personally customized text that represents years of use — is usually the most valuable physical object in the library. It is also the least commercially transferable, because its value comes precisely from the personal annotations that make it specific to that practitioner's method. Heirs who are practitioners themselves may find it useful; heirs who are not typically sell it to a Scribe or institution that can extract the originator content and republish the relevant parts.

## Copying Rights

If the practitioner held originator declarations on any spells — registered under the [[Trade Guilds|guild]] system in Terravelle — those rights pass to the estate. What the heirs do with them depends on whether anyone in the family has the institutional knowledge to maintain them and whether the volume of copies sold is worth the administration.

Most small originator rights are abandoned within a generation. The practitioner who developed a useful variant of [[Common Grimoire#Lockward|Lockward]] had a meaningful claim during their lifetime; their heirs, who are merchants or farmers with no guild affiliation, typically allow the declaration to lapse rather than maintain the certification relationship. The spells enter general circulation without attribution.

## High-Tier Libraries

A practitioner who holds [[Rare Grimoire|Rare]]-tier or above grimoires faces a specific inheritance problem: the texts are valuable enough that heirs who are not qualified to use them (because they lack the [[Control Tier|tier]]) are holding property they cannot activate and that institutions will pay significant amounts to acquire.

In [[Auralis]], the Council or affiliated institutions have standing offers for Rare and above texts from estates, priced at rates that make outright sale attractive relative to attempting to maintain the texts in private hands. Practitioners who want their libraries to remain in family hands sometimes pre-arrange transfer to a qualifying family member before death; those who do not plan ahead often find the estate process involves institutional buyers moving quickly.

In Terravelle, institutional holdings are managed through guild libraries and city archives. High-tier texts from private estates are occasionally donated, more often sold at auction to qualified buyers, and sometimes simply held by heirs who understand what they have well enough to wait for the right buyer.

## Contested Estates

The most complicated grimoire inheritances involve practitioners who held original works — particularly if those works have not yet been formally published or registered. An unpublished annotated grimoire representing a practitioner's lifetime research may have significant commercial and academic value, and heirs who do not understand the text well enough to evaluate it are in a poor negotiating position relative to institutions or Scribes who do.

[[Rell Hadv]]'s certification notes represent the kind of record that creates this problem: a working practitioner's documentation of a lifetime of guild work, held in informal form. The institutional interest in acquiring such records is real. So is the difficulty in valuing them fairly without deep expertise.
