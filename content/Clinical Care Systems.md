Routine medical care in [[Solumora]] is not organized around hospitals. It is organized around access radius, cost tolerance, and who a patient can convince to see them quickly.

In both [[Terravelle]] and [[Auralis]], most people receive treatment from practitioners in the [[Control Tier|T0-T2]] range, usually in small clinics attached to markets, temples, guild halls, or converted homes. The high-tier medicine people read about in elite case reports matters for edge cases. For daily injuries and illness, the system is a patchwork built to keep people working, not to restore ideal health.

## Intake Paths

Patients usually enter care through one of four routes:

1. Walk-in clinic assessment for fever, wound care, bone setting, and infection control.
2. Market or district healer for lower-cost treatment with minimal documentation.
3. Temple-affiliated infirmary for low-income households and children.
4. Guild referral for workers whose employers have negotiated practitioner accounts.

In [[Solhaven]], harbor workers often start at the northern temple infirmary before being referred to a paid clinic if follow-up is needed. In [[Valdenmoor]], guild-affiliated crews typically use pre-arranged accounts with district practitioners. In [[Halveth]], expedition crews buy treatment access in advance because delays can end a crossing before it starts.

## What Clinics Actually Do

Daily clinical work is repetitive and practical:

- wound cleaning and sealing;
- infection monitoring and staged antibiotic-herbal protocols;
- fracture stabilization with splints and low-tier Flux support;
- respiratory and gastrointestinal management from poor water or crowded housing;
- post-fever weakness care so workers return gradually instead of collapsing on shift.

Most clinics triage by urgency and recoverability, not by formal diagnosis labels. A practitioner asks three questions first: can this patient survive without escalation, can this patient pay for full treatment, and can this patient safely return to work.

## Cost Bands and Time Loss

Cost is usually measured against missed wages.

- Basic assessment: 1-3 [[Watts]]
- Standard treatment cycle (3-5 visits): 4-12 Watts
- Complicated wound/fever episode: 10-25 Watts
- Specialist referral intake: 12-30 Watts before treatment

For households in [[Life in Rural Terravelle|rural Terravelle]] and [[Life in Rural Auralis|rural Auralis]], travel costs can exceed treatment fees. A two-day trip to a city practitioner can mean lost labor equal to the treatment itself.

## Referral Patterns

Referral is the system's pressure valve and its biggest failure point.

T1-T2 practitioners can usually identify when a case exceeds their capacity, but referral success depends on transport, money, and social standing. The wealthy fail referrals less often because they can pay for immediate transfer and retain specialist attention. Poor households are more likely to be told to "return if worse" and then arrive later with harder cases.

Referrals most likely to fail:

- suspected [[Flux Pathway Damage|pathway damage]] without obvious external trauma;
- deep infection after delayed wound care;
- childbirth complications outside urban transport range;
- mixed trauma plus dehydration in [[Equatorial Desert|desert-edge]] communities.

## Public Health as Local Habit

Neither kingdom runs a modern centralized public health authority. Disease control works through local habit, guild incentives, and temple campaigns.

- Dock and market districts use seasonal boil-water advisories.
- Midwives and clinic assistants track neighborhood fever clusters informally.
- Guilds subsidize treatment when labor continuity matters more than immediate savings.
- Temples distribute low-cost filtration packets in high-risk months.

Outbreak control is strongest where trust networks are stable. It weakens in transient districts where workers rotate quickly and nobody owns neighborhood monitoring.

## System Stress

Under stress, clinics prioritize throughput and stabilization over complete care.

During trade disruptions or conflict spikes, three things happen in sequence:

1. medicine stocks thin and substitution rises;
2. follow-up visits drop because people cannot miss shifts;
3. preventable complications rise two to three weeks later.

The system continues to function, but outcomes degrade unevenly. Elite districts maintain continuity through private retainers; labor districts absorb the drop in quality.
