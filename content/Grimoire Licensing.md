Grimoire licensing is the body of conventions — formal in [[Terravelle]], informal in [[Auralis]] — that governs who has the right to copy and sell a given spell, and what compensation flows back to the originator or publisher. It is not a clean legal system in either kingdom, and a significant portion of the [[Common Grimoire|Common]]-tier market operates outside whatever conventions technically apply.

## Originator Rights

The practitioner who develops a new spell has a recognized claim to be identified as its originator. In Terravelle, this claim is registered through the [[Trade Guilds]] — a Scribe certifying a new spell for Common distribution files an originator declaration, which is recorded in the guild ledger and gives the originator a modest royalty interest in copies sold through the certification network.

In Auralis, originator recognition is more a matter of academic credit than economic right. Scholars who develop or refine spells are cited; the citation matters for institutional standing and patronage more than for direct compensation. There is no formal royalty mechanism in the Auralis Scholar network.

## What Licensing Actually Covers

Licensing in practice covers two things: the right to produce certified copies, and the right to use the originator's name as a quality credential. A Scribe who says a spell is "authorized reproduction of [originator]'s master" is making a claim about source quality that carries commercial value. The claim can be audited — it either traces back to the authenticated master or it does not.

The right to produce copies without authorization is not prohibited, strictly speaking, in most contexts. Common-tier spells are not legally monopolized in either kingdom — the tier is too broadly distributed for exclusivity to be enforceable. The licensing system matters economically because authorized copies command a price premium and can be sold through institutional channels that unauthorized copies cannot access.

## Contested Areas

Licensing disputes cluster around three situations:

**Spell refinements**: when a Scribe or practitioner improves an existing spell — better efficiency, cleaner geometry, reduced cost — who holds the rights to the improved version? In Terravelle, the improver can file a new originator declaration if the change is substantial enough to constitute a new sigil. What counts as "substantial" is argued case by case.

**Re-derivation**: a practitioner who independently arrives at the same sigil construction as an existing spell. If they can demonstrate independent development, they can file their own originator declaration. In practice, most re-deriver situations settle through negotiation rather than guild proceedings.

**Cross-kingdom copies**: a spell developed in Terravelle under guild licensing has no formal standing in Auralis's Scholar network. The reverse is also true. Cross-kingdom copies of licensed spells generate revenue for no one but the Scribe who makes them. This is the largest gap in the licensing system and the one with the most money moving through it.

## Effective vs. Nominal Rights

In practical terms, licensing rights are only enforceable up to the point where a rights-holder has the institutional leverage to act on them. Large guilds enforce vigorously against unauthorized copies of high-volume spells — the revenue matters enough to fund enforcement. Small originators filing individual declarations rarely have the means to pursue copyists who ignore the attribution.

This asymmetry shapes what gets officially published versus what circulates informally. A practitioner with a genuinely useful original spell faces a choice between entering the formal licensing channel (lower revenue per copy, higher volume, institutional credibility) and selling directly (higher margin per transaction, lower reach, no institutional protection).
