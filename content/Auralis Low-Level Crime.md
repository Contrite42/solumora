# Auralis Low-Level Crime

Low-level crime in [[Auralis]]—neighborhood theft, assault, minor fraud—is prosecuted through [[Auralis Enforcement Bureau]] patrol division and district courts. Unlike [[Terravelle]]'s informal neighborhood resolution, Auralis treats crime as state concern requiring institutional investigation and prosecution. Formal law enforcement is more visible but also more constrained by capacity limits; many crimes go uninvestigated despite formal institutional mandate.

## Enforcement Bureau Structure and Patrol Operations

Auralis Enforcement Bureau divides into Patrol Division and Investigation Division. Patrol Division provides visible street presence, responds to immediate incidents, and handles routine law enforcement in populated areas.

**Patrol Composition.** Each major city employs 15-40 patrol officers depending on population. Patrol officers are typically [[Control Tier|T1-T2]] practitioners with basic Flux detection training and combat capability. Officers work shifts covering geographic districts, patrolling streets during business hours and evening, responding to citizen reports and incidents they observe.

**Response Priority.** Patrol officers respond to:
- Violent crimes in progress (assaults, robberies)
- Crimes reported by high-status citizens
- Crimes in merchant districts (theft affecting commerce)
- Disturbances threatening public order

Patrol officers do not respond uniformly to all reported crimes. A theft from a wealthy merchant's shop gets rapid patrol response. A theft from a poor household in a low-priority district may not receive patrol response at all—officer is already responding to three concurrent situations and cannot dedicate resources to all reported crimes.

**Immediate Crime Response.** When patrol officers arrive at crime scene, their priorities are:
1. Secure scene preventing further harm
2. Apprehend perpetrator if accessible
3. Provide first aid to injured
4. Gather preliminary witness statements
5. Document scene conditions for investigation division

Patrol officers lack investigative training. Their role is containment and apprehension, not evidence gathering. Investigation happens afterward.

## Investigation Division

Professional investigators handle evidence-gathering, suspect interviews, and case preparation for prosecution. Investigators are typically T2-T3 practitioners with forensic and interview training. Most major crimes (serious theft, assault, fraud, suspected Flux crimes) are investigated by Investigation Division before prosecution.

**Investigation Process.** Once crime is reported (either by patrol or directly by victim), Investigation Division assigns case to available investigator. Investigation involves:
- Physical evidence collection (weapon, clothing, materials from crime scene)
- Witness and victim interviews (recorded in written statements)
- Suspect interviews and interrogation (if suspect apprehended)
- Physical evidence analysis (Flux residue testing, forensic examination)
- Prosecutor consultation (whether evidence supports conviction)

Investigation timeline varies from 2-3 days for straightforward cases (witnessed assault with clear perpetrator) to 4-8 weeks for complex cases (burglary requiring forensic analysis, multiple witness coordination, suspect location).

**Capacity Constraints.** Each investigations division is understaffed relative to caseload. Average investigator handles 15-25 cases simultaneously in various investigation stages. Priority assignment reflects:
- Victim status (wealthy victim → priority investigation)
- Crime severity (murder → rape → assault → theft)
- Evidence quality (strong evidence case → weak evidence case)

As a practical result, theft cases undergo minimal investigation. Investigator gathers initial evidence and victim statement, conducts basic suspect interview if suspect is apprehended, then forwards case to prosecutor. Sophisticated theft investigation (surveillance, suspect background research, financial analysis) happens only for significant value thefts (500+ Watts) or crimes by known organized criminals.

## District Court Prosecution and Sentencing

Alleged perpetrators are prosecuted in District Courts where judges determine guilt and impose sentences. Most low-level crimes are prosecuted in District Courts; serious crimes go to Regional Courts.

**Burden of Proof.** Prosecutors must prove guilt "beyond reasonable doubt"—standard requiring evidence strong enough that reasonable person would believe guilt. This is higher standard than civil cases ("preponderance of evidence") but lower than absolute certainty. Reasonable doubt exists when perpetrator's guilt is not clearly established despite substantial evidence.

**Guilty Pleas.** Many perpetrators plead guilty rather than proceed to trial. Guilty plea bypasses trial, reducing timeline from 2-3 months to 2-3 weeks. Perpetrator who pleads guilty typically receives slightly reduced sentence in exchange for accepting guilt without trial. A perpetrator might plead guilty to assault expecting 6 months imprisonment rather than go to trial risking 12 months imprisonment if convicted.

**Trial Process.** When perpetrator contests guilt, trial is held before judge (no jury system in Auralis). Prosecutor presents evidence (witnesses, physical evidence, circumstantial evidence), defendant presents counterEvidence (alibi, character witnesses). Judge assesses evidence credibility and determines guilt.

**Sentencing Guidelines.** Conviction is followed by sentencing based on standardized guidelines with judicial discretion. Guidelines distinguish:
- **First Offense** (no prior convictions): reduced sentence
- **Repeat Offense** (2+ prior convictions): enhanced sentence
- **Aggravating Factors** (crime involved violence, was planned, defendant position of trust): increased sentence
- **Mitigating Factors** (defendant young, was provoked, expressed remorse): reduced sentence

## Common Crimes and Sentences

**Theft.** Petit theft (value under 50 Watts): 1-3 months jail. Grand theft (value 50-500 Watts): 3-12 months jail. Theft above 500 Watts: 1-3 years imprisonment. Sentences reduced by 50% for first-time offenders.

**Assault.** Simple assault (minor injury): 15-30 days jail. Aggravated assault (serious injury): 2-6 months jail. Assault with weapon: 6-18 months jail. Aggravating factors (victim is elderly, child, or authority figure): double sentence.

**Fraud.** Fraud under 100 Watts: 1-4 months jail. Fraud 100-500 Watts: 3-12 months jail. Organized fraud: 1-5 years imprisonment. First-time offenders get reduced sentences.

**Drunken Disturbance.** Public intoxication with disturbance: typically fines (2-5 Watts) rather than jail. Repeated offenses: brief jail (3-5 days). Violence during intoxication: same as assault charges.

## Incarceration and Punishment

Auralis maintains three types of penal institutions:

**Local Jails.** Temporary detention for defendants awaiting trial and short-term sentences (under 3 months). Jails are chronically overcrowded, poorly maintained, and breeding grounds for disease. Conditions are harsh: 6-10 prisoners in cells designed for 2-3, minimal sanitation, limited food. Wealthy prisoners can pay for improved conditions (private cells, better food) creating two-tier system within jail.

**Regional Prisons.** Longer-term imprisonment (3 months to 3 years) in dedicated facilities. Conditions are better than local jails but still harsh. Prisoners work assigned labor (maintenance, crafts, construction) for minimal compensation. Escape is rare because prisons are isolated in unpopulated areas making flight detection likely.

**Hard Labor Camps.** Sentences over 3 years are served in labor camps where prisoners work in mining, quarrying, forestry, or infrastructure maintenance. Conditions are extremely harsh; mortality is high from disease, accidents, and malnutrition. Labor camp sentences are often effectively life sentences for older prisoners unlikely to survive.

**Execution.** Capital punishment exists for murder and certain Flux crimes. Executions are carried out by judicial decree; appeal is minimal (only High Court can stay execution). Execution is uncommon; it requires clear evidence and is typically reserved for multiple murders or crimes of exceptional brutality.

## Arresting Innocent People

Formal law enforcement occasionally arrests and prosecutes people who are genuinely innocent:

**Wrong Perpetrator Arrest.** Witness identifies person as perpetrator; arrest is made and prosecution initiated. Later investigation reveals witness was mistaken or misidentified innocent person. If conviction had occurred before evidence of innocence emerged, reversal is possible but procedurally difficult and rare. Example: three people fit description of perpetrator, witness identifies one person, that person is arrested and convicted despite being wrong person. Investigation never reveals true perpetrator.

**Coerced Confession.** Suspect is interrogated extensively and issues false confession under pressure. Confession leads to conviction despite suspect innocence. Reversals are rare; confessions are heavily weighted in trial and judges tend to credit defendant's own admission of guilt.

**Evidence Destruction/Manipulation.** For Flux crimes, evidence can be contaminated or destroyed. Investigator collects evidence which investigator later manipulates or misplaces. Defendant cannot prove innocence because "missing" evidence would have exonerated. T3+ practitioners can destroy evidence perfectly, leaving no trace prior crime occurred.

**Racial/Status Bias.** Poor defendants and those from marginalized communities face discrimination. Wealthy victims receive better investigation quality. Wealthy defendants hire better lawyers and receive lighter sentences. System is not uniformly unfair but operates with substantial bias favoring wealth and status.

## Crimes Police Cannot Solve

Substantial crimes go unsolved and unprosecuted due to:

**Lack of Evidence.** Burglar leaves no physical evidence, witnesses didn't see perpetrator, victim cannot identify suspect. Investigation reaches dead-end with no suspect identified.

**Tier Ceiling.** T1-T2 investigators cannot detect T3+ Flux crimes. High-tier practitioners commit crimes that low-tier investigators cannot investigate. Bureau lacks resources to contract T3+ practitioners for all complex investigations.

**Insufficient Witness Cooperation.** Witness refuses to testify due to fear of retaliation, shame (victim in sexual assault case), or distrust of authorities. Without witness testimony, conviction is difficult despite other evidence.

**Resource Unavailability.** Investigator is overwhelmed with other cases and cannot dedicate time to lower-priority crimes. Case goes dormant; solution waits for new evidence or investigator who discovers case file years later.

**Perpetrator Disappearance.** Many perpetrators flee prior to arrest, relocating to another city or kingdom. Investigation becomes impractical if perpetrator cannot be located. Arrest warrant remains on record but is rarely executed if perpetrator stays out of Auralis.

Result: formal justice system captures some criminals but substantial crime remains unpunished. Victims of crimes with low evidence quality or high-tier perpetrator involvement have minimal recourse. System functions adequately for crimes with clear perpetrators and straightforward evidence, but fails for more complex or sophisticated crimes.

## Comparison with Terravelle

Auralis law enforcement is more formal and visible than Terravelle's minimal constable presence. Auralis invests institutional resources in patrol, investigation, and prosecution. Terravelle relies on neighborhood resolution and guild mediation.

**Auralis Advantages:**
- Formal institution can handle complex crimes
- Patrol presence discourages some crime through visibility
- Professional investigators gather better evidence
- Trials provide public accountability
- Appeals provide some protection against injustice

**Auralis Disadvantages:**
- Expensive formal system pricing poor defendants out of quality legal representation
- Capacity constraints mean many crimes go uninvestigated
- Slow timeline (months to verdict)
- Bias against poor and marginalized

**Terravelle Advantages:**
- Swift informal resolution
- Low cost
- Community understanding of disputes
- Conservation of relationships

**Terravelle Disadvantages:**
- Unreliable (depends on victim's reputation and community standing)
- No appeal or formal review
- Enforcement gaps for organized crime
- No protection against powerful perpetrators

Both systems function but have different strengths and weaknesses.
