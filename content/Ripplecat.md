# Ripplecat

**Ripplecat** is a mid-sized feline predator ranging through [[Maren Valley]] and temperate-zone forests across central [[Solumora]]. Standing roughly 75cm at shoulder height and weighing 35-50 pounds, ripplecat fill ecological niche between small game hunters and large territorial predators. They are not apex carnivores — larger predators and human hunters represent consistent competition and predation threat — but are effective hunters of mid-sized prey (rabbits, birds, small cervix subspecies) and competent scavengers capable of processing carrion too large for smaller predators.

Distinctive physical feature is ripplecats' semi-aquatic hunting adaptation. While terrestrial in permanent settlement preference, ripplecat demonstrate sophisticated aquatic behavior: hunting along river systems, diving to pursue prey in water, and exhibiting physiological adaptations (webbed paw structure, streamlined profile, modified hearing) optimized for amphibious hunting rather than obligate terrestrial predation.

## Flux Relationship

Ripplecat demonstrate minimal Flux relationship compared to [[Equatorial Fauna]] or [[Flux Scorpion]]. Ambient Flux measurement around ripplecat populations measures approximately 105-115% baseline, indistinguishable from normal regional variation. No documented evidence of Flux channeling behavior or conscious Flux utilization.

However, ripplecat behavior exhibits patterns suggesting subtle Flux sensitivity. Ripplecat show reliable preference-avoidance of Flux-dense zones (avoiding areas with artifact concentration, rift-proximity zones, heavy installation of grimoire-based defensive systems). This avoidance appears driven by sensory discomfort rather than rational threat assessment — ripplecat enter Flux-dense zones reluctantly and withdraw rapidly upon contact, suggesting sensory irritation rather than fear response.

## Ecological Role

Ripplecat function as generalist mid-tier predators maintaining population control on small-prey species. Individual ripplecat establish territory spanning 2-4 kilometers of river-adjacent range and actively defend against intrusion by competing ripplecat. Territory size correlates with prey density — rich river systems support smaller, more numerous territories, while marginal habitat supports larger, more dispersed territories.

Hunting success rate suggests ripplecat capture approximately 40-60% of attempted hunts, substantially higher than terrestrial-only feline predators (typically 15-25% success). This elevated success rate reflects aquatic-hunting adaptations providing significant predatory advantage. Ripplecat consequently maintain higher population densities in river-valley ecosystems than terrestrial habitat would typically support.

Behavioral observation indicates ripplecat exhibit minimal aggression toward human presence outside direct resource competition context. Ripplecat attacks on humans are documented but rare — typically occurring when ripplecat inhabit specific territory humans suddenly begin intensive harvesting in, or when human children approach ripplecat with inadequate supervision. Established coexistence patterns suggest ripplecat learn to avoid areas with consistent human activity boundaries.

## Resource Value

Ripplecat represent sustainable harvesting opportunity with moderate resource value. Population densities in [[Maren Valley]] and river systems throughout [[Solumora]] are sufficiently robust to support light-to-moderate hunting pressure without population collapse. Unlike apex predators or Flux-adapted species, ripplecat can be hunted using conventional techniques (trapping, tracking, conventional weapons) making harvesting accessible to non-Flux practitioners.

**Fur:** Primary resource value. Ripplecat pelts are dense, water-resistant, and distinctive enough to command premium in luxury clothing markets. Single pelt yields 20-50 Watts depending on region (fur quality varies with environmental zone — colder-territory ripplecat produce denser fur). Water-resistant properties make ripplecat fur valued specifically for maritime community clothing.

**Meat:** Secondary resource. Ripplecat meat is edible and nutritionally adequate, though not preferred over [[Mountain Game Collective]] or managed-hunting supplies. Processing ripplecat meat is straightforward compared to larger game, making it accessible to individual hunters rather than requiring institutional processing infrastructure. Estimated 2-5 Watts market value per animal.

**Bones/Organs:** Minimal specialized value. Standard utility for bone-meal production and craft materials. Approximately 0.5-1 Watts market value per skeleton.

## Hunting Methods

Ripplecat harvesting employs multiple methods reflecting their semi-aquatic nature and mid-tier threat profile:

**Passive Trapping:** Snare-systems and baited cage-traps positioned along hunting trails and river-access points. Traps managed by individual trappers or family operations rotating check-routes every 2-3 days. This method requires minimal active hunting skill and generates moderate, consistent yield. Tapping operations yield approximately 4-8 ripplecat monthly during optimal seasons.

**Active Tracking:** Professional hunters track ripplecat through territory identification (scouting for territorial markers, recent kills, den-sites) and systematic search to locate specific animals. Active hunting produces higher-value pelts (minimal damage compared to trap-captured animals) but requires skill and field time investment. Professional hunters harvesting via tracking might yield 8-15 ripplecat monthly during intensive season.

**Aquatic Pursuit:** Specialized hunting technique involving pursuit into aquatic environment using boats or swimming-based approach to corner ripplecat at river locations. This method requires swimming competence and exceptional familiarity with specific river system geography. Practiced primarily by river-settlement communities where aquatic movement is fundamental life skill. Minimal institutional practice.

## Specialized Ripplecat Hunting Community

[[Maren Valley]] harbors informal network of ripplecat specialists — roughly 40-60 semi-professional hunters operating across valley territories, maintaining territorial trapping-rights agreements, and managing ripplecat populations at sustainable-harvest levels. This community parallels [[Mountain Game Collective]] structure but operates at smaller scale with less formal institutional structure.

**Morn Kelst** (Control Tier T2, age 37) coordinates primary valley trapping operation encompassing 60+ traps distributed across 30-kilometer river corridor. Kelst has operated traplines for 19 years and maintains detailed records: seasonal yield patterns, population trends, territorial mapping. His operation yields approximately 400-500 ripplecat pelts annually, processed through valley fur merchants and exported toward [[Emberfall]] luxury fashion markets.

**Dorn Vress** (T0, age 23) represents emerging specialization in aquatic-pursuit hunting. Vress grew up in [[Thel Orvl]] river settlement and maintains exceptional swimming competence and river-reading skills. His personal hunting yields approximately 100-150 ripplecat annually but produces exceptionally high-quality pelts (minimal damage). Currently attempting to establish professional hunting reputation — initial negotiations with [[Emberfall]] fur merchants to supply premium-exclusive stock.

## Contemporary Pressure

War preparation creates elevated demand for ripplecat fur. Military forces require water-resistant clothing for river-crossing operations, amphibious unit logistics, and maritime-adjacent operations. Institutional purchasing has driven ripplecat pelt prices upward 35-50% from historical baseline, attracting amateur hunters attempting to capitalize on pricing surge.

This demand increases pressure on ripplecat populations in accessible valley territories. [[Maren Valley]] trapline operators report encounter frequency rising 25-30% (more trap-setting activity competing for same resource base). Population data is insufficient to determine if current pressure exceeds sustainable-harvest threshold, but established specialists including Kelst are raising concerns that amateur harvesting pressure (which lacks structured population management protocols) may create localized depletion zones.

Secondary consequence: Military procurement contracts for ripplecat fur have displaced traditional luxury fashion market demand. Fur merchants report civilian clothing industry demand has dropped 20-30% as merchants consolidate inventory toward military buyer preference. This has created secondary pressure on professional hunters to maximize institutional-contract quantities rather than maintaining diverse buyer networks.
