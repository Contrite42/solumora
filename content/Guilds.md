[[Guilds]] organize professional practice, trade standards, and institutional coordination across [[Solumora]]. They function differently in the two kingdoms — [[Terravelle]]'s guilds operate with substantial autonomy and govern commercial life directly, while [[Auralis]]'s guild-equivalent structures function under Council oversight and regulatory supervision.

## Guild Types Across Solumora

**Trade Guilds:**

[[Trade Guilds]] manage commercial activity in both kingdoms, but with sharply different institutional authority. In Terravelle, Trade Guilds function as de facto city governance — licensing, standards enforcement, labor dispute resolution, infrastructure maintenance allocation. [[Valdenmoor]]'s Guild Council operates independently of Crown authority, reporting to guild members who elected it rather than to [[Eddan Voss]].

In Auralis, Trade Guild equivalents operate under [[The Council of Auralis]] regulatory supervision. Guild decisions can be overridden by Council directive, membership requires Council-approved registration, and guild leadership positions are subject to Council review. This creates functional guilds but removes the institutional autonomy that defines Terravelle's system.

**Guild Scrivener Network:**

The [[Guild Scrivener Network]] maintains grimoire production, authentication, and distribution standards across both kingdoms. Scrivener guilds certify copyists, establish quality standards, and provide authentication marks that verify grimoire legitimacy. Cross-kingdom coordination exists because grimoire notation is universal — a grimoire authenticated in [[Solhaven]] functions identically in [[Emberfall]] if produced to standard.

Scrivener Guild authentication prevents [[Black Market Grimoire Circulation]] from completely displacing legitimate trade, though black market operations like [[Solhaven Copymark]] and [[Northern Lens]] exploit gaps in enforcement reach. Guild authentication costs 8-22 Watts for [[Common Grimoire]] texts, 45-85 Watts for [[Uncommon Grimoire]], and 200+ Watts for [[Rare Grimoire]] — pricing that creates economic incentive for unauthorized copying in rural areas where guild access is limited.

**Specialty Guilds:**

[[Terravelle Guild System]] organizes Flux instruction across specialized trade guilds: Scribe Guild (grimoire reproduction), Thermal Guild (heat and cold management), Warden Guild (security and barriers), Maritime Guild (navigation and ship-focused work), Agricultural Guild (crop protection and pest deterrence). Each guild controls training, certification, and standards within its domain.

Auralis maintains equivalent specialist organizations but structured as Council-supervised professional registries rather than autonomous guilds. [[Crestward Flux Institute]] and [[Emberfall]] function as research institutions rather than training guilds, creating different pathways to specialist certification.

## Certification Systems

Guild certification establishes professional competence through standardized progression: apprenticeship → journeyman status → certification assessment.

**Terravelle Certification:**

Apprenticeship runs 2-4 years under a certified guild member, producing documented practice records. Journeyman status enables independent work but not training authority. Certification assessment by guild-appointed assessors reviews documentation and observes live work across standard test conditions.

Most Flux-integrated trades maintain tier-graded certifications: T1 (entry-level, residential work), T2 (standard commercial grade), T3 (skilled specialist work), T4 (expert grade for civil works and high-stakes applications). [[Trade Guilds]] do not typically certify T5+ — practitioners at that tier operate through reputation and contract rather than guild standards.

Certification is trade-specific and non-transferable. A T3 certification in structural sigil application does not transfer to transport flux work. [[Osvin Brack]], a T3 certified assessor in Valdenmoor, documents this in his [[Osvin Brack's Assessment Ledger|Assessment Ledger]]: candidates with high tier capacity regularly fail certification because tier measures Flux capability, not professional competence in specific trade applications.

**Auralis Registration:**

Auralis maintains Council-supervised professional registries that function similarly to guild certification but with centralized oversight. [[Council Orders Registry]] sets standards that apply across registries. Registration assessment measures both technical capability and alignment with Council institutional priorities — practitioners whose work serves Council strategic goals receive preferential access to advanced training and high-tier grimoire materials.

This creates stratification different from Terravelle's system: Auralis practitioners advance based on institutional service as much as technical skill, while Terravelle practitioners advance based on demonstrated trade competence measured against guild standards.

## Documentation Standards

Guild systems require concurrent documentation — records created in real-time during work rather than reconstructed afterward.

**Why Documentation Standards Exist:**

Structural failures, cargo losses, and medical errors cannot be investigated without field records showing what was applied where and in what sequence. A bridge failing three years after construction requires documentation to determine whether the failure resulted from design error, material defects, or execution mistakes. Without concurrent records, institutional learning is impossible.

[[Terravelle Guild System]] enforces this explicitly: T3 and T4 certification in civil works requires that documentation be maintained simultaneously with casting. Many technically capable candidates fail certification because maintaining both documentation and casting focus is a distinct skill requiring specific practice. Guild review committees that set these standards have documented bridge failure history that preceded documentation requirements.

**Failure Mode — Documentation Gaps:**

When practitioners face resource pressure or accelerated timelines, documentation is often the first standard that degrades. [[Keln Ridge Mines]] operates under Council-mandated acceleration; [[Voss Keld]] documents that practitioner shortage forces choosing between maintaining concurrent documentation and maintaining operational pace. He chooses operational pace and maintains retrospective records — creating documentation adequate for administrative review but inadequate for incident investigation.

[[Rell Hadv]], a T2 certified practitioner in Valdenmoor, experienced this directly: a structural sigil application inspection failed because his concurrent documentation showed timing gaps that made it impossible to verify proper curing intervals. His work was technically correct, but certification standards require proof, not assertion. The project was re-cast at client expense.

## Kingdom Differences in Guild Authority

**Terravelle — Autonomous Governance:**

Terravelle guilds operate independently of Crown authority. [[Eddan Voss]] can revoke guild charters but has done so twice in a century — both times creating more problems than solved. Guild councils in [[Valdenmoor]], [[Ashford]], [[Greyveil]] handle functions that in other kingdoms might be city government responsibilities.

High-tier practitioners advance through guild leadership rather than toward royal favor. A T5 specialist in Terravelle holds influence through guild position — training next-generation practitioners, setting trade standards, arbitrating complex disputes — rather than through proximity to Crown institutions.

**Auralis — Supervised Function:**

Auralis guild-equivalent structures function under Council oversight. [[The Council of Auralis]] can override guild decisions through [[Council Orders Registry]] directives. Membership requires Council-approved registration. Leadership positions are subject to Council review — ensuring that trade organizations align with Council strategic priorities.

This produces functional efficiency but reduced institutional autonomy. When Auralis needed to redirect Guild Scrivener resources toward military grimoire production, the Council issued directives that guilds implemented within months. When Terravelle faces similar pressure, guild councils resist or slow implementation through distributed decision-making.

**Cross-Kingdom Coordination:**

Despite governance differences, guilds maintain cross-kingdom coordination in domains where technical standards are universal. [[Guild Scrivener Network]] authentication protocols work identically in both kingdoms because grimoire notation is standardized. [[Material Refinement]] processes use guild-established quality standards regardless of kingdom. [[Watts|Currency]] authentication relies on guild-maintained verification procedures coordinated across borders.

This coordination persists during diplomatic tension because both kingdoms benefit from maintained standards. When [[Border Trade]] friction increases, customs inspection intensifies, but guild authentication marks are still recognized. The alternative — requiring re-authentication of every cross-border work product — would paralyze legitimate commerce.

## Failure Modes Under Pressure

Guild systems degrade predictably under resource constraints and institutional pressure.

**Credential Disputes:**

As wartime pressure increases, both kingdoms face practitioner shortages. Auralis addresses this through accelerated training and reduced certification standards — allowing T2 practitioners to perform work previously requiring T3 certification. Terravelle guilds resist this pressure through maintained standards, creating bottlenecks where work cannot proceed because certified practitioners are unavailable.

[[Torn Hess]], coordinator of [[Council Compliance Directorate]], documents this pattern: institutions report non-compliance with Council orders because required practitioner tier is unavailable, Directorate confirms the constraint is real, Council chooses between resourcing additional training or accepting reduced standards. Increasingly, the Council accepts reduced standards, shifting risk from timeline pressure to execution quality.

**Enforcement Gaps:**

Guild enforcement depends on inspection capacity and member compliance. [[Black Market Grimoire Circulation]] exploits enforcement gaps — unauthorized copyists operate in rural areas where [[Guild Scrivener Network]] has limited inspection reach, authentication forgers like [[Solhaven Copymark]] produce marks sophisticated enough to pass casual review.

Guilds lack criminal enforcement authority; they can only report violations to kingdom authorities. When authorities lack resources to pursue enforcement, guild standards become voluntary. [[Northern Lens]] operates openly in rural [[Terravelle]] because Guild Scrivener enforcement resources cannot cover the territory and kingdom authorities prioritize higher-threat activity.

**Internal Guild Conflicts:**

Guild councils manage disputes between members through internal arbitration, but unresolvable conflicts escalate. [[Terravelle Guild System]] describes this failure mode: when guild assessors disagree on certification standards, or when guild councils divide on policy changes, resolution requires consensus-building that can take years. During that time, standards are inconsistently applied, creating practitioner confusion and client uncertainty.

[[Massen Olvr]], who failed multiple certification assessments in Valdenmoor, documented that different assessors applied different standards to his work — one assessor focused on concurrent documentation quality, another prioritized execution speed, a third emphasized theoretical understanding. Guild review identified the inconsistency but took eighteen months to resolve through assessor standardization training.

## Wartime Pressure Impacts

Contemporary military preparation is straining guild systems in both kingdoms.

**Practitioner Conscription:**

Both kingdoms conscript practitioners for military service, removing them from civilian guild work. [[Solhaven Flux Practical Research Collective]] lost 2 of 18 active practitioners to conscription, creating research capacity gaps. [[Material Refinement]] operations face practitioner shortages despite increased demand, constraining processing capacity.

Guilds cannot refuse conscription orders but document the capacity impacts. [[Terravelle Administration]] receives guild reports showing projected service availability declining 15-20% over two years if conscription continues current pace. Auralis faces similar constraints but addresses them through accelerated training and reduced certification standards rather than adjusting conscription policy.

**Material Scarcity:**

Wartime resource prioritization redirects materials from civilian guild work toward military applications. [[Keln Ridge Mines]] produces Flux-reactive minerals primarily for military research, creating scarcity for civilian practitioners requiring those materials. Guild members with material access face unofficial pressure to supply military contractors preferentially, disrupting normal commercial relationships.

[[Maren Timber Collective]] experiences this as contract pressure: military procurement offers below-market pricing (4 Watts/ton versus 8-10 Watts/ton market rate) but with institutional pressure to accept. Collective maintains market pricing and accepts reduced military contracts, preserving commercial relationships but facing Crown scrutiny for "insufficient cooperation."

**Institutional Capture Risk:**

As [[Expansion Faction]] advances political goals, Auralis guild-equivalent structures face increasing pressure to align with Council strategic priorities rather than member service. Guild leadership positions are reviewed for "institutional alignment." Guild decisions that slow military preparation face Council override.

Terravelle guilds face similar pressure but resist more effectively through distributed authority. No single Crown directive can redirect Terravelle guild activity because guild councils make decisions independently. Crown can pressure individual guilds, but coordination across guild councils enables collective resistance that individual institutions cannot sustain.

**Sustainability Concerns:**

[[Casten Miel]], T4 director of [[Crestward Flux Institute]], observes: "Guild systems are designed for steady-state operation — training matches retirements, certification maintains standards, work volume matches practitioner availability. Wartime acceleration breaks all three. Training is accelerated but produces lower-quality practitioners. Standards are reduced to match availability. Work volume exceeds capacity. The system functions but degrades. Three years of this and we'll need a decade to restore what we're losing now."

Both kingdoms' guild systems show this strain. Certification pass rates are declining (candidates failing standards rather than standards adjusting downward). Documentation quality is degrading (concurrent records replaced by retrospective summaries). Dispute resolution is slowing (arbitration backlog growing as attention shifts to crisis work). The guilds continue operating, but institutional memory and standards erosion will outlast the immediate wartime pressure.

They document the strain. They grant exceptions. They maintain what standards they can. The system bends. It has not yet broken.
