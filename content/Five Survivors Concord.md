# Five Survivors Concord

The Five Survivors Concord is the informal compact formed among the last confirmed [[High Demons]] after terminal persecution conditions in the late [[The Ancient Persecution Era|Persecution Era]].

It was never publicly ratified, never written as a formal treaty, and survived primarily through oral continuity and encoded personal records. Its influence on modern events is disproportionate to its visibility.

## Formation Context

By the final persecution decade, demon survival probability in open society had collapsed. Identification systems, property incentives, and religious-political mobilization had combined into a resilient persecution apparatus.

Five surviving demons, each operating under separate concealment networks, converged on one strategic conclusion: no individual survival strategy was durable without collective behavioral constraints.

The Concord emerged as those constraints.

## Core Principles

Historical reconstruction across demon memoir fragments identifies five principle commitments:

### 1. Anonymity Before Influence
No survivor would pursue visible political, religious, or military influence that could trigger renewed detection at scale.

### 2. Compartmented Identity Lifecycles
Each survivor would rotate identities on long intervals, minimizing pattern accumulation while preserving stable social function.

### 3. Non-Intervention Default
No direct intervention in major human conflict unless extinction-level dimensional risk exceeded concealment risk.

### 4. Evidence Preservation Without Publication
Persecution documentation would be retained in private archives to preserve truth without provoking immediate recurrence.

### 5. Mutual Signal Protocol
If one survivor determined non-intervention had become morally or structurally impossible, a signal cascade would notify others to evaluate coordinated action.

## Moral Tension

The Concord protected surviving demons and reduced immediate persecution triggers, but imposed a severe ethical burden:
- survivors routinely witnessed preventable harm while maintaining cover,
- intervention thresholds were set extremely high, often beyond ordinary mass-casualty contexts,
- self-preservation and public duty remained in unresolved conflict for centuries.

This tension defines Verath's modern choice. The corridor crisis crossed the Concord's non-intervention threshold by combining mass-casualty probability with dimensional cascade risk.

## Relationship to Modern Reveal

The Fifth Bell disclosure effectively suspended two Concord principles:
- anonymity before influence,
- non-intervention default.

It did not erase the Concord's logic. Rather, it demonstrated its contingency clause: when system-scale collapse is imminent, concealment ceases to be ethically defensible.

## Institutional Misreading Risks

Modern factions frequently misread the Concord as:
- evidence of coordinated demon infiltration,
- proof of long-term strategic manipulation,
- justification for retroactive containment regimes.

Available evidence supports a different reading: it was a minimal-survival ethics framework built in response to persecution incentives that made open coexistence nonviable.

## Ongoing Significance

As coexistence governance matures, the Concord serves as a reminder that current institutions are being evaluated not by declarations but by whether they reduce the structural conditions that made concealment rational in the first place.

If those conditions return, concealment logic returns.
