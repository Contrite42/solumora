# Auralis Trade Dispute Resolution

Trade disputes between merchants in [[Auralis]] are resolved through [[Auralis Civic Courts]] using formal civil procedure. Unlike [[Terravelle]]'s specialized [[Guild Arbitration Bench]], Auralis uses general-purpose courts applying uniform law to merchant disputes. This creates delays and costs but also provides appeal mechanisms and formal precedent [[Terravelle]] lacks.

## Court Jurisdiction and Choice

**District Courts.** Handle routine merchant disputes under 500 Watts value. Disputes under 100 Watts may be heard in small courts with simplified procedure. Cases 100-500 Watts follow standard civil procedure. District court judges are generalists, not trade specialists.

**Regional Courts.** Handle merchant disputes 500+ Watts and appeals from District Courts. Less frequent for routine trade disputes; more common for appeals or exceptionally complex cases.

## Types of Trade Disputes

**Delivery Failures.** Supplier contracts for 100 bolts textile at specified price and delivery date; delivers only 80 bolts claiming supply shortage. Buyer refuses payment for shortfall, claims lost business opportunity. Dispute centers on whether contract required full delivery and what damages buyer is entitled to.

**Quality Disputes.** Buyer claims goods are lower quality than samples and refuses payment. Seller claims goods meet contract specifications, buyer misidentified quality standards, or samples were inaccurate representations. Dispute may require expert witness testimony about textile quality standards.

**Payment Disputes.** Seller performs delivery; buyer delays payment beyond contract terms. Seller demands payment plus late fees. Buyer claims goods were delayed or defective, justifying payment withholding.

**Contract Interpretation.** Merchants disagree about contract term meaning. Did 30-day payment term mean 30 days from delivery or 30 days from invoice? Ambiguous contract language creates dispute. Courts interpret contract language to determine parties' intent; testimony about parties' understanding is heard.

**Termination and Damages.** Merchant terminates contract citing buyer's non-performance (late payments, missing deliveries). Buyer sues breach claiming damages from sudden supply cutoff. Dispute centers on whether termination was justified and what damages flow from breach.

## Pre-Trial Process

**Complaint Preparation.** Merchant suing (plaintiff) hires legal representative to draft complaint describing dispute and damages claimed. Complaint must detail: what contract existed, what each party was obligated to perform, how defendant breached, what damages plaintiff suffered.

**Service and Response.** Complaint is served on defendant merchant. Defendant has 3-4 weeks to file response (answer or motion to dismiss). Response must address allegations specifically and raise any legal defenses.

**Discovery.** Parties exchange documents and information:
- **Document Requests:** Production of contracts, invoices, correspondence, payment records
- **Interrogatories:** Written questions requiring detailed answers about transaction details
- **Depositions:** Out-of-court testimony from parties and witnesses (recorded by court clerk)
- **Expert Reports:** If technical issues (quality disputes), expert witnesses prepare written analysis

Discovery can last 2-3 months for complex disputes. Discovery is more extensive than in [[Terravelle]], enabling thorough factual development but increasing cost significantly.

**Pre-Trial Motions.** Defendant can move to dismiss (complaint doesn't state legal claim) or move for summary judgment (facts are undisputed, law clearly favors defendant). Plaintiff can move for summary judgment (facts undisputed, plaintiff clearly entitled to relief). If motions succeed, trial is avoided.

## Trial

**Evidence Presentation.** Plaintiff presents evidence and testimony, followed by defendant cross-examination. Defendant presents evidence and testimony, followed by plaintiff cross-examination. Trade disputes often involve expert witness testimony about quality standards, market prices, or industry practice.

**Document and Commercial Evidence.** Contracts, invoices, correspondence, and payment records are presented as evidence. Written communication between parties is particularly important; clear evidence of parties' understanding reduces dispute significantly.

**Expert Testimony.** If goods quality is disputed, expert textile inspectors or industry specialists testify about whether goods met contract specifications. Expert opinions about market prices are presented in damages disputes. Expert testimony is expensive (50-100 Watts per case depending on complexity) and is used primarily in large-value disputes.

**Judge Assessment.** Judge weighs evidence credibility, determines factual issues, and applies law. For contract interpretation disputes, judge determines what contract language means based on parties' testimony and written evidence about parties' intent.

## Remedies and Damages Calculation

**Expectation Damages (Most Common).** Plaintiff is compensated for lost benefit of bargain—what plaintiff would have gained if contract were performed. Calculation: value plaintiff would have received if contract properly performed minus value plaintiff actually received (or must pay to replace performance).

Example: Buyer contracts for 100 bolts linen at 5 Watts per bolt (500 Watts total). Seller delivers only 50 bolts. Buyer must purchase remaining 50 bolts elsewhere at 6 Watts per bolt (300 Watts instead of 250 Watts). Expectation damages: 50 Watts (additional cost needed to complete purchase).

**Reliance Damages (Less Common).** Plaintiff is compensated for losses incurred in reliance on contract. Calculation: costs plaintiff incurred in preparation for contract performance minus benefits received.

Example: Buyer prepares warehouse space, hires labor, and plans marketing campaign for goods buyer expected to receive. Seller cancels contract. Plaintiff's reliance costs (warehouse rental, labor, marketing) are unrecovered. Reliance damages compensate these out-of-pocket costs.

**Mitigation Requirement.** Plaintiff has obligation to minimize damages by taking reasonable steps to reduce losses. If supplier cancels, buyer must attempt to find replacement supplier at reasonable cost. Buyer cannot ignore cancellation and purchase replacement goods at premium cost, then demand extra damages. Excessive loss-minimization costs are not recoverable.

**Consequential and Special Damages.** Foreseeable losses beyond direct contract value are sometimes recoverable. If supplier is late and buyer loses business opportunities, consequential damages (lost profit) might be recoverable if parties foresaw delivery delays could cause profit loss. But courts are conservative about consequential damages because they are difficult to calculate and can become very large.

## Comparison with Terravelle Arbitration

| Factor | Auralis Court | Terravelle Arbitration |
|--------|--------------|----------------------|
| Judge/Arbitrator Training | Formal legal training | Industry experience, less legal training |
| Speed | 3-6 months | 3-4 weeks |
| Cost | 40-100 Watts total | 8-15 Watts filing |
| Evidence Standards | Strict rules of evidence | Flexible, practical assessment |
| Precedent | Strict, binding precedent | Loose consultation |
| Appeal | Full appellate process available | Limited guild council review |
| Enforcement | Institutional court machinery | Guild membership consequences |
| Specialization | Generalist judges | Trade specialist arbitrators |
| Cross-Kingdom Enforcement | Can enforce against Terravelle defendants | Cannot enforce against Auralis defendants |

Auralis courts are slower, more expensive, and more formal. Terravelle arbitration is faster and cheaper. Auralis provides appeal rights Terravelle lacks. Terravelle arbitration is more specialized.

## Cross-Kingdom Dispute Issues

**Choice of Forum.** Contracts specify whether Auralis courts or [[Terravelle Guild Arbitration|Terravelle arbitration]] applies. Auralis merchants prefer Auralis courts (home advantage, known judges, easier enforcement). Terravelle merchants prefer arbitration (specialized, fast, cheap).

**Enforcement Gaps.** If dispute is decided by Auralis court against Terravelle merchant, Terravelle lacks automatic enforcement mechanism. Auralis court judgment is not recognized by Terravelle courts or guild authorities. Plaintiff must persuade Terravelle court to enforce Auralis judgment in separate proceeding, which occurs rarely and requires showing Auralis judgment is consistent with Terravelle law.

**Jurisdiction Questions.** If contract is silent on forum choice, both kingdoms claim jurisdiction. Auralis courts claim jurisdiction because defendant is Auralis merchant or because contract performance involved Auralis. Terravelle claims jurisdiction because plaintiff is Terravelle merchant or contract was Terravelle-originated. Parallel litigation results—case is filed in both kingdoms, proceedings advance independently, outcomes may differ.

**Conflict Resolution.** High Court judges attempt to coordinate with Auralis judges when cross-kingdom disputes arise. Formal agreements between kingdoms establish some principles (contract choice-of-law is respected if clearly stated), but enforcement remains difficult. Many cross-kingdom disputes end in failure—judgment obtained in home kingdom but not enforced against foreign defendant.

**Result.** Cross-kingdom merchants often require performance guarantees (partial prepayment, third-party escrow, letter of credit from trusted institution) reducing credit risk from cross-kingdom disputes.

## Disadvantages for Small Merchants

Auralis courts create barriers for small merchants:

**Cost Prohibitively.** Filing fees (8-15 Watts), legal representation (20-50 Watts), expert witnesses, and extended timeline all make litigation expensive. Small merchants disputing 30-Watt goods cannot afford 40 Watt litigation costs.

**Procedural Complexity.** Rules of evidence, discovery procedures, and trial formality create need for experienced legal representation. Small merchants unfamiliar with procedures are disadvantaged against merchants hiring lawyers.

**Delay Risk.** Extended timelines mean merchants must continue normal business during litigation. Cash flow is stressed during 3-6 month dispute resolution. Small merchants with limited cash reserves face bankruptcy risk from litigation costs rather than from dispute itself.

**Difficulty Proving Damages.** Expert witnesses are expensive and necessary for quality disputes. Small merchants cannot afford experts, making quality claims difficult to prove. Verbal contract terms are harder to prove than written contracts; small merchants using oral agreements suffer from lack of documentation.

**Result.** Small merchants often settle for reduced payment or accept loss rather than litigate. Large merchants with resources can pressure small merchants into unfavorable settlements by threatening expensive litigation.

## Advantages for Large Merchants

Large merchant houses have advantages in Auralis courts:

**Resources.** Can afford legal representation, expert witnesses, and extended litigation timeline without cash flow stress.

**Documentation.** Maintain detailed records of contracts, correspondence, and transactions. Written evidence strongly favors party with good documentation.

**Reputation.** Established merchant houses are known to judges; reputation for honest dealing creates credibility advantage.

**Strategic Litigation.** Can use threat of litigation to extract favorable settlements from small merchants who cannot afford defending against large merchants' legal resources.

**Result.** Auralis courts function better for large merchants than small merchants, creating system bias toward wealth.

## Practical Implications

Small merchants operating in [[Auralis]] often:
- Require payment before delivery (reducing credit risk requiring dispute resolution)
- Use third-party escrow for major transactions (neutral party holds payment until both parties confirm satisfaction)
- Obtain performance bonds from guild offices (insurance against non-performance)
- Trade primarily with merchants they have long-term relationships with (reputation-based informal enforcement reducing formal dispute likelihood)

These practices indicate that formal court system is too expensive and unreliable for routine small-scale commerce. Merchants substitute informal mechanisms (reputation, partial prepayment) reducing dependence on formal courts.
