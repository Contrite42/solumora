# Creatures

[[Solumora]]'s fauna represents both resource base and threat vector. Creatures inhabit every ecosystem zone: high-altitude predators in [[Wolfpoint]] uplands, exotic Flux-adapted insects in [[Desert Zakros]], semi-aquatic hunters along river systems, and specialized coastal scavengers supporting maritime resource economies. Understanding creature ecology, resource value, and hunter cultures is essential context for explaining how [[Solumora]]'s populations survive, what food security actually means, and why predator-control operations consume permanent institutional attention.

This is not wilderness romanticization. Creatures kill people regularly. Hunter culture exists because civilian survival depends on continuous predation pressure management, and professional expertise separates "managed harvesting that produces sustainable resource value" from "random predation that destabilizes communities."

## Major Creature Categories

### Flux-Adapted Fauna

[[Desert Zakros]] ecosystem produces creatures with direct Flux channeling capacity absent from temperate and northern regions. These represent evolutionary development (or ancient deliberate modification, unclear) producing organisms capable of conscious Flux utilization:

- **[[Equatorial Fauna]]** — General desert ecosystem: Flux-dependent arachnids and insects adapted to extraordinary heat and ambient Flux density. Non-aggressive by default and avoidable through competent expedition protocols.
- **[[Flux Scorpion]]** — Apex Flux predator with ionized-venom capacity and territory-saturation hunting strategy. Lethal threat approaching [[Flux Demons]] danger differential.

### Temperate Regional Predators

Non-Flux-adapted creatures dominating central [[Solumora]] ecosystems provide sustainable resource value generating ongoing institutional harvest operations:

- **[[Ripplecat]]** — Semi-aquatic feline hunter found in river systems across [[Maren Valley]] and temperate zones. Moderate resource value (fur, meat), manageable threat profile. Sustainable harvesting supports rural hunting economies.
- **[[Prowlpack]]** — Social pack predator characteristic of upland territories. Sophisticated coordination produces elevated predation impact on [[Upland Herding Network]] operations. Requires active predator-control programs.

### Northern Specialized Fauna

[[Wolfpoint]] cold-climate environment produces apex predators and specialized scavengers:

- **[[Bloodshrike]]** — Large aerial predator specialized for high-altitude hunting. Valuable feather and bone resources. Sustainable non-lethal harvesting developed by specialists including [[Kels Vorn]].

### Maritime Coastal Species

Coastal salt-marsh and deepwater regions support scavenger-based economies with extraordinarily high resource value:

- **[[Boneshell Crab]]** — Coastal scavenger supporting highest per-specimen resource value of any common creature. Commercial harvesting involves professional and semi-professional operations.

### Status: Rift-Adjacent Fauna

Some creatures concentrate near [[Ancient Ruins]] and [[Rift Incursion]] zones, potentially representing pre-collapse evolution or Flux-dimension originated populations. Current understanding insufficient for detailed classification. [[Dorn Sels]] research ongoing.

## Hunter Culture Framework

Professional hunting and creature harvesting constitute specialized domain with distinct culture, economic logic, and institutional relationships distinct from agricultural or manufacturing work.

### Specialization Structure

Professional hunters fall into multiple specialties based on target species and harvesting methodology:

**Sustained Passive Harvesters:** Primarily trapline-system operators (ripplecat fur, boneshell crab) establishing fixed equipment (traps, lines, nets) in productive territories and managing check-routes rotating periodically. These practitioners achieve consistent moderate yields with minimal direct creature engagement. Represents economic option accessible to semi-specialist participants without apex-level Flux capacity. Operating costs relatively low, risk relatively low, profitability moderate.

**Active Territory Pursuit:** Specialists pursuing live dangerous predators (bloodshrike, prowlpack) through direct tracking, positioning, and engagement. Requires exceptional field-craft skill, environmental reading capability, and often Flux-based support capacity for defensive/precision applications. Higher risk than passive harvesting but substantially higher revenue per successful operation.

**Institutional Extraction:** Specialized practitioners working under institutional contract (primarily [[Mountain Game Collective]] predator-control operations, military creature-resource harvesting). Working under institutional protocols reduces individual income variability but obligates practitioners to institutional scheduling and operational parameters. Provides stable income base attractive to practitioners seeking reduced entrepreneurial uncertainty.

**Apex Specialists:** Extraordinarily rare practitioners focusing on highest-risk, highest-value targets ([[Flux Scorpion]] venom harvesting, selective predator-pack elimination in complex scenarios). Estimated 10-30 active specialists across all [[Solumora]] regions. Income potential extremely high (5000-15000 Watts annually) but approach risk of career termination through casualty.

### Knowledge Transmission Pathways

Professional hunting knowledge predominantly transmits through apprenticeship rather than formal institutional training:

**Mentorship Apprenticeship:** Young potential practitioners (age 15-25) work alongside established hunters for 5-10 years, absorbing practical field methodology through direct observation and iterative participation. Standard pathway remains informal — no certification, licensing, or institutional gatekeeping. Reputation and observed competence constitute primary credential indicators.

**Family Knowledge Transmission:** Hunting families maintain multi-generational knowledge structures. Children grow up with creature-reading skills, environmental knowledge, and risk-assessment habits embedded through childhood socialization. Family transmission accelerates learning trajectory — family-raised hunters typically achieve competence levels requiring 10+ years formal apprenticeship within 5-7 years of focused training due to embedded foundational knowledge.

**Institutional Training:** Emerging secondary structure for military-aligned harvesting. [[Control Tier]] T3+ practitioners increasingly receive formal training in specialized harvesting operations directed toward military supply objectives. This training occurs through combination of institutional military programs and contracts with established specialists like [[Kels Vorn]].

### Economic Structure

Professional hunting operates as hybrid economic domain mixing direct commodity harvesting, institutional contracting, and specialist service provision:

- **Commodity Revenue:** Direct sale of harvested materials (fur, meat, venom, bone) to merchants, processing operations, or institutional buyers. Commodity prices fluctuate based on demand cycles, material abundance, and institutional purchasing.
- **Institutional Contracting:** Educational contracts with [[Mountain Game Collective]], [[Council of Auralis]], military institutions providing payment for population management services, research participation, or territorial assessment activities. Contract revenue stable but responsive to institutional budgeting constraints.
- **Territory Licensing:** Where formal territorial structure exists (ripplecat trapline operations in [[Maren Valley]], boneshell crab harvesting rights along coastal regions), practitioners pay percentage-based licensing fees to community organizations managing territorial allocation. Declining practice due to wartime administrative breakdown.

Current wartime commodity-surge creates pricing incentives toward maximum-extraction practice contradicting sustainability frameworks that define professional hunting culture. Professional tensions escalating between institutional demand for acceleration and practitioner recognition that over-harvest produces ecological collapse risk.

## Contemporary Pressures

### Military Personnel Requisition

War preparation conscripts skilled practitioners from civilian hunting operations. [[Mountain Game Collective]] reports difficulties maintaining staffing for seasonal operations — military requisitions remove approximately 25-35% of potential team personnel during peak-season periods. This reduces civilian harvesting capacity precisely when military demand surge for creature products (bloodshrike feathers, boneshell meat, ripplecat fur) increases commodity value 40-60%.

Result: Commodity becomes simultaneously more valuable and more difficult to produce, creating economic paradox where potential revenue increases while production capacity declines.

### Territorial Incursion

Military encampments, training operations, and defensive installations occupy territories historically used for professional hunting. Bloodshrike specialists relocated from upland operations, maritime harvesting disrupted by military naval operations, predator-control programs disrupted by military animal requisitions. Territorial displacement forces practitioners toward marginal hunting zones with lower productivity.

### Commodity Surge Instability

Institutional demand for creature products generates pricing spikes attracting non-specialist participant participation. Amateur hunters lacking survival competence, environmental knowledge, and sustainable-practice discipline attempt to capitalize on wartime pricing. Result:

- Increased predation victim rates (amateur hunters experiencing higher casualty rates in hazardous harvesting operations)
- Ecosystem disruption from inefficient harvesting (wasted effort, animal wounding without recovery, territory disruption)
- Market destabilization (aggressive pricing competition reducing sustainability of professional operations)

### Sustainability Recognition

Professional practitioners including [[Kels Vorn]] and researcher [[Dorn Sels]] are increasingly documenting sustainability concerns regarding current harvest pressures. Specific concerns:

- Bloodshrike territorial depletion risk from lethal over-extraction
- Boneshell crab congregation timing disruption potentially reducing population reproductive success
- Predator-pack selective elimination potentially approaching total population reduction rather than sustainable cull rates
- Amateur participation in harvesting reducing knowledge transfer capacity for sustainable-practice transmission

Current institutional environment proves reluctant to implement sustainability restrictions conflicting with military resourcing priorities.

## Professional Perspectives

### [[Kels Vorn]] - Sustainable Harvesting Philosophy

Emphasizes territorial fauna as renewable resource, advocates selective non-lethal extraction where feasible, recognizes wartime pressure as temporary threat to established practices. Current concern: whether institutional environment will permit sustainability-conscious approach or force transition to extraction methodology incompatible with professional values.

### [[Dorn Sels]] - Ecological Impact Assessment

Focuses on documenting environmental consequences of harvesting practices, evaluating institutional decision impacts, and modeling ecosystem response to modified harvesting regimes. Increasingly concerned about wartime sustainability trajectory. Institutional role limits advocacy capacity — recommendations remain moderate and sustainable-yield-focused despite growing personal concern.

### [[Mountain Game Collective]] Administration

Balances multiple institutional pressures: maintaining civilian food security, meeting military supply contracts, protecting ecosystem sustainability. Resource constraints force allocation prioritization. Current consensus: military supply obligations supersede sustainability considerations but require attention to avoid complete ecological collapse within 10-20 year timeline.

## Broader Ecological Context

Creature populations interact with Flux infrastructure through patterns not fully understood. [[Equatorial Fauna]] avoidance of high-Flux zones, ripplecat territory shifts correlating with military installation deployment, and boneshell crab congregation timing disruption all suggest complex creature-Flux-environment interaction requiring specialized research.

[[Dorn Sels]] research programs are attempting systematic documentation. Current institutional funding constraints limit research pace. Wartime timeline pressures create environment where ecological understanding trails behind institutional decision-making.
