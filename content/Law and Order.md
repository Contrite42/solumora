Law and order in [[Solumora]] varies significantly between [[Auralis]] and [[Terravelle]], reflecting each kingdom's broader cultural priorities. [[Auralis]] operates formal legal institutions with codified law, professional judges, and standardized enforcement. [[Terravelle]] relies on guild mediation, community accountability, and reputation-based justice. Both systems function within their cultural contexts but struggle when handling cross-border disputes or crimes involving [[Flux]].

## Challenges Unique to Flux Crimes

Crimes involving [[Flux]] present detection, evidence, and enforcement problems that ordinary criminal justice cannot address effectively.

**Detection Requires Higher Tiers:** Most [[Flux]]-based crimes — evidence destruction through chemical sigils, memory tampering, concealed barriers, remote communication — require [[Control Tier|T3+]] practitioners to detect. A T1 guard cannot identify whether a death was caused by mundane poison or a precisely-applied thermal sigil. A T2 investigator cannot detect whether a witness's testimony has been Mind-influenced. Detection capability creates enforcement asymmetry: high-tier criminals can commit crimes that low-tier enforcers cannot investigate.

**Evidence Can Be Manipulated:** Flux enables evidence destruction that leaves no trace. A practitioner can chemically dissolve physical evidence, thermally disrupt sigil markings, or use detection-blocking fields to prevent investigation. Courts must assess whether evidence absence indicates innocence or competent evidence destruction. This uncertainty undermines conviction confidence and creates reasonable doubt in cases where guilt is functionally certain but unprovable.

**Jurisdiction Is Complex:** Flux crimes often cross kingdom borders. A Terravelle practitioner uses a remotely-linked communication sigil to coordinate with an Auralis accomplice. Neither kingdom has clear authority. Crimes committed in [[Equatorial Desert|Desert Zakros]] during crossings fall into jurisdictional gaps — neither kingdom claims legal authority over the desert interior, and enforcement in that environment is practically impossible.

**Power Asymmetry:** Non-Flux citizens facing Flux-capable criminals operate at severe disadvantage. A T0 merchant who is defrauded by a T4 practitioner has limited recourse. The merchant cannot detect the crime, cannot gather evidence, and cannot defend themselves if the practitioner retaliates. Legal systems attempt to compensate through specialized enforcement units, but resource constraints mean most victims never receive justice.

## Auralis Legal System

[[Auralis]] operates a formal legal system with codified law, professional courts, and institutionalized enforcement. The system is designed for urban environments, complex commercial disputes, and cases requiring documented precedent.

**[[Auralis Civic Courts]]** handle criminal and civil cases through professional judges appointed by [[The Council of Auralis]]. Judges are typically T2-T4 practitioners with legal training and government service history. Courts follow documented procedures, precedent-based rulings, and standardized sentencing guidelines. The system prioritizes consistency and institutional legitimacy.

**[[Auralis Enforcement Bureau]]** manages criminal investigation and enforcement through civic investigators, patrol officers, and specialized Flux crime units. Most enforcement personnel are T1-T3 practitioners. High-tier Flux crimes require escalation to specialist investigators or contracted high-tier practitioners. The Bureau operates under formal mandate but is limited by resource constraints and tier ceilings among personnel.

Auralis legal philosophy emphasizes that law should be knowable, consistent, and equally applied regardless of social status. In practice, wealthier defendants hire better legal representation, and high-tier practitioners often escape prosecution because evidence gathering requires resources the Bureau cannot allocate to every case.

## Terravelle Justice System

[[Terravelle]] operates justice through guild mediation, community accountability structures, and reputation-based consequences. Formal courts exist but handle only cases that guild mediation cannot resolve or that involve non-guild-affiliated parties.

**[[Terravelle Guild Mediation]]** resolves most disputes within guild communities. Guild elders assess facts, hear testimony from involved parties and witnesses, and impose resolutions ranging from financial restitution to guild expulsion. Mediation emphasizes restoring community function rather than punishing wrongdoing abstractly. A guild member who defrauds a client is typically required to compensate the victim, publicly acknowledge fault, and demonstrate reformed behavior before regaining full guild standing.

**[[Terravelle Reputation Justice]]** manages behavior through social consequences. A practitioner expelled from their guild loses professional network access, client referrals, and community trust. This reputation collapse often ends careers more effectively than formal imprisonment. For guild-affiliated practitioners, reputation accountability is more consequential than legal penalties.

Formal Terravelle courts handle serious crimes (murder, assault, major theft) and disputes involving parties outside guild structures. These courts operate with less procedural formality than Auralis courts but follow similar investigation and trial processes. Judges are typically long-serving community members with dispute resolution experience, not trained legal professionals.

## Cross-Border Jurisdiction

When crimes involve parties or actions in both kingdoms, jurisdiction becomes contested. Neither kingdom has established formal extradition treaties or clear jurisdictional protocols. Cross-border disputes are resolved through ad-hoc negotiation, often involving:

**[[Border Coordination Offices]]** maintain informal communication between kingdom enforcement agencies. These offices do not have legal authority to impose cross-border resolutions but facilitate information sharing and cooperative investigation when both kingdoms have shared interest in resolving a case.

**Diplomatic Negotiation:** High-profile cross-border cases requiring resolution escalate to diplomatic channels. [[Auralis]] and [[Terravelle]] negotiate case-specific agreements allowing one kingdom's courts to handle prosecution while the other provides evidence or witness access. These negotiations are slow, politically sensitive, and often fail to produce timely justice.

**Mutual Abandonment:** Many cross-border crimes are never prosecuted because neither kingdom has sufficient evidence, jurisdiction, or political will to pursue cases across the border. Criminals exploit this gap by committing crimes in one kingdom and fleeing to the other.

## Notable Cases and Precedents

**[[The Halveth Evidence Manipulation Case]]** established that Auralis courts could convict based on "absence of expected evidence" when a high-tier practitioner's known capability to destroy evidence created reasonable inference of guilt. Controversial ruling that remains debated.

**[[The Guild Expulsion Appeal]]** was a Terravelle case where a guild member challenged expulsion in formal courts, arguing guild mediation lacked due process. Courts ruled they had no authority to override guild internal discipline, reinforcing guild autonomy.

**[[The Desert Crossing Jurisdiction Dispute]]** involved a murder during a cross-border expedition in Desert Zakros. Neither kingdom claimed jurisdiction. Case was never prosecuted, establishing practical precedent that desert interior crimes fall into enforcement gaps.

## Key Personnel

**[[Cordan Vess]]** is an Auralis T4 Civic Court judge with 18 years experience, known for handling complex Flux crime cases and establishing several important evidence precedents.

**[[Sten Keld]]** leads Terravelle's North Guild Council mediation panel, resolving disputes across multiple guild jurisdictions and maintaining cross-guild cooperation.

**[[Renna Drost]]** is an Auralis Enforcement Bureau specialist investigator focusing on high-tier Flux crimes, operating with expanded authority to contract external practitioners when cases exceed Bureau capabilities.

## Systemic Limitations

Both kingdoms' justice systems struggle with:
- Resource constraints preventing investigation of all reported crimes
- Tier ceiling limitations among enforcement personnel
- Evidence destruction by competent Flux practitioners
- Cross-border crime coordination gaps
- Power asymmetry between victims and high-tier criminals
- Political interference in high-profile cases
- Delayed justice due to procedural requirements or mediation timelines

Neither system is designed to handle the full scope of Flux-enabled criminal activity. Both manage what they can, accept losses they cannot prevent, and adapt incrementally as new crime patterns emerge.
