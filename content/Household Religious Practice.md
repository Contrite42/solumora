Religion in [[Solumora]] does not live primarily in temples or formal ceremonies. It lives in what people do at home — the small rituals that mark daily life, the inherited practices that families maintain without always naming their meaning, and the disagreements about what is essential and what is performance.

Household religious practice varies by tradition, by region, and by how seriously a particular family takes the framework. A farming family in rural [[Terravelle]] practicing the [[Covenant of Measure]] may consider their entire household routine an expression of proportionality, while a working family in [[Solhaven]] may attend [[Ascendant Path]] ceremonies twice a year and think no more about it. Both consider themselves practicing the religion they were raised in.

## Ascendant Path Households

In [[Auralis]], families aligned with the [[Ascendant Path]] mark advancement through household ceremony. When a child's [[Control Tier|tier]] is assessed, the family gathers for a formal acknowledgment — often hosted by a [[Flux Users#The Luminants|Luminant]], sometimes conducted by the highest-tier practitioner in the family if they feel confident doing it themselves. The ritual is brief: the child's tier is spoken aloud, the family witnesses it, and the Luminant (or family elder) offers a Path-framed blessing about the child's journey ahead.

What happens after this varies. A T3 or higher assessment often triggers extended family visits, congratulations from neighbors, and a level of attention the family may or may not want. A T0 or T1 assessment is acknowledged more quietly. The Path's theology says every tier is a beginning, but the social weight of that beginning is not distributed evenly, and household practice reflects this.

Daily practice in Path households is minimal. Some families maintain a small shrine — usually a candle and a Path symbol, sometimes a worn copy of one of the core texts. Lighting the candle before a significant casting is common. Lighting it before every casting would be considered excessive by most working families, though some devout households do it anyway.

The friction point in Path households is around T0 children. The official theology offers T0s a framework of intellectual contribution, but the household culture often defaults to practical disappointment. How a family manages this gap — whether they lean into the Path's compensatory theology, quietly absorb the social weight, or reject the Path's framing entirely — is one of the more visible fault lines in Auralis domestic life.

## Covenant of Measure Households

[[Covenant of Measure]] households in [[Terravelle]] do not have shrines. The tradition does not produce objects of veneration. What it produces is a habit of attention: noticing when something is wasted, noticing when more [[Flux]] is being used than a task requires, noticing when display is overtaking function.

Children in Covenant households are taught to inscribe their own [[Birth Runes|birth rune]] as soon as they're old enough to hold a stylus steady. This is not ceremony — it is practice. The rune is already inscribed in the kingdom records, but the child learns to do it themselves because the Covenant holds that understanding the mark you carry is part of carrying it responsibly.

Meals in observant Covenant households often include a moment before eating where someone — usually the person who prepared the food — acknowledges the proportionality of the meal: enough for everyone, nothing wasted. This is not prayer. It is a reminder. In rural communities, this acknowledgment is more common and more serious. In cities, it persists in some families and has faded in others.

The conflict in Covenant households is generational. Younger practitioners, especially those who have spent time in [[Valdenmoor]] or [[Ashford]], sometimes regard the tradition's restraint as holding them back. The ethical stance that says "use only what you need" can feel, to someone trying to prove themselves in an urban environment, like a prohibition against ambition. Older family members regard this tension as evidence that the younger generation has not yet understood what the Covenant is for. This disagreement is rarely resolved. It is inherited.

## Ancestral Current Households

Families practicing the [[Ancestral Current]] maintain routines around the loss of a practitioner more than around their presence. The first year after a practitioner's death is managed carefully: their workspace is not disturbed, their tools are left in place, and the family avoids making significant changes in the spaces where that person worked.

This is not mourning in the sentimental sense. It is maintenance. The Current holds that [[Flux]] presence dissipates slowly, and the family's obligation is to let that dissipation happen without interference. After the first year, the workspace can be rearranged, the tools can be passed to someone else, and life continues. But during that year, you do not touch it.

In families with multi-generation practitioner lineages, certain objects accumulate significance. A workshop table used by three generations of formulists is still a table, but it is treated with more care. When someone in the family needs to make a significant decision about their work, they often make that decision in proximity to these objects. The tradition does not teach that the ancestors are listening. It teaches that the accumulated presence of their work creates a space where clarity is easier to find.

The friction in Current households is about belief. Younger family members sometimes regard the whole practice as superstition — Flux dissipates into the environment; it does not "remain" in a meaningful sense. Older practitioners counter that whether you call it presence or pattern or habit, the spaces where serious work happened for decades feel different, and ignoring that is carelessness, not skepticism.

## The Quiet Work Households

Households aligned with [[The Quiet Work]] in the far north, particularly around [[Wolfpoint]], practice the tradition so informally that visitors often do not recognize it as religious practice at all. There are no ceremonies, no objects, no moments set aside for acknowledgment. What there is: a pervasive attention to precision in all Flux work, a family culture that treats sloppy casting as both technical and ethical failure, and a continuous low-level conversation about what it means to "speak" [[Flux]] more accurately.

Children in these households are taught early that Flux is not something you command. You propose; it responds. If your proposal is unclear or inconsistent, the response will be unclear or inconsistent. The household's role is to teach the child how to propose clearly, and this teaching happens through example more than instruction.

There is no formal coming-of-age in Quiet Work families. At some point, usually in adolescence, a family member (often a parent, sometimes an elder) tells the child: "You're speaking clearly enough now." That is the entirety of the ceremony. The child is considered adult from that point, and the family's relationship with them shifts accordingly.

The tradition's primary household tension is around outsiders. [[The Voss Line|The Voss family]]'s articulation of the Quiet Work through [[Hypertext]] has made the tradition more visible than it was historically, and some Quiet Work families regard this visibility as a betrayal of the tradition's fundamental character. The Quiet Work is supposed to be practiced, not explained. [[Sera Voss]]'s insistence on making Hypertext accessible to people outside the tradition is, to some northern families, a category error.

## Returner Households

Families aligned with [[The Returners]] are concentrated near [[Ancient Ruins|ruins sites]], in desert-edge communities like [[Halveth]], and in the older quarters of [[Emberfall]]. Household practice centers on respect for what came before — not worship, but acknowledgment that the ruins are not merely archaeological sites but potential points of contact.

Children in Returner households are taught not to treat ruins as dead places. No climbing on inscribed stone, no taking fragments as souvenirs, no casual exploration. This is not superstition; it is the tradition's actual position. The ruins are sites of possible communication, and treating them carelessly is the equivalent of shouting over someone who is trying to speak.

Returner households often maintain what they call a "listening practice" — a few minutes each day spent in deliberate silence, paying attention to whether anything is being communicated. This is not meditation in the mystical sense. It is the practice of becoming familiar with what background silence feels like, so that if something interrupts it, you notice.

The conflict within Returner households, and between Returner families and the broader community, is about what the ruins actually are. The prevailing position in both [[Auralis]] and [[Terravelle]] is that the pre-[[Flux]] civilization is gone, and the ruins are evidence of collapse. The Returners' insistence that the civilization withdrew rather than vanished — that contact is possible if approached correctly — is regarded by most institutional representatives as wishful thinking at best and dangerous delusion at worst.

Returner families do not argue this point publicly. They practice their tradition, they teach their children to treat the ruins with care, and they wait.

## Mixed Households

In border towns, in communities with significant migration, and in families where the parents come from different traditions, household practice is necessarily hybrid. A family in [[Ashford]] with one Ascendant Path parent and one Covenant of Measure parent might attend Path ceremonies for births and tier assessments while maintaining Covenant habits around daily Flux use. The children absorb both frameworks and, as adults, tend to lean toward whichever feels more coherent to them.

This mixing is more common than the formal institutions of either tradition acknowledge. The [[Flux Users#The Luminants|Luminants]] prefer to frame religious identity as something chosen and committed to; the Covenant's elder practitioners speak of the tradition as something you either understand or do not. But most people's actual religious practice is less doctrinally pure than this. It is assembled from what their family did, what their community expects, and what makes sense to them personally.

The result is a landscape of household practice that is far more diverse than the five major traditions would suggest. Families create their own versions, keep what works, drop what does not, and pass the result to the next generation as "how we do things."
