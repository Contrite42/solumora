# Watts as Currency

The [[Solumora]] economy is denominated in Watts, a currency system unified across both [[Terravelle]] and [[Auralis]] despite the kingdoms' institutional separation. The Watt is simultaneously a unit of [[Flux]] energy and a standard medium of exchange. This dual nature creates unique economic properties: money has physical basis as transferable Flux capacity, verification can be performed by any capable [[Control Tier|T3+]] practitioner, and financial security rests on Flux-detection infrastructure that is technically difficult to counterfeit or conceal.

## Historical Basis

The relationship between Flux and currency emerged during the [[Grimoire Economy|grimoire market development]]. Early market operations used direct Flux-energy equivalent transactions: spell services valued using pure Flux-cost equivalence (T1 spell = 1 Watt, T5 spell = 25 Watts, etc). As commercial complexity grew, standardized currency became necessary, and Watts simply extended the existing Flux-based pricing logic into broader economic use.

Both kingdoms adopted the Watt as their standard currency without formal coordination — the system emerged pragmatically through trade relationship consistency that made bilateral agreement unnecessary. Terravelle conducts some internal transactions in regional equivalents (ancient grain-standard contracts persist in rural [[Maren Valley]], salt-weight denominations appear in coastal settlements), but all formalized trade, inter-kingdom commerce, and institutional accounting uses Watts.

## Denominations and Physical Representation

The Watt is abstract economic unit: there is no physical "1 Watt coin" or standardized token. Instead, Watts exist as:

**Flux-Marked Currency** — The primary form for large transactions and institutional storage. A Flux-marked note or object carries encoded Flux signatures that represent specific Watt denominations (typically 10W, 50W, 100W, 500W, and 1000W increments). The Flux marks are created using authentication sigils that are difficult to forge without T4+ capacity. Removing or altering the marks causes them to collapse into inert Flux noise — making tampering self-evident to any practitioner attempting to verify the object.

**Ledger Accounts** — Secondary form for inter-institutional transactions. Banks and institutional partners maintain ledger accounts tracking Watt balances, with transfers recorded through signed documentation and verified through institutional authentication protocols. Most high-value transactions between houses, guilds, and kingdoms occur through ledger accounting rather than physical currency transfer.

**Direct Service Payment** — Functional approximation wherein services are priced in Watts but paid through direct Flux transfers or specialized equipment provision. A guide charging 50W per day expedition service accepts either physical currency, ledger transfer, or equivalent Flux capacity delivered by the employer. This operates in both formal and informal contexts, particularly in rural areas where physical currency is scarce and direct capability exchange is more practical.

**Personal Flux Capacity** — Least formal denomination. A practitioner potentially represents a negotiable asset if third parties trust their Flux capacity against future obligation. This functions primarily in close-community contexts (family arrangements, mentor-apprentice relationships, trusted professional partnerships) rather than in institutional trade. Most courts and authorities recognize these arrangements only when formal documentation exists.

## Verification and Security

Watts-denominated items can be authenticated using Flux-detection capacity. A practitioner with T3+ ability can examine a Flux-marked note and verify:

- **Denomination accuracy** — The encoded Flux signatures produce characteristic tonal qualities at specific power levels. T3-T4 practitioners can identify rough denomination ranges. T5+ practitioners can specify exact values with high precision.
- **Authenticity** — Genuine Flux marks carry complex layered signatures created using institutional techniques. Forgeries lack the layering and typically collapse when examined at certain power thresholds. Sophisticated forgeries exist but require T5+ capacity to create and fail under intensive scrutiny.
- **Alteration history** — Flux marks record imperfect traces of modification attempts. Removing or partially altering marks leaves characteristic damage patterns visible to skilled examiners. Complete forgery (creating entirely new marks) requires materials and expertise beyond black-market capacity.

**Authentication Costs** — Personal authentication by a T3 practitioner costs approximately 2-5W for verification of a single note/object. Institutional authentication (bank-scale verification using specialist practitioners and equipment) operates at lower per-unit cost but requires formal access to bank services. Informal merchants may request authentication from traveling T3+ practitioners (paying verification fees from the transaction), while rural communities often trust personal relationships over formal verification.

**Centralized Verification Infrastructure** — Both [[Terravelle]] and [[Auralis]] maintain institutional authentication services. [[Guild Scrivener Network|Terravelle's guild system]] operates verification through certified authenticators who maintain authentication standards and handle dispute resolution. [[The Advancement Corps|Auralis's institutional banking]] maintains authentication through Council-certified practitioners. Cross-kingdom verification occurs through formal institutional channels that charge premium fees (5-15W per authentication) for documentation that will satisfy authorities in either kingdom.

## Economic Properties

The Watt-as-currency system creates unusual economic features:

**Practitioner-Based Valuation** — Currency value is partially anchored to [[Flux]] utility rather than pure commodity scarcity. As [[Control Tier|T3+]] practitioner population increases, authentication becomes cheaper and more accessible, which technically reduces verification barriers but does not inflate currency directly. High-tier practitioners are economically valuable not because they create more Watts but because they reduce friction costs in currency authentication.

**Detection-Based Constraints** — Hoarding large quantities of Watts creates vulnerability: any practitioner with sufficient Flux capacity can detect accumulated Flux-marked currency at medium distances. This creates security dilemmas for merchants carrying large cash quantities (vulnerability to theft detection and institutional observation) and creates practical advantages for ledger-account systems that avoid carrying physical currency.

**Institutional Transparency Requirements** — The capacity to detect Flux-marked currency allows institutional monitoring of movement. [[The Council of Auralis|Council]] and high-tier authorities can theoretically track large currency movements through detection of Flux signatures. In practice, institutional oversight is limited to specific investigations rather than universal surveillance, but the technical capability creates persistent vulnerability for those attempting to hide economic activity.

**Practitioner Income Advantages** — T3+ practitioners have valuation advantages: they can authenticate currency without paying verification fees, can potentially create Flux-marked currency (through learned authentication techniques), and can negotiate transactions denominated in direct Flux transfer rather than physical currency. Substantial practitioner income in both kingdoms includes Flux-transfer components that avoid physical currency entirely.

## Conversion and Cross-Kingdom Exchange

Both kingdoms accept Watt-denominated currency, but conversion and institutional exchange carries complexity:

**Institutional Exchange Fees** — Converting large quantities of currency between kingdom banking systems incurs fees (2-4% of transaction value) to handle logistics, authentication verification, and institutional overhead. Merchants operating in both kingdoms typically maintain accounts in both settlements to avoid repeated conversion costs.

**Market Rate Fluctuation** — Minor fluctuations in currency value occur based on Flux material availability and practitioner population changes. Standard fluctuation ranges 0.5-2% annually under normal conditions, but war-preparation disruption has increased volatility to 2-5% during recent years. Institutional authorities attempt to stabilize rates through coordinated currency policy, but enforcement mechanisms are limited across kingdom boundaries.

**Institutional Trust Requirements** — Cross-kingdom ledger transfers require formal institutional relationships. A merchant house in [[Auralis]] cannot directly transfer funds to [[Terravelle]] partners without routing through established banking channels. This creates merchant-house dependency on formal banking relationships and introduces costs that favor large established houses over independent operators.

**Currency Substitution in Border Areas** — Settlements along kingdom borders (particularly [[Halfridge]], [[Halveth]], [[Ashford]]) maintain unofficial local-denomination systems: trading in commodity equivalents (grain weight, metal quantity, Flux-marked object accumulations) to avoid formal currency conversion friction. These systems operate alongside Watts but are not formally recognized, creating ambiguity about whether border-area transactions follow institutional rules.

## Contemporary War Preparation Impact

Military preparation has created financial system strain:

**Institutional Funding Crises** — [[The Council of Auralis|Council]] military expenditures have expanded dramatically in recent years, creating pressure to increase taxation and institutional borrowing. Elevated taxation has reduced private capital availability and created civilian merchant-class frustration. [[Terravelle]]'s decentralized structure has been less affected, but [[Valdenmoor|northern kingdom resources]] are strained supporting military logistics in [[Ashford]].

**Currency Instability** — Institutional borrowing to fund military operations has increased Watts in circulation without corresponding economic productivity increases. Minor currency inflation (2-3% estimated annually) has created persistent upward price pressure across consumer goods. Institutions attempt to manage inflation through constraining new currency creation, but enforcement is difficult across distributed banking systems.

**Institutional Lending Pressure** — Banks and merchant houses increasingly prioritize lending to military-related projects (equipment production, supply logistics, force mobilization) over civilian ventures. Interest rates for civilian commercial loans have increased 0.5-2% (to 4-8% annual rates for merchants, 8-12% for individuals), while military-related lending operates at 2-3% institutional-preference rates. This creates competitive disadvantage for civilian merchants and accelerates consolidation toward institutional vendors with preferential lending access.

**Currency Verification Vulnerability** — Military demand for large quantities of currency creates verification bottlenecks. Authentication practitioners are increasingly conscripted or directed toward military support roles, reducing civilian authentication availability and increasing verification costs (now 5-10W for personal authentication in institutional zones). Institutional currency remains more secure through centralized verification, while civilian-merchant currency verification becomes unreliable.

**Capital Flight Pressure** — Merchants concerned about institutional instability increasingly convert Watts to goods, Flux capacity, or cross-kingdom accounts held outside institutional control. This reduces domestic currency circulation and creates deflationary pressure on institutional currency pools, which authorities respond to by increasing taxation to restore currency supply — creating vicious cycle pressure.

## Contemporary Currency Status

The Watt-based currency system remains functional but displays visible strain. Both kingdoms continue accepting Watt-denominated transactions, and merchant networks continue operating, but fundamental questions about institutional backing and long-term currency stability have emerged. Practitioners and merchants increasingly employ hedging strategies: maintaining multiple currency forms, building commodity reserves, converting to Flux-capacity holdings, and establishing cross-kingdom accounts distributed across multiple banking relationships to reduce exposure to single-institution risk.

The system persists because the alternatives (commodity-based barter, regional currency fragmentation, institutional monopoly over transactions) would impose worse costs. But the contemporary baseline of institutional disruption and war preparation pressure has created underlying fragility that would likely cascade into crisis if military operations escalate further or if any major banking institution experiences failure.
