[[Tier Assessment]] is the first formal sorting event most children encounter, typically between ages eight and twelve. It is treated publicly as neutral measurement and privately as trajectory assignment.

Assessment does not determine a person's worth. It does determine which institutions invest in them next.

## Assessment Practice

Both kingdoms run recurring assessment circuits.

- urban districts: scheduled assessment blocks through temples, guild halls, and civic nodes;
- rural districts: traveling assessors and clergy visits;
- disputed-edge settlements: mixed authority assessments accepted by practical consensus.

Assessments usually include repeat checks when readings are unstable, but households with fewer resources are less able to secure repeats.

## Immediate Placement Effects

Once recorded, tier results influence placement channels:

- T0-T1: labor-ready tracks, low-cost practical instruction, local apprenticeship waiting lists;
- T2-T3: formal apprenticeship and specialist preparatory routes;
- T4+: institutional sponsorship attempts, Path or elite guild attention, relocation pressure.

The social effect appears before formal enrollment. Families and employers adjust expectations immediately.

## Terravelle Placement Logic

In [[Terravelle]], placement flows through guild networks and local reputation.

A moderate tier plus strong reliability record can outperform higher tier with weak work history. Guild masters value retention and discipline because apprentices are productivity investments.

This makes Terravelle comparatively permeable for late bloomers and steady T0-T2 workers, but also inconsistent across regions.

## Auralis Placement Logic

In [[Auralis]], placement aligns with [[Ascendant Path]] structure and district hierarchy.

Recorded tier results travel farther through institutions and influence:

- school continuation options;
- district sponsorship access;
- eligibility for advanced training channels.

The system is legible and scalable, but children from lower-status districts face stronger screening pressure even at identical tier results.

## Repeat Assessment and Appeals

Households sometimes seek reassessment when results conflict with observed capability. Success depends on cost, travel access, and social leverage.

- repeat assessment fees and transport burden poor households;
- documentation quality influences whether appeals are taken seriously;
- families with institutional connections resolve disputes faster.

In practice, appeals are a quality-control mechanism used unevenly by class.

## Drop-Off Point

The first major drop-off occurs one to two years after assessment, when families must pay the ongoing cost of placement:

- tools and materials;
- transport time;
- foregone wages from child labor;
- social compliance with track expectations.

Formal opportunity exists at assessment. Sustained opportunity depends on what the household can carry after assessment.
