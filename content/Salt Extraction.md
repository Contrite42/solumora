# Salt Extraction

Salt extraction operations harvesting mineral salt from coastal marine sources, producing preservation salt essential to food preservation, creature-product processing, and industrial applications across both kingdoms. [[Hedun]] coastal district maintains the largest institutional salt-extraction infrastructure, supplying approximately 60% of inter-kingdom trade salt volume. Regional secondary operations maintain distributed supply capacity and local market supply.

## Extraction Methods

**Solar evaporation** (primary method) relies on seasonal water evaporation from seawater in controlled basin systems. Operation sequence: seawater is pumped or diverted into shallow collection basins, natural solar heating and wind exposure evaporate water over 4-8 weeks depending on seasonal conditions and climate zone. Residual salt crystals are mechanically harvested, rinsed to remove brine residue, and sun-dried to final product form.

**Thermal acceleration** (institutional operations) uses Flux-enhanced heating to accelerate seawater evaporation, reducing processing time to 1-2 weeks but consuming Flux resources. High-capital operations can justify Flux acceleration; small operations cannot.

**Salt-deposit mining** occurs in coastal regions with geological salt formations. Direct mineral extraction from salt beds requires less infrastructure than seawater-based extraction but produces inconsistent-purity product and is geographically limited to specific formations.

## Production and Supply

**Hedun district** maintains multiple basin facilities across approximately 8-10 miles of coastal frontage. Typical annual production: 40-80 tons, with seasonal variation based on weather and water conditions. Hedun operations employ 40-60 permanent workers plus 20-30 seasonal workers during peak seasons. Capital infrastructure includes basin construction, water-diversion systems, mechanical harvesting equipment, and storage facilities.

**Regional secondary operations** scattered along accessible coastlines maintain smaller-scale production (5-20 tons annually), typically family-operated or merchant-house backed. These operations serve local and regional markets, often unable to economically compete with Hedun distribution for inter-kingdom trade.

**Supply distribution** from Hedun flows north through [[Outer Coast Packet Line]] maritime transport to [[Ashford]], then inland to settlements requiring salt. Annual Ashford-bound salt volume averages 25-30 tons, representing approximately 35-40% of Hedun's total output.

## Labor and Operations

**Field extraction workers** (T0-T2 practitioners, non-practitioners) manage basin water-level maintenance, crystal harvesting, basic processing (rinsing, preliminary drying). Work is seasonal and physically demanding — summer heat intensifies during extraction season, forcing peak-season work during hottest months. Wages: 12-18 Watts weekly during season; minimal off-season employment.

**Processing supervisors** (T2-T3 practitioners) manage quality control, purity assessment, batch processing, and inventory management. Year-round employment; supervisor skill directly affects product quality and hence market pricing.

**Flux operators** (T3-T4 practitioners, institutional operations only) manage thermal acceleration systems when applicable. Specialized skill with expensive equipment; limited availability outside institutional operations.

**Transport coordinators** manage logistics between extraction sites, regional distribution, and maritime shipping coordination. Critical role requiring reliability and merchant-network familiarity.

## Product Specifications

**Preservation-grade salt** (primary market) is coarse-crystal salt with 97-99% purity, suitable for food preservation and creature-product processing. Wolfpoint [[Mountain Game Collective]] is largest single consumer, requiring 8,000-10,000 pounds annually. Pricing: 1-2 Watts per 50-pound load through institutional contracts, slightly higher for spot-market retail.

**Fine-grade salt** (specialty market) is finely processed with higher purity standards required for industrial and medicinal applications. Higher processing cost; markets among specialized institutions and high-tier practitioners.

**Culinary salt** (luxury market, limited scale) serves elite markets with premium pricing. Minor volume output; notable profit margin but negligible impact on overall production economics.

**By-product minerals** extracted during salt processing (trace minerals, magnesium salts) have limited secondary markets and are often discarded unless specific institutional buyer demands particular minerals.

## Contemporary Pressure

**Institutional procurement consolidation** — [[Auralis]] Council and merchant-house operations are securing exclusive purchase contracts with Hedun operations, reducing availability for independent and regional buyers. Hedun's recent commitment of 70-80% of annual output to institutional buyers has created market shortage for non-institutional consumers.

**Preservation demand acceleration** — Military food-supply operations and expanded institutional food preservation are increasing salt demand. Estimated 20-25% increase in institutional demand within past 12 months, with further increases projected. Supply constraints are becoming visible: some regional operations report inability to fulfill standing contracts.

**Environmental concern** — Repeated seawater extraction from enclosed bays is creating salinity imbalances and affecting local fish populations. Hedun district is showing early signs of marine resource degradation from extraction intensity.

**Labor constraints** — Salt-extraction work is considered low-opportunity employment (minimal skill development, minimal career advancement). Younger workers show declining interest. Conscription is removing experienced supervisors, forcing operations toward less-experienced management.

**Quality-control pressure** — High-purity requirements for industrial applications are reducing usable output percentage from extraction operations. Typical extraction produces 85-90% usable product; remaining 10-15% is below-specification. Institutional buyers increasingly demand higher purity, further reducing usable production.

Salt extraction is adequate to current demands but shows emerging capacity constraints if institutional demand continues accelerating beyond current projection levels.
