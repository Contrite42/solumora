**Yena Falt** is thirty-nine years old and works as a private arbitrator in [[Ashford]]'s commercial district. The work is resolving cargo disputes — between operators and dock authorities, between merchants and the guild review offices, between any two parties who have a claim and want it settled without the formal review process, which is slower and more public than most commercial disputes benefit from.

## Appearance

Yena is thirty-nine with the sharp, composed appearance of someone who has spent fourteen years making decisions that people accept even when they don't like them. Her dark hair is kept in a neat braid that's practical without being austere, and she dresses in the professional style of Ashford's commercial arbitrators: well-made tunics and skirts in dark blues and grays that project authority without ostentation, everything chosen to look equally appropriate in a warehouse inspection or a formal dispute hearing.

Her face is expressive when she wants it to be and utterly unreadable when she doesn't, a controlled neutrality that comes from years of listening to conflicting accounts and revealing nothing about which one she believes. Her eyes are darker brown and intensely focused when reviewing documentation, and she has the arbitrator's particular skill of looking directly at whoever's speaking with complete attention while simultaneously tracking everyone else's reactions. Her hands are ink-stained from constant document work, and she wears reading glasses on a chain around her neck for manifest review — though she never wears them during actual hearings, preferring to maintain direct eye contact without obstruction.

She moves through Ashford's commercial district with quiet confidence, greeting merchants and dock authorities by name, understanding the web of relationships and grudges that underlie every dispute without announcing that understanding publicly. When conducting hearings her posture is upright and still, projecting the calm authority that makes disputing parties feel heard even when her decision goes against them. Off-duty she has a dry, precise wit and a tendency to listen more than she speaks, the professional habit of someone who has learned that people reveal more when you let them talk. The leather case she carries everywhere contains current dispute files, reference notes, and nothing else — she maintains no permanent records beyond what the parties require, understanding that too much memory would compromise future judgment.

## Work

She is not a guild official. Her authority in any given case is the authority of the parties' agreement to use her, and that agreement comes from her track record: fourteen years of decisions that the parties, in aggregate, have considered fair. She maintains this record through a combination of [[Control Tier|T3]] casting — useful for document verification, manifest confirmation, detecting certain categories of alteration in cargo records — and a developed sense of where the disputed facts actually are as opposed to where each party's account places them. The casting is the smaller contribution.

She does not advertise what she can do with T3 work. The service she sells is judgment. The casting is infrastructure.

She learned the work from a man named Arvan who ran the same practice for thirty years before his health made it impractical. He trained her for four years and then retired to a town on the northern coast. She gets a letter from him once a year in which he asks how the work is going and she writes back with a case or two and he writes back with what he would have done differently. She does not always agree with him. She finds the disagreements useful.

She keeps no records of decisions beyond what the parties require for their own documentation. Anything that becomes reference material for future disputes gives the parties information about how she reasons, which would change how they present cases, which would change the information available to her when she reasons. She explained this to Arvan once. He said he had worked this out around year eight and she had saved herself some time.
