# The Lantern Decrees

The Lantern Decrees were a sequence of emergency legal orders issued during years 6-11 of [[The Ancient Persecution Era|Persecution Era]] by municipal-civic authorities in both [[Auralis]] and [[Terravelle]].

They are historically significant because they normalized persecution logic at neighborhood scale through administrative procedure rather than battlefield violence.

## Origin

After initial demon-identification campaigns produced high panic and low institutional confidence, central authorities faced a practical problem: they could not sustain citywide sweeps without paralyzing trade and civic operations. The solution was procedural localization.

Under Lantern Decree language, district magistrates gained temporary authority to:
- impose dusk-to-dawn documentation checks,
- compel household registry verification,
- authorize "temporary custody" based on suspicion convergence,
- restrict travel between wards without sponsor attestations.

The decrees were marketed as short-duration safety controls. They remained active in some districts for over five years.

## Mechanism of Harm

The Decrees did not require proof standards equivalent to criminal law. They required administrative confidence thresholds defined by local officials and witness reports.

This created three structural distortions:
- **Rumor elevation:** repeated allegation became procedural evidence.
- **Incentive drift:** local offices gained budget and authority by expanding enforcement scope.
- **Documentation asymmetry:** targeted households carried burden of proving normalcy while accusers bore minimal risk.

In practice, the Decrees converted ordinary civic infrastructure into a distributed identification engine.

## Typical Enforcement Pattern

A recurring pattern appears in surviving records:
1. Initial anomaly rumor in market or worship district.
2. Local magistrate issues temporary check order.
3. Registry office expands list based on association chains.
4. Custody requests escalate faster than verification capacity.
5. Appeals backlog forms, keeping suspected individuals detained by delay rather than verdict.

Modern historians describe this as "procedural persecution by queue."

## Resistance and Collapse

Resistance emerged first from logistics and trade offices rather than royal courts. Municipal coordinators documented that decree checkpoints disrupted food and medicine flow, causing preventable deaths unrelated to demon incidents.

The Decrees began collapsing when civic administrators could prove that districts with strict lantern enforcement were less safe in aggregate due to infrastructure disruption and retaliatory violence.

Final repeal in major cities required joint action from:
- merchant-guild emergency councils,
- religious mediators fearful of institutional legitimacy collapse,
- exhausted municipal judges facing unmanageable appeals dockets.

## Legacy

The Lantern Decrees became a central cautionary case in anti-persecution legal scholarship:
- emergency language without expiration enforcement becomes permanent coercion,
- suspicion-based detention regimes create self-validating data loops,
- civic procedure can produce persecution outcomes even when formal criminal law remains unchanged.

Modern coexistence safeguards, including provisions in the [[Ashford Coexistence Oversight Commission]], explicitly cite Lantern Decree patterns when rejecting rumor-based detention proposals.

## Relevance to Current Era

Post-revelation legal debate repeatedly reproduces Decree-era argument structure: urgency claims, lowered thresholds, and "temporary" exceptions. Historical analysis of the Lantern Decrees provides the clearest precedent that such structures expand faster than institutions can safely govern.
