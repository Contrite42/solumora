Ilke Vann trades the coastal circuit between [[Ashford]] and [[Halveth]] on a shallow-draft vessel they have owned for eleven years. They are forty-three, [[Control Tier|T2]], and have been working the coast since they were twenty-five. The vessel is named _Reasonable Margin_, which was a joke about the economics of the first year that has outlasted the circumstances that prompted it.

## Appearance

Ilke is forty-three with the lean, weather-beaten appearance of someone who has spent eighteen years working coastal waters — fit in the specifically maritime way that comes from constant sailing work, and sun-damaged beyond their years from extended exposure to reflected light off water. Their light brown hair is kept short and practical, often showing the salt-stiffness that comes from constant sea spray, and they dress in layers of practical sailing gear that can handle the shifting conditions of coastal runs: heavy wool, oiled canvas, everything showing the wear and salt-staining of years at sea.

Their hands are densely calloused from rope work and sail handling, scarred from the inevitable injuries that accumulate over eighteen years of maritime work, and they move with the unconscious balance of someone whose body has adapted to shifting decks and unreliable footing. When reading weather conditions their expression becomes distant and focused, the look of someone performing T2 atmospheric sensing that lets them forecast conditions six hours out — the skill that makes the difference between arriving before a weather system or waiting it out in a cove for days.

They move through coastal ports with the easy familiarity of someone who has run the same circuit for eighteen years, greeting harbor masters and other sailors by name, understanding local conditions and tide patterns with automatic competence. Their vessel _Reasonable Margin_ is maintained with meticulous care — not beautiful, but functional and reliable, showing the attention of someone who depends on it absolutely. Off-duty they have a thoughtful, somewhat reserved manner and spend notable time writing long, detailed letters to someone in Halveth, describing what the coast looks like from the water in different conditions with the careful attention of someone for whom observation has become its own kind of practice.

## Work

The round circuit takes approximately three weeks depending on weather and cargo. Ilke carries whatever is moving — preserved fish and salt north from Ashford, timber goods and certain Halveth manufactures south, the occasional passenger who needs to move without using the inland roads and doesn't want to explain why. They do not ask. The coast between Ashford and Halveth is not demanding sailing, but it is specific: the current behavior at the cape halfway through, a stretch of cliff coast that requires timing against tidal windows, the sand bar at the Halveth harbor mouth that shifts with the season and has caught at least two other operators Ilke is aware of.

Their T2 casting is weather-reading, primarily — the kind of extended atmospheric sensing that tells you what the sky will do six hours before the sky announces it. On a coastal run, six hours is the difference between arriving before a system or waiting it out in a cove for two days. The certification cost more than the boat. They have never regretted it.

They have been writing letters to a person in Halveth for fifteen years. The letters go in both directions. Ilke's are longer and more specific, particularly about what the coast looks like from the water at different times of year, in different weather, in different light. They have not examined why this is. The letters continue regardless.

- Tidal windows and weather forecasts force rapid go/no-go decisions.
- Passenger anonymity needs create tension with port verification checks.
- Seasonal bar shifts make normal harbor approaches temporarily unusable.

## Secrets

- Ilke's long-running letters encode observational intelligence beyond personal correspondence.
- They can identify abnormal patrol patterns before most operators notice.
- One repeat passenger line is tied to a network that avoids inland oversight.
