Working notes on [[Equatorial Desert|Desert Zakros]] crossing procedure, maintained by [[Avel Coss]]. Not intended for general distribution.

---

**On the decision to cross**

I decline crossings when the following conditions apply, individually or in combination: client party larger than eight, client party that has not crossed before and cannot demonstrate physical condition sufficient for the northern approach, proposed departure timing that puts the midpoint during the dead-heat window, or any prior indication that the client party will not accept direction without discussion during the crossing.

The last condition is the most common reason I decline. Discussion during the crossing is not a negotiating style. It is a way of dying at the pace your argument takes.

I do not explain these rules in detail during client consultations. I explain that I have rules and that the rules are not adjustable. Clients who ask what the rules are get an explanation. Clients who accept the rules without asking them I watch more carefully on the first day.

**On the desert itself — what the accounts miss**

Zakros in the crossing season smells of salt and something underneath salt that I have not found a word for. Not mineral. Not biological. Specific to Zakros, specific to the crossing season, present from the moment the transition zone thins into open desert and present until you are two days back out on the Auralis side.

The ground in the early crossing is not sand. It is salt flat, compressed, surface temperature roughly forty degrees above air temperature at midday. The shimmer that crossers describe as visual distortion is partly thermal and partly something the [[Flux]] field does in high-density areas — the desert runs high ambient Flux in a way that affects visual processing over extended periods. I have learned to factor this into navigation. I tell clients on day two that what they are seeing at distance may not be what is at distance, and to use my read rather than their own.

The anomalous sites — I have passed near two of them on different crossings, never through them. Both times the [[Flux]] behavior in the surrounding area changed in ways that are difficult to describe as malfunction and difficult to describe as anything else. My casting became imprecise at range. My sense of direction required active correction rather than passive maintenance. Both times I moved us south and the conditions resolved over the following hours.

I have put these observations in a report for the [[Auralis]] expedition network three times, in the interest of other operators having the information. The reports were received. I do not know what was done with them.

**On the return**

Every crossing I have completed, I have counted the party out on the far side before allowing myself to register that the crossing is done. Forty-seven in. Forty-seven out. I do this as a practice. The alternative — crossing eleven times and letting the record accumulate into something I might feel entitled to — creates a specific kind of confidence that Zakros corrects at unpredictable intervals.

The two partial crossings I count separately. The first, I turned us back. The decision was correct. The second, a sand system moved faster than my reading of the season predicted, and we spent four days on the Auralis approach waiting it out before conditions allowed return. No one died. The conditions could have killed all seven of us. These are the same event and they are different events and I have written about both of them enough times that I know where I stop being able to be precise.

I cross Zakros because I am better at it than almost anyone currently working, and because the alternative is to let people who are worse at it continue to guide people across. That is the complete explanation. I have tested it for other components and have not found any.
