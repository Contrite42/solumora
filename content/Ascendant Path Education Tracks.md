The [[Ascendant Path]] education model in [[Auralis]] links instruction, ranking, and social legitimacy into one pipeline. It is more standardized than guild apprenticeship and more sensitive to formal status markers.

For many families, Path tracks represent the clearest route to institutional mobility. For others, they represent a costly system with visible rules and uneven access.

## Track Structure

Path education typically moves through stages:

1. district foundational instruction;
2. tier-confirmed advancement screening;
3. specialization placement;
4. supervised institutional practice;
5. formal credential recognition.

The structure is legible and attractive to families planning long-term upward movement.

## Who Advances

Advancement depends on more than tier:

- documented performance consistency;
- conduct and sponsor evaluations;
- ability to sustain attendance across years;
- district standing and institutional recommendation quality.

Children from lower terraces can advance, but they must usually clear more procedural friction than peers from stronger districts.

## Costs Hidden as Compliance

Direct tuition may be limited in some tracks, but indirect costs are persistent:

- travel and time costs;
- required materials and uniform standards;
- foregone wages from delayed work entry;
- unpaid preparatory labor expected for advancement dossiers.

These costs select for households with stability, not only talent.

## Tier Effects

T2-T4 students are the pipeline center. T0-T1 students often remain in practical tracks with fewer advancement branches. T5+ students attract intense institutional attention but also tighter behavioral and documentation scrutiny.

This creates broad mid-tier throughput and narrow high-tier gatekeeping.

## Drop-Off Points

Most attrition occurs at two transitions:

- from foundational instruction to specialization placement;
- from supervised practice to recognized credential.

At both points, administrative quality and sponsorship access matter as much as capability.

## Relationship With Non-Path Tracks

Path systems do not replace guild systems. They compete and interlock.

Many households hedge by placing one child in Path preparation and another in early apprenticeship to diversify risk. This is common in mixed-income districts where no single track is reliably affordable end-to-end.
