Solumora does not have a unified religion. What it has is a shared metaphysical substrate — [[Flux]] is real, it permeates the world, it can be touched by anyone with the capacity to do so — and a set of distinct cultural traditions that interpret what this means.

The major traditions are organized below. They are not fully separate: practitioners in [[Terravelle]] know what the [[Ascendant Path]] teaches, Auralis residents know the broad outlines of the [[Covenant of Measure]], and border communities practice hybrid versions that institutional representatives of both traditions would find impure. Religion in Solumora is less a set of exclusive commitments than a set of orientations that people inhabit with varying seriousness.

## The Five Traditions

**[[Ascendant Path]]** — [[Auralis]]'s dominant religion. Institutionally organized, politically integrated with the Council, runs schools and officiates life ceremonies. Core doctrine: Flux mastery is spiritual advancement; [[Control Tier|tier]] is a measure of where you are on a lifelong path toward the most developed version of yourself. Has formal clergy (Luminants), extensive theological literature, and significant political relationships. The most powerful religious institution in either kingdom.

**[[Covenant of Measure]]** — [[Terravelle]]'s dominant spiritual tradition, resists being called a religion. No clergy, no theology in the formal sense. Core principle: proportionality — use Flux for the task, no more, nothing wasted. Ethical practice is the entirety of the tradition. Ceremonies are functional (birth rune inscription) rather than elaborate. Influences the [[Trade Guilds|guild]] culture's standards for what responsible Flux use looks like.

**[[The Quiet Work]]** — the northern tradition, concentrated in [[Wolfpoint]] and the far north. Undocumented, informal, closer to a professional ethic than a religion. Holds that Flux is a language and life is the practice of learning to speak it more precisely. No clergy, no ceremonies, no afterlife doctrine. Entirely absorbed into the [[The Voss Line|Voss family's]] approach to [[Hypertext]] — their insistence on Hypertext as a more honest form of expression is, in their culture, as much theological as technical.

**[[The Returners]]** — fringe tradition found mainly in communities near [[Ancient Ruins|ruins sites]], in the desert-edge territories, and in the older quarters of [[Emberfall]]. Holds that the civilization that preceded both kingdoms did not vanish but withdrew — that it persists in some form accessible through the ruins, and that the right approach, the right understanding, would allow contact. Does not advocate crossing the ruins sites violently; treats them as places of possible communication. Regarded as fringe by both kingdoms' establishments.

**[[The Ancestral Current]]** — a folk tradition widespread in rural communities across both kingdoms, rarely institutionalized but persistently present. Holds that a practitioner's Flux capacity does not vanish at death — it dissipates gradually, leaving a detectable presence in places where the practitioner spent their life. Not ghosts in the dramatic sense; more like a residue that can be sensed, honored, and occasionally heard. Taboo: disturbing a practitioner's home site within the first year of death. No formal clergy; practiced through family tradition and local custom.

## Cross-Tradition Notes

The traditions are not mutually exclusive in practice. A farming family in rural Terravelle may practice the Covenant, maintain Ancestral Current customs around their grandparents' graves, and have some vague awareness of Returner beliefs from trade contact with desert-edge communities. None of this creates internal contradiction because none of these traditions claims exclusive truth in ways that require the others to be false.

The exception is the Ascendant Path, which is explicit enough in its theology that orthodox Luminants do distinguish between Path-consistent and Path-inconsistent beliefs. The Ancestral Current's persistence in southern Auralis is something senior Luminants address diplomatically — the folk tradition does not contradict Path theology exactly, but its theological implications are not developed within the Path framework, and Luminants prefer to manage what it means rather than suppress what it is.
