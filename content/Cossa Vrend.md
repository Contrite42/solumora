**Cossa Vrend** is thirty-one years old and manages the physical document holdings at [[Wolfpoint]]'s archive. [[Control Tier|T2]]. She arrived at twenty-six following a five-year career in document preservation at a commercial archive in [[Valdenmoor]], where she learned the technical work of maintaining records and developed a professional interest in document condition problems that standard preservation techniques do not address. She applied to Wolfpoint's archive through a correspondence channel she found in a professional guild publication. She was offered the position based on her technical qualifications. The content of what she would be preserving was not described in detail in the offer.

## Appearance

Cossa is thirty-one with the precise, methodical appearance of someone who has spent five years managing document preservation at Wolfpoint's archive — practical and focused rather than scholarly, carrying herself with the careful attention of someone who handles fragile materials daily. Her light brown hair is kept in a tight braid for practical reasons, and she dresses in the functional style of archive workers: simple tunics and skirts that won't shed fibers onto sensitive documents, neutral colors that don't distract from the work, everything chosen for cleanliness and durability.

Her hands are the most notable thing about her: remarkably steady from five years of handling degraded manuscripts and Flux-saturated materials, with light chemical staining on her fingers from preservation treatments and the distinctive callus pattern of someone who spends significant time carefully turning fragile pages. She wears thin cotton gloves for most handling work, and reading glasses on a cord for detailed condition assessment, and moves through the archive with the cautious efficiency of someone who understands that a single careless gesture can destroy something irreplaceable.

When working with documents her expression is focused and analytical, reading physical condition patterns without necessarily understanding the content — she can identify Hypertext sequence notation by visual structure and categorize script types she doesn't read, the practical taxonomy of someone whose job is preservation rather than interpretation. Her workspace is organized with systematic thoroughness: preservation materials labeled and inventoried, condition logs maintained with meticulous accuracy, notes on novel degradation problems that standard training didn't cover.

Off-duty she has a quiet satisfaction visible in how she discusses the work — speaking about document condition problems with the engaged interest of someone who has found professional challenges that require developing solutions not documented anywhere in guild literature. She keeps detailed notes on these preservation techniques, and colleagues note that she becomes notably animated when explaining how Flux-saturated materials behave differently from standard degraded documents, the specific category of problem that makes Wolfpoint's archive more interesting than the commercial work she left in Valdenmoor.

## Work

The Wolfpoint archive holds documents in states of degradation ranging from fully legible to fragmentary to illegible-but-Flux-saturated in ways that affect how they are handled. A significant portion of the holdings are in scripts she does not read. A meaningful portion appear to be in [[Hypertext]] sequence notation that she has learned to identify by visual pattern without being able to parse. Her job is to ensure the physical materials remain stable, that the cataloguing is maintained, and that practitioners who need access to specific holdings can find what they are looking for.

She has learned a working amount about [[Hypertext]] over five years — not how to execute it, but how to recognize it, classify it, and understand broadly what the practitioners who come to the archive are looking for. This knowledge is entirely practical. She has never cast a Hypertext sequence. She does not expect to.

What she finds: the archive is the most interesting place she has worked, and it does not require her to understand what makes it interesting. The preservation problems here are problems that standard training does not address, which means she has spent five years developing solutions that do not exist in the professional literature. She keeps notes on these solutions. She has considered publishing them. She has not determined where she would publish them, since the professional guild literature does not have a category for archive work of this type.
