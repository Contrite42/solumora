[[Ashford]] operates on a principle that most [[Terravelle|Terravellian]] cities do not: it does not insist that you exist officially. A person without a [[Birth Runes|birth rune]] can move through the city without administrative interception. Work can be found. Housing can be rented. Goods can be purchased. The city has built an entire substrate of commerce that does not require proof of registration, and it runs beneath the official record like a second city, equally functional, equally complex, and significantly less stable.

This is not accidental. [[Ashford]]'s administrative apparatus has made a conscious choice — perhaps because the volume of port work exceeds the volume of documented workers, perhaps because the city understands that it needs people who cannot be easily searched for, perhaps because the profit margins on warehoused goods improve when labor carries no documentation. The machinery of the city operates on the assumption that some of its residents are not there, and everyone involved has learned to make this work.

## Who Cannot Be Found

The largest single population of undocumented people in [[Ashford]] consists of those who have no choice in the matter. A person born into an undocumented community — a refugee settlement, an informal labor encampment, the dense neighborhoods where people have drifted and stopped moving — can reach adulthood without ever encountering a mechanism for registration. For many of these people, undocumented status is not a deliberate choice but a simple fact: the [[Birth Runes]] system missed them, and by the time they might have caught up, moving to fix it required access they did not have.

These people tend to occupy the lowest tiers of the informal economy. Dock labor, where the work is dangerous and the wage is thin. The markets where fish are sold still stinking of salt, where the margin between rent and starvation is a matter of which ships come in. The boarding houses where a room is a cot in a room with seven other cots. For these people, the question is never whether they are documented — it is whether there will be work tomorrow, and whether that work will pay enough to keep the current arrangement from collapsing.

A child born into this layer will reach their teens understanding several fundamental truths: that the city recognizes them incidentally, that advancement requires either luck or a mechanism they do not yet understand, and that the stability available to them is the kind that can evaporate with a single bad month. Some of these children will find another path. Many will accept the arrangement and spend their lives in its middle, neither desperate nor optimistic, simply moving from day to day in the dense, worn neighborhoods near the water.

## Who Chose It

A smaller, more volatile population consists of people who came to [[Ashford]] specifically because the city does not ask questions. Deserters from military service. People with debts they could not service in their home settlements. People whose faces would mean danger if they could be attached to names. Political refugees from the northern territory, or from [[The Council of Auralis|Auralis]] itself, or from places so far that the city has never heard of them.

For these people, [[Ashford]] is sanctuary with an expiration date. Some of them are working on establishing a new identity, gathering money for passage to somewhere further, accumulating the resources needed to be someone different. Some of them are hiding from something specific enough that [[Ashford]]'s indifference is precisely what they need, and they will stay until the thing they are hiding from arrives or disappears.

These people do not form a cohesive group. They arrive individually and move through the city with the particular caution of people who understand that exposure is worse than poverty. The boarding house keepers know which buildings house people like this and price accordingly. The bar owners in the waterfront district know which patrons do not want to be remembered. The dock masters know which workers will accept the highest-risk jobs and ask the fewest questions about origin.

A person fleeing something specific can maintain reasonably stable residence in [[Ashford]] for months or years if they are careful, if they work consistently, if they do not draw attention. The more elaborate their past, the more they must simplify their present. These people tend toward quiet occupations — cargo handling, night shifts, work that does not require them to present themselves repeatedly to new people. They tend toward solitude, or toward small, closed networks of others like themselves. They tend toward careful spending, toward never suggesting stability they might not be able to maintain.

## Who Move Between

A third population exists at the boundary: people with [[Birth Runes|birth runes]] who work in the undocumented economy, or undocumented people who occasionally slip into the formal one. A merchant with registration might operate a business that buys from undocumented workers and sells to undocumented buyers, moving goods through the informal economy while maintaining a legitimate front. An undocumented laborer might occasionally find work in a [[Sigils|sigil]]-based operation that requires documentation, using forged papers or borrowed names, then return to the informal district when that employment ends.

These people are the connective tissue between the two cities. They understand both systems enough to navigate both, which is valuable but also precarious. A merchant who moves too much capital through undocumented channels risks audit. An undocumented worker using forged credentials risks exposure if caught. The advantage is adaptability — they have options when one path becomes dangerous. The disadvantage is that they are never quite safe in either world.

These intermediaries tend to be pragmatic to the point of cynicism. They understand how the city actually works and have few illusions about the categories it maintains. Some of them are using undocumented work as a temporary arrangement, building capital for something else. Some have simply realized that the boundary between registered and unregistered is permeable enough to exploit. Some have family on both sides and navigate accordingly.

## The Substance of Daily Life

A person without [[Birth Runes|birth runes]] in [[Ashford]] does not live under siege. The city does not actively hunt for undocumented residents. The experience is not one of hiding, but of existing in the gaps the city's official structure has left open. This is materially different from persecution, and it shapes the daily texture of the experience.

Work is available in the informal markets — dock labor, cargo handling, construction, repair, the jobs that turn over frequently enough that workers do not need to establish identity beyond what they can demonstrate by showing up. The wage is lower than documented work and the terms are worse, but work exists. A person without a rune can reasonably expect to find employment on a daily or weekly basis, can accumulate enough money to cover rent and food. They simply cannot accumulate much beyond that, and they cannot leverage that accumulation into advancement as easily as someone with documentation might.

Housing in the undocumented districts is crowded and deteriorating. Boarding houses where a room is shared with five other people. Apartment buildings where the landlord does not ask for proof of registry and rents accordingly — higher fees for less security, no lease, terms that can change with little notice. Buildings in the quarters near the water where the air is perpetually damp and where the distinction between "waterfront residential" and "flooding risk" is a question of how high the tide comes.

Food is available in the markets, where undocumented currency is acceptable. The [[Watts|Watts]] system, which operates on the principle that all transactions leave a trace on the ledger, includes informal participants — people trading fruit or fish without official registration, operating in the margins of the formal economy. Prices in these markets are often higher than in the official ones, but the goods are available, and the transaction requires no documentation.

Law enforcement in the undocumented districts is minimal and inconsistent. The [[Terravelle|Terravellian]] city guard patrols the official streets but does not conduct systematic sweeps of the informal neighborhoods. When violence occurs, it is often resolved within the community rather than escalated to authorities. When theft happens, there is no formal recourse. The undocumented districts operate under their own mechanisms of order, which tend toward rough justice and social pressure rather than formal consequence.

## Children

A child born into an undocumented family reaches [[Control Tier|tier]] assessment age without the mechanism that would normally process them. Some assessors, working through charitable institutions or religious organizations, reach into the undocumented districts and perform assessments anyway — the [[Ascendant Path]] has a presence even in [[Ashford]]'s lowest quarters. A child assessed at high tier in an undocumented family faces a choice: they can attempt to formalize their existence, which requires resources and navigation of systems, or they can remain in the informal layers despite their potential.

A child assessed at low or average tier in an undocumented family faces a simpler question: what work is available, and how quickly can they contribute to the household's survival. The ambition that might structure a child's life in [[Solhaven]]'s middle terraces — the sense that advancement is possible and worth striving for — does not take the same shape here. What takes its place is often practicality, or fatalism, or a different kind of ambition: the hope that they might climb into the formal economy, or that they might simply avoid the worst outcomes that befell previous generations.

## What [[Ashford]] Does to People

The undocumented districts of [[Ashford]] do not cultivate ambition the way [[Solhaven]] does. They cultivate endurance. A person can spend their entire life in the informal layers — working, eating, sleeping, having children, growing old — without ever establishing formal existence. This is not sustainable as a long-term strategy, but it is viable as a daily practice. The city's indifference, which is the condition of their presence, is also the condition of their invisibility. They can exist in [[Ashford]] precisely because [[Ashford]] refuses to acknowledge existence as the primary dimension on which it evaluates people.

For some, this is liberation. The absence of documentation is also the absence of the [[Terravelle|Terravellian]] administrative apparatus's ability to monitor, constrain, or claim labor. A person without a rune cannot be conscripted. Cannot be compelled to pay official taxes. Exists in a genuine gap between the systems of control. For others, this is simply the condition of survival — not a choice, but a circumstance to be navigated.

The undocumented districts develop their own culture, their own social structures, their own ways of ensuring that basic transactions happen. These neighborhoods are not lawless, but they operate on principles different from the formal city. Reputation matters more than law. Community relationships matter more than official institutions. The solidarity that develops is not sentimental — it is practical, born from the fact that these people cannot rely on the larger systems to protect them and so must protect themselves.

A person and people living undocumented in [[Ashford]] understand certain truths clearly: that the city benefits from their existence while refusing to acknowledge it, that their stability is conditional and temporary, that advancement out of the informal layers is possible but requires either luck, significant resources, or both. They understand that they are necessary and invisible. Most of them accept this and move forward anyway.
