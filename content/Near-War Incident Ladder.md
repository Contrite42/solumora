# Near-War Incident Ladder

The **Near-War Incident Ladder** is the practical escalation pattern used by mid-level operators in [[Auralis]] and [[Terravelle]] to describe how routine border friction becomes strategic crisis.

It is not an official doctrine in either kingdom. It is an observed sequence drawn from records in [[The Three Near-Wars]], current border reporting, and recurring operational behavior in [[Border Trade]] and [[Cross-Border Trade]].

## Why Operators Track It

Policy language treats incidents as isolated events. Operators handling shipments, couriers, and inspection reports see connected causality instead:

1. Minor procedural shift creates local delay.
2. Delay is interpreted as intentional pressure.
3. Reciprocal enforcement appears on the opposite side.
4. Communication confidence degrades.
5. Political actors frame uncertainty as hostile intent.

At that point, routine correction channels stop working quickly enough.

## Ladder Stages

### Stage 1 - Procedural Friction

A customs office changes inspection weighting, paperwork format, or review thresholds. Officials describe this as normal compliance adjustment.

Operational effect: queue growth, contract slippage, and rising exception handling.

### Stage 2 - Selective Burden

Specific cargo classes or carriers begin receiving repeated enhanced review without explicit policy declaration.

Operational effect: merchants re-route, communication traffic concentrates on fewer channels, and local rumors outpace official guidance.

### Stage 3 - Incident Signaling

A visible event occurs (seizure, courier hold, convoy turn-back, delayed release) and is publicized as evidence of counterpart bad faith.

Operational effect: both sides escalate monitoring and reporting cadence from periodic to continuous.

### Stage 4 - Reciprocal Hardening

Counterpart authorities respond with symmetrical or slightly higher friction, usually framed as defensive parity.

Operational effect: baseline delays become structural. System throughput declines even when no formal trade suspension exists.

### Stage 5 - Information Fracture

Merchants and administrators stop trusting whether delays are procedural or strategic. Intelligence units absorb customs signals into threat models.

Operational effect: precautionary behavior multiplies, false positives increase, and de-escalation proposals are treated as traps.

### Stage 6 - Crisis Threshold

A high-visibility trigger appears (detained officials, convoy confrontation, major seizure claim). Political leadership is forced into public position-taking.

Operational effect: administrative flexibility collapses. Crisis management replaces normal trade administration.

## Core Drivers

The ladder activates when three conditions overlap:

- **Capacity mismatch:** Enforcement volume rises while specialist staff declines.
- **Narrative pressure:** Public messaging rewards hardline posture over correction.
- **Ambiguous authority:** Local offices can escalate faster than central policy can clarify intent.

## Current Positioning

Cross-border operators increasingly assess current conditions as late Stage 3 to early Stage 4:

- repeated inspection surges in key crossings
- more frequent incident memoranda
- reduced confidence in shared documentation norms

No formal near-war declaration exists. The ladder model exists because waiting for formal declaration means intervention windows have already passed.

## Operational Use

The ladder is used for practical triage:

- prioritize de-escalation channels before Stage 5
- separate reversible procedural disputes from symbolic incidents
- identify where neutral technical fixes can still work

The model does not prevent crisis by itself. It helps practitioners identify when ordinary corrections are still possible and when political intervention becomes unavoidable.
