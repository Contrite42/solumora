# Material Refinement

Material refinement transforms raw extracted resources into usable products through smelting, processing, treatment, and manufacturing operations. Refinement represents critical value-addition step converting low-value ore, timber, and raw materials into high-value metals, dimensional lumber, and specialized components. The sector operates across both kingdoms with geographic concentration near resource-extraction sites or major market centers depending on transport economics and [[Flux]] infrastructure requirements.

Contemporary war preparation accelerates refinement demand beyond sustainable capacity while simultaneously removing skilled practitioners through military conscription. Result: quality degradation, environmental impact, and supply-chain bottlenecks constraining military production despite increased raw-resource extraction.

## Smelting and Metalwork Refining

**Iron Smelting:** Converts iron ore (35-40% metal content) into usable iron ingots through high-temperature reduction. Standard operation requires:

- Ore preparation: crushing and sorting to uniform size
- Furnace loading: layered ore, fuel (charcoal or coal), and flux agents
- Sustained high heat (1200-1400 degrees) melting metal, separating slag
- Casting molten iron into ingot molds
- Cooling and finishing

Output efficiency: 100 kilograms ore yields 35-40 kilograms finished iron ingots. Processing time: 8-12 hours per batch. Fuel consumption: 2-3 tons charcoal per ton iron produced.

**Flux-Enhanced Smelting:** [[Control Tier|T2-T3]] practitioners maintain precise temperature control through Flux-driven heat management, improving metal quality and reducing fuel consumption 20-30%. Capital cost: 3000-5000 [[Watts]] for Flux-enhancement infrastructure (inscribed furnace linings, heat-regulation sigils, precision casting equipment). Limited to larger operations with institutional or merchant-house backing.

**Specialty Metal Processing:** Copper, tin, lead, and alloy production. Similar process adaptations for each metal's specific temperature and chemical requirements. Bronze (copper-tin alloy) production requires precise ratio control — Flux-based practitioners manage composition measurement improving final product consistency.

**Contemporary Challenges:** Charcoal scarcity (deforestation from over-logging reduces fuel availability) increasing smelting costs. [[Control Tier|T2-T3]] practitioners conscripted for military service — remaining refineries operate with reduced Flux-enhancement capacity or hire less-experienced practitioners at premium wages. Quality degradation evident: increased slag content, inconsistent ingot composition, higher defect rates.

## Timber Processing and Treatment

**Sawmill Operations:** Convert raw logs into dimensional lumber through precision cutting. Operation sequence:

- Log debarking and initial assessment
- Primary sawing producing rough-dimensioned boards
- Secondary processing (planning, edge-squaring, final dimensioning)
- Drying/seasoning (air-dried 6-12 months or kiln-dried 2-4 weeks)
- Final grading and bundling for transport

Output: Raw log (1 cubic meter) yields approximately 0.6-0.7 cubic meters finished lumber (remainder lost to bark, sawdust, defects, dimensional waste).

**Flux-Enhanced Precision:** [[Control Tier|T1-T2]] practitioners manage blade temperature and cutting-force regulation, reducing waste and improving surface finish quality. Cost: 200-400 Watts per enhanced saw blade, replaced annually with heavy use.

**Wood Treatment:** Preservation treatments extending timber durability in construction applications. Traditional: tar coating, salt treatment. Advanced: Flux-based preservation embedding decay-resistance into wood structure. Treatment cost: 5-10 Watts per cubic meter basic treatment, 25-40 Watts per cubic meter Flux-enhanced preservation.

**Contemporary Challenges:** Raw log supply constraints (logging output cannot meet mill capacity). Mills operate 50-70% capacity utilization despite high demand. Seasoning time reduced (rushing unseasoned lumber to market produces inferior products but meets immediate military construction timelines). Skilled sawyers conscripted — replacement workers produce higher waste rates and lower-quality output.

## Flux-Reactive Material Processing

Specialized processing converting raw [[Mining Operations|Flux-reactive minerals]] into usable components for [[Grimoires|grimoire]] production and high-tier applications. Extremely hazardous work requiring [[Control Tier|T3-T4]] supervision.

**Crystalline Refinement:** Raw Flux-responsive crystals require precision cutting, polishing, and stabilization. Material volatility risk: uncontrolled Flux discharge during processing produces practitioner injury, equipment damage, or material destruction. Processing success rate: 60-75% (remaining material too unstable or damaged for utilization).

**Powder Production:** Some grimoire applications require powdered Flux-reactive material. Grinding process releases substantial waste heat and occasional spontaneous discharge events. Standard safety protocol: remote grinding equipment, protective barriers, [[Control Tier|T3+]] monitoring. Processing cost: 40-60 Watts per kilogram finished powder (excluding raw material cost).

**Stabilization Treatment:** Flux-responsive materials stabilized through controlled exposure reducing volatility while preserving reactive properties. Technique requires sustained [[Control Tier|T4]] Flux manipulation over 8-12 hour processing windows. Success improves material shelf-life from weeks (unstable) to years (stabilized). Treatment cost: 80-120 Watts per kilogram.

**Contemporary Challenges:** Raw material supply increasing from accelerated [[Mining Operations|mining]] but processing capacity constrained by practitioner availability. [[Control Tier|T3-T4]] practitioners in high demand across multiple sectors — refineries compete for limited specialist pool driving wages upward (60-90 Watts weekly current rates versus historical 45-65 Watts). Some operations accept higher volatility risk operating with [[Control Tier|T2]] practitioners where [[Control Tier|T3+]] standard previously required — casualty incidents increasing accordingly.

## Geographic Distribution and Specialization

**[[Auralis]] Refining Centers:**

- [[Solhaven]] metalworking district: Multiple iron smelters, specialty metal processing, centralized location servicing [[Keln Ridge Mines]] output and military procurement
- Coastal shipbuilding support: Sawmills and metal fabrication near [[Keln Harbor]] supporting maritime construction
- [[Emberfall]] specialty processing: Flux-reactive material refinement supporting research operations
- Distributed rural operations: Small-scale smelters and sawmills serving local market demand

**[[Terravelle]] Refining Centers:**

- [[Valdenmoor]] industrial quarter: Concentrated metalwork and timber processing serving northern kingdom demand
- [[Maren Timber Collective]]: Integrated sawmill operations within logging territory (cost savings from eliminating raw-log transport)
- [[Ashford]] border processing: Mixed operations serving cross-kingdom trade
- Guild workshop networks: Distributed small-scale refineries operated by craftspeople within guild structures

**Specialization Rationale:** Heavy materials (ore, rough timber) expensive to transport long distances. Refineries locate near extraction sites when possible (reducing transport cost of heavy raw materials; finished products lighter and more economically transported) OR near major markets when raw-material transport via water enables economical supply.

## Equipment Infrastructure

**Smelting Furnaces:** Stone-built structures with ceramic linings withstanding sustained high temperatures. Base construction cost: 1500-2500 Watts depending on capacity. Flux-enhancement adds 2000-3000 Watts. Service life: 8-15 years before lining degradation requires rebuilding.

**Sawmill Equipment:** Water-powered (if river-adjacent) or animal-powered saw mechanisms. Initial investment: 800-1200 Watts basic sawmill, 2000-3000 Watts Flux-enhanced precision equipment. Maintenance cost: 150-250 Watts annually (blade replacement, mechanism repair, building maintenance).

**Flux-Processing Containment:** Specialized equipment managing volatile materials. Shielded work chambers, remote manipulation tools, safety barriers. Cost: 3000-5000 Watts initial installation. Maintenance expensive due to damage from processing accidents (100-300 Watts quarterly depending on incident frequency).

## Labor and Skill Requirements

**Smelter Operators:** Require sustained physical endurance (managing furnace operations), technical knowledge (temperature assessment, timing precision), and hazard awareness (molten metal handling). Training: 2-4 years apprenticeship. [[Control Tier]]: primarily T0-T2 for basic operations, T2-T3 for Flux-enhanced processes. Wages: 18-28 Watts weekly base operations, 40-60 Watts weekly Flux-enhanced roles.

**Sawmill Workers:** Physical strength, precision cutting skill, equipment maintenance capability. Training: 1-3 years apprenticeship. [[Control Tier]]: T0-T2. Wages: 15-25 Watts weekly.

**Flux-Material Processors:** Advanced technical knowledge, sustained [[Control Tier|T3-T4]] capacity, hazard-response training. Training: 5-8 years including theory, supervised practice, and emergency protocols. Extremely limited practitioner pool (estimated 40-60 qualified specialists across both kingdoms). Wages: 60-90 Watts weekly, higher for institutional contracts.

**Refinery Managers:** Coordinate operations, supply procurement, quality control, worker supervision, and merchant coordination. Combination business and technical skills. [[Control Tier]]: typically T2-T3. Compensation: 50-80 Watts weekly depending on operation scale.

## Value Chain Economics

**Value Multiplication Through Refinement:**

- Iron ore (3 Watts/kilogram raw) → Iron ingots (18 Watts/kilogram refined) → Finished tools/weapons (60-150 Watts per item)
- Raw logs (12 Watts/cubic meter) → Dimensional lumber (40 Watts/cubic meter) → Construction/furniture (variable, 100-500+ Watts finished products)
- Flux-reactive raw material (80 Watts/kilogram) → Processed/stabilized (200 Watts/kilogram) → Grimoire components (500-2000+ Watts per application)

Refinement captures substantial value-addition justifying capital investment and specialist labor costs. However, profit margins compress under wartime conditions: raw material costs rising (scarcity), labor costs increasing (practitioner shortage), output prices suppressed (institutional requisition pricing below market).

## Environmental and Safety Consequences

**Smelting Waste:** Slag (metal-extraction byproduct) accumulates at refinery sites. Toxic compounds leach into groundwater affecting downstream water quality. Proper waste management expensive — wartime priorities defer environmental considerations producing long-term contamination.

**Air Pollution:** Furnace emissions produce smoke, particulates, and toxic gasses. Workers experience respiratory illness; nearby settlements report health impacts. No regulatory constraints limit emissions under current conditions.

**Processing Accidents:** Molten metal spills, furnace failures, Flux-material volatility events produce regular injury and occasional fatalities. Documented casualty rates: 6-8 deaths per 1000 refinery workers annually under accelerated schedules versus historical 3-4 per 1000.

**Noise and Dust:** Sawmill and crushing operations produce sustained noise exposure (hearing damage) and airborne dust (respiratory illness). Workers develop chronic health conditions over years of exposure. No compensation systems exist for occupational illness — workers absorb long-term health costs personally.

## Contemporary Institutional Conflicts

**Capacity Versus Demand Mismatch:** Raw extraction accelerated but refinement capacity constrained by practitioner availability and equipment limits. Result: raw-material stockpiling at extraction sites (ore piles, unseasoned timber) waiting for processing capacity. Bottleneck creates inefficiency reducing effective production despite increased extraction effort.

**Quality Degradation Acceptance:** Military procurement accepts lower-quality refined products rather than delaying supply. Iron ingots with higher inclusion rates, unseasoned lumber, incompletely-stabilized Flux materials entering supply chains where previously would be rejected. Downstream consequences: equipment failure rates increasing, construction defects, grimoire production difficulties.

**Institutional Price Suppression:** [[The Council of Auralis|Council]] requisitions mandate below-market pricing for refined products serving military applications. Refineries lose profitability forcing either operation at loss (sustained by expectation of post-war price normalization) or closure (transferring capacity to institutional-controlled operations capturing remaining market).

**Environmental Accountability Deferred:** Long-term environmental consequences from accelerated refinement (groundwater contamination, air pollution, waste accumulation) evident but unaddressed. Post-war remediation costs will substantially exceed short-term cost savings from ignoring proper waste management. Future generations absorb costs current operations generate.
