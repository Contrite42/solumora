Ren Valdst manages the southern holdings of [[Keld Basin]], a grain-farming community in the [[Maren Valley]]. She is 52 years old, [[Control Tier|Tier 1]], and has held this role for eighteen years.

## The Position

Managing holdings in Keld Basin means coordinating work across four separate family farms — approximately 320 people — to maintain the collective harvest schedule that feeds into [[Valdenmoor]]'s markets. Ren does not own the land, does not direct individual holdings' planting decisions, and has no formal authority to compel cooperation. What she has is trust, built over two decades of making decisions that distributed risk and reward fairly.

She tracks soil conditions, weather patterns, pest pressure, and market signals. When the southern holdings need to shift harvest timing, adjust pest-control coordination, or respond to supply-chain disruptions on the [[Maren Freight Chain]], Ren makes the call that everyone follows because they have watched her be right often enough and transparent when she was wrong.

## The Buyer Problem

Two seasons ago a Valdenmoor buyer offered the southern holdings a significant premium to deliver grain two weeks ahead of the basin's standard cycle. Ren declined. The premium was real but the cost was structural — early delivery would have required external seasonal labor and would have broken the basin's internal work-share rhythm.

This season the buyer returned with a higher offer. Ren declined again for the same reasons. But three of the smaller holdings have begun asking whether the basin's collective system still serves them or simply prevents them from taking opportunities.

Ren has not changed her position. The collective delivery system exists because grain farming in Keld Basin is a timing problem: plant too early and seedlings rot in wet soil, harvest too late and autumn rain turns fields to mud. The coordination that keeps 800 people aligned to narrow weather windows is the same coordination that keeps the supply line to Valdenmoor stable.

Breaking the cycle for one season's premium would not destroy the system. But it would establish that individual gain can override collective agreements, which is a precedent that changes what coordination means.

## The Calculation

Ren knows exactly what the buyer is offering and what she is refusing. She also knows that if three holdings decide to break from the collective schedule, she cannot stop them. The question is not whether she is right. The question is whether being right will matter if enough people decide the old system no longer fits their circumstances.

She has called a basin-wide meeting for next month. She will present the case for maintaining collective coordination. She will listen to the counter-arguments. And if the basin decides to change the system, she will help them do it in a way that does not collapse the entire structure.

That is her job.
