Scrivener Regulation is the set of rules, standards, and enforcement mechanisms that govern [[Grimoires|grimoire]] copying work within the [[Guild Scrivener Network]] and, to a lesser extent, among [[Independent Scrivener|independent practitioners]]. Regulation exists to maintain quality, reduce fraud, and protect both scriveners and buyers from disputes. It operates through guild oversight in [[Terravelle]] and a mix of guild and governmental oversight in [[Auralis]].

## Guild Standards

The [[Guild Scrivener Network]] enforces minimum quality benchmarks for all member scriveners. A grimoire copy must reproduce [[Sigils]] with sufficient precision that the spell functions as intended when cast by a practitioner of appropriate [[Control Tier]]. Copies are inspected before release — either through random sampling, buyer complaint investigation, or systematic review for higher-tier work.

Inspection focuses on sigil geometry, ink consistency, and page durability. A poorly drawn sigil, ink that fades too quickly, or binding that fails after minimal use all constitute quality failures. Scriveners found producing substandard work face escalating penalties: warnings for first offenses, fines for repeated issues, and suspension or expulsion for persistent negligence or fraud.

Guild members must also maintain documented lineage for their source texts. When producing a copy, the scrivener records which authenticated source they worked from, when, and under what conditions. This creates an audit trail for [[Grimoire Authentication]] disputes and helps trace errors back to specific source texts if patterns emerge.

## Tier Certification

Scriveners are certified by tier based on demonstrated capability. A T2 practitioner can reliably copy [[Common Grimoire]] spells but may struggle with [[Uncommon Grimoire|Uncommon]] or higher-tier work. Guild certification tests a scrivener's ability to reproduce sigils at increasing complexity levels, ensuring they don't accept commissions beyond their skill.

Certification is renewed periodically (commonly every 3-5 years) and can be revoked if a scrivener's work quality declines. Higher-tier certifications require additional fees, ongoing professional development, and periodic re-testing. This creates a natural bottleneck: most guild scriveners hold T1-T2 or T1-T3 certifications and focus on [[Common Grimoire]] production where demand is highest.

Certification violations — accepting a commission for a grimoire the scrivener isn't certified to copy — result in immediate investigation. If the work is defective, the scrivener faces suspension and potential liability for buyer damages. Even if the work is acceptable, the violation itself carries fines.

## Pricing Regulation

The [[Guild Scrivener Network]] publishes recommended pricing schedules, though they're not strictly binding. Pricing is structured per-page with tier multipliers, adjusted regionally based on local demand and cost of materials. Guild members are expected to work within these ranges — charging significantly below the schedule undercuts other scriveners and can indicate quality shortcuts; charging significantly above suggests price gouging.

Pricing disputes are handled through guild arbitration. If a buyer claims a scrivener overcharged, the guild reviews the contract, compares it to published schedules, and may order partial refunds if the pricing is unjustified. If a scrivener claims a buyer refused agreed payment, the guild investigates and can pursue collection on the scrivener's behalf.

## Cross-Border Regulation

[[Terravelle]] and [[Auralis]] maintain separate guild systems with coordination agreements but independent enforcement. A scrivener in good standing with [[Terravelle]]'s guild network isn't automatically recognized in [[Auralis]] and vice versa. Cross-border commissions require mutual recognition agreements, which exist but don't cover all edge cases.

This creates regulatory gaps. A scrivener expelled from [[Terravelle]]'s guild for quality violations might relocate to [[Auralis]] and continue working through independent channels or seek [[Auralis]] guild membership without full disclosure of their history. Information sharing between kingdoms is slow and incomplete.

## Independent Scrivener Regulation

[[Independent Scrivener|Independent scriveners]] operate outside guild regulation but are still subject to general fraud and contract law in both kingdoms. A buyer purchasing a defective grimoire from an independent can pursue legal action, but enforcement is inconsistent and often depends on the buyer's resources and persistence.

Some independent scriveners form informal self-regulation networks — groups of practitioners who vouch for each other's work quality and share reputation information. These networks aren't legally binding but provide social enforcement: a scrivener producing poor work gets excluded from the network and loses access to referrals.

## Governmental Oversight

In [[Terravelle]], the government largely defers to guild self-regulation. [[Trade Guilds]] handle scrivener standards, and the government intervenes only in cases involving [[Grimoire Rights and Restrictions|restricted texts]] or major fraud.

In [[Auralis]], the [[The Council of Auralis|Council of Auralis]] exercises more direct oversight. The Council maintains an approved scrivener registry separate from guild membership, and higher-tier copying work requires Council approval regardless of guild certification. This adds bureaucratic overhead but gives [[Auralis]] more control over what grimoires circulate and who produces them.

## Enforcement Challenges

Regulation is hardest to enforce for lower-tier work produced in high volume. A scrivener copying dozens of [[Common Grimoire]] pages per week can't have every copy inspected, so enforcement relies on sampling and buyer complaints. Most defects go unnoticed unless a spell fails obviously or a buyer tests systematically.

[[Black Market Grimoire Circulation]] operates entirely outside regulation, and enforcement is reactive: authorities pursue black market scriveners only when their work causes visible harm or a buyer reports fraud. This makes black market scrivening persistently viable despite legal and guild prohibitions.
