Grimoire fraud is the deliberate misrepresentation of a copy's quality, tier, or origin. It is distinct from honest copying error — a [[Grimoire Copyists|Scribe]] who makes an unintentional transcription mistake is making an error; a Scribe who knowingly sells a degraded copy as certified work is committing fraud. The distinction is legally and reputationally significant in [[Terravelle]] and practically significant everywhere.

## Common Fraud Types

**Mis-tier presentation**: selling an [[Uncommon Grimoire|Uncommon]]-tier spell labeled as [[Common Grimoire|Common]]-tier. A buyer who does not meet the actual [[Control Tier]] requirement will fail to activate the spell and may not understand why. The copy is useless to them, but they have paid for it. This is the most common grimoire fraud by volume — the markup opportunity is real, and the buyer often assumes the failure is their own deficiency rather than investigating the source.

**Degraded master sourcing**: producing copies from a damaged, incomplete, or unauthorized master while presenting them as certified copies from an authenticated source. The resulting spells may activate partially, misfire, or fail without warning. A [[Common Grimoire#Hearthlight|Hearthlight]] copy from a degraded master might produce unstable light that flickers irregularly; a [[Common Grimoire#Forcepulse|Forcepulse]] copy might discharge at reduced power or in an incorrect direction. Neither failure necessarily signals fraud to an inexperienced user.

**False originator attribution**: attaching a well-known practitioner's name to copies they did not produce, as a quality signal. Particularly common in Auralis where Scholar-network vouching is the primary quality mechanism. A name that carries credibility can be forged more easily than a guild certification mark.

**Incomplete transcription**: providing a partial sigil — enough of the geometry to look complete, not enough to activate correctly. Most often seen in black market [[Rare Grimoire|Rare]]-tier or [[Uncommon Grimoire|Uncommon]]-tier copies, where buyers cannot easily verify completeness and where the seller benefits from the buyer's inability to distinguish their own activation failure from the copy's defect.

**Tier-adjacent substitution**: substituting a lower-tier variant of a spell for a more expensive version. Selling a [[Common Grimoire#Warmbreath|Warmbreath]] copy as a [[Uncommon Grimoire#Heatshield|Heatshield]] copy, for instance — the spells look similar in effect under casual examination, but [[Uncommon Grimoire#Heatshield|Heatshield]] provides significantly better sustained thermal protection. A buyer who does not know both spells well enough to compare may not realize what they received.

## Detection

Most grimoire fraud is discovered by practitioners who are experienced enough to recognize activation anomalies. A spell that activates strangely, requires more effort than expected, or produces wrong-shaped effects is worth reinspecting. The inspection is not always conclusive — activation problems can also arise from the caster's own Flux management rather than the copy quality — but repeated anomalies from the same source is a reliable indicator.

In [[Terravelle]], guild certification authentication can be verified against the registry ledger. A certification mark on a grimoire that has no corresponding registry entry is evidence of fraud. This is the guild's primary enforcement leverage and why forging the mark rather than the registration is the higher-risk fraud.

In [[Auralis]], because the vouching system has no equivalent public ledger, false attribution is harder to detect and more prevalent. Practitioners who buy primarily through institutional networks are better protected; those buying from independent sellers in markets have limited recourse.

## Consequences

Fraud in the Common grimoire market is addressed through guild enforcement in Terravelle — a Scribe found selling fraudulent copies loses certification and is barred from guild channels. The economic consequence is significant in a market where certification drives price and institutional access.

In Auralis, consequences for fraud are reputational rather than institutional, which means they are enforced unevenly. A Scholar-network member who defrauds a peer will face social and professional consequences. A market seller with no network affiliation faces no such check.
