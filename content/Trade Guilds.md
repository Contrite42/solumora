The guild system is [[Terravelle]]'s primary institutional structure below the level of the crown. Guilds organize trades, set standards, train practitioners, certify competence, arbitrate disputes, and provide the social infrastructure that formal government does not reach. In [[Valdenmoor]] and the other major cities, the guilds function as the effective government of working life. The formal administrative apparatus handles taxation, external relations, and legal adjudication. The guilds handle everything else.

The guild system is not unique to Terravelle — [[Auralis]] has analogous certification structures — but Terravelle's version is older, more decentralized, and considerably less tied to [[Control Tier|tier hierarchy]] in its organization.

## How Certification Works

Guild certification follows a standard three-stage progression: apprenticeship, journeyman work, and certification assessment. The stages vary in length by trade and in difficulty by the tier grade being sought.

Apprenticeship is typically two to four years, during which the practitioner trains under a certified member and performs documented practice work. The documentation requirement is not bureaucratic formality — it creates the record that the certification assessor reviews. Apprentices in technical trades are expected to develop a practice log detailed enough to demonstrate the range of work they have performed, under what conditions, and with what results.

Journeyman status is granted after apprenticeship by the training guild member. At journeyman level, a practitioner can accept work independently but cannot certify or train others. Journeyman work typically lasts one to three years, accumulating the independent practice record required for certification assessment.

The certification assessment is conducted by a guild-appointed assessor, usually a senior certified member in the relevant trade. The assessor reviews documentation and observes live work across several standard test conditions. For trades with [[Control Tier|tier-graded]] certifications — most [[Flux]]-integrated trades — the assessment is also where the tier grade is formally established for that specific application.

## Tier Grades and Certification

Most trades have multiple certification grades tied to [[Control Tier]] capability. The grades are trade-specific rather than universal: a T3 certification in structural sigil application means something different from a T3 certification in transport flux, and neither certification transfers to the other trade.

Common tier grade structure for Flux-integrated trades:
- **T1 certification**: Entry-level, basic applications, limited scope. Most residential and consumer-facing work falls here. High-volume, lower-complexity operations.
- **T2 certification**: Standard working grade. Where most commercial practice operates. Broad scope with documented conditions.
- **T3 certification**: Skilled practitioner grade. Indicates capability that goes beyond the standard workflow. Sought after for complex projects, specialist work, supervision roles.
- **T4 certification**: Expert grade. Uncommon. Required for civil works, advanced structural applications, certain medical practices, high-stakes cargo transport. The certification assessment for T4 includes requirements — like the civil works documentation standard — that many technically capable candidates fail on grounds that have nothing to do with their casting ability.

The guild system does not certify T5 and above as a rule. Practitioners at that tier are considered to have capabilities that exceed the guild's ability to set meaningful standards for, and their work is governed by contract, reputation, and the informal structures that operate at that level rather than by guild certification.

## The Documentation Standard

Guild documentation requirements are not optional conditions on certification — they are themselves part of what is being certified. A practitioner who can perform the work but cannot maintain accurate field records during the work has not demonstrated the full competency the certification covers.

This standard exists for reasons that the guild system documents openly: in trades where errors cause structural failures, cargo losses, or health consequences, the field record is frequently the only way to reconstruct what went wrong. A bridge that fails three years after construction, with no documentation of what was applied where and in what sequence, cannot be investigated, learned from, or corrected. The guild's interest in documentation is not administrative. It is institutional memory about what happens when it is absent.

Several certification grades, particularly in civil works, require that documentation be maintained *concurrent* with casting — meaning the record is created in real time during the work, not reconstructed afterward. Many candidates who are technically capable of the casting fail this standard because maintaining both simultaneously is its own skill, and one that requires specific practice. The guild review committees that set these standards have heard the argument that the requirement is excessive. They are familiar with the documented bridge failures that preceded the standard.

## Social Function

Guild membership in [[Terravelle]] carries weight beyond trade certification. The guilds provide dispute resolution, wage standards, seasonal assistance funds, apprenticeship placement, housing recommendations, and the informal social infrastructure that connects practitioners within a trade. The seasonal gatherings, the celebrations of certifications, the dinners marking retirements — these happen in guild halls and through guild social networks.

The [[Covenant of Measure]] uses guild halls as ceremonial space in [[Valdenmoor]] and other cities, which is not accidental. The guild structure and the Covenant's emphasis on proportional use of capacity share an underlying assumption: that capability is measured by what it produces in practice, and that the purpose of skill is the quality of the work, not the status of the practitioner.

A guild membership in Valdenmoor, once established, is stable in a way that most Terravellian employment is not. The social identity of "guild member in this trade" persists through lean seasons and difficult clients and the normal range of professional failures. People who have it tend to defend it accordingly.

## Guild Assessors

The assessors who conduct certification examinations are senior certified members formally appointed to the assessment role by the guild. The role requires additional guild certification — in Valdenmoor, the guild has developed a specific certification for assessors that the assessment chapter administers, the logic being that the quality of the certification system depends on the quality of the people running it.

Assessors have discretionary authority in how they conduct examinations but not in what standards they apply — the standards are set by guild review committees and revised periodically. An assessor who believes a standard is inadequate or outdated can raise the concern with the review committee, who will note it. Whether the committee acts is a separate question.
