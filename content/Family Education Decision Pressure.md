Education choices in [[Solumora]] are household portfolio decisions made under uncertainty, not abstract merit contests.

Families are usually choosing between several imperfect options:

- immediate wage contribution;
- delayed earnings through apprenticeship;
- high-risk mobility via institutional tracks;
- mixed-track hedging across siblings.

The decision is less "what is best" and more "what failure can we survive."

## Decision Variables

Most families evaluate:

- current debt and rent pressure;
- expected health stability of adult earners;
- transport and material costs for training tracks;
- social reliability of sponsors and institutions;
- likelihood of child completion versus dropout.

A technically superior track is often rejected when household volatility is high.

## Class Pressure

Class pressure appears as risk tolerance asymmetry.

- wealthy households can absorb delayed returns and failed attempts;
- working households need predictable short-cycle income;
- poor households cannot survive multi-year uncertainty without external support.

This is why "open access" systems still produce stratified outcomes.

## Sibling Strategy

Many households diversify education risk across children.

Common pattern:

- one child enters immediate labor;
- one child attempts apprenticeship;
- one child pursues institutional track if tier and sponsorship align.

This strategy is rational but can create long-term inequality inside the same family.

## Kingdom Differences

In [[Terravelle]], family strategy is shaped by guild access and reputation networks. In [[Auralis]], it is shaped by institutional legibility and district hierarchy.

The stress is similar in both kingdoms: one failed placement can destabilize the entire household budget.

## Emotional Cost

Families frame choices as practical, but emotional strain is persistent:

- guilt when a child leaves training for wages;
- conflict when higher-tier children are pushed into tracks they dislike;
- resentment when siblings carry unequal economic burdens.

These dynamics are rarely documented in institutional reports and often drive dropout more than formal curriculum difficulty.

## Main Bottleneck

The decisive bottleneck is not talent. It is household resilience over time.

Where resilience is thin, education pipelines narrow regardless of policy intent.
