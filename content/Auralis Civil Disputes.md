# Auralis Civil Disputes

Civil disputes in [[Auralis]]—contract breaches, debt collection, property conflicts, inheritance disputes—are resolved through [[Auralis Civic Courts]] operating at District, Regional, and High Court levels. The formal court system provides standardized procedures, documented precedent, and institutional enforcement, making it more predictable than neighborhood mediation but slower and more expensive.

## Types of Civil Disputes

**Debt Collection.** Creditor sues debtor for non-payment of personal loan, business debt, or wage-advance. Creditor must prove: debt was incurred, creditor provided funds/goods, debtor was obligated to repay, repayment is overdue. Debtor defends by claiming debt was forgiven verbally, was contingent on conditions unmet, or was already partially paid.

**Contract Disputes.** Buyer and seller disagree about performance obligations. Seller contracts to deliver 100 bolts cloth by specific date; delivers late. Buyer rejects goods claiming damage from delayed delivery. Dispute centers on whether late delivery justified rejection and whether seller owes damages.

**Property Disputes.** Neighbors dispute property boundaries, building encroachment, or usage rights. Plaintiff claims defendant's fence encroaches on property and demands removal. Defendant claims fence location reflected established boundary. Court must examine property records or obtain boundary survey.

**Inheritance and Estate Distribution.** Heirs dispute deceased's will distribution or whether valid will existed. Potential claims: will is invalid (defendant forged signature), testator lacked capacity (was mentally ill), will did not reflect testator's intent, other heirs were omitted or entitled to greater share.

**Partnership Dissolution and Business Disputes.** Partners dissolve business and disagree on asset division. One partner claims other partner misappropriated business funds prior to dissolution. Court must partition assets, calculate each party's contribution, and determine whether misappropriation occurred.

**Landlord-Tenant Disputes.** Landlord allegedly failed to maintain livable conditions; tenant refused rent payment. Landlord sues recovery unpaid rent. Tenant counterclaims housing conditions were substandard, justifying rent withholding. Court must assess whether conditions were actually substandard and whether tenant's response was proportionate.

## Pre-Trial Process

**Filing and Service.** Plaintiff files complaint with District Court clerk, paying filing fee (8-15 Watts depending on claim amount). Court serves defendant with summons and complaint. Defendant has 3-4 weeks to respond.

**Defendant's Response.** Defendant files answer admitting or denying allegations, raising legal defense, and potentially counterclaiming. Answer must directly address each complaint allegation.

**Discovery.** Both parties exchange evidence and information. Procedures include: document requests (compelling production of relevant documents), interrogatories (written questions requiring detailed answers), depositions (out-of-court testimony under oath). Discovery is more formal and extensive than in Terravelle, requiring full disclosure of evidence both sides possess.

**Pre-Trial Motion.** Judge can dismiss case if complaint fails to state legal claim even if facts are true (complaint addresses non-legal matter or asks for impossible remedy). Judge can grant summary judgment if facts are undisputed and law clearly favors one party (no trial needed).

## Trial Procedure

**Trial Setup.** Judge is sole fact-finder (no jury). Both parties have opportunity to present opening statements describing their case, question (direct examination) and challenge (cross-examination) witnesses, present documentary evidence, and give closing arguments.

**Evidence Standards.** Auralis courts are stricter about evidence than Terravelle courts:
- Hearsay (statements made out-of-court): generally inadmissible unless exception applies
- Character evidence: generally inadmissible unless specifically relevant
- Relevance: evidence must directly address disputed facts
- Authentication: documentary evidence must be verified as valid prior to admission

Stricter standards mean some truthful evidence cannot be presented to judge, potentially producing unfair outcomes. But overall consistency and clarity is increased because both parties know what evidence will be accepted.

**Testimony and Credibility.** Witnesses testify under oath, are subject to cross-examination, and can be impeached (credibility challenged if prior dishonesty demonstrated). Judges assess witness credibility based on: plausibility of account, consistency with other testimony, witness demeanor, incentive to lie.

**Judgment.** After both parties present cases, judge deliberates privately and issues written judgment. Judgment includes: factual findings (what judge decided was true), legal conclusion (how law applied to facts), and remedy (what plaintiff recovers or what defendant must do).

## Remedies in Civil Court

**Monetary Damages.** Most common remedy. Amounts include:
- **Compensatory damages:** actual value of loss plaintiff suffered (cost of goods, repair costs, lost income)
- **Consequential damages:** foreseeable losses flowing from breach (example: failure to deliver goods prevented resale, losing profit plaintiff would have made)
- **Punitive damages:** extraordinary damages when defendant acted intentionally or recklessly (rare in civil cases, used when defendant's misconduct was especially egregious)

**Specific Performance.** Court orders defendant to perform contractual obligation rather than pay damages. Used when damages cannot fully compensate plaintiff. Example: defendant contracts to sell unique property; court orders defendant to transfer property rather than pay damages.

**Injunction.** Court orders defendant to stop doing something (cease trespass, stop using defendant's trademark) or start doing something (maintain property, pay support payments). Violations of injunction can trigger contempt charges.

**Rescission.** Contract is canceled and parties returned to pre-contract status. Used for fraud (defendant deceived plaintiff about contract terms) or unconscionable contracts (terms were extremely unfair to one party).

## Appeal Process

Losing party can appeal within 30 days. Appeals are expensive (20-50 Watts in filing and other fees) and lengthy (2-4 months from appeal filing to decision). Appellate judges review whether trial judge's legal reasoning was correct and whether trial procedure was fair. Appellate courts do not revisit factual findings or witness credibility assessments—those are trial judge's role.

**Appellate Scope.** Appellate judges can overturn judgment if:
- Legal error occurred (judge applied law incorrectly)
- Procedural error was committed (evidence was wrongly admitted/excluded, affecting outcome)
- Judgment is clearly against weight of evidence (judge's factual findings were clearly wrong)

**Appeal Likelihood.** Appeals are rare for disputes under 100 Watts because appeal costs often exceed appeal value. Appeals are common for larger disputes (500+ Watts) where appellate reversal changes outcome by large sum. Appeals can take 4-6 months, extending total litigation timeline to 8-12 months or more.

## Enforcement of Judgments

Once judgment is obtained, plaintiff faces enforcement challenge:

**Voluntary Compliance.** Best case: defendant pays judgment without court enforcement machinery. Many defendants pay quickly to avoid enforcement costs.

**Execution of Judgment.** If defendant does not pay voluntarily, plaintiff files execution petition requesting court enforce judgment. Court officers attempt:
- **Wage Garnishment.** Defendant's employer is ordered to withhold percentage of wages toward judgment
- **Property Seizure and Sale.** Court seizes defendant's property and sells it at auction to raise funds
- **Bank Account Freezes.** Court prevents defendant from accessing bank accounts until judgment satisfied
- **Judgment Lien.** Claim is placed on defendant's real property; property cannot be sold or mortgaged without satisfying judgment

**Problems.** Enforcement faces practical obstacles:
- Defendant may have no attachable assets (unsalaried, owns no property, has no bank account)
- Defendant may have multiple creditors; limited assets are allocated proportionally
- Defendant may hide assets or transfer property to relatives
- Enforcement requires plaintiff to repeatedly petition court and pursue collection actively; system is not automatic

**Result.** Substantial percentage of judgments are never fully collected. Collection rates for poor defendants are especially low; judgment may be technically valid but practically uncollectable.

## Fairness and Access

Access to Auralis courts is theoretically equal but practically unequal based on wealth:

**Lawyer Representation.** Formal legal representation exists in Auralis; educated people trained in legal procedure can represent clients. Quality varies: experienced lawyers command premium fees (20-50 Watts per case), while novice representatives charge less (5-10 Watts). Poor parties cannot afford experienced representation.

**Expert Witnesses.** Technical disputes (property boundaries, contract interpretation, technical quality assessment) may require expert witnesses. Experts are expensive (10-30 Watts per testimony); poor parties cannot afford experts, placing them at disadvantage.

**Cost Barriers.** Poor parties face barriers discouraging litigation:
- Filing fees (8-15 Watts) are substantial for poor people earning 20-40 Watts monthly
- Legal representation costs
- Witness expenses (travel, lost work time)
- Extended litigation timeline (victim loses income while case is pending)

**Result:** Wealthy defendants can afford quality representation and extend litigation (appeals, endless discovery). Poor victims cannot afford equivalent resistance. Out-of-court settlement often favors wealthy defendants who can outlast poor plaintiffs' ability to fund litigation.

Formal courts provide consistency and fairness in theory; inequality in resources creates unfairness in practice. System works well for disputes between parties of similar wealth/resources. System is skewed when party wealth is drastically different.

## Comparison with Terravelle

| Factor | Auralis | Terravelle |
|--------|---------|-----------|
| Judge Training | Formal legal education, government appointment | Community reputation, limited formal training |
| Procedures | Rigorous, rule-bound, formal | Flexible, judge-directed, informal |
| Evidence | Strict standards, rules of evidence | Common-sense assessment |
| Timeline | 3-6 months typical | 2-4 weeks typical |
| Cost | 30-80 Watts total | 10-30 Watts total |
| Precedent | Strict adherence, binding | Loose consultation, flexible |
| Appeal | Available (expensive) | Limited (guild council review only) |
| Enforcement | Institutional strength | Weak, reputation-based |
| Accessibility | Formal but cost-prohibitive | Informal and cheaper |

Auralis courts are more formal, consistent, and slower. Terravelle courts are less formal, faster, and cheaper.
