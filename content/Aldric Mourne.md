_(Council Member, Auralis — Culmination Faction)_

**Aldric Mourne** spent thirty years as a military logistics commander before [[Drest]] asked him to apply those skills to the council's expedition program. That was twelve years ago. He has since organized fourteen desert crossings, managed the resupply and documentation of three Reach Academy field seasons, and overseen the return processing of whatever fragments the surviving expeditions brought back. He does not ask what the expeditions are looking for.

## Character

Aldric is sixty-seven, square-jawed, unhurried, and operates entirely in logistics. Give him a destination, a timeline, a resource list, and a personnel count and he will produce a functional plan. Give him a philosophical debate about the nature of the ancient civilization and he will wait politely for it to end.

He is not incurious. He simply has a discipline that he applies to the question of what he engages with: his attention goes to what he can affect, and he cannot affect the interpretation of inscription fragments or the theological implications of Flux anomaly readings. He can affect whether the right casters reach the right site with the right equipment and sufficient Flux reserve to get back. He focuses there.

This produces a particular kind of relationship with [[Drest]], which is almost entirely professional and periodically tense when Drest wants assurances that Aldric does not consider it his place to provide. Drest wants to be told the next expedition will return. Aldric provides probabilities. Drest finds this insufficient. Aldric has offered to discuss his methodology.

## Attrition

Approximately one in three Auralis desert expeditions does not return. Aldric has this number memorized. He has also memorized the attrition rates broken down by season, route, personnel experience level, departure settlement, and equipment tier. He has presented this analysis to Drest twice. He has recommended, both times, reducing expedition frequency and increasing preparation time per crossing.

Both recommendations were declined. Aldric has filed this outcome, adjusted his per-expedition resource calculations to account for the reality of the funding cycle, and continued working.

He does not find this troubling in the way that people outside his profession find it troubling. He has seen what happens when good soldiers die in bad operations, and he has made his peace with the gap between what a logistics officer can control and what falls outside it. What he does find difficult — not troubling, difficult — is the documentation discrepancy: the expeditions that are not acknowledged as lost officially. He maintains a private record. He has not decided what to do with it.
