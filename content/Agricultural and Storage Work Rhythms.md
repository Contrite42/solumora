# Agricultural and Storage Work Rhythms

The agricultural cycle governs the survival timeline for most inhabitants of [[Solumora]]. What gets planted, when harvests complete, and how food stores are managed determine whether households remain solvent or face winter food crises.

## The Agricultural Year

**[[Terravelle]] Cycle (Temperate Northern Agriculture):**

### Planning and Preparation (Colden through Firstgreen)

Winter is not rest. It is planning season. Farmers review the previous year's harvest records, assess soil condition, negotiate field rotations with neighbors (shared land management prevents conflicts), and plan which fields will grow what.

Seed selection happens January-February. Terrified farmers — seeds that fail mean full-year crop loss — evaluate stored seeds for viability, conduct germination tests on doubtful stock, and source replacement seeds from merchants operating the seed trade. Seed prices spike winter/early-spring because demand concentrates here. A family discovering their saved seed was contaminated by mold must buy emergency replacement at 2-3x normal price or accept reduced planting area.

Equipment repair happens simultaneously. Plows, hoes, scythes — all must be ready by late February or early March when ground thaw allows fieldwork. Farmers bring breaks to blacksmiths. Smiths face bottleneck: repairs for 50+ farming households arrive in 2-3 week window, capacity does not expand, queues back up, equipment returns late, farming schedules compress.

Those without capital to pay blacksmith fees — lower-tier households — improvise repairs or work with marginally functional equipment.

### Planting (Firstgreen through Plantmoon)

Ground thaw triggers planting frenzy. Temperature still risks frost, so timing is critical: too early and seeds rot, too late and harvest window shortens unacceptably. Farmers in [[Maren Valley]] rely on [[The Solumoran Calendar]]'s dates — historical patterns suggest Plantmoon first-week as optimal planting window for major crops.

Planting is physically demanding: rows must be straight (affects harvest efficiency), seed depth must be consistent (affects germination rates), spacing must be precise (affects mature plant yield). Families work long days. Child labor is routine — children set seeds, families follow spreading prepared rows.

Soil preparation work (turning edges, removing old plant matter, establishing drainage) often precedes actual seed placement by 2-4 weeks, creating 6-8 week continuous work window with minimal rest.

### Maintenance (Brightward through Harvestfirst)

Post-planting work is critical for survival:

- **Pest management:** Insects and creatures threaten crop survival. Farmers use manual removal (where labor allows), [[Flux Users]] applying repellent sigils (where expense can be afforded), and crop rotation patterns designed to interrupt pest life cycles.
- **Weed management:** Hand-weeding must happen 3-4 times across growing season or weeds will choke out crop.
- **Water management:** In dry years, irrigation-capable farms direct water from rivers/streams. Non-irrigated farms depend on rainfall — unpredictable and insufficient.
- **Monitoring for disease:** Crop diseases spread rapidly. Early detection requires constant field assessment.

This work is less physically intensive than planting but more knowledge-intensive: knowing when weeds threaten yield, how to identify crop disease, what can be salvaged if pest damage occurs — this practical knowledge is what separates skilled farmers (20-30% loss acceptable annual variation) from struggling farms (50%+ loss years common).

### Harvest (Harvestfirst through Coolward)

Harvest is emergency work. Ripened grain exists for 2-4 week window before quality degrades. Extended rain can rot grain in fields. A night frost can kill soft crops. Weather variations create year-to-year uncertainty: the harvest window might be 4 weeks one year and 2 weeks another, or conditions might deteriorate suddenly.

Every family member works: children handle sorting, adults cut and bundle, elderly manage processing. Harvest crews extend 12-14 hour workdays. Injuries are common — sickle accidents, physical collapse from heat, tendon tears from repetitive motion.

Households typically hire temporary harvest labor during this window — day wage workers come from settlements for the 3-4 week intensive labor spike. Wages are highest of the year: farmers desperate to complete harvest quickly and harvest season compressed scarcity drives daily wage rates 2-3x base yearly rate.

The economics create tension: hiring labor reduces family burden but consumes profit margin. A poor harvest means expensive temporary labor was wasted. A good harvest justifies the labor cost and improves final income.

### Processing and Storage (Coolward through Darkmoon)

Post-harvest work is continuous. Grain must be dried (2-3 weeks in good weather, longer if damp conditions), threshed to separate grain from chaff (physically demanding, labor-intense work), cleaned, and stored in conditions that prevent mold, rodent damage, and insect infestation.

**Storage Methods:**
- **Granaries:** Shared community structures, sometimes guild-owned, where grain is stored in controlled environment (elevation prevents flooding, ventilation reduces mold)
- **Household storage:** Dried grain in sealed containers, kept in cool/dry location
- **Underground caches:** Some farming regions dig storage into hillsides or use natural caves where temperature stays cool year-round
- **Salted/dried agriculture:** Vegetables dried or preserved in salt for longer shelf life

Poor storage = losing 10-30% of stored harvest to mold, pests, and environmental exposure. Good storage = preserving 90%+.

Quality matters: grain stored in humid conditions may appear intact but is already partially degraded by mold, tastes worse, and provides less nutrition than properly stored grain. Families drinking/eating substandard grain do not "starve" but become malnourished — showing up as persistent sickness during winter months.

### Winter Maintenance (Darkmoon through Colden)

Once storage complete, winter work shifts to field preparation and planning. Manure is spread (winter application for spring incorporation), drainage improvements are made, minor equipment repairs occur. This is also the social season — [[Terravelle]] farmers gather for guild meetings, community work requirements, family obligations.

Food consumption pattern drives winter: households eating from stores, rationing carefully. Depletion of stored food is measured daily. A household that miscalculated and depleted supplies by April faces 4-6 weeks of inadequate nutrition before spring crops begin producing edible yield.

## [[Auralis]] Cycle (Temperate Southern Agriculture)

[[Auralis]] climate is warmer and enables 1.5x the growing season length of [[Terravelle]]. The cycle is compressed differently:

**Spring (Firstgreen through Brightward):** Earlier planting, rapid growth. Spring frost risk lower. Summer harvest can come earlier.

**Summer (Highdry through Harvestfirst):** Multiple harvest cycles for fast-growing crops. Irrigation-dependent farming more valuable here (seasonal rainfall lower). Pest populations higher in extended warmth.

**Autumn (Harvestfall through Coolward):** Main harvest compressed into 4 weeks (temperature decline more abrupt). Post-harvest processing rapid (drying faster in warm conditions).

**Winter (Darkmoon through Colden):** Milder conditions allow some field work. Storage stress less acute (warmer winter means stored food lasts better with less spoilage risk). But some crops can grow through winter, creating possibility of year-round minor crop contribution (harder than [[Terravelle]] winter agriculture).

The [[Auralis]] advantage: longer growing season, higher production potential. The disadvantage: pest pressure and disease risk higher with warmer climate.

## Storage Infrastructure and Pressure

**Pre-War Baseline:**
- Major settlements maintain granaries storing 4-6 month supply surplus
- Coastal regions store 3-4 months (relying on shipping to continue)
- [[Wolfpoint]] maintains 8-12 month surplus (preparation for winter isolation)

**Contemporary Pressure:**
- Military requisitions (10-20% of grain stocks diverted to army supply)
- Population pressure (refugees from border regions increase urban consumption)
- Harvest acceleration stress (war preparation drives 25-30% harvest volume increase, exceeding drying and storage infrastructure capacity)

The result is [[Auralis]] and [[Terravelle]] operating with reduced storage buffers in 626-627 FE. A bad harvest season + military draft of labor + reduced storage capacity creates genuine shortage risk.

## Harvest Failure Modes

### Environmental Failure

**Drought:** Insufficient rain causes crop withering. [[Auralis]] southern regions (lower baseline rainfall) experience critical drought risk every 5-7 years on average. Drought years produce 20-40% of normal yield. Households dependent on that year's harvest face crisis.

[[Terravelle]] northern regions experience localized drought less frequently but with more severe consequences (infrastructure less developed for drought mitigation).

**Flooding:** Excessive spring rain floods low-lying fields, washes away topsoil, kills crops. Flood years in [[Maren Valley]] can reduce yield 30-60%. Recurrent flooding on same land makes those fields marginally viable long-term.

**Frost and Freeze:** Unexpected frost during growing season kills early growth. Late autumn frost kills ripening grain. Early autumn frost can destroy entire harvest before completion.

**Pestilence:** Crop disease (grain fungus, insect outbreaks, creature invasions) can reduce yield 40-60% or eliminate crop entirely if untreated. Widespread disease creates multi-household crisis.

### Labor Failure

**Epidemic or Emergency:** Conscription for military service removes harvest labor exactly when needed. A village losing 20-30% of working-age population to military service faces harvest incomplete. Partially-processed grain in field rots. Yield loss cascades to winter shortage.

**Equipment Failure:** Plow breaks mid-season, blacksmith cannot repair in time, field remains unimplemented for year. Tool shortage compounds as military demands metalwork for weapons/armor rather than agricultural equipment.

### Storage Failure

**Moisture Infiltration:** Improperly constructed storage allows humidity. Mold develops. Stored grain becomes inedible. A household losing 30% of stored harvest to mold faces winter shortfall.

**Pest Destruction:** Rodents, insects, or creatures eats into stored grain. Contamination makes remaining grain inedible (rodent droppings = no longer food-safe).

## Seasonal Employment and Income

### Who Depends on Seasonal Agricultural Work

- **Tenant farmers:** Work shared land, split yield with owner (typically 40/60 to 50/50 split)
- **Day laborers:** Hire into farms seasonally, earn daily wages (peak 20-25 Watts/day during harvest, minimal 5-8 Watts/day during winter maintenance)
- **Craft support:** Blacksmiths, tool makers, grain millers earn from agricultural economy (income concentrates supply-demand bottlenecks: spring equipment needs, autumn grain processing)
- **Storage and transport:** Granary workers, mill staff, grain merchants

### Income Volatility

A day laborer's annual income ($5000-10000 Watts in good year) concentrates in 6-month window (Brightward-Coolward). Remaining 6 months generates minimal income unless supplementary work (craft, trading, day labor in other sectors) exists.

This creates household cash-flow crisis: earn 70% of annual income in 6 months, spend it across 12 months, miscalculate and face winter debt.

Seasonal income makes debt vulnerable: taking a loan in autumn expecting to repay from next spring's harvest is rational if harvest comes on schedule, catastrophic if harvest fails.

## Class Implications

Small landowners and independent farmers can absorb 1-2 yield-failure years through savings/credit.

Tenant farmers losing yield still owe landlord share — failure transfers proportionally to tenant rather than reducing obligations.

Day laborers have no income during non-agricultural season. Reliance on credit and subsistence food during winter months compounds with injury or old age making them unemployable during peak season.

The seasonal cycle advantages capital holders and land owners. It creates vulnerability in day laborers and poor tenants.
