**Flux Food Preservation** encompasses the methods by which [[Flux Users]] manipulate physical and chemical properties of food to extend storage time, reduce spoilage, and maintain nutritional value beyond what traditional preservation can achieve. This is not luxury technology — it is infrastructure that allows settlements to survive winter, expeditions to cross the [[Equatorial Desert]], and merchants to transport perishable goods across hundreds of miles. Without Flux preservation, food security across [[Sol umora]] would collapse.

## Core Preservation Technologies

### Temperature Control ([[Coldbox Seal]])

The most common Flux preservation method: inscribing containers or storage spaces with temperature-reduction effects that slow bacterial growth and chemical degradation.

**Technical Operation:**
- [[Control Tier|T2-T3]] practitioners inscribe [[Sigils]] onto container surfaces (wood, metal, leather, or specialized materials)
- Inscription creates persistent cooling effect maintaining temperatures 5-15 degrees below ambient
- Effect duration: 24-72 hours depending on practitioner skill, sigil quality, and container insulation
- Renewal required when effect degrades (observable temperature increase signals need for re-inscription)

**Applications:**
- Household food storage (wealthy and middle-class households maintain coldbox storage)
- Commercial food vendors ([[Celde Morv]]'s provisioning business uses coldbox storage in [[Solhaven]])
- Maritime transport (ships carrying perishable cargo use shipboard cold storage)
- Expedition supplies (desert crossings use portable coldbox containers for heat-sensitive materials)

**Costs:**
- Initial inscription: 15-25 [[Watts]] (varies by container size, complexity, practitioner rates)
- Renewal inscription: 8-12 Watts (shorter process than initial setup)
- Weekly operational cost for household: 10-15 Watts (assumes two renewals per week)
- Annual cost: 500-750 Watts (prohibitive for working-class households)

**Limitations:**
- Does not freeze food (maintains cool temperatures but not freezing)
- Requires regular renewal (effect degrades predictably)
- Container damage breaks inscriptions (must be re-inscribed after repairs)
- Effectiveness decreases in extreme heat (desert environments require more frequent renewal)

### Chemical Stabilization

High-tier practitioners can manipulate food chemistry directly to inhibit spoilage mechanisms.

**Technical Operation:**
- [[Control Tier|T3-T5]] practitioners analyze food at molecular level (requires advanced Flux sensing)
- Targeted modifications: neutralize enzymes that cause decay, inhibit bacterial growth, stabilize fats that become rancid
- Effect is permanent or semi-permanent (modified food remains stable without renewal)
- Requires expertise in chemistry and biological processes (few practitioners have necessary training)

**Applications:**
- High-value preserved meats (noble households, luxury market)
- Expedition rations requiring multi-month stability
- Medical nutrition preparations (specialized dietary needs for illness/injury recovery)
- Export goods requiring long transport times

**Costs:**
- Preparation: 40-100 Watts per pound of food (labor-intensive, requires rare expertise)
- Accessible only to wealthy clients or institutional purchasers
- [[Auralis]] invests in this technology more than [[Terravelle]] (research funding, practitioner training)

**Limitations:**
- Does not reverse existing spoilage (must be applied to fresh food)
- Some modifications alter texture or flavor (trade-off between preservation and palatability)
- Requires T3+ practitioners (bottleneck on availability)
- Not all foods respond equally (works better for meats and fats than fruits and vegetables)

### Desiccation Enhancement

Flux-assisted drying removes moisture more completely and quickly than traditional air-drying.

**Technical Operation:**
- T1-T2 practitioners use controlled heating to accelerate evaporation
- Alternatively: direct moisture extraction pulling water out of food without heat
- Process is faster (hours instead of days for traditional drying) and more complete (less residual moisture)

**Applications:**
- Dried fruits and vegetables for winter storage
- Jerky and dried meat (lighter weight, longer storage than salted meat)
- [[Equatorial Desert]] expedition rations (maximum calorie density, minimum weight)
- Emergency food reserves (lightweight, stable for years)

**Costs:**
- Processing: 2-5 Watts per pound (relatively affordable)
- Accessible to middle-class households and commercial processors
- Rural communities often coordinate on communal drying events (single practitioner processes entire settlement's harvest surplus)

**Limitations:**
- Reduces food weight and volume (desirable for transport, but some consumers prefer non-dried food)
- Texture changes are irreversible (dried food can be rehydrated but never returns to original texture)
- Nutritional loss (some vitamins degrade during drying process)

### Spoilage Detection

Flux sensing allows practitioners to identify spoilage before visible signs appear.

**Technical Operation:**
- T2-T3 practitioners sense chemical markers indicating bacterial growth or decay processes
- Non-invasive (does not require opening containers or cutting into food)
- Provides advanced warning (can detect spoilage 1-3 days before odor or discoloration becomes obvious)

**Applications:**
- Food vendors confirming inventory is safe to sell
- Households checking stored foods before consumption
- Expedition leaders verifying ration integrity before departure

**Costs:**
- Inspection: 1-3 Watts per food item or container
- Often bundled with other services (provisioners like [[Celde Morv]] include spoilage checks as part of sales)

**Limitations:**
- Requires access to T2+ practitioner (not universally available)
- Detection is qualitative ("this is safe" vs "this is unsafe") rather than quantitative ("spoilage will begin in 4 days")
- Some spoilage types are harder to detect than others

## Traditional Preservation (Non-Flux)

Flux preservation supplements but does not replace traditional methods. Most food preservation across [[Solumora]] still uses generations-old techniques.

### Salt-Curing

The most widespread preservation method: salt extracts moisture and creates inhospitable environment for bacteria.

**Applications:**
- Preserved fish (coastal [[Coastal Settlement Network]] economy depends on this)
- Cured meats (pork, game meat from [[Mountain Game Collective]])
- Cheese preservation (extends storage time beyond fresh dairy)

**Limitations:**
- Requires significant salt (expensive commodity, must be imported to many settlements)
- High salt content affects flavor (limits how preserved food can be prepared)
- Does not work for all food types (vegetables respond poorly, some meats cure better than others)

**Costs:**
- Salt supply: 8,000-10,000 pounds annually for settlement like [[Wolfpoint]] ([[Ashford Supply Contract]] negotiations)
- Salt represents 15-25% of food preservation costs for commercial operations

### Smoking

Hot smoke dries food while depositing preservative compounds from wood combustion.

**Applications:**
- Fish smoking (standard in coastal settlements)
- Meat preservation ([[Wolfpoint]] cold-smoking produces export-quality preserved game)
- Cheese flavor enhancement (smoked cheese is regional specialty)

**Limitations:**
- Requires sustained wood fire (fuel cost, labor to maintain fire)
- Produces strong flavor (some consumers prefer unsmoked preserved food)
- Storage structure required (smokehouse construction and maintenance)

**Costs:**
- Smokehouse construction: 500-2000 Watts (one-time capital investment)
- Operational fuel and labor: 50-100 Watts per batch of food processed

### Fermentation

Controlled bacterial growth converts food into stable forms while creating distinctive flavors.

**Applications:**
- Cheese production ([[Maren Valley]] dairy operations, [[Upland Herding Network]])
- Fermented vegetables (pickled cabbage, turnips — winter vegetable storage)
- Alcoholic beverages (ale, beer — separate ecosystem from food preservation but related process)

**Limitations:**
- Requires specific environmental conditions (temperature, moisture, bacterial culture)
- Process is time-consuming (weeks to months for proper fermentation)
- Failure rate exists (incorrect conditions produce spoiled food instead of preserved food)

**Cultural Context:**
- [[Covenant of Measure]] communities view fermentation positively (transforms surplus into stable form, reduces waste)
- Some fermented foods are acquired tastes (strong flavors limit market beyond traditional consumers)

### Root Cellaring

Cool, dark, humid underground storage extends shelf life for root vegetables, apples, and similar foods.

**Applications:**
- Rural settlement food security (most rural households maintain root cellars)
- Urban middle-class households (those with property space for construction)
- Commercial operations (taverns, food vendors store bulk ingredients)

**Limitations:**
- Requires suitable geography (soil conditions, drainage, temperature regulation)
- Storage time is limited (months, not years)
- Some foods store better than others (apples store well, leafy vegetables do not)

**Costs:**
- Construction: 200-800 Watts (depends on size, soil conditions, labor availability)
- Maintenance minimal (periodic inspection, pest control)

## Hybrid Systems

Most effective preservation uses combination of traditional and Flux methods.

### Flux-Enhanced Traditional Preservation

**Salt-Curing with Chemical Stabilization:**
- Initial salt cure using traditional methods
- Flux practitioner applies chemical stabilization for extended storage
- Result: Meat preserved for 12-18 months instead of 6-9 months with salt alone

**Cold Storage with Flux Detection:**
- Root cellar or coldbox provides cool environment
- Periodic Flux spoilage detection identifies items approaching failure
- Allows optimization (consume soon-to-spoil items first, extend overall storage efficiency)

**Smoking with Temperature Control:**
- Traditional smokehouse enhanced with Flux-controlled temperature regulation
- Produces more consistent results, reduces failed batches
- Allows precise control over smoke exposure and drying rate

### Practical Examples

**[[Celde Morv]]'s Provisioning Operation:**
- Maintains coldbox storage for fresh/semi-perishable goods ([[Solhaven]])
- Sources preserved foods from suppliers using traditional methods
- Employs T2 practitioner (part-time) for spoilage detection and coldbox renewal
- Combines Flux and traditional methods to offer variety at multiple price points

**[[Wolfpoint]] Game Preservation:**
- [[Mountain Game Collective]] uses cold-smoking (traditional method, enhanced by natural winter cold)
- Employs chemical stabilization for export-grade meat (extends transport shelf life)
- Internal consumption relies on traditional smoking without chemical treatment (reduces cost)

**[[Halveth]] Expedition Supply:**
- [[Seln Watch]] vendors prepare expedition rations using Flux desiccation
- Combines traditional dried foods (grain, fruit) with Flux-enhanced dried meat
- Result: Maximum calorie density per weight, multi-month desert crossing stability

## Economic and Social Impact

### Food Security

Flux preservation directly affects settlement survival:
- Urban centers depend on coldbox storage to maintain stable food supply despite seasonal variation
- Rural settlements use Flux desiccation to preserve harvest surplus for winter consumption
- Expedition viability depends on Flux-preserved rations (traditional methods insufficient for desert crossing durations)

### Class Stratification

Access to Flux preservation correlates with wealth:
- **Upper class:** Maintain household coldbox storage, purchase chemically stabilized luxury foods
- **Middle class:** Occasional access to coldbox storage, primarily use traditional preservation with periodic Flux spoilage detection
- **Working class:** Rely on traditional preservation, no regular access to Flux methods

Result: Wealthier populations eat more varied, higher-quality food year-round. Working-class populations experience seasonal food monotony.

### Regional Economies

Flux preservation enables trade:
- [[Wolfpoint]] exports preserved game meat to [[Terravelle]] markets via [[Outer Coast Packet Line]] (impossible without preservation)
- Coastal fish reaches inland settlements through combination of traditional and Flux preservation
- Cross-border food trade depends on preservation extending shelf life beyond transport duration

### Practitioner Employment

Food preservation is significant income source for low to mid-tier Flux practitioners:
- T1-T2 practitioners earn 300-600 Watts annually from preservation work (part-time supplement to other employment)
- T3-T4 practitioners specializing in chemical stabilization earn 1500-3000 Watts annually (full-time viable)
- Rural practitioners perform preservation services for entire communities (payment often in-kind: food, labor, housing rather than currency)

## Preservation [[Grimoires]]

Flux preservation requires documented techniques. Multiple grimoires address this domain.

### Common Preservation Grimoires

**"Coldmark Methods" (Common Grimoire):**
- Basic temperature-reduction sigils ([[Coldbox Seal]] variants)
- Accessible to T1-T2 practitioners
- Standard reference for household and commercial coldbox operations

**"Desiccation Processes" (Uncommon Grimoire):**
- Controlled moisture extraction techniques
- Heat management for accelerated drying
- Requires T2 minimum for reliable execution

**"Chemical Food Stability" (Rare Grimoire):**
- Advanced techniques requiring T3-T4 practitioners
- Molecular-level food manipulation
- Restricted access (high cost, tier verification required)

### Research and Development

[[Auralis]] invests in preservation research through [[Crestward Flux Institute]]:
- Goal: Develop cheaper, more accessible preservation methods
- Recent focus: Reducing coldbox inscription costs, improving desiccation efficiency
- Commercial application: New grimoires released every 3-5 years with incremental improvements

[[Terravelle]] research is less centralized:
- [[Trade Guilds]] fund practical preservation research serving commercial interests
- Innovations emerge from guild practitioners experimenting with traditional methods
- Focus on robust, low-cost solutions rather than cutting-edge techniques

## Contemporary Concerns

**War Preparation Impact:**
- Both [[Auralis]] and [[Terravelle]] requisition higher-tier practitioners for military preparation
- Civilian preservation access decreases (fewer available practitioners, higher costs)
- Settlements revert to traditional preservation methods (adequate but lower efficiency)

**Preservation Infrastructure Strain:**
- As cross-border tensions increase, [[Auralis]] prioritizes preservation for military supply chains
- Civilian food preservation receives less practitioner attention and reduced subsidy
- Price increases disproportionately affect working-class populations

**Supply Chain Disruptions:**
- Salt is critical for traditional preservation but requires trade (many settlements lack local sources)
- [[Ashford Supply Contract]] negotiations affect [[Wolfpoint]] preservation capacity
- Border customs delays increase spoilage risk for preserved foods in transit

## Future Developments

**Potential Innovations:**
- Passive preservation systems (inscriptions that self-renew using ambient Flux)
- Nutritional enhancement (preservation that improves food value rather than merely maintaining it)
- Scaled preservation infrastructure (community-level coldbox facilities serving multiple households)

**Constraints:**
- Flux theoretical limits (some desired preservation effects may be physically impossible)
- Economic constraints (development requires sustained funding, uncertain returns)
- Political priorities (war preparation diverts resources from civilian innovation)
