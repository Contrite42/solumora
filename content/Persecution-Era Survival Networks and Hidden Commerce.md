# The Persecution Era - Survival Networks and Hidden Commerce

Below institutional conflict, ordinary people created informal networks that kept refugee populations economically viable and information flowing across persecution boundaries.

## Food Supply Chains

Desert and mountain refuge populations couldn't sustain themselves on local cultivation alone. **Hidden supply networks** moved grain, preserved foods, and trading goods into refuge zones:

- **Cook-house operatives**: Women in town markets who sold "unusual quantities" of food to individual traders with refugee contacts
- **Miller's daughter networks**: Grain mills that recorded refugee-supporting merchants as "bulk buyers for expedition provisioning" (their actual purpose)
- **Merchant rotation**: Traders who traveled established routes carrying supplies wrapped in legitimate trade goods

These networks operated in plain sight by being economically normal. A merchant buying grain from a miller wasn't suspicious. That grain eventually reaching mountain communities was the informal truth underneath the official record.

## Information Flow Systems

Hunts succeeded episodically because they exploited information monopolies. **Counter-intelligence networks** maintained refugee awareness of hunt timing and locations:

- **Guild dispatch networks**: Merchant guilds had communication structures that pre-dated persecution; they repurposed them for refugee alerts
- **Tavern proprietor chains**: Establishments in trade towns shared information about unusual movements, garrison preparations, known hunter locations
- **Itinerant craftspeople**: Blacksmiths, weavers, repair specialists who traveled regular routes and carried messages embedded in gossip

## False Documentation Production

Refugee reintegration required civil identity. **Documentation networks** created fraudulent but functionally acceptable records:

- **Scribe conspiracies**: Town record-keepers (many sympathetic) produced birth certificates for refugees under assumed names
- **Guild membership forging**: Trade certifications created for refugees moving into new locations
- **Estate household documentation**: Refugees incorporated into noble household records as "longtime employees"

None of these were perfectly secure. But they were secure enough that casual institutional inspection wouldn't reveal them.

## Economic Integration

Most successful long-term refugee survival involved genuine economic contribution:

- **Craft apprenticeship networks**: Refugees trained in valuable skills (metalwork, weaving, skilled trades) integrated into production
- **Estate labor**: Refugees as permanent household staff, valued for reliability and loyalty because dependent on protection
- **Merchant partnerships**: Trade ventures funded by sympathizers that generated revenue supporting broader refugee populations

By persecution end, some refugees had become significant economic actors. Magistrates protecting them weren't charitably hiding resistance; they were protecting profitable economic partnerships.
