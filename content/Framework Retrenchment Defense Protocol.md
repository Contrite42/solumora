# Framework Retrenchment Defense Protocol

The **Framework Retrenchment Defense Protocol** is a constitutional-legal safeguard layer enacted thirty months after the [[Stories/The First Casualty|first casualty crisis]] to prevent rapid political rollback of coexistence protections during election cycles, panic surges, or factional realignment windows.

## Why It Exists

By year two, institutions had proven they could correct enforcement overreach through the [[Coexistence Enforcement Failure Review Board]] and [[Emergency Reintegration Mandate]]. By thirty months, a different risk emerged: lawful rollback attempts using ordinary legislative process to disable review, narrow standing, and restore discretionary immunity for high-intensity enforcement.

Rollback advocates framed proposals as "efficiency restoration" and "local sovereignty recovery." In practice, draft language repeatedly targeted:
- Public false-positive reporting requirements
- Independent observer presence in mass operations
- Automatic reintegration triggers after correction events

The Protocol formalizes defensive steps that slow retrenchment before irreversible legal damage occurs.

## Core Mechanisms

**Sunset Delay Lock**
- Any bill reducing coexistence safeguards cannot take effect for two full reporting quarters after passage.
- Delay period requires publication of district-level impact simulation.

**Dual-Jurisdiction Review Gate**
- If one kingdom proposes retrenchment affecting cross-border operations, counterpart review hearing is mandatory under treaty cooperation norms.
- Prevents unilateral legal narrowing from destabilizing shared institutions.

**Harm Forecast Requirement**
- Proponents must publish projected false-positive, remediation-lag, and participation-loss impacts using standard metrics from the [[Independent Compliance Metrics Office]].

**Emergency Preservation Trigger**
- If rollback passes during active destabilization period, core accountability functions remain in force until review-board clearance.

## Political Effects

The Protocol does not forbid retrenchment. It makes retrenchment visible, slower, and evidence-bound.

Supporters describe it as procedural guardrail against panic-law cycles.
Critics call it anti-majoritarian obstruction that prevents elected governments from rebalancing security priorities.

[[Cassia]] publicly endorsed the Protocol as "constitutional memory in operational form." [[Kess Tarren]] supported it narrowly, arguing that legitimacy depends on institutions proving they can be constrained even when popular fear shifts.

## Strategic Role

Post-reveal governance now treats rollback attempts as normal and recurring, not exceptional. The Protocol marks the transition from crisis-response architecture to constitutional durability architecture: frameworks are no longer defended by founders alone, but by deliberate friction built into law.
