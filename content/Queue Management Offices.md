# Queue Management Offices

**Queue Management Offices** are municipal-administrative units that stabilize public access during service strain by converting informal waiting lines into controlled priority systems.

They are most visible in dense districts of [[Auralis]] and major logistics towns in [[Terravelle]], where clinic intake, ration claims, and work-assignment demand spike at the same time.

## Why They Exist

During normal periods, most services run on relationship networks, guild referrals, and local habit. Under strain, those informal systems fail first.

Queue offices create a shared order of access when:

- clinic throughput drops below baseline
- ration allotments tighten or shift by district
- temporary shelter demand exceeds registered beds

Without queue structure, the strongest and best connected are served first. With queue structure, outcomes are still unequal but less chaotic.

## Core Functions

Queue offices perform four practical functions:

- issue queue tokens linked to district registry and service class
- publish daily service capacity windows
- define priority categories (critical care, dependent households, continuity labor)
- document rollover claims when capacity is exhausted

The point is not comfort. The point is predictability under scarcity.

## Friction and Failure

Queue systems fail when priority rules are opaque or changed without notice.

Common failure patterns:

- token invalidation after policy shifts
- duplicate registration by multi-household claimants
- local favoritism under political pressure
- queue migration between districts after rumor spikes

These failures are administrative, but their social effects are immediate: neighborhood conflict, market panic, and reduced trust in [[Clinical Care Systems]] and local food distribution.

## Relationship to Care and Rationing

Queue offices are now tightly linked to [[Care Triage Workflows]] and [[Ration Substitution Practices]].

In practical terms, a household with downgraded food allocation is more likely to appear in low-priority clinical lanes within two to three weeks, especially for pediatric and elder care complaints tied to nutrition and fatigue.

## Current Trajectory

Operators report more queue formalization and less queue confidence.

People still use the system because they need it. They trust it less because policy cadence changes faster than local communication can keep up.
