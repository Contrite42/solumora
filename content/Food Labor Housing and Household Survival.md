**Food Labor, Housing, and Household Survival** documents the integration of food-system work into household survival strategy, showing how food employment, housing costs, and household member allocation of labor determine whether families maintain nutritional adequacy or slip into malnutrition.

## Household Income and Food Budget

The fundamental equation determining food security.

### Baseline Calculation: Working-Class Household (Family of 4)

**Household income:** Typical working-class household (two adults, two children) earning 8-15 Watts per person daily across adult earners: 16-30 Watts daily, ~450-700 Watts weekly, ~2300-3600 Watts annually.

**Food budget (55% of income, baseline adequacy):** 1265-1980 Watts annually (~24-38 Watts weekly).

**Sufficiency calculation:** Baseline food cost per person daily: 0.4-0.5 pounds grain equivalent at ~1.2 Watts per pound = 0.5-0.6 Watts per person daily minimum, 2.0-2.4 Watts daily household. Additional protein (fish, meat weekly) 2-3 Watts weekly. Annual total required: 2500-3000 Watts minimum for vegetative survival (grain base, minimal protein, no vegetables, no luxury calories).

**Comparison:** Household income 2300-3600 Watts versus requirement 2500-3000 Watts shows razor-thin margin. Households at bottom income tier (2300 Watts) cannot afford vegetative minimum; depend on credit, relatives, relief.

### Seasonal Household Income Variation

Food budget adequacy depends on seasonal employment patterns.

**Spring surge (Firstgreen-Brightward, 8 weeks):** Surge employment (equipment preparation, garden labor, road work) provide peak income: 18-25 Watts daily per earner, 36-50 Watts household daily. Household can maintain normal diet (adequate grain, more protein access). Some surplus accumulation possible.

**Summer maintenance (Brightward-Harvestfirst, 10 weeks):** Moderate income (12-18 Watts daily per earner), 24-36 Watts household. Food budget sustainable at adequate level.

**Autumn crunch (Harvestfall-Coolward, 6-8 weeks):** Harvest work peak (15-25 Watts daily per earner), 30-50 Watts household daily. Income maximized. Food abundant (harvest supply peak). Surplus accumulation for winter.

**Winter austerity (Colden through Thawmark, 12-16 weeks):** Employment collapses (3-8 Watts daily, inconsistent availability), 6-16 Watts household daily (if second earner finds partial work). Income 30-60 Watts weekly. Food budget collapses 40-70% below adequacy. Households relying on accumulated surplus, credit, or institutional relief.

### Household Coping Strategies

**Surplus accumulation (autumn):** During harvest peak (abundance + peak income), households buy additional grain (if income permits) and preserve goods. Goal: build 4-8 week buffer for winter.

**Credit purchasing (winter):** When surplus depleted, households purchase on credit (vendor trust, family lending, moneylenders). Debt accumulates during winter. Repayment during spring peak income stretched 3-6 months into spring (new spring income committed to old winter debt).

**Labor flexibility (children):** Household with school-age children can shift children into wage work during peak seasons (harvest, spring preparation). Child earnings 3-6 Watts daily (less than adult, but contribution material to household budget).

**Household member specialization:** Households may specialize members: one adult in stable employment (guild work, municipal labor), one mobile (seasonal labor, day work). Stabilized income helps budget; mobile income maximizes total capture.

**Institutional relief:** When all private coping strategies exhausted, households rely on settlement relief programs (grain distribution to destitute, tax relief for disabled households). Access requires means-testing and social humiliation (admission of poverty, loss of status).

## Food Work and Household Survival

Food-system employment provides multiple roles within household economies.

### Family Farming (Small Owner/Tenant)

Seasonal agricultural work (planting, harvest) employs family members. Income: 500-800 Watts annually from farming (small holding of 1-2 acres, yield ~200-400 pounds grain, sales at market ~150-250 Watts plus household consumption). Supplemented by off-season work (urban labor, craft, seasonal trades): 200-400 Watts.

**Margin:** Household income 700-1200 Watts against requirement 2500-3000 Watts reveals fundamental problem: family farming insufficient for self-sufficiency. Requires supplementary income (spouse in different work, children in seasonal labor, credit supplementation).

**Advantage:** Household controls portion of food supply directly (grain production consumption, can store, process at will). Reduced dependence on market prices (own portion of food is cost-free once harvested).

### Seasonal Agricultural Labor (Farm Worker)

Harvest employment (8-10 weeks, Harvestfirst-Coolward): 15-25 Watts daily = 600-1000 Watts seasonal income. Off-season employment (winter/spring urban labor): 3-8 Watts daily, 200-400 Watts annual off-season. Total: 800-1400 Watts annually against requirement 2500-3000 Watts.

**Household survival strategy:** Agricultural labor as primary income component, supplemented by spouse urban labor (20-30 Watts weekly), plus children labor (when applicable): 400-800 Watts annually. Household total: 1200-2200 Watts. Still short of requirement; significant credit/relief dependence necessary.

### Urban Day Labor

Consistent urban wage (3-15 Watts daily) provides more predictable income than seasonal agricultural work: 1000-1500 Watts annually, more stable quarterly distribution. Better for household budget planning (no seasonal collapse).

**Household strategy:** Primary earner in day labor (1200-1500 Watts), spouse seasonal agricultural (summer only, 300-500 Watts), plus relief/credit fills remainder. Total: 1500-2000 Watts household (still inadequate, but more predictable than fully seasonal).

### Food-Specific Employment

Tavern work (kitchen, service): 15-25 Watts weekly provided moderate income, more stable than seasonal agricultural. Food-sector employment (market labor, fishing, preservation) 8-12 Watts daily provides similar stability. 

**Household advantage:** Food-sector employment provides both income and informal food access (small amounts of food product, discounts, access to discarded-but-edible materials). Tavern worker households may have access to meal service (workplace meal provided). Fishing family households may have access to catch (legal obligation to retain portion for personal use, or informal market-fish access).

**Integration:** Food sector employment combined with non-food work stabilizes household budget while food access component reduces actual cash food requirement.

## Housing-Food Trade-offs

Housing and food compete for household budget (together ~70-80% of working-class budget).

### Rent and Housing Costs

Rent for shared housing: 2-3 Watts weekly (working-class standard). Annual housing cost: 100-150 Watts (3-6% of household budget). Not high but consistent.

**Rent pressure years:** Wartime or economic downturn sometimes increases rent (landlord requisitions, property damage repair costs, reduced competition for occupancy). Rent increase to 4-5 Watts weekly removes 50-100 Watts annually from household (diverted from food to housing).

### Housing Quality and Food Budget Trade-off

Cheaper housing (single room, poor ventilation, shared facilities): saves 0.5-1.0 Watts weekly. Saved annual ~25-50 Watts available to food budget.

Problem: poor housing creates illness (disease, injury, cold-related illness), driving healthcare costs and productivity loss. Measured: households in poor housing spend 3-5% more on medical treatment, lose 5-10% more work days to illness. Net effect: savings from cheaper housing (25-50 Watts) offset by increased illness costs (50-150 Watts medical, lost income) and productivity reduction.

**Result:** Optimum housing quality trades toward middle of range (not cheapest, not spacious; adequate water, basic heating, basic sanitation). Cheapest housing proves economically inferior despite initial attraction.

## Gender, Children, and Food Labor Allocation

Household member allocation of labor determines both total income and food-preparation capacity.

### Female Labor Allocation

Primary female task: food preparation and household management (2-3 hours daily), plus childcare. In food-dependent households, additional female labor: preservation work (seasonal, 5-10 hours weekly during autumn), water-carrying (0.5-2 hours daily, if purchased), fuel collection (2-5 hours weekly).

**Employment decision:** Does female member engage in wage work, sacrificing household management hours? Analysis:

- Female wage earner earning 6-10 Watts daily (lower tier): 200-300 Watts annually.
- Cost of food purchased (prepared externoons, losing household preparation time) instead of prepared: +0.1-0.2 Watts daily = 36-72 Watts annually.
- Cost of hired household help (water, preservation) otherwise done by female: +30-50 Watts annually.
- Net gain: 200-300 minus 36-72 minus 30-50 = 95-150 Watts annual gain.

**Result:** For most working-class families, female wage participation produces net household gain of 50-150 Watts annually (improvement worth 3-5 weeks of adequate diet). But requires household management trade-off (less home preservation, less complete food preparation, more purchased/prepared meals at premium cost).

**Practice:** Two-earner households common (estimated 35-45% of working-class households have female wage earner). Female earners concentrate in seasonal/part-time work (maintain household management capacity), food-sector employment (gain food-access benefit), or when children old enough for childcare alternatives.

### Child Labor Allocation

Children in working-class households often split between schooling (growing literacy value, long-term benefit) and wage work (immediate income). Strategic allocation:

- **Young children (5-9 years):** Minimal wage work, focused on household labor (water carrying, fuel collection, sibling childcare). Frees adult time for wage work.
- **Older children (10-14 years):** Seasonal wage work (peak income seasons, 3-6 Watts daily), school during off-season.
- **Teenagers (15+):** Choose trade apprenticeship (career path, delayed income) or full-time wage work (immediate income).

**Household calculations:** 10-14 year old child in seasonal work: 200-400 Watts annually. Child in school full-time: zero immediate income, but 2-3% lifetime income increase (calculated actuarially). Household desperate for current cash (avoid credit/malnutrition) chooses child work; household with margin chooses school investment.

**Documented pattern:** Wartime or economic downturn correlates with increased child work (households abandoning school for immediate income); peacetime expansion correlates with increased school enrollment (households able to invest in future).

## Debt Cycles and Food Insecurity

Food insecurity creates debt, which perpetuates food insecurity.

### Winter Deficit and Spring Debt

Household entering winter with inadequate stored surplus must purchase supplemental grain on credit (vendor trust, informal loans, moneylender). Winter credit: estimated 100-300 Watts accumulated by end winter (Darkmoon).

**Spring income:** Peak spring income (15-25 Watts daily per earner) 70% directed to debt repayment. Example: 35-50 Watts household daily, 50-70% debt repayment = 17-35 Watts daily, remaining food budget drops 50% compounded with rising spring food prices. Spring is paradoxically high-income but tight-budget.

**Debt interest:** Formal moneylenders charge 10-25% interest on loans. Informal vendor credit typically interest-free but carries implicit debt (obligation to future purchases, trust relationship collapse if default). Compound year-over-year: household borrowing 200 Watts one winter, next winter facing 200 Watts original plus 20-50 Watts interest.

### Intergenerational Debt

Persistent poverty families accumulate multigenerational debt: parent borrows, dies still owing, child inherits obligation. Documented: some families in permanent debt state (always owe money, never achieve surplus accumulation, perpetually cash-constrained). Intergenerational debt correlates with lowest-income households and chronic malnutrition.

## Contemporary Pressure on Household Survival (623 FE)

Current wartime context strains all household survival strategies:

- **Conscription:** Adult male removal reduces earning capacity (one earner lost in two-earner household). Household income drops 40-60% immediately (if conscripted earner was primary). Shifts female member into primary earner role (if capable) or relies on children/elderly supplementation.
- **Unemployment surge:** Military demand contracts civilian labor market (workers directed to war effort). Unemployment spikes 20-30% in non-essential work (lower wages, fewer positions). Day-laborers face reduced earning opportunity, forcing shift to military employment or wage reduction.
- **Food prices:** War impacts (requisitions, supply disruption, market uncertainty) raise grain prices 30-50%. Food budget requirement increases from 2300-3000 Watts to 3000-4500 Watts while household income may fall (conscription, unemployment).
- **Housing strain:** Wartime requisitions of housing (military needs, refugees, institutional storage) reduce housing availability, raising rents. Housing cost pressure 10-30% increase diverts funds from food.

**Combined effect:** Household survival margin collapses. 50-60% of working-class households move from adequate-to-marginal status into marginal-to-insufficient-food status. Malnutrition becomes widespread among vulnerable dependents.
