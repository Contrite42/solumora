# Interkingdom Accountability Compact

The **Interkingdom Accountability Compact** is a three-year post-revelation governance accord between [[Auralis]] and [[Terravelle]] that standardizes public accountability obligations for shared coexistence institutions. It extends beyond ceasefire logic and codifies which failures must be disclosed, corrected, and externally reviewed across both kingdoms.

## Context

By year three, both kingdoms had built layered safeguards:
- [[Coexistence Enforcement Failure Review Board]] for high-impact failures
- [[Emergency Reintegration Mandate]] for rapid trust restoration
- [[Framework Retrenchment Defense Protocol]] for rollback resistance

What remained unstable was jurisdictional inconsistency. Similar incidents still produced different disclosure timelines and correction standards depending on which side of the border they occurred.

The Compact resolves that gap with harmonized accountability baselines.

## Core Provisions

**Shared Disclosure Clock**
- Cross-border relevant incidents use one publication timeline regardless of jurisdiction.
- Public chronology release is mandatory within fixed reporting windows.

**Reciprocal Audit Access**
- Designated oversight teams may inspect counterpart evidentiary annexes and remediation logs.
- Redaction rules are narrow and independently reviewable.

**Correction Equivalency Rule**
- If an action would trigger mandatory reintegration obligations in one kingdom, equivalent obligations apply in the other when impact profile matches.

**Cross-Border Standing Expansion**
- Affected non-citizen residents and workers may file review petitions when incidents involve shared institutions or treaty operations.

## Strategic Significance

The Compact shifts coexistence accountability from parallel systems to an interoperable system. It does not erase political differences; it constrains them where secrecy and delay historically produced escalation.

[[Cassia]] described the Compact as "the point where mutual suspicion became mutual procedure."

[[Merra Veld]] characterized it more plainly: "If we share risk, we share receipts."
