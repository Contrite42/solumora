# Coastal and River Shipping Cycles

Shipping in [[Solumora]] operates within rigid seasonal constraints shaped by weather patterns, river conditions, and maritime hazards. The profitability and viability of shipping depend entirely on understanding and working within these cycles rather than trying to circumvent them.

## The Cycle Patterns

**Coastal shipping** and **river transport** operate on different seasonal calendars because they respond to different environmental pressures. Coastal operations face storm seasonality; river operations face water-level and ice conditions.

### River Cycles

**Spring Thaw Surge (Thawmark through Firstgreen):**
River ice breaks, water levels rise 40-60% above baseline, and current velocity increases dramatically. This creates a narrow window of maximum capacity: a single day of good weather can move the annual timber supply from logging camps to downriver settlements. Barges pile up at gathering points. Log-driving crews (seasonal labor) work 12-14 hour days moving material that winter accumulated. The work is dangerous — log-jam breaks kill people regularly, drowning is common, and the pace allows no margin for error or exhaustion.

Water levels remain elevated through Plantmoon, then gradually decline through summer. By Brightward, river traffic has returned to moderate levels.

**Summer Low Water (Highdry through Harvestfirst):**
River levels drop 40% from spring peaks. Deep-draft vessels cannot navigate most sections. Transport shifts to lighter barges or stops entirely along marginal routes. Grain transport to downriver settlements becomes limited — mills and grain processors must accept summer supply gaps even as harvest preparation demands maximum output.

**Autumn Transition (Harvestfall through Coolward):**
Water levels stabilize at sustainable mid-range. Shipping resumes normal capacity. Grain harvest requires rapid transport to storage — this is the second major shipping surge but less intense than spring because flood-level water is absent. Autumn shipping typically operates 60-70% of spring peak volume.

**Winter Ice (Colden through Thawmark):**
Rivers freeze in northern [[Terravelle]], become impassable. [[Auralis]] rivers remain navigable but weather-dependent. Some operations adapt to ice — log movement using sledges, cargo on frozen river surfaces — but these are discontinuous solutions rather than sustainable transport. Winter shipping effectively stops in [[Terravelle]] river systems.

### Coastal Cycles

**Storm Complexity (Thawmark through Plantmoon):**
Spring storms are unpredictable and violent. Coastal settlements experience 2-4 major storm systems monthly. Fishing fleets stay in port. Long-distance coastal traffic (like [[Outer Coast Packet Line]]) reduces volume by 50-70%, extends sailing times, and accepts higher casualty rates. Ships that must move negotiate reduced crews, increased insurance, and delayed schedules.

[[Maritime Storm Coordination]] reaches maximum operational intensity. Coastal [[Flux Users]] (T2-T3 practitioners) maintaining weather-sense watch for constant hours. Warnings reach settlements via courier relay and Flux-projection networks.

**Optimal Window (Highdry through Peaklite):**
Summer weather stabilizes. Storm frequency drops to 1-2 per month (often minor). The [[Outer Coast Packet Line]] operates on maximum schedule: ships depart every 6-8 weeks rather than 10-12 weeks. Fishing fleets fully deployed. Merchant vessels complete contracts on near-guaranteed timelines.

From captain and merchant perspective, losing 4 weeks of summer represents economic catastrophe — this is when annual revenue concentrates. A unexpected storm that cancels two planned departures can force a merchant house into financial crisis.

**Autumn Hazards (Harvestfall through Coolward):**
Autumn storm patterns shift. Cyclone systems (uncommon but severe) emerge September through November. Wind patterns change from spring predictability to autumn unpredictability. Coastal traffic remains robust (lower volume than summer but higher risk per voyage) because harvest season demands continued transport despite weather hazard.

**Winter Austerity (Colden through Thawmark):**
Northern routes (like [[Outer Coast Packet Line]] to [[Wolfpoint]]) reduce frequency by 50%. Ships operating this region accept extended transit times, potential ice damage, and mechanical stress. Southern [[Auralis]] coastal routes remain economically viable — weather is milder, storm frequency moderate, transport can continue at 70-80% of summer volume.

## Economic Implications

### Revenue Concentration

Shipping revenue concentrates in summer. A captain might earn 60-70% of annual income June-August. This creates:

- **Cash flow crisis during winters:** Most shipping workers earn minimal income October-March despite continued seasonal work.
- **Multi-seasonal contracting:** Merchants book vessels for spring-summer obligations in advance (autumn-winter prior year) to secure capacity during peak season.
- **Credit dependency:** Fishing crews, port workers, cargo handlers all depend on credit or subsistence food stores to survive winter income drought.

### Labor Volatility

Shipping employment patterns reflect seasonal demand:
- **Summer peak:** 2-3x base workforce deployed across all coastal and river operations
- **Seasonal worker recruitment (winter prior):** Settlements recruit, train, and house temporary labor starting November-December
- **Wage volatility:** Summer daily wages 1.5-2x winter rates (when work exists)
- **Child labor prevalence:** Desperation during winter drives seasonal employment of younger workers at substandard compensation

### Infrastructure Stress

Ports and settlements experience compressive stress during season transitions:

**Spring surge bottleneck:** Logging camps dump accumulated winter inventory into rivers. Downriver settlements face simultaneous arrival of timber/goods/grain that exceeds storage capacity. Processing facilities (mills, grain stores) cannot keep pace. Congestion creates 2-4 week queues where goods wait for processing assuming weather doesn't force delays.

**Autumn harvest rush:** Agricultural regions require simultaneous grain processing, storage preparation, and transport to urban buyers. This compresses normally-extended harvest timelines — a settlement might need to move 40% of annual grain volume in 6 weeks.

The seasonal infrastructure bottleneck is not fiction. It is the primary constraint limiting production increases: even settlements with excess productive capacity cannot exceed shipment volume because transport infrastructure physically cannot handle more concentrated temporal density.

## Regional Adaptations

### [[Terravelle]] River System

[[Terravelle]] optimizes for spring flood season. Settlements along [[Maren River]] pre-position massive grain stockpiles, coordinate multi-boat convoys, and deploy trained log-driving crews specifically trained for high-water season. The pre-positioned supply system is why [[Maren Valley]] communities survive winter — they accumulated transport capacity during spring flood and moved entire seasons' worth of products within a 4-6 week window.

Failure in spring season translates directly to winter food shortage. This is not hypothetical: a year with abnormally early spring thaw (1-2 weeks faster than average) can scramble logistics enough that downriver settlements receive 15-20% less grain, which cascades to downstream food rationing.

### [[Auralis]] Basin and Southern Routes

[[Auralis]] relies on more distributed coastal shipping because river systems are less powerful and river seasons less extreme. [[Hedun-Solhaven Coastal Run]] operates year-round with weather adjustments rather than seasonal shutdown. Grain moves south continuously through autumn-winter (slower, more expensive via maritime transport) rather than concentrating into spring river flood.

This distributed model is more expensive (maritime transport costs more than river bulk movement) but more resilient to seasonal variations. [[Auralis]] culture accepts higher transport costs as insurance against the risk concentration that [[Terravelle]]'s spring-surge system creates.

### Northern Isolation

[[Wolfpoint]] experiences extreme winter isolation: [[Outer Coast Packet Line]] reduces to 4-6 runs per year, weather windows shrink, and the settlement must accumulate enough supplies during brief summer-autumn window to sustain winter months of minimal external contact.

Wolfpoint's entire economy functions around this isolation principle. The preserved game, wool production, and cold-forge metalwork are specifically chosen export products because they can be pre-positioned during summer and sold continuously throughout winter when shipping has paused.

## Risk Factors and Failure Modes

### Weather Deviation

One month of extended spring rain (storm systems saturating [[Terravelle]] river regions before normal ice breakup) can delay log-driving by 2-4 weeks, compressing the entire seasonal window and creating export backlogs.

One early autumn freeze (rivers icing earlier than expected) forces end-of-season rush that exceeds infrastructure capacity.

### Cascade Effects

A late summer storm that cancels three scheduled [[Outer Coast Packet Line]] departures creates merchant-house cash flow crises — contracts require delivery by specific dates, missed shipments trigger default penalties, penalties compound if rescheduled cargo arrives late and incurs additional storage costs.

### Infrastructure Collapse

Repeated seasonal stress eventually breaks equipment: mills overheat from continuous operation during autumn crush, port facilities cracks from overloading, barge fleets suffer higher losses as expedited schedules eliminate scheduled maintenance.

Maintenance windows exist only in off-season periods when no cargo flows. This creates a structural tradeoff: seasonal infrastructure can keep pace with current volume only by postponing maintenance, which reduces capacity and reliability the following season.

## Contemporary Pressure

War preparation has disrupted seasonal patterns:

- Military shipping (supply convoys, equipment transport) takes priority allocation, reducing merchant cargo space
- Conscription removes experienced shipping workers from labor pools during peak season
- Uncertainty about route safety (military activity, border tension) forces longer transit timelines
- Insurance costs rise as casualty and loss rates increase

[[Auralis]] and [[Terravelle]] simultaneously try to maintain commerce while diverting shipping capacity to military logistics. The result is merchant schedules operating on compressed capacity with reduced labor availability, concentrated within existing narrow seasonal windows.

This is operationally unsustainable beyond 18-24 months: one bad season that coincides with military scheduling conflict exposes fragility and cascades into shortages.

## Practical Implications

For most Solumora inhabitants, seasonal shipping cycles are not abstract concepts — they determine employment availability, income timing, and survival margin during lean months.

A household's ability to survive winter depends on whether summer wages were sufficient to fund 4-5 months of minimal income. Families with members working in seasonal shipping industries time their household consumption, debt repayment, and celebration expenditures around the calendar of shipping-season revenue. 

A year with poor summer shipping (bad weather, reduced military priority allocation, infrastructure failures) means winter food rationing, skipped debt payments, and potential house insolvency.

The shipping cycle is the heartbeat of whether ordinary people remain solvent or slip into crisis.
