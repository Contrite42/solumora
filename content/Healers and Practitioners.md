Most medical work in [[Solumora]] is done by people who are competent, overbooked, and one missed payment away from losing a month of stability.

The public image of medicine centers on high-tier specialists. The operational reality is T0-T2 practitioners carrying everyday care: wound cleaning, fever management, fracture stabilization, birth support, infection watch, and recovery supervision.

## Workforce Structure

The practitioner ladder is practical rather than ceremonial.

- **T0 care workers:** assistants, herbal prep workers, transport aides, and recovery attendants.
- **T1 general healers:** walk-in assessment, standard treatment cycles, routine follow-up.
- **T2 advanced generalists:** wider diagnostics, difficult wound repair, referral triage.
- **T3+ specialists:** city chapters, guild-certified clinics, complex trauma and pathway cases.

Because T3+ staffing is thin outside major cities, T1-T2 workers make most consequential decisions about whether to escalate care.

## Training Routes

Three routes dominate:

1. **Guild apprenticeship** in [[Terravelle]] through clinic sponsorship and practical case logs.
2. **Path certification** in [[Auralis]] through [[Ascendant Path]] training tracks with formal assessments.
3. **Informal apprenticeship** in rural zones where experienced healers train kin or local assistants.

All routes produce capable practitioners. The difference is credential portability. Guild and Path certificates travel across districts. Informal training often does not, even when skill quality is equal.

## Income and Workload

Income bands vary by location and patient mix.

- T0 assistants: 8-18 [[Watts]] weekly
- T1 practitioners: 18-40 Watts weekly
- T2 practitioners: 35-70 Watts weekly
- T3 chapter practitioners: 70+ Watts weekly with volatile emergency bonuses

Workload is measured in interruptions, not scheduled appointments. Harbor and market practitioners can see 30-60 brief contacts per day during seasonal peaks. Rural practitioners see fewer patients but manage longer care windows due to travel constraints.

## Specializations at Low Tier

Specialization exists below elite tiers, usually through repetition:

- childbirth and postpartum recovery;
- wound closure for labor and dock injuries;
- heat and dehydration management in desert-edge settlements;
- infection containment in dense districts;
- chemical exposure filtering for refinery and workshop crews.

These specializations are rarely prestigious but directly tied to survival rates in working districts.

## Bottlenecks

The system's recurring bottlenecks are structural:

- credential gates limit who can bill formal institutions;
- referral queues delay T3-T4 review for pathway cases;
- supply volatility forces substitutions that reduce treatment precision;
- class bias affects who is believed when symptoms are not visibly severe.

A skilled healer can stabilize many cases. They cannot solve missing transport, missing money, or missing specialist slots.

## Risk and Attrition

Practitioners leave the field for predictable reasons:

- cumulative stress from preventable deaths;
- debt from unpaid treatment cycles;
- political pressure from guild or district patrons;
- self-injury from repeated Flux exposure.

Attrition is highest in regions that rely most heavily on a few named practitioners. When one healer withdraws, a whole district can lose effective first-line care for months.

## Daily Reality

A practitioner's day is mostly ordinary work done with insufficient slack. The heroics happen when ordinary systems fail. Those moments are memorable, but the system lives or dies on routine competence: who kept notes, who checked infection twice, who convinced a stubborn worker to return for follow-up.
