Both kingdoms regulate grimoires, but they regulate different things through different mechanisms. [[Terravelle]]'s approach is institutional and documented; [[Auralis]]'s approach is structural and relational. Neither fully controls what circulates, and neither is trying to.

## Terravelle: Guild Certification

Terravelle's regulation flows through the [[Trade Guilds]]. The core mechanism is certification: grimoires sold through commercial channels carry a guild mark that indicates they have been reviewed against a registered master copy. A Scribe who produces certified copies works within a guild-managed chain of custody — from authenticated originator to certified master to verified reproduction to marked sale.

What this regulates: quality standards in the [[Common Grimoire|Common]] and [[Uncommon Grimoire|Uncommon]] market, originator attribution, and the institutional record of who holds what. It also gives the guilds a meaningful revenue stream through certification fees and makes the guild libraries the most authoritative repositories of master copies.

What this does not regulate: copies produced outside guild channels, imports from Auralis, and high-tier texts that circulate through private arrangement rather than market sale. The guild system is a quality and attribution system, not a restriction system. It does not prohibit uncertified copies — it simply means uncertified copies cannot claim the credibility that certification provides.

The guild libraries in [[Valdenmoor]] and [[Ashford]] hold authenticated masters for most documented Common and Uncommon spells. The Ashford library has a modest Rare collection. Neither library permits copying access for Rare-tier texts without a certified purpose statement.

## Auralis: Network Control

Auralis regulates grimoires through access control rather than certification. The [[The Council of Auralis|Council]] does not operate a public certification system. Instead, it controls distribution of [[Rare Grimoire|Rare]] and above texts through Scholar-network gatekeeping: the institutions that hold master copies decide who gets copying access, and the decision is made through relationships, institutional affiliation, and assessment of the requester's purpose.

At Common and Uncommon tiers, Auralis has relatively light regulation — grimoires circulate commercially with informal quality signals rather than certified marks. The social weight placed on grimoire ownership as a status indicator does some of the work that Terravelle's certification system does institutionally, in that Auralis buyers in high-status contexts care enough about provenance to pay for quality.

At Rare tier and above, the Council's control is substantive. Access to Rare-tier masters requires Scholar network standing or [[The Advancement Corps|Advancement Corps]] sponsorship. Practitioners who are not institutionally affiliated have no formal pathway to Rare-tier originals, which is partly by design: the Council's influence over high-tier practice is one of the mechanisms by which it maintains structural authority over high-tier practitioners.

## Cross-Kingdom Gaps

Neither system has effective jurisdiction over copies that cross [[Equatorial Desert|Desert Zakros]]. A spell developed in Terravelle and certified through the guild system has no standing in Auralis's Scholar network and vice versa. This gap is not accidental — both kingdoms benefit from being the primary regulator in their own sphere, and cross-kingdom regulatory cooperation would reduce that control.

In practice, the gap means that cross-kingdom copies circulate freely in the [[Black Market Grimoires|informal market]], that guild certification means nothing in Auralis, and that Scholar-network vouching means nothing in Terravelle. Practitioners operating in both kingdoms maintain separate relationships with each system and accept that neither fully serves their needs.

## Restricted Disciplines

Both kingdoms maintain informal restrictions on certain spell disciplines — primarily high-tier mind-affecting spells, specific Flux-structural manipulation tools, and spells that have been used in documented atrocities or military incidents. In Terravelle these are noted in the guild restriction ledger; in Auralis they are held as Scholar-network consensus about what should not be freely distributed.

The restrictions are not absolute. They make production and sale of restricted-discipline texts commercially unavailable through official channels, which redirects that demand to the informal market rather than eliminating it.
