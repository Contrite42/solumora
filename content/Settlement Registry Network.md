# Settlement Registry Network

The **Settlement Registry Network** is [[Terravelle]]'s distributed record-keeping system operating through city-level registries in [[Valdenmoor]], [[Ashford]], [[Greyveil]], and several smaller settlements. Each registry maintains documentation of births, deaths, [[Control Tier]] certifications, guild memberships, land holdings, contract precedents, and [[Guild Arbitration Bench]] rulings for its locality.

The Network does not centralize these records. It makes them searchable and cross-referenceable across settlements through shared standards and reciprocal access agreements.

## What the Registry Does

Each settlement registry performs core documentation functions:

**Vital records**: Birth registrations establishing legal identity and family lineage. Death records enabling property transfer and contract termination. Marriage contracts (where formalized rather than handled through [[Covenant of Measure]] ceremony). These records create the documentary foundation for inheritance disputes, guild membership verification, and land title claims.

**Control Tier certification**: Official documentation of practitioner tier assessments, updated when [[Flux Users|practitioners]] undergo retesting or achieve higher tiers through demonstrated capability. Certification records are essential for guild admissions, contract bidding (higher-tier practitioners command higher rates), and legal accountability when Flux use causes harm.

**Guild membership records**: Documentation of which guilds a practitioner belongs to, what certifications they hold within each guild, disciplinary actions if applicable, and membership status changes. These records enable cross-city guild coordination — a Valdenmoor merchant can verify that an Ashford contractor holds claimed certifications before signing contracts.

**Land and property records**: Title documentation, transfer history, encumbrances (liens, easements, shared rights). Critical for resolving property disputes and preventing fraudulent sales. [[Terravelle]]'s guild system depends on stable property rights because guilds operate through chartered facilities and owned infrastructure.

**Commercial contracts**: Archived contracts for transactions above specified value thresholds (typically 50+ [[Watts]]). Provides precedent for dispute resolution and documentation that contracts were executed as claimed if later contested.

**Arbitration rulings**: Comprehensive log of [[Guild Arbitration Bench]] decisions including case summaries, rulings, reasoning, and precedent citations. Makes Bench precedent searchable for guild members, merchants, and arbitrators in other cities.

The registry does not police accuracy — it registers what parties claim. If someone registers birth documentation with falsified parents, the registry records the claim. If the falsification is later proven, the registry updates records but does not retroactively verify all prior entries. The system assumes honest reporting with correction mechanisms for discovered fraud rather than comprehensive verification at entry.

## How the Network Functions

Each city's settlement registry operates independently under local guild or civic authority:

**Valdenmoor Registry**: Largest operation, 9 permanent staff, handles highest volume of records due to city size and commercial activity. Registrar [[Korven Mast]] coordinates. Funded through registry fees (2-5 Watts per registration) and Valdenmoor Guild Council budget allocation.

**Ashford Registry**: 6 permanent staff, handles both civic records and cross-kingdom commerce documentation. Registrar [[Seld Vorn]] coordinates. Unique among Terravelle registries because border position requires coordination with [[Auralis]] documentation standards for cross-kingdom contract enforceability.

**Greyveil Registry**: 4 permanent staff, emphasizes agricultural land records and [[Covenant of Measure]] ceremony documentation. Registrar [[Lanna Kel]] coordinates. Maintains closer relationship with Covenant than other registries because rural Terravelle relies heavily on Covenant practitioners for governance functions.

**Smaller settlement registries**: Operate through part-time guild clerks or civic administrators rather than dedicated registry staff. Submit records to nearest major city registry quarterly for inclusion in distributed network.

The registries achieve network functionality through **shared documentation standards** and **reciprocal access agreements**:

### Shared Standards

Registries use common formats for core record types. Birth registration follows identical template across all settlements. Guild certification records use standard notation developed by [[Guild Scrivener Network]] and adopted by all major registries. Arbitration ruling logs follow format established by Guild Arbitration Bench in Valdenmoor and replicated by Ashford and Greyveil.

Standardization allows registries to exchange records without translation. A Valdenmoor merchant requesting verification of an Ashford contractor's guild certification receives documentation in familiar format, enabling rapid assessment without specialized interpretation.

Standards evolve through quarterly **Registry Coordination Sessions** where registrars from major cities meet to discuss documentation challenges, propose format revisions, and maintain consistency. The sessions are voluntary — no central authority compels participation — operating through professional consensus that shared standards serve everyone's interests.

### Reciprocal Access

Registries grant each other access to their archives. A guild coordinator in Ashford can send inquiry to Valdenmoor Registry requesting verification of practitioner's claimed tier certification. Valdenmoor Registry responds within **3-5 business days** (2-3 days for urgent requests costing additional fee).

Access fees fund registry operations: **3-8 Watts per inquiry** depending on complexity and urgency. A simple yes/no verification of guild membership costs 3 Watts. A comprehensive practitioner background check including tier history, guild disciplinary records, and contract performance documentation costs 8 Watts and requires more extensive staff time.

The fee structure creates mutual incentive for cooperation. Valdenmoor Registry earns revenue from Ashford inquiries, Ashford earns from Valdenmoor inquiries. Both registries maintain service quality because poor responsiveness damages reputation and reduces future inquiry revenue.

### Documentation Couriers

Physical records still dominate despite [[Flux]]-based communication development. Most registry documentation exists on paper because permanent records require material permanence. Flux-recorded data degrades over decades; paper endures across generations if properly maintained.

Registries use **dedicated documentation couriers** for cross-city record transfers. Couriers carry certified copies of vital records, contract archives, and precedent documentation between settlements. [[Northern Relay Office Consortium]] handles most courier operations under contract with registry network.

Urgent documentation (needed within 1-2 days rather than standard 5-7 days) travels via priority courier at premium rates. Legal disputes requiring rapid access to archived contracts or arbitration precedent routinely pay courier premiums to avoid waiting for standard delivery.

## Regional Variations

The three major city registries emphasize different domains reflecting their economic contexts:

**Valdenmoor** prioritizes commercial contract archives and guild certification records. High merchant activity volume means contract disputes occur frequently, requiring robust precedent documentation. Registry maintains searchable contract archive going back 60+ years with detailed indexing by transaction type, parties involved, and dispute outcomes.

**Ashford** prioritizes cross-kingdom documentation coordination. Maintains parallel records in both Terravelle and [[Auralis]] formats to support cross-border commerce. [[Seld Vorn]]'s registry staff include two specialists trained in Auralis administrative standards who translate documentation between kingdom formats.

**Greyveil** prioritizes agricultural land records and Covenant ceremony documentation. Land transfers occur frequently as farms change hands, requiring clear title chains. Covenant ceremony records (births, marriages, coming-of-age rituals) carry legal weight in rural Terravelle where Covenant authority exceeds guild presence.

## Archival Integrity and Fraud Prevention

The registry network's distributed structure creates resilience but also vulnerability. No central authority verifies that all registries maintain consistent standards or prevent fraudulent entries.

Prevention mechanisms include:

**Entry fees**: Charging 2-5 Watts per registration discourages casual fraud. Someone attempting to register false documentation must pay repeatedly for each fraudulent claim, creating cost barrier.

**Registrar reputation**: Registry officials operate under their own names. [[Korven Mast]]'s professional reputation depends on Valdenmoor Registry maintaining accurate records. Registrars discovering after-the-fact fraud update records and note correction, maintaining institutional credibility through transparent error handling.

**Cross-verification requirements**: High-stakes records (Control Tier certifications, guild membership, land titles) often require corroborating documentation from multiple sources. A guild certification claim requires both applicant statement and guild administrator confirmation. Land title transfer requires both seller and buyer signatures plus witness attestation.

**Precedent documentation**: Guild Arbitration Bench rulings and commercial contract archives create paper trails that subsequent fraud must circumvent. Someone fabricating guild membership faces difficulty if no corresponding entry exists in guild's own records or if claimed contract history doesn't match archived documentation.

Fraud still occurs, particularly in:
- **Rural vital records** where small settlement registries lack resources for thorough verification.
- **Historical certification claims** where practitioners claim tier certifications from decades past when records were less systematically maintained.
- **Cross-kingdom documentation** where Terravelle registries cannot easily verify Auralis claims or vice versa.

[[Korven Mast]] documents approximately **12-15 fraud cases annually** in Valdenmoor Registry records, most discovered when discrepancies arise during dispute resolution or cross-verification inquiries. Discovered fraud results in record correction, potential guild discipline if guild member involved, and possible Crown legal action if financial harm substantial.

## Relationship with Guild System

The Settlement Registry Network operates in parallel with [[Trade Guilds]] internal record-keeping rather than subordinate to it. Guilds maintain their own membership records, apprenticeship documentation, and certification databases. The registry archives guild records for cross-city searchability and permanent preservation, but does not control guild documentation.

This creates occasional tension: guild records and registry records should match but sometimes diverge. A guild updates member certification status in their internal systems but delays notifying registry. Registry contains outdated information until updated. Disputes about whose records are authoritative get resolved through [[Guild Arbitration Bench]] when parties contest.

Most guilds cooperate with registry network because searchable archives serve guild interests:
- **Cross-city verification**: Guilds benefit from ability to verify that practitioners claiming membership in other cities actually hold claimed credentials.
- **Precedent access**: Contract and arbitration archives help guilds draft better contracts and resolve disputes faster through precedent reference.
- **Institutional memory**: Registries maintain records across decades, preserving documentation after original guild administrators retire or die.

[[Guild Coordination Office]] relies heavily on registry archives when drafting implementation directives. Historical contract precedent helps coordinators understand which directive language has caused problems previously. Arbitration rulings show which coordination approaches guilds accepted versus contested.

## Differences from Auralis Record-Keeping

[[Auralis]] operates centralized registry systems. [[Council Orders Registry]] maintains comprehensive directive logs in [[Solhaven]]. [[Aldrane Academy]] maintains centralized practitioner certification database. Land records are kept by district administrators reporting to Crown authority.

**Terravelle's distributed network** creates duplication and potential inconsistency but also resilience. If Valdenmoor Registry building burned down (has happened twice historically, most recently 80 years ago), Ashford and Greyveil registries preserve cross-referenced records enabling partial reconstruction. Auralis's centralized systems face single-point failure risk that Terravelle's distribution avoids.

**Auralis registries** are Crown-controlled institutions. Updates, standards, access policies are set by central authority and applied uniformly.

**Terravelle registries** are locally controlled. Each city's registry operates under that city's guild or civic authority. Consistency emerges through voluntary coordination, not hierarchical enforcement.

**Auralis practitioners** expect centralized verification. Any Crown institution can query Academy records for practitioner certifications. Cross-institution verification is fast and systematic.

**Terravelle practitioners** expect distributed verification. Cross-city queries require inter-registry inquiries costing 3-8 Watts and taking 3-5 days. Verification is slower but also less vulnerable to institutional capture by single authority.

## Resource Constraints and Wartime Pressures

The registry network operates on modest budgets funded through filing fees and local guild allocations. Total annual network operations cost approximately **800 Watts** across all registries (Valdenmoor ~320 Watts, Ashford ~280 Watts, Greyveil ~180 Watts, smaller settlements ~20 Watts combined).

Revenue from cross-registry inquiries partially offsets costs but does not fully fund operations. Guild Council allocations cover gaps. During resource constraints, registries reduce services rather than increase fees because high fees restrict access and undermine registry usefulness.

Current pressures include:

**Increased inquiry volume**: Cross-city verification requests rising as wartime disruptions create more commercial disputes requiring documentation. [[Korven Mast]] reports Valdenmoor inquiry volume increased **22% over 18 months** while staff capacity remained constant, producing longer response times (now 4-6 days standard instead of 3-5 days).

**Documentation backlog**: Registry entries accumulating faster than staff can properly index and archive. Ashford Registry experiencing particular stress due to border trade documentation complexity — [[Seld Vorn]] reports **3-4 week delays** for complex cross-kingdom contract archiving that previously took 1-2 weeks.

**Courier disruption**: [[Northern Relay Office Consortium]] experiencing delivery delays due to road conditions and increased security checks on documentation couriers. Physical record transfers taking longer, reducing registry responsiveness for urgent inquiries.

**Fraud increase**: Resource stress correlating with increased documentation fraud attempts. Korven notes fraud cases rising from 12 annually to 18 over past year, straining verification capacity and creating additional backlog.

**Standards drift**: Registry Coordination Sessions happening less regularly (now semi-annually instead of quarterly) as registrars face operational demands reducing time for professional coordination. Risk emerging that documentation standards begin diverging across cities as each registry adapts independently to local pressures.

Korven Mast and Seld Vorn project that without additional staff or reduced inquiry volume, **inquiry response times will extend to 8-10 days within next 12 months**, making registry verification slow enough that commercial parties may bypass official documentation in favor of informal reputation networks and personal guarantees, undermining registry system credibility.

The registries coordinate informally when able. Korven, Seld, and [[Lanna Kel]] maintain correspondence sharing practices for handling increased caseloads, discussing documentation fraud patterns, and maintaining standards consistency. The coordination is voluntary professional network rather than institutional requirement, persisting because registrars recognize their operations are interdependent.
