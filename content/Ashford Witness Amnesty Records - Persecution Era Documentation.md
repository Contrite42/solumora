# Ashford Witness Amnesty Records - Persecution Era Documentation

The [[Ashford Witness Amnesty Records]] represent the most complete institutional documentation of [[The Persecution Era|Persecution Era]] survival networks. They exist because [[Ashford]] magistrate leadership made deliberate choice to preserve rather than destroy records of refuge protection.

## Why Records Were Preserved

After persecution formally ended (~200 years ago from current era), most kingdoms destroyed refuge network documentation to prevent liability claims and political embarrassment.

Ashford magistrate [[Krell Voss]] (contemporary of current-era's [[Teren Voss|Verath]]) made opposite choice: institutionalize the records. His reasoning: "If we erase what we did, we ensure we repeat it."

Records were sealed in restricted archive with access limited to magistrate successors and, eventually, the [[Ashford Coexistence Oversight Commission]].

## Record Categories

**Protection Network Documentation** (1,200+ entries)
- Refugee names and assumed identities across 150-year period
- Locations of active refuges and support structures
- Magistrate personnel involved in protection (names, dates, actions)
- Economic flows supporting refugee populations
- Lineage documentation for refugees with known descendants

**Hunt Action Records** (800+ entries)
- Lantern Hunt coordinates, dates, declared targets
- Actual sweeps conducted vs. reported sweeps
- Known false positive prosecutions
- Documented instances of magistrate misdirection

**Community Protection Decisions** (600+ entries)
- Communities on record as deliberately harboring refugees
- Institutions (guilds, trade companies, estates) with refugee connections
- Individuals prosecuted for sympathy vs. individuals protected through magistrate discretion

## Historical Revelations

Records confirm:
- Five High Demons continuously resident in recognized locations (three identified, two unconfirmed)
- Approximately 8,000-12,000 refugees sheltered across 150-year period
- Magistrate protection was deliberate policy, not random compassion
- Multiple communities made organized decisions to protect specific High Demons as community members

Records also document systematic trauma:
- Families separated when some members fled to refuges, others remained in official communities
- Generations of psychological damage from institutional paranoia
- Economic productivity lost to institutional purging of suspected sympathizers

## Modern Access and Impact

The records were gradually declassified starting 100 years ago, becoming fully public 30 years ago.

They serve as foundational documentation for:
- [[Survivor Lineage Preservation|Current survivor lineage identification]]
- [[Emergency Reintegration Mandate]] justification for rapid repair protocols
- [[Framework Retrenchment Defense Protocol]] arguments against institutional amnesia
- Legal recognition of persecution-era survivors and their descendants

Possession of Ashford records is why the city played disproportionate role in current coexistence negotiations. Ashford had proof of institution-level decision-making; other regions had only denial or destruction.

## Contested Aspects

Some groups argue the records:
- Selectively include certain communities while excluding others
- Minimize magistrate violence toward refugees during hunts
- Disproportionately document Ashford networks while ignoring other regional structures

Archivists counter that incompleteness reflects what was preserved, not what happened. Ashford destroyed some records too. The question is whether partial truth is more useful than deliberate forgetting.
