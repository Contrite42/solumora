Guild apprenticeship is the most common bridge between childhood learning and skilled work in [[Terravelle]], and a major secondary route in parts of [[Auralis]] where trade districts operate with strong guild presence.

An apprenticeship is not just training. It is labor, screening, socialization, and economic dependency in one contract.

## Entry Conditions

Most guild tracks screen for:

- basic literacy and arithmetic;
- attendance reliability;
- household references;
- tier fit for specific work hazards.

T0 and T1 applicants are common and often preferred for process-stable work. T2-T3 candidates are routed into technically demanding lanes when available.

## Core Track Types

Recurring tracks include:

- port and warehouse logistics;
- ledger and contract copy workflow;
- workshop production and repair;
- route support for caravan and courier chains;
- low-tier safety support in Flux-adjacent operations.

Not all tracks promise upward mobility. Many produce durable, modest-income stability instead.

## Pay and Dependency

Apprentices are usually paid below full-worker rate for one to three years while carrying near-worker output expectations.

This creates a family paradox:

- apprenticeship can raise lifetime earnings;
- short-term apprentice wages may be lower than immediate unskilled labor.

Families with no savings are pushed toward direct labor even when apprenticeship is better long-term.

## Completion and Failure Modes

Common completion blockers:

- household income shock forcing immediate full-wage work;
- relocation and transport disruption;
- injury or chronic illness without recovery buffer;
- sponsor withdrawal during demand downturn.

Completion rates are highest where guilds run retention funds and lowest where apprentices absorb all risk individually.

## Terravelle vs Auralis

In [[Terravelle]], guild apprenticeship carries broad legitimacy and local adaptability. In [[Auralis]], apprenticeship often competes with [[Ascendant Path]] credential channels and can be seen as lower-status despite strong job outcomes.

The practical result is similar labor placement with different social prestige labels.

## Bottleneck

The primary bottleneck is mentor bandwidth. Skilled workers can train only so many apprentices without output loss. During high-demand cycles, training quality drops as mentors are pulled into production targets.

Where mentor capacity erodes, apprentices finish with narrower skills and lower progression potential.
