# Lenna Tors

**Lenna Tors** coordinates wool trade between [[Upland Herding Network]] and [[Wolfpoint]]'s textile workshop, managing the receiving operations that convert raw wool from scattered herding families into finished textiles for export. She is fifty-two years old, [[Control Tier|Tier 2]], and has run wool operations for eleven years. Her job is preventing bottlenecks: if too much wool arrives simultaneously, processing saturates and families wait days for payment. If deliveryis too sparse, textile workers sit idle and export commitments risk delays.

The Network generates approximately 20-25% of [[Wolfpoint]]'s physical export revenue through wool textiles. Lenna makes sure wool flows smoothly from upland camps to southern buyers without creating operational failures at either end.

## Wool Quality Assessment

Herding families bring wool to Wolfpoint's trade posts seasonally (spring shearing produces the bulk of annual supply). Lenna evaluates incoming wool for:
- **Fleece density** (thicker wool from well-managed breeding produces premium textile stock).
- **Cleanliness** (contamination from poor storage reduces processing efficiency).
- **Processing readiness** (wool requires initial cleaning and carding before textile work can begin).

Quality assessment determines pricing: premium wool commands 30-40% higher rates than standard-quality equivalents. Lenna's evaluations are respected across the Network because she applies standards consistently and explains quality gaps when herding families dispute assessments.

## Balancing Flow

Wool production is seasonal. Textile processing capacity is constant. Lenna's challenge is matching variable supply to steady demand.

She adjusts timing through pricing signals and delivery scheduling:
- **Early-season bonuses** — herding families delivering wool in the first two weeks after shearing receive 10% pricing premium, incentivizing early delivery before the seasonal surge.
- **Storage coordination** — Lenna schedules deliveries so wool arrives as textile workers finish previous batches, minimizing both idle capacity and overwhelming surges.
- **Quality prioritization** — when volume exceeds processing capacity, premium wool gets processed first, ensuring highest-value export inventory reaches [[Outer Coast Packet Line]] cargo windows.

This flow management has become sophisticated over eleven years. Most herding families now coordinate deliveries with Lenna's schedules voluntarily — they learned that coordination produces faster payment and better pricing than showing up unpredictably.

## Relationship with Herding Families

Lenna maintains working relationships with approximately 40-50 herding families across the Network. She knows which families deliver consistently high-quality wool, which are developing operations that might improve over time, and which produce marginal-quality stock that Wolfpoint accepts only when overall supply runs low.

[[Wolfpoint Trade Registry]] uses Lenna's quality assessments to determine grain allocation priorities: families delivering premium wool receive preferential access to imported grain through [[Ashford Supply Contract]] relationships. This creates economic incentive for quality improvement beyond direct wool pricing.

Lenna participates in Network seasonal meetings where herding families coordinate range-sharing agreements. Her role is explaining Wolfpoint's wool needs and market conditions, giving families information that informs their breeding and management decisions. This transparency builds trust and improves supply-demand alignment over multi-year timescales.

## Economic Dependency Management

The Network depends on [[Wolfpoint]] for grain access — herding families cannot grow grain in cold upland zones. [[Wolfpoint Trade Registry]] maintains grain reserves (funded through wool export revenue) and prioritizes Network families during shortage periods.

Lenna implements this prioritization: when grain imports face delays, she coordinates with [[Renn Taska]] about which herding families receive allocation first, typically favoring families with multi-year quality delivery history and those positioned to support Network stability through range coordination leadership.

This dependency is reciprocal: Wolfpoint depends on the Network for textile exports and supplementary food supply through herding-family meat and dairy production. Lenna's job includes maintaining relationships so the dependency remains cooperative rather than extractive.

## What She Sees

Eleven years of wool operations give Lenna visibility into trends most people miss:
- Herding family turnover (new operations attempting cold-zone pastoral work, many failing within 2-3 years).
- Range pressure signals (when wool quality declines across multiple families simultaneously, often indicates grass degradation or disease spread).
- Market demand shifts (changes in southern buyer preferences for wool types, thickness, or processing states).

She reports these patterns to Renn Taska quarterly. The intelligence informs [[Wolfpoint Trade Registry]]'s strategic decisions about export diversification and supply-security planning.

## Daily Work

Lenna spends most days at the wool receiving facility: evaluating incoming deliveries, negotiating pricing with herding families, coordinating with textile workers about processing schedules, and managing the documentation that tracks wool flow from upland camps to exported finished goods.

She reviews quality standards with new herding families attempting to enter the Network. She confirms next week's delivery schedule with established families. She updates processing capacity projections for Renn Taska's quarterly review. The wool flows. Wolfpoint's textile exports continue.
