# Corridor Stabilization Treaty

The Corridor Stabilization Treaty (CST) is the first formal bilateral treaty signed after the post-revelation ceasefire period, codifying rules for dimensional-risk governance, military movement constraints, and mixed-species technical cooperation.

It was ratified 176 days after the first casualty, replacing rolling emergency clauses in the [[Ashford Emergency Ceasefire Framework]] with renewable but legally durable treaty articles.

## Strategic Purpose

The treaty addresses a central post-crisis problem: emergency frameworks prevented immediate collapse, but their legitimacy degraded over time under factional pressure. The CST converts crisis improvisation into enforceable structure while preserving rapid-response channels.

## Core Articles

### Article I - Red-Band Corridor Law
- Permanently designates high-risk corridor sectors as treaty-governed zones.
- Requires bilateral authorization for high-tier movement in designated sectors.
- Defines unauthorized movement as treaty breach with automatic incident review.

### Article II - Joint Technical Authority
- Elevates the [[Joint Rift Monitoring Cell]] from emergency body to treaty institution.
- Grants binding risk classifications for corridor movement windows.
- Requires publication of six-hour risk bulletins and ten-day incident audits.

### Article III - Crisis Intervention Protocol
- Establishes lawful pathway for extraordinary technical intervention, including non-human actors.
- Requires predeclared intervention limits, observer presence when feasible, and post-action parameter logs.
- Imposes proportionality review through civilian oversight layer.

### Article IV - Civil Safeguard Guarantees
- Incorporates anti-persecution constraints: no identity-screening ordinances, no rumor-trigger detention, no association-chain detention expansion.
- Requires judicial evidentiary standards for any non-human threat investigation.
- Mandates sanctions for suppression of contradictory technical evidence.

### Article V - Market and Logistics Continuity
- Protects essential corridor trade lanes under technical clearance windows.
- Requires synchronized closure notices and contingency routing publication.
- Prohibits strategic use of risk bulletins for disguised economic blockade.

## Enforcement and Breach Handling

The CST uses a three-stage breach mechanism:
1. **Immediate hold:** JRMC issues temporary movement freeze in affected sectors.
2. **Joint review:** technical and civic teams publish preliminary finding in 24 hours.
3. **Sanction tier:** graduated penalties (operational restriction, compensation mandates, command-level censure).

Repeated bad-faith violations trigger mandatory treaty conference under public docket conditions.

## Political Tradeoffs

The treaty succeeds by distributing discomfort:
- hardline military blocs lose unilateral movement freedom,
- demon-protection maximalists accept oversight and audit constraints,
- civic institutions accept faster exception windows than normal peacetime law.

No faction receives preferred endstate. The treaty remains viable because each faction can survive it better than it can survive renewed cascade risk.

## Relationship to Existing Structures

The CST supersedes emergency movement clauses in the [[Ashford Emergency Ceasefire Framework]] but preserves its communication architecture.

It formalizes coordination with:
- [[Joint Rift Monitoring Cell]] (technical authority),
- [[Ashford Coexistence Oversight Commission]] (civil oversight),
- emerging adjudication body: [[Anti-Persecution Compliance Tribunal]].

## Current Status

The treaty is active with initial six-month review cycle. Early compliance is mixed but improving, with two minor breach findings resolved through sanction-tier one measures and no corridor-level collapse events since ratification.

Its durability remains contingent on whether institutions continue to treat technical truth as binding when politically inconvenient.
