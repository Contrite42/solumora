# Ruin Salvage Operations

Ruin salvage operations systematically recover pre-collapse artifacts, construction materials, and salvaged mechanical components from ancient sites scattered throughout the [[Equatorial Desert]] periphery and accessible archaeological zones. The sector operates at higher risk and lower capital predictability than established extraction industries, but commands premium pricing for rare and Flux-reactive materials unavailable through current resource production.

## Salvage Sites and Accessibility

**Desert interior ruins** (primary sites) are geologically extensive and artifact-rich but suffer from high access cost, environmental hazards, and uncertain ownership. [[Halveth Guide Bureau]] manages expedition logistics for institutional parties; independent salvage operations typically operate through informal desert-guide relationships.

**Accessible archaeological sites** (secondary locations) are scattered throughout inhabited regions — abandoned settlements, tunnel systems, buried construction sites accessible without full desert-crossing expedition support. These sites have lower exploration cost but also lower artifact density and typically lower-value findings.

**Urban ruins** (tertiary sites) in collapsed settlement zones contain building materials and mechanical components but have largely been stripped by previous generations, offering diminishing salvage opportunity.

## Recovered Materials

**Flux-reactive metals** (pre-collapse origin, unclear source) are premium salvage material commanding 5-10x standard ore pricing. Recovered materials typically show evidence of intentional modification not understood by contemporary metalworking — weight-efficient construction, peculiar crystalline structures, unusual corrosion resistance. Institutional buyers (military operations, high-tier Scholars) prioritize acquisition; market limitations prevent public supply.

**Pre-collapse ceramics and composite materials** demonstrate material science exceeding current understanding. Limited commercial market; primarily academic and institutional research value. Occasionally salvaged for architectural specialty applications (unique visual properties, unknown durability).

**Mechanical components** (gears, joints, articulation systems) are salvaged primarily for reverse-engineering research and occasionally for specialized equipment manufacture. Material quality often exceeds current production, suggesting superior alloy or manufacturing techniques.

**Structural materials** (stone, pre-shaped materials) are salvaged for architectural applications or raw material use. Economically marginal unless operation logistics permit transport concentrating highest-density valuable material.

**Glass and lens systems** salvaged from interior sites contain optical properties not fully replicated in current manufacturing. Premium market for intact or repairable systems; destroyed components salvaged for raw material.

## Salvage Operations Structure

**Institutional salvage expeditions** (Auralis Council backed, occasionally Terravelle merchant-house backed) operate with professional guidance teams (T3-T5 practitioners, experienced desert operatives), equipment resources, and military escort capacity. Typical expedition size: 8-15 personnel, 2-4 week duration, 1,000-2,000 Watts cost. Results: highly variable material recovery, but higher-probability success and lower casualty rates.

**Independent salvage operations** (merchant-backed or consortium-backed) operate with contract guides, 2-8 personnel, 2-3 week timelines, 500-1,000 Watts cost. Results: higher material variability, moderate casualty risk, frequently inadequate for complex sites requiring substantial team stability.

**Prospecting partnerships** (small independent groups, 2-4 person teams) operate with minimal funding, personal risk management, minimal professional guidance. Occasionally discover valuable sites or artifact concentrations; frequently discover nothing, occasionally suffer fatal casualties.

## Operational Risks

**Environmental hazards** — Desert heat, rift phenomena, creature encounters, water scarcity. Standard crossing risk multiplied by site-access time and interior-site duration. Casualty rate typically 5-15% per expedition depending on site location and party capabilities.

**Structural hazards** — Ancient site structural degradation creates collapse risk. Unexplored interior passages may contain trapped gases or water. Vertical shafts and pit traps are common pre-collapse architectural features. Casualty rate from structural failure: 2-5% per expedition at accessible sites, significantly higher at deep interior sites.

**Artifact curse theories** — Persistent folklore suggests recovered artifacts carry misfortune or supernatural risk. No systematic evidence supports theories; nonetheless, some recovered materials (particularly Flux-reactive components) show unusual field effects when activated. Research into recovered artifacts is ongoing; risks to handlers remain partially unknown.

**Claim disputes** — Salvage sites lack clear ownership; territorial claims frequently conflict between institutions, merchants, and independent teams. Disputes occasionally escalate to violence. [[Border Coordination Offices]] manage dispute resolution with limited authority; enforcement is weak.

## Economic Viability

**Systematic salvage** (organized operations with multi-expedition history) can achieve profitability through recovered material volume, experiential site knowledge, and operational efficiency development. Established salvage teams typically operate at 30-50% profit margins on total expedition cost.

**Sporadic salvage** (irregular operations responding to discovery or opportunity) operates at high risk and uncertain returns. Most sporadic operations break even or lose investment; occasional spectacular discoveries drive interest despite poor statistical outcomes.

**Prospecting** (independent small-team operations) is economically marginal and frequently unprofitable. Attracts participants motivated by prospect of catastrophic-return discovery rather than reasonable economic expectation.

**Institutional salvage** is justified by research value and strategic material access rather than economic profitability. Costs are absorbed as infrastructure investment or research budget; material recovered is valued at cost plus research premium rather than commodity market price.

## Contemporary Pressure

**Military material demand** — Pre-collapse Flux-reactive metals are militarily valuable; [[Auralis]] institutional operations are aggressively pursuing interior-site expeditions specifically targeting Flux-reactive material recovery. Increased expeditionary activity is creating congestion at accessible sites and driving deeper exploration into higher-risk interior zones.

**Research prioritization shift** — Academic institutions traditionally prioritized artifact recovery for research. Military demand is shifting institutional resources toward material-extraction focus rather than knowledge-preservation focus, affecting expedition objectives and selection criteria.

**Guide supply constraint** — Experienced desert guides are conscripted for military operations or diverted toward institutional expeditions with higher compensation. Independent salvage operations are experiencing difficulty securing qualified guide services.

**Site degradation** — Increased expedition frequency at accessible sites is causing accelerated structural degradation and artifact loss. Some historically productive sites are approaching point of collapse, reducing future salvage opportunity.

Ruin salvage is transitioning from exploratory research-focused activity to extraction-focused industrial sector optimized for immediate material recovery rather than knowledge preservation or sustainable-use management.
