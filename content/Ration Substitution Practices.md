# Ration Substitution Practices

**Ration Substitution Practices** are household and district-level methods for maintaining calorie continuity when standard allocations thin, arrive late, or shift in composition.

They are not famine protocols. They are pressure-management routines used during prolonged instability in [[Border Trade]] and inland distribution networks.

## Baseline Pattern

Most low- and mid-income households in [[Terravelle]] and [[Auralis]] operate from a three-layer food plan:

- primary staples (grain, legumes, preserved protein)
- replacement staples (lower-preference but stable alternatives)
- emergency fillers (cheap volume foods with low nutrition density)

Substitution begins at layer two long before official scarcity language appears.

## Common Substitutions

Observed substitutions under routine strain include:

- wheat allocation replaced by mixed barley-root blends
- preserved fish replaced by pulse-heavy broth rotations
- cooking oil reduced and replaced by thickening starch methods
- fresh produce windows shortened and replaced with brined stores

These changes preserve meal count but reduce micronutrient quality over time.

## Household Adaptation Logic

Households optimize for continuity, not nutrition ideal.

Priority order is usually:

1. maintain predictable meal timing for workers and children
2. preserve enough protein for manual labor capacity
3. defer personal adult intake to protect dependent members

The economic threshold for this shift is often small: a weekly increase of 3-7 [[Watts]] in staple bundle cost can trigger long-cycle substitution behavior.

## Administrative Interface

Substitution decisions are shaped by [[Queue Management Offices]] and allocation policy from the [[Basin Grain Allocation Board]].

When claim windows compress or district quotas rotate, households adopt defensive purchasing patterns that amplify local shortages even without national supply collapse.

## Care-System Impacts

Clinical teams in [[Clinical Care Systems]] report predictable downstream effects:

- increased fatigue and slow-healing complaints
- higher pediatric low-weight flags in dense labor districts
- increased triage pressure for preventable complications

These are not dramatic crisis markers. They are cumulative performance losses spread across ordinary households.

## Cultural Framing

In both kingdoms, substitution is framed as responsibility rather than deprivation.

That framing preserves social order and reduces panic, but it can delay policy correction because visible compliance is mistaken for stable capacity.
