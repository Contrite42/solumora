Working notes and observations maintained by [[Sable Morn]], trader, [[Valdenmoor]]–[[Ashford]]–[[Greyveil]] circuit. Begun in her third year on the route.

---

**Early spring, southern leg — Valdenmoor to Ashford**

The road east out of Valdenmoor is wide for the first forty miles and narrow for the next sixty. The narrowing happens at the river crossing at Calden, where the bridge was built for foot traffic and hasn't been widened since. I know three other operators who run the eastern road. We have all had the same argument with the same guild representative about the bridge width. The guild representative is sympathetic. The bridge is the same.

Past Calden the road drops into the river valley and follows the water for most of the day. Good road when dry. In early spring it is not dry. I add half a day to the eastern leg in spring and price my Ashford quotes accordingly. Clients who ask why get the explanation. Clients who don't ask pay the same rate.

The wayhouse at Brenn's Crossing has changed ownership twice in my fifteen years on this route. The current owner is the best of the three and keeps the east-facing room free of the infestation problem that plagued both previous operators. I ask for the east room. If it's occupied I sleep in the wagon. This is not sentiment — the east room gets morning light that makes the early start easier, and I start early.

---

**Ashford — turnaround notes**

The eastern docks have a rhythm I've learned to read. The first three days of the month are slow — cargo arriving from the northern runs needs processing time before it's ready to move inland. The last week of the month is fast and crowded and I don't enjoy it but the freight rates are better. I time my arrivals for the second week when I can, which gives me four to five days to work contacts and load out before the month-end rush.

Dock master Pold on the secondary wharf has a system for undocumented cargo that I don't ask about and that has never created a problem for me. I mention this as a note, not a recommendation. Problems with undocumented cargo create problems for the operator, not for the dock master.

There is a woman who works the eastern docks whose name I have never learned in fifteen years. She loads and unloads. She knows where everything goes without asking. When there's a question about a cargo, she knows the answer, and the question is asked of her by people who have been there longer than I have. I have watched her work. The docks would be slower without her and I suspect the docks know this.

---

**Northern leg — Ashford to Greyveil**

The road north from Ashford follows the coast for the first day and a half before cutting inland through the forest belt. The coastal section is the nicest stretch on the circuit in good weather and the worst stretch in bad weather. The wind comes off the water and is indifferent to cargoes that need to stay dry.

At the forest cutoff there is a wayhouse run by a family called Drest — no relation to anyone of consequence that I am aware of — that has been there since before I started this run. The food is adequate. The barn is clean. The family eldest has offered to buy my wagon route twice in ten years. The price has increased both times. I have not sold. I tell this to myself as evidence that the route is worth keeping.

Greyveil approaches through the ore formations east of the city. The road through the formations smells of iron after rain in a way I have never found described anywhere but which is one of the specific things I know about this road. The outfitter family Marren at the north end of Greyveil's trade district has what I need for the return leg, has had it for twelve years, and will have it when I arrive before I've told them I'm coming. At some point in the past decade they stopped asking what I needed and started having it ready. I don't know when this happened.

---

**Return south — Greyveil to Valdenmoor**

There is a section of the inland road between Greyveil and the first river crossing where the trees are old enough that the canopy closes over the road completely. In summer this is relief from the heat. In autumn it is one of the more specific things I know about this part of the world: the light in that canopy in the second week of autumn, when the leaves are turning but the road is still dry, filtered through yellow and orange in a way that makes the wagon shadow look like something else entirely.

I have driven this section in every season for fifteen years. The section will be the same after I have stopped running this route, which I will at some point, and I find this satisfying rather than otherwise.
