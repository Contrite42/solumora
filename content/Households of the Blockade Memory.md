_(Intergenerational Trauma, Civic Memory)_

**Households of the Blockade Memory** describes families in [[Ashford]] whose daily practices were shaped by inherited knowledge from the first two generations of restriction governance. These households preserved practical survival routines and political caution habits that outlasted the original crisis conditions.

## Core Household Practices

Families with strong blockade memory typically maintain:

- Duplicate document archives stored in separate locations
- Rotating supply buffers for medicine, salt, grain, and lamp oil
- Cross-check habits for route announcements and checkpoint policy changes
- Kinship support chains that bypass single-point institutional failure

These practices are not nostalgia. They are risk management systems developed under repeated procedural shocks.

## Social Consequences

Blockade-memory households often display:

- Low trust in short-notice government assurances
- Preference for local coordinators over distant officials
- High respect for clerks and document workers
- Reluctance to engage in visible protest unless basic survival thresholds are crossed

The result is social resilience mixed with political pessimism.

## Present-Day Relevance

As [[Auralis]] and [[Terravelle]] move through legitimacy conflict, blockade-memory behaviors are spreading outside Ashford. Communities relearning these patterns are effectively preparing for prolonged procedural instability rather than short crisis spikes.
