# Terravelle Low-Level Crime

Low-level crime in [[Terravelle]]—neighborhood theft, assault, minor fraud, drunken disturbance—is prosecuted through informal resolution when victims and perpetrators know each other, and escalates to formal guild mediation when restitution requires institutional pressure. Street-level crime is nearly invisible to formal justice systems; neighborhood resolution preceded any guild involvement, often producing satisfaction before perpetrators were ever formally accused.

## Crime Types and Frequency

**Theft** from homes and small businesses dominates street-level crime. A neighbor steals tools or goods worth 20-50 Watts, a drunk takes fruit from a market stall, a servant takes money from household funds. Most theft is opportunistic rather than professional—low-value items snatched when access presented, not planned criminal operation.

**Assault** from drunken disputes is common. Bar fights between patrons, disputes between neighbors escalating to pushing and punching, domestic violence within households. Most assault is non-lethal injury (bruises, minor cuts, broken bones that heal in weeks). Sexual assault occurs but is dramatically under-reported because victims face reputation damage alongside trauma, discouraging formal reporting.

**Fraud** involves deception for small goods or coin. A merchant misrepresents fabric quality, a laborer claims completed work he only started, a lodger leaves without paying rent. Most fraud is discovered afterward when victim realizes misrepresentation, creating delayed conflict rather than immediate crime scene.

**Public Nuisance** includes drunken disturbance, defecation in public spaces, screaming arguments at all hours. Neighborhoods tolerate mild nuisance but escalate complaints when behavior disrupts sleep or creates health hazards.

## Initial Response: Neighborhood Resolution

When a crime is discovered, the immediate response is almost always neighborhood rather than legal:

**Direct Confrontation.** Victim confronts perpetrator directly: "I know you stole the tools. I need them back by tomorrow or I'm going to the arbitration bench." Most minor theft is resolved immediately or next day—perpetrator returns goods or victim accepts payment.

**Witness Facilitation.** If victim and perpetrator don't know each other, a witness or intermediary approaches perpetrator on victim's behalf: "The cart owner says you stole grain. He wants restitution in coin or he'll press formal charges." Perpetrator, faced with imminent formal accountability, often pays restitution to avoid guild mediation costs.

**Reputation Signaling.** Victims talk publicly about theft: "Don't leave your door unlocked in the north quarter—someone stole my mother's blankets last month." Information spreads through neighborhood networks. Perpetrators become known: "Marksen steals from homes that look empty." Reputation pressure motivates restitution because continued reputation damage becomes costlier than immediate payment.

**Retaliation Threat.** Victims sometimes implicitly or explicitly threaten private retaliation if formal resolution occurs. "If you don't return my mother's ring, I'm telling your employer and your guild." Threat of reputation damage is often sufficient to motivate compliance.

## When Informal Resolution Fails

When direct confrontation doesn't produce restitution, victims escalate to institutional pressure:

**Guild Coordination.** If perpetrator is guild-affiliated, victims approach the perpetrator's guild with theft report. Guild officers summon perpetrator, verify allegations, and impose internal discipline—warnings for first offense, monetary sanctions, or expulsion for repeated behavior. Pressure is informal (reputation, guild standing) rather than legal, but often effective because guild affiliation creates vulnerability.

**Guild Arbitration Escalation.** If perpetrator refuses guild resolution, victim formally petitions Guild Arbitration Bench paying 8-15 Watts filing fee. Bench summons perpetrator, hears evidence, and issues binding ruling requiring restitution. Perpetrator who ignores Bench ruling faces enforcement through guild membership consequences (certification suspension, commercial isolation).

**Formal Court Petition.** If perpetrator is non-guild-affiliated or Bench jurisdiction unclear, victim petitions formal Terravelle courts. Process is slower (2-6 month timeline) and evidence standards are more formal than guild mediation. Courts cannot compel Flux practitioners to comply, making high-tier criminal prosecutions difficult.

## Street-Level Enforcement Gaps

Terravelle maintains minimal formal law enforcement presence. Major cities have constables (typically 5-15 per city depending on population) who respond to violent crimes, maintain public security, and occasionally investigate serious theft. Constables lack investigative authority equivalent to Auralis Enforcement Bureau—they manage immediate chaos rather than solve crimes after perpetrators depart.

**Constable Response Triggers:**
- Murder or attempted murder (immediate response)
- Violent assault causing serious injury (investigation and witness collection)
- Burglary of major merchant property (investigation if victim wealthy enough to petition)
- Drunken disturbance creating public hazard (dispersal, occasionally arrest for overnight detention)
- Street-level gang activity creating territory violence (patrol presence, coordination with guild security)

**Constable Limitations:**
- No investigative power outside immediate crime scenes
- Cannot compel Flux practitioners to stand down (dangerous confrontation avoided)
- No holding cells beyond overnight detention (arrested perpetrators released pending guild mediation)
- No prosecution authority (courts initiate prosecution, not constables)
- Minimal training in theft investigation or fraud detection
- Often responding to 5-10 concurrent situations with 2-4 available personnel

Most perpetrators are never prosecuted formally. A theft from a wealthy merchant might trigger constable investigation and guild Bench petition, producing restitution within 3-4 weeks. A theft from a poor household triggers only reputation spread through neighbors and possibly direct confrontation, producing restitution only if perpetrator fears reputation damage or responds to personal pressure.

## Assault and Neighborhood Justice

Assault cases reveal tension between victim satisfaction and formal fairness:

**Drunken Assault Resolution:** Two neighbors fight outside tavern. Witnesses separate them, someone fetches water. Within 30 minutes, one neighbor is bloodied, the other disheveled. Both are detained by witnesses until clarity returns. Victim wants compensation for broken tooth and torn clothing (cost: 8-12 Watts repair). Assault perpetrator offers 5 Watts coin and apology. Victim accepts or rejects based on whether 5 Watts adequately compensates lost tooth and humiliation. If accepted, conflict ends. If rejected, victim petitions guild Bench. Process takes 2-3 weeks. Bench rules perpetrator owes 12 Watts restitution plus victim compensation for tooth replacement (likely 3-5 Watts from guild-recommended tooth restoration services). Perpetrator pays or faces guild membership restrictions.

**Domestic Violence.** Husband strikes wife during argument. Wife leaves home, stays with neighbor. Community women approach husband urging restraint and compensation (6-8 Watts). Husband refuses, cites wife's disobedience. Wife contacts guild if husband is guild-affiliated; if unaffiliated, she contacts formalbench or remains separated until husband accepts mediation. Formal process takes 4-8 weeks. Court-issued restraining orders are theoretically enforceable but perpetrators often ignore orders because enforcement requires victim to re-petition court when violation occurs, creating cycle of formal petition, judicial review, strengthened order, perpetrator defiance. Effective remedy often requires victim's separation from household (moving to another neighborhood, staying with family in different city) removing perpetrator's access rather than compelling behavioral change through legal penalty.

**Sexual Assault.** Victim reports assault to constables, guild, or court depending on perpetrator's status and victim's confidence in institutional response. Conviction is extremely difficult:
- Physical evidence (injury, torn clothing) is present but doesn't prove non-consent; many sexual encounters involve rough contact
- Witness testimony is rare (assaults typically occur in private)
- Victim's reputation is scrutinized (was victim dressed provocatively? had prior relationships? was drinking? all factors guilt-associated)
- Perpetrators often claim victim consented then retracted claim, creating "disputed consent" scenario where victim's word against perpetrator's

Result: most sexual assaults are not formally prosecuted. Victims who report face reputation damage (she claimed assault? is she credible? what did she do to provoke?), making false accusation social cost high enough that reporting occurs only when victim extremely confident of support or assault was extremely violent (beating accompanying assault, multiple perpetrators, weapon threat). Perpetrators operate with substantial impunity; victims resolve assault through separation, avoidance, and reputation-spreading rather than institutional remedy.

## Fraud and Small-Scale Deception

Fraud is endemic to commerce and is partially managed through reputation rather than law:

**Merchant Fraud:** Fabric seller claims cloth is fine linen; it is cotton. Buyer discovers during first wash (cloth shrinks and loses sheen). Buyer confronts seller: "You sold me cotton as linen." Seller responds with denial, minimization, or partial refund. If 2-3 other buyers come forward with identical complaint, seller's reputation is damaged, customers decline to purchase, reputation damage exceeds fraud profit, seller reforms or business fails. If isolated complaint, seller refuses refund; buyer accepts loss or petitions guild Bench (costly for 15-Watt fraud amount).

**Labor Fraud:** Hired laborer claims experience with carpentry; completes poorly. Employer discovers work is unusable (roof leaks, joints separate). Employer confronts laborer: "This work is substandard. Fix it at your cost." Laborer refuses or claims materials were inadequate. Employer pays another carpenter to fix (additional 20-40 Watts cost), files complaint with guild if laborer guild-affiliated, or accepts loss if laborer unaffiliated.

**Rental Fraud:** Lodger rents space promising payment on wage day. Wage day arrives, lodger claims employer delayed payday, needs additional week. Owner knows this is likely false (lodgers frequently delay) but tolerates delay because eviction (legally permissible but logistically complex) costs more than waiting. Lodger eventually pays or disappears, owner absorbs loss.

Formal fraud prosecution occurs for large-value deceptions (50+ Watts) or repeated fraud pattern establishing criminal intent. Individual merchants committing one-time 10-20 Watt fraud face no formal accountability; reputational pressure is primary deterrent.

## Organized Crime and Territorial Violence

Major cities have street-level gang activity—organized groups claiming neighborhood territory, demanding protection payments from merchants, engaging in recreational violence establishing hierarchy. Gang activity is more common in poor neighborhoods where formal enforcement is lightest and reputation-based community control is weakest.

**Gang Territory Claims.** Young men organize into groups claiming geographic territory (harbor district, northern quarter, market district), demanding 5-10 Watts monthly "protection payment" from merchants in claimed territory. Payment theoretically buys protection from gang violence; most merchants pay to avoid conflict. Guild coordination with constables occasionally breaks up organized gang operations, but enforcement is sporadic because constable attention focuses on violence-producing crimes rather than extortion patterns.

**Gang Violence.** Competitive violence between gangs for territory dominates street-level assault statistics. Gangs fight for control of docks, market access, or street-level theft territory. Combat is brutal (stabbing, bludgeoning, occasionally Flux use by T1 gang members) and results in deaths, permanent injuries. Community residents avoid involvement. Constables respond to deaths and serious injuries but lack intelligence networks to anticipate violence.

**Gang Membership Pull.** Economic desperation drives young people to gang membership. Gang provides identity, income, protection from other threats. For T0 individuals with no guild affiliation and no family wealth, gang offers stable income source unavailable through legitimate labor. Guild apprenticeships provide long-term economic mobility but require upfront payment or family connection; gangs provide immediate coin.

## Comparison with Guild Mediation

Street-level crime resolution is primarily neighborhood-based, using reputation and direct restitution. Guild participation is intermediate (guild officers coordinating restitution for guild-affiliated perpetrators). Formal courts are tertiary (involved only when informal and guild resolution fails).

This creates fundamental tension: victims prefer swift neighborhood resolution with restitution. Perpetrators prefer avoiding institutional accountability. Communities prefer rapid dispute resolution restoring normalcy. Formal institutions exist for cases that can't be resolved informally, but the burden of proof, timeline, and costs mean many victims never pursue formal remedy for small-value crime.

Street-level crime is undercounted in formal statistics precisely because most crime is resolved before institutional involvement. A theft rate of 40+ incidents monthly in a major city might involve only 3-4 formal guild Bench filings (for unresolved theft), meaning official crime statistics significantly underrepresent actual crime occurrence.
