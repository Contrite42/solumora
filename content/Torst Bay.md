Torst Bay is a coastal fishing settlement of approximately 400 people anchored around fleet coordination, seasonal catch management, and preserved fish processing for inland markets. It sits on [[Auralis]] southern coast between [[Hedun]] and [[Solhaven]] — one of several stopping points along the [[Hedun-Solhaven Coastal Run]] where packet ships take on provisions and fishing fleet intelligence.

The settlement exists because the bay provides shelter from open-ocean conditions while maintaining direct access to productive fishing grounds. The coastline here forms a natural harbor with minimal Flux-work required for breakwater protection, which made it viable for settlement three generations ago when fishing operations expanded beyond port-city capacity.

## The Fishing Economy

Torst Bay operates fifteen mid-scale fishing boats and coordinates with approximately thirty independent single-craft operators who stage from the bay during seasonal runs. The settlement does not own the boats — most belong to family partnerships or small company arrangements based in [[Solhaven]] — but it provides the coordination, maintenance, storage, and processing infrastructure that makes sustained fishing operations possible.

The distinction matters: Torst Bay is not a boat-owning port. It is a logistics settlement that makes boat operations more profitable than they would be if crews had to operate directly from major ports. The trade-off is straightforward: boats get faster turnaround, better maintenance, current fishing intelligence, and shore support. The settlement gets landing fees, maintenance contracts, processed catch for inland distribution, and coordination service fees.

[[Lorn Markel]] runs the Bay Coordination Office, which is not a guild-chartered institution but effectively functions as one. His role is to track which boats are working which grounds, maintain current intelligence about catch quality and tonnage in different zones, coordinate harbor space allocation during peak season, and resolve conflicts that would otherwise delay operations. He has been doing this for seventeen years. Most boat captains know him personally, trust his judgment on operational matters, and pay coordination fees without negotiation because the alternative — operating without current fishing intelligence and harbor coordination — costs more than the fees.

Lorn's operation has become the de facto maritime intelligence hub for the southern coastal run. Packet ships making the [[Hedun-Solhaven Coastal Run]] stop at Torst Bay specifically to get current ocean-condition reports and weather pattern assessments that affect their transit schedule. Merchant vessels sometimes divert to Torst Bay for storm avoidance intelligence rather than risk running through uncertain conditions. This was not planned — it emerged because fishing fleet coordination requires accurate ocean-condition monitoring, and that intelligence turned out to be valuable to non-fishing maritime traffic.

## The People

**Lorn Markel**, [[Control Tier|Tier 3]], 46 years old, runs the Bay Coordination Office. His background is twenty-three years of boat operations before he transitioned to shore coordination. He knows fishing grounds, seasonal patterns, boat capabilities, and crew reputations well enough to arbitrate disputes through direct problem-solving rather than formal procedures. He uses [[Flux]] primarily for weather-sense operations — reading incoming storm patterns, assessing wind behavior, detecting the subtle pressure shifts that indicate changing ocean conditions. This is not dramatic Flux work. It is sustained low-intensity environmental sensing that requires more endurance than power, which is why T3 is sufficient for the role.

**Mira Kels**, [[Control Tier|Tier 2]], 31 years old, manages the preservation and processing operations. Torst Bay's inland market contracts depend on preserved fish reaching [[Auralis]] markets in saleable condition, which means processing must happen within hours of boats landing catch. Mira coordinates twelve preservation workers, maintains quality standards that satisfy merchant buyers, and manages the timing logistics that keep processed stock moving through the [[Hedun-Solhaven Coastal Run]] distribution chain. Her operation is the economic bridge between fishing boats and inland markets — if it fails, the boats still catch fish but cannot sell most of what they catch at profitable margins.

**Kess Torven**, [[Control Tier|Tier 4]], 38 years old, operates one of the settlement's independent boats with her brother. She has been fishing southern waters for twenty years and knows the seasonal migration patterns well enough to consistently outperform larger fleet operations. Her success comes from reading environmental cues — water temperature shifts, current behavior, subtle Flux distortions that indicate where fish congregate — and adjusting her operations hours or sometimes days before other boats recognize what is happening. She does not share her methods in detail, but she does share directional intelligence through Lorn's coordination system because good fishing across the fleet improves bay-wide operations, which improves her own support infrastructure.

**Ven Orl**, [[Control Tier|Tier 1]], 54 years old, maintains boat repair and hull-integrity operations. His work is traditional carpentry, rope-work, hull-sealing — the constant maintenance that keeps fishing boats functional through entire seasons without expensive docking at major ports. He uses minimal [[Flux]] (enough for precision measurement and wood-treatment efficiency), which means his services are accessible to boat operations running tight margins. His workshop at the bay's northern edge is where most crews spend at least one day per month doing preventive maintenance, because a boat that fails mid-season loses more money than the maintenance costs.

## Storm Management

Torst Bay's location on open coast means it experiences the full range of ocean weather conditions without the geographic protection that port cities like [[Hedun]] and [[Solhaven]] benefit from. Storm management is not optional infrastructure — it is survival requirement.

The settlement's primary defense system is early warning combined with rapid response. Lorn's weather-sense operations give the bay approximately 18-24 hours of advance notice before major storms arrive. This is enough time to secure boats, reinforce harbor structures, move vulnerable equipment inland, and prepare the settlement for sustained wind and wave impact.

The actual storm defense is engineered: rock breakwaters extended through decades of placement, raised boat storage that keeps smaller vessels above surge level, reinforced processing buildings that can handle sustained wind loads. None of this requires continuous Flux maintenance. The settlement was built to handle storms through physical infrastructure that persists without ongoing energy cost.

When storms do arrive, boats are already in protected positions and crews are ashore. The settlement weathers the storm and resumes operations once conditions clear. The efficiency of this system is why fishing operations stage from Torst Bay rather than from major ports — advance warning and rapid recovery mean less operational downtime per season compared to port-based operations that might have better storm protection but worse coordination and response speed.

## The Pattern

Torst Bay is one node in a distributed coastal economy. Similar settlements exist along both [[Auralis]] and [[Terravelle]] coasts wherever geography creates viable harbor conditions and fishing grounds justify the logistics investment. The settlements communicate through the packet-ship network — [[Hedun-Solhaven Coastal Run]] on the southern coast, [[Outer Coast Packet Line]] on the western coast — sharing fishing intelligence, ocean condition reports, and storm warnings that improve collective maritime operations.

The system works because each settlement specializes in what its location supports. Torst Bay does fishing fleet coordination and preserved catch processing. Other settlements handle different elements: boat construction, deep-repair operations, specialized equipment fabrication. No single settlement tries to provide complete maritime infrastructure. Together, they create a distributed system that supports fishing operations, merchant shipping, and packet-line communications across coastal waters that would be economically inaccessible without this coordination layer.

Lorn describes it as "infrastructure without institutional recognition." The guild system acknowledges port cities. The kingdom administration tracks major harbors. But the distributed coastal settlement network that actually makes sustained maritime operations possible outside major ports exists through direct coordination among people who need it to work, with minimal formal oversight and no centralized management.

This invisibility is functional. If Torst Bay were subject to formal guild regulation, port authority oversight, and kingdom administrative requirements, the operational costs would exceed what fishing fleet margins can support. As an informal coordination hub, it imposes minimal overhead while providing maximum operational value — which is why it continues to function across multiple generations.
