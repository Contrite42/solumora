# Guild Arbitration Bench

The **Guild Arbitration Bench** is [[Terravelle]]'s standing inter-guild dispute resolution body, operating across [[Valdenmoor]], [[Ashford]], and [[Greyveil]]. It exists to handle contract breaches, route interference, certification conflicts, and operational disputes that individual guilds cannot resolve internally or where parties belong to different guilds with competing interests.

The Bench does not govern the guilds. It ends arguments before they break logistics.

## What the Bench Does

The Bench handles disputes where:
- **Contract performance conflicts** between members of different guilds (cargo delivery failures, payment disputes, scope disagreements).
- **Route or territory disputes** when trade operations overlap and neither guild has clear precedent for authority.
- **Certification contests** where one guild's standards conflict with another's or where cross-trade work creates ambiguous jurisdiction.
- **Resource allocation conflicts** when shared infrastructure (docks, warehouses, transport corridors) faces competing claims.

Cases reach the Bench through formal petition. Both parties must agree to arbitration — the Bench has no authority to compel participation. In practice, refusal to arbitrate signals bad faith and damages reputation within [[Trade Guilds]] networks, creating strong incentives to participate even when one party expects to lose.

The Bench operates on a **15-30 day resolution timeline** for standard cases. Emergency disputes threatening immediate commercial disruption can receive preliminary rulings within **2-4 days**, with full hearings following. Rulings are binding on guild members. Enforcement happens through guild membership consequences: refusal to comply with a Bench ruling results in loss of certification, guild exclusion, and commercial isolation.

## Structure and Organization

The Bench operates through **regional panels** in Terravelle's three major cities:
- **Valdenmoor Bench** (11 arbitrators, handles northern guild disputes, maritime-overland coordination, Maren Freight Chain route conflicts).
- **Ashford Bench** (9 arbitrators, handles border trade disputes, cross-kingdom commerce conflicts, Dock Compact jurisdiction cases).
- **Greyveil Bench** (7 arbitrators, handles western agricultural disputes, seasonal allocation conflicts, Covenant-guild coordination).

Each panel operates independently but shares precedent through a distributed rulings log maintained by **Settlement Registry Network**. Arbitrators cite prior cases from other panels when precedent applies, creating consistency without centralized control.

Arbitrators are **appointed by guild consensus**, not royal authority. Each major guild in a city nominates candidates. The existing panel members review nominations and approve appointments by majority vote. This creates panels selected by the institutions they serve, which ensures arbitrators understand guild operations but also means panels reflect guild power structures.

[[Corvin Helt]] chairs the Valdenmoor Bench. [[Merra Koss]] coordinates the Ashford panel. [[Lorn Savel]] coordinates Greyveil's smaller operation. None holds formal authority over the others, but Corvin's 14-year tenure and extensive precedent documentation give Valdenmoor's panel informal leadership.

## Arbitration Process

Cases follow a structured procedure:

1. **Filing**: Petitioner submits formal complaint with evidence package (contracts, correspondence, witness statements, financial records). Filing fee: **8-15 Watts** depending on case complexity, paid to the Bench's operating fund.

2. **Response window**: Respondent has **10 days** to submit counter-documentation and evidence. Non-response triggers default judgment if petitioner's case has minimum evidentiary support.

3. **Preliminary review**: Panel assigns case to 3-arbitrator hearing panel (or 5 arbitrators for high-stakes cases affecting multiple guilds). Hearing panel reviews submissions, identifies points of agreement/dispute, requests additional documentation if gaps exist.

4. **Hearing**: Both parties present cases in structured session. Arbitrators ask clarifying questions. Witnesses can testify. Sessions are closed — not public spectacle — to encourage honest testimony without reputation concerns dominating proceedings.

5. **Deliberation**: Hearing panel deliberates privately, reviews precedent, assesses evidence credibility. Most cases resolve through majority vote. Dissenting opinions are noted in case log.

6. **Ruling**: Panel issues written decision specifying outcome, reasoning, timeline for compliance, consequences for non-compliance. Rulings are logged in distributed registry for precedent reference.

7. **Compliance monitoring**: Guild councils enforce rulings through membership mechanisms. Bench tracks compliance but does not directly enforce — enforcement happens through guild authority over certification and commercial access.

## Precedent and Documentation

The Bench maintains a **searchable rulings log** distributed across all three panels. Arbitrators cite prior rulings when precedent applies, creating consistency without requiring centralized legal authority. The log includes:
- Case summary (parties, dispute type, core facts).
- Ruling outcome (which party prevailed, what remedy was ordered).
- Reasoning (key evidence, precedent cited, rationale for decision).
- Dissenting opinions if applicable.

The log is **accessible to guild members** who pay annual access fees (12 Watts for Valdenmoor registry access). Merchants, guild coordinators, and legal specialists consult precedent when drafting contracts to avoid disputes that established rulings have already decided.

Precedent evolves through incremental refinement. A ruling on cargo delivery failure in Valdenmoor becomes precedent for similar cases in Ashford. When new fact patterns arise where precedent doesn't cleanly apply, arbitrators distinguish the case, explain why prior rulings don't govern, and establish new precedent. Over decades, this produces a common body of guild commercial law that operates across [[Terravelle]] without royal legislation.

## Types of Cases

### Contract Breach Disputes

Most common case type. Merchant A claims Merchant B failed to deliver cargo per contract terms. Merchant B claims cargo was delivered but Merchant A's specifications were ambiguous. The Bench reviews contract language, delivery documentation, correspondence establishing mutual understanding, and rules on whether breach occurred and what remedy applies.

Standard remedies: monetary compensation (payment of specified damages), performance completion (order to fulfill remaining obligations), contract voiding (release both parties from unperformed obligations).

[[Corvin Helt]] notes that most contract cases reveal poor drafting rather than bad faith. Bench rulings frequently include guidance on contract language that would have prevented the dispute, which gradually improves guild contracting practices through accumulated precedent.

### Route and Territory Conflicts

Two guilds claim authority over the same commercial operation. Ashford Dock Compact claims exclusive dock-floor handling authority for all maritime cargo. A merchant guild argues their charter includes dock operations for cargo they own. The Bench reviews charter language, historical practice, prior precedent, and determines jurisdiction boundaries.

These cases are politically sensitive because they affect guild power and revenue. Arbitrators face pressure from both sides. Rulings establish boundary lines that guilds accept because the alternative — open conflict or Crown intervention — serves neither party's interests.

### Certification Disputes

A practitioner certified by one guild seeks to perform work that another guild claims requires different certification. Or a practitioner disputes a guild's refusal to grant certification, arguing assessment was conducted improperly. The Bench reviews certification standards, assessment documentation, cross-trade precedent, and determines whether the guilds' jurisdiction claims are reasonable.

The Bench cannot force guilds to grant certification — guild certification authority is protected by charter. But it can rule that a guild's refusal was arbitrary or discriminatory, which damages guild reputation and can trigger Crown review if patterns establish systematic abuse.

### Resource Allocation Conflicts

Multiple guilds need access to limited shared infrastructure. Maren Freight Chain needs river dock capacity during spring peak shipping. Maritime Guild needs the same docks for fishing fleet maintenance before summer season. Both have legitimate claims based on charter and historical practice. The Bench establishes allocation schedules, priority rules, and overflow procedures.

These disputes require ongoing relationship management. Single rulings rarely resolve the underlying structural problem (infrastructure capacity insufficient for all users). The Bench often issues seasonal allocation orders valid for one year, then renewed with adjustments based on changing conditions.

## Funding and Independence

The Bench is funded through:
- **Filing fees** (8-15 Watts per case, generates ~600 Watts annually across all three panels).
- **Charter maintenance contributions** from participating Trade Guilds (annual contributions based on guild size, total ~450 Watts annually).
- **Registry access fees** (precedent log access at 12 Watts per guild member annually, generates ~280 Watts).

Total annual Bench operations cost approximately **1,330 Watts** across three panels, covering arbitrator stipends (not full-time salaries — arbitrators maintain guild practices simultaneously), clerk wages for documentation, facility maintenance for hearing chambers.

Funding structure creates independence from [[Eddan Voss]]'s Crown. The Bench does not receive royal funding and does not require royal approval for operations. This insulates arbitration from Crown political priorities but also means the Bench cannot compel parties outside guild membership to participate.

## Relationship with Crown Authority

The Bench operates under guild charter authority, not Crown supervision. [[Eddan Voss]] has final appellate jurisdiction over disputes "that cannot be resolved at lower levels," but appeals to the Crown are rare. Filing Crown appeal after Bench ruling signals rejection of guild authority structure itself, which creates lasting commercial costs.

In the past decade, **three cases** escalated from Bench rulings to Crown review:
1. A certification dispute where Ashford rulings conflicted with Valdenmoor precedent, creating jurisdiction ambiguity Voss resolved by clarifying charter language.
2. A multi-guild resource conflict involving five guilds where Bench could not achieve consensus ruling, requiring Crown to impose settlement.
3. A contract breach case where losing party refused Bench ruling compliance, was expelled from guilds, then appealed to Crown claiming guild authority abuse. Voss upheld the Bench ruling and refused readmission, establishing precedent that Crown will not override Bench decisions absent clear evidence of procedural failure.

The relationship is symbiotic: the Bench keeps disputes out of Crown jurisdiction, preserving Eddan Voss's remove from ordinary governance. The Crown's final authority gives Bench rulings enforceability beyond guild membership through threat (rarely exercised) of royal intervention if parties refuse participation in good faith.

## Enforcement and Compliance

Bench rulings are **enforced through guild membership consequences**, not direct legal power:
- **First refusal**: Guild warning, case noted in member record, reputation damage within guild networks.
- **Continued refusal**: Suspension of certification, preventing member from taking guild-certified contracts.
- **Sustained refusal**: Expulsion from guild, loss of commercial access, exclusion from shared infrastructure controlled by guilds.

Most compliance happens at the warning stage. Merchants understand that guild exclusion ends careers. Sustained refusal is rare because the costs exceed any individual dispute outcome.

Cross-guild enforcement functions through reciprocal recognition. If Valdenmoor's Bench rules against a merchant who then refuses compliance, Ashford and Greyveil guilds honor the ruling by denying that merchant access to their facilities. This creates kingdom-wide enforcement without centralized authority.

The system breaks down when parties operate outside guild structures. Black Market Grimoire Circulation operators, practitioners without guild certification, merchants operating in regulatory gray zones — these actors cannot be compelled by Bench rulings because they have no guild membership to lose. The Bench does not handle criminal matters or Crown law violations; those escalate to Crown jurisdiction directly.

## Limitations and Failure Modes

The Bench works well for guild-internal disputes where both parties value continued guild access. It struggles with:
- **Cross-kingdom disputes** where one party is [[Auralis]]-based and not subject to Terravelle guild authority. These require bilateral arbitration agreements that Bench precedent cannot compel.
- **Structural resource conflicts** where underlying infrastructure capacity cannot support all legitimate claims. The Bench can allocate limited resources fairly, but it cannot expand dock capacity or create new trade routes.
- **Power asymmetry cases** where one party has significantly more guild influence than the other. Bench arbitrators are selected by guilds; panels can favor institutional interests over individual members even when formally neutral procedures followed.
- **Precedent fragmentation** when regional panels establish conflicting rulings without mechanism for reconciliation. Valdenmoor and Ashford precedent sometimes diverge on similar fact patterns, creating forum-shopping incentives.

[[Corvin Helt]] documents that **case filings have increased 35% over 6 years** while arbitrator headcount remained constant, producing longer resolution timelines. Average case duration increased from 18 days to 26 days. Emergency rulings still meet 2-4 day targets, but backlog is growing for standard cases.

## Wartime Pressures

Current conditions stress the Bench in predictable ways:
- **Increased cross-kingdom disputes** as [[Border Trade]] friction creates more contract failures and delivery delays blamed on customs rather than merchant performance.
- **Resource conflict acceleration** as wartime priorities redirect infrastructure capacity, forcing guilds to compete more aggressively for scarce dock space, transport routes, and practitioner availability.
- **Precedent pressure** where standard peacetime precedent doesn't account for wartime disruptions, forcing arbitrators to issue rulings that distinguish "normal" contract breach from "force majeure disruption beyond party control."
- **Enforcement difficulty** as merchants expelled from one city's guilds relocate to another city where guild networks are weaker and enforcement less reliable.

Corvin Helt and [[Merra Koss]] coordinate more frequently now, attempting to maintain precedent consistency as case volume increases and fact patterns diverge from established norms. The coordination is informal — no

 centralized Bench authority exists to impose consistency — operating through professional relationships and shared interest in maintaining Bench credibility.

[[Lorn Savel]] projects that without additional arbitrator appointments, Greyveil's backlog will reach **90+ days within 18 months**, making Bench resolution slower than Crown court proceedings and undermining the speed advantage that makes guilds prefer arbitration.
