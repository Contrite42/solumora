*Year 4 of practice*

Loading injury, right shoulder, secondary nerve involvement. The nerve involvement was not visible in the initial presentation. I found it on second assessment. If I had not done a second assessment I would have treated the shoulder and discharged him and the nerve damage would have progressed to functional loss within a year. I now do second assessments on all shoulder presentations regardless of initial findings.

*Year 7 of practice*

Three presentations in six weeks with the same exposure pattern: respiratory involvement consistent with a specific solvent used in the waterproofing work on the eastern warehouses. I reported the pattern to the guild occupational health office. Their records showed the same compound flagged by a practitioner in [[Ashford]] eighteen months prior, unaddressed. I asked what the follow-up procedure was. I was told the report had been received.

I began keeping notes on what I report and what response I receive. This is a separate record.

*Year 11 of practice*

A woman came in who had been seen twice by the certified chapter and discharged as resolved both times. She was not resolved. What she had was a [[Control Tier|T4]]-diagnostic presentation in a T0 patient, which means the certified practitioners with standard certification had looked at the surface and found nothing because the problem was not at the surface. I sent her to [[Fen Orel]] with a note. Fen saw her the same day. She was referred for residential treatment by the end of the week.

The certified system did not miss her because the practitioners were careless. They missed her because the certification grade for the walk-in tier does not include the diagnostic depth required to find what she had. I wrote this down as a note to myself about what the T2 certification boundary actually means for the work I can and cannot do.

*Year 14 of practice*

The pattern I have tracked over fourteen years in this district: occupational injury clusters move with the labor market. When warehouse work expands, I see more loading injuries. When construction runs, I see more fall and impact trauma. When the docks bring in seasonal labor, I see the presentations associated with workers who have not eaten or slept adequately and are doing physical work anyway.

None of this is surprising once you see it. I did not see it clearly for the first three years because I was seeing individual patients rather than the district the patients were coming from.

*Year 17 of practice*

A boy, eleven years old, brought by his father. The father works loading, T0, did not want to be there, was there because the boy had been having episodes the father could not explain. The episodes were small seizures, Flux-adjacent, consistent with an early T6 presentation in a child whose Control Tier has not been formally assessed.

I explained what I was seeing. The father was quiet for a long time.

He asked if his son would have to go to Solhaven.

I told him what the assessment process looked like and what the options were. I told him his son's ability, if my read was correct, would be his son's to use as he chose. I told him the assessment did not commit to anything.

He took the boy for assessment the following week. The assessment came back T6. I do not know what they decided after that.
