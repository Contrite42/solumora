# Refuge Networks of the Persecution Era

The **Refuge Networks** were distributed hiding infrastructure that enabled High Demon survival and sympathizer protection across the two centuries of organized persecution. They were not centrally organized but rather emerged from independent local decisions to hide refugees, eventually connecting into informal trade and communication webs.

## Geographic Distribution

Early refuges clustered in geographically defensible locations:
- **Desert Interior**: Remote settlements in [[Equatorial Desert|desert]] heat zones where Lantern Hunt formations were operationally infeasible
- **High Mountains**: Border region peaks accessible from either kingdom but administratively remote from both
- **Coastal Trading Posts**: Settlements with high population turnover and economic incentive to protect mobility
- **Underground Cavern Systems**: Literal subterranean refuges in caves used historically for trade goods storage, adapted for residence

The most durable refuges were those that also served legitimate economic functions—spice processing in desert towns, pearl diving in coastal posts, rare mineral extraction in mountain sites. This dual purpose provided cover for refugee population and resources to sustain them.

## Operational Structure

Refuges operated under secrecy protocols:
- **Single-point contact model**: Each refuge maintained relationship with only one or two external contact points
- **Rotational presence**: Refugees changed locations every 2-3 years to prevent establishment of detectable patterns
- **False identity systems**: Documentation networks that created civil records for refugees under new names
- **Economic integration**: Requiring refugees to participate in productive work rather than subsisting on charity

## Population Flows

Over the course of persecution, refuge networks sheltered:
- Estimated 40-60 High Demon beings at any given time (5 identified, 35-55 suspected or unconfirmed)
- 5,000-10,000 sympathizer residents annual cycle
- 200-400 permanent refuge operators/maintainers spanning both kingdoms

Movement through networks followed seasonal and panic patterns:
- Hunts triggered surges of new arrivals
- Perceived hunt cooldowns enabled people to attempt return to official society
- Border disruptions forced population shifts toward geographically remote refuges

## Why Networks Survived

Lantern Hunts failed to eliminate refuge networks because:
1. **Distributed vulnerability**: No central authority made destruction of entire system impossible
2. **Economic incentive**: Local magistrates and traders profited from refugee resource flows
3. **Sympathizer numbers**: Scale of protection required would mandate genocide-level violence if networks were forced into full visibility
4. **Information fragmentation**: Hunts relied on informant networks, but loyalties in refuge-supporting communities remained distributed insufficient for systematic disclosure

By persecution end (year 200), refuge networks had become semi-institutionalized: magistrates maintained public hunt compliance while privately protecting certain routes and communities.
