[[The Bone Sea]] is a fractured salt basin on the Zakros-facing side of the Halveth frontier, known less for open travel than for staged movement through marked lanes. From a distance it looks flat. At ground level it is broken plates, wind-cut channels, and brittle crust that can hold a loaded cart for hours and then fail without warning.

The name is practical, not ceremonial. Old crossing crews used it to describe what the terrain feels like underfoot: dry, white, and rigid until it snaps. The basin sits inside the wider risk envelope of the [[Equatorial Desert]], but it is treated as its own operating zone because route behavior there is different from dune and shelf crossings.

## Why It Matters For Travel

The Bone Sea is used for three things:
- Route staging and load testing before parties commit to full crossing profiles.
- Cache transfer and recovery work for teams running [[Relay-Stage Crossing]] logistics.
- Controlled salvage and survey around exposed debris fields tied to [[Ancient Ruins]] activity.

Most traffic originates from [[Halveth]], where outfitters and depot staff decide whether a party is ready for Bone Sea work or should stay on safer support contracts. Crews that misjudge this usually lose equipment before they lose people, but both outcomes are common enough that experienced guides treat the basin with the same discipline they use in open-desert operations.

## Operational Risks

The primary risk is surface instability under variable heat load. Morning routes can become unusable by afternoon as crust layers separate. Secondary risk comes from visibility distortion in abrasive wind, which makes lane markers harder to read and raises the chance of drift into untested sections.

Rift-linked hazards are less frequent than in deep-desert approach zones but still present. When local activity rises, support crews coordinate with [[Rift Incursions]] observers and cut nonessential movement until conditions settle.

Because the basin is a logistics zone rather than a conquest objective, no kingdom treats it as a formal jurisdiction priority. In practice, standards are enforced by contract reputation and by who can still move goods through it safely next season.
