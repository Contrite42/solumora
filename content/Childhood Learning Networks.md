Childhood education in [[Solumora]] begins as household survival training long before it becomes formal instruction.

Between ages five and twelve, children learn three layers at once: domestic competence, local work rhythm, and kingdom-specific social expectations. Formal teaching exists in both kingdoms, but attendance and quality vary by district wealth, seasonal labor pressure, and how far a household lives from stable institutions.

## Terravelle Pattern

In [[Terravelle]], childhood learning is community-distributed.

- families teach practical arithmetic, record habits, and task reliability;
- guild-adjacent neighborhoods run shared instruction circles;
- local clergy of the [[Covenant of Measure]] provide literacy and conduct framing;
- older children rotate into low-risk work support before adolescence.

The advantage is flexibility. The cost is uneven depth: children in stable trade districts receive better instruction than children in isolated rural belts.

## Auralis Pattern

In [[Auralis]], childhood learning is more institution-linked.

- district schools align curriculum with [[Ascendant Path]] expectations;
- attendance is formally encouraged but practically constrained by household labor needs;
- early records are maintained more consistently in urban centers;
- performance and behavior documentation begins before tier assessment.

The advantage is predictable structure. The cost is hierarchy pressure early in life, especially for children from low-status districts.

## Non-Flux Households

For households with no high-tier adults, education planning is risk management rather than prestige strategy.

Most non-Flux families prioritize:

- literacy sufficient for contracts, wages, and debt records;
- numeracy sufficient for market and transport work;
- discipline habits valued by apprenticeships;
- reputation behaviors that preserve future placement options.

Children are taught to be employable first, aspirational second.

## Seasonal Interruptions

School continuity breaks along work cycles.

- harvest seasons pull children into labor support;
- port surges in [[Solhaven]] and [[Valdenmoor]] increase absenteeism;
- border logistics in [[Ashford]] and expedition season in [[Halveth]] reassign older children to household income tasks.

Drop-off begins when short-term wage contribution is worth more than long-term training probability.

## Early Filtering

By age ten to twelve, communities form durable expectations about each child:

- likely apprenticeship fit;
- likely Path-track fit;
- likely direct labor entry.

These expectations are not final decisions, but they shape which adults invest time in a child before formal placement events.

## Structural Bottleneck

The largest bottleneck is not absence of schools. It is household slack. Families with no spare income cannot sustain interrupted labor, transport costs, or unpaid training windows.

This is why education inequality appears first as time scarcity, then as credential scarcity.
