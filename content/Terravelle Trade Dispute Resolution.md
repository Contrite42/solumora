# Terravelle Trade Dispute Resolution

Trade disputes between merchants are resolved through [[Guild Arbitration Bench]] operating across [[Valdenmoor]], [[Ashford]], and [[Greyveil]]. Guild Arbitration is faster, cheaper, and more specialized than formal courts, making it the standard mechanism for merchant conflict. For cross-kingdom commerce involving [[Auralis]] parties, resolution mechanisms are more complex because Auralis parties may not accept Terravelle guild jurisdiction.

## Guild Arbitration Bench Overview

The Bench operates three independent regional panels coordinated through shared precedent and quarterly coordination sessions. Each panel accepts trade disputes from guild merchants and issues binding rulings enforceable through guild membership consequences.

**Valdenmoor Panel:** Coordinated by [[Corvin Helt]], 11 arbitrators, processes 110-130 disputes annually, average resolution time 26 days (degraded from 18-day baseline due to caseload increase). Filing fee 8-15 Watts depending on dispute value.

**Ashford Panel:** Coordinated by Merra Koss, 9 arbitrators, processes 85-95 disputes annually, average resolution time 22 days. Filing fee 8-15 Watts.

**Greyveil Panel:** Coordinated by Lorn Savel, 7 arbitrators, processes 60-70 disputes annually, average resolution time 19 days. Filing fee 8-15 Watts.

## Types of Disputes

**Delivery Failures.** Merchant contracts with supplier for 50 barrels flour at agreed price and delivery date. Supplier delivers only 40 barrels, claims supplier-side shortage. Merchant refuses partial shipment and payment. Dispute centers on whether contract required full delivery or permitted partial fulfillment.

**Payment Disputes.** Merchant agrees to accept goods on 30-day payment terms; 30 days passes without payment. Supplier wants coin; merchant claims goods were damaged in transport or quality was substandard. Dispute centers on whether goods acceptance obligates payment despite subsequent damage or quality complaints.

**Quality Disputes.** Merchant contracts for fine linen fabric; goods delivered consist of lower-quality linen than samples showed. Merchant demands refund or replacement. Supplier claims merchant misidentified quality or merchant's usage requirements justified only lower-quality goods.

**Termination and Contract Breach.** Merchant contracts for ongoing supply relationship; supplier unilaterally terminates, leaving merchant without sourcing. Merchant demands damages for lost business. Supplier claims merchant failed to meet minimum order quantities or payment terms, justifying termination.

**Cross-Guild Conflicts.** Dispute between merchants from different guilds (Textile vs. Food, for example) where each guild claims different standards apply. Arbitration must reconcile conflicting guild precedents or apply meta-standards both guilds accept.

## Arbitration Process

**Filing.** Plaintiff merchant files formal complaint with the regional Bench in jurisdiction where defendant merchant operates (or where dispute occurred). Complaint includes written statement of facts, contract details, damages claimed, and requested remedy. Arbitration fee is 8-15 Watts depending on dispute complexity.

**Summons.** Arbitration office notifies defendant merchant, providing copy of complaint and summoning to hearing. Defendant has 10 days to file response denying allegations, raising defenses, and potentially counterclaiming.

**Arbitrator Assignment.** Arbitrators are merchants with 10+ years experience, known for fair judgment, and responsible for knowing precedent. Panel of 3 arbitrators is assigned based on: dispute type (linen disputes assigned to textile-specialist arbitrators), merchant reputation if controversial, and arbitrator availability. Neutral arbitrators acceptable to both parties is standard practice.

**Pre-Hearing Preparation.** Parties prepare evidence: contracts, written communications, witness lists, expert assessments if quality is disputed. Hearing is scheduled typically 7-14 days after defendant response.

**Hearing.** Both parties present oral statements (15-30 minutes each), examined by panel and occasionally questioned. Witnesses testify subject to cross-examination. Physical evidence (damaged goods, fabric samples, letters) is examined. Hearing typically lasts 1-3 hours depending on complexity.

**Deliberation.** Panel meets privately, discusses facts and evidence, consults precedent from distributed Bench rulings log. Arbitrators attempt unanimous decision; if 1-2 arbitrators dissent, decision goes forward with dissenting opinions recorded for future reference.

**Decision.** Panel issues written ruling including: factual findings, applicable precedent citations, reasoning explaining decision, and ordered remedy. Ruling is binding and registered in distributed rulings log accessible to future arbitrators.

## Remedies

**Payment/Restitution.** Most common remedy: plaintiff receives partial or full value of disputed goods. Example: merchant claimed 50 Watts for damaged fabric goods; arbitrator awards 30 Watts finding goods were partially salvageable.

**Specific Performance.** Defendant is required to deliver goods as contracted. Used when goods cannot be replaced on market and plaintiff needs specific goods, not money. Example: merchant contracts for wool speciality items; supplier attempts to substitute standard items; arbitration orders specific items be delivered.

**Contract Reformation.** Parties misunderstood contract terms; arbitration clarifies terms binding both parties going forward. Example: dispute about whether ongoing supply contract included exclusive-dealing clause; arbitration definitively resolves whether clause was intended.

**Voiding.** Contract is declared void, returning parties to pre-contract status. Used when contract was procured through fraud or unconscionable terms. Rare remedy because it requires demonstrating intentional deception.

**Damages.** Plaintiff receives compensation for losses caused by breach beyond goods value. Example: supplier failure forced merchant to buy emergency supplies at premium prices; arbitration awards not just goods value but additional premium cost representing real business loss.

## Enforcement

Binding arbitration rulings are enforceable through guild mechanisms:

**Guild Membership Enforcement.** Defendant who ignores ruling faces guild sanctions progressively escalating from warning to expulsion:
1. **Formal Warning** (first violation): Recorded in merchant's guild file, indicating failure to comply with arbitration.
2. **Business Restriction** (second violation): Merchant loses access to certain guild services or cannot participate in guild-coordinated commerce for 3-6 months.
3. **Certification Suspension** (third violation or major amount): Merchant's certification to conduct trade is suspended for 6-12 months; cannot legally engage in commercial business.
4. **Expulsion** (persistent non-compliance or egregious breach): Expelled from guild, losing all guild benefits and access to guild networks, ostracizing merchant from formal commerce.

**Commercial Isolation.** Guild expulsion creates practical impossibility of continued trading. Expelled merchant cannot access guild-negotiated prices, is viewed with suspicion by other merchants as someone who ignores binding arbitration, and loses reputation enabling business relationship formation.

**Cross-Guild Coordination.** Regional coordination sessions between Valdenmoor, Ashford, and Greyveil arbitrators maintain consistent enforcement. If Valdenmoor expels merchant for refusing arbitration ruling, Ashford and Greyveil Benches recognize expulsion status and honor trade restrictions.

**Residual Enforcement Problem.** Expelled merchant can relocate to another city or kingdom and continue trading using different identity or relocated business. Enforcement is geographically limited; Auralis does not automatically enforce Terravelle guild sanctions. This creates enforcement gap where merchant willing to lose guild standing and change identity can escape ruling compliance.

## Costs and Timeline

| Factor | Arbitration | Formal Court |
|--------|-------------|--------------|
| Filing Fee | 8-15 Watts | 15-30 Watts |
| Representation | Merchant representative (self) or informal advisor | Formal legal representative (expensive) |
| Preparation Time | 1-2 weeks | 1-2 months |
| Hearing Duration | 1-3 hours | 1-4 sessions |
| Decision Timeline | 7-14 days post-hearing | 4-8 weeks post-hearing |
| Total Duration | 3-4 weeks | 2-4 months |
| Appeal | Limited (guild council review) | Full appellate process (expensive) |
| Enforcement | Guild mechanisms | Court officers + property seizure |

Merchants strongly prefer arbitration because cost is 50% lower, timeline is 10x faster, and arbitrators understand trade-specific issues better than general-purpose judges.

## Cross-Kingdom Complications

When dispute involves merchants from both Terravelle and Auralis:

**Jurisdiction Choice.** Contract specifies whether Terravelle arbitration or Auralis courts apply. Most contracts favor origin-kingdom jurisdiction: Terravelle merchants initiate contract → Terravelle arbitration applies. Auralis-initiated contracts → Auralis court jurisdiction.

**Auralis Merchant Non-Compliance.** If Auralis merchant loses Terravelle arbitration ruling and declines to comply, Terravelle lacks enforcement mechanism. Auralis does not automatically enforce Terravelle guild discipline. Remedy is practical: Terravelle merchants stop trading with non-compliant Auralis merchant (reputation consequence), which damages Auralis merchant business. This reputation enforcement spans kingdoms partially but is not perfectly reliable—reputation damage in one kingdom doesn't necessarily affect Auralis operations.

**Arbitrability Questions.** Auralis courts are skeptical of Terravelle arbitration outcomes on precedent and enforcement grounds. If Auralis merchant is sued in Auralis court for not complying with Terravelle arbitration, Auralis court might rule Terravelle arbitration is not binding on Auralis merchant, requiring formal Auralis court adjudication of same dispute. This re-litigation creates expensive, time-consuming parallel proceedings.

**Practical Result.** Cross-kingdom disputes are slower to resolve and more uncertain in enforcement. Merchants prefer avoiding cross-kingdom contracts when possible, or require extensive guarantees (escrow accounts, third-party payment assurance) reducing cross-kingdom commerce volume.

## Dispute Prevention

Successful merchants invest in dispute prevention through:

**Clear Contracts.** Written contracts specifying: delivery date and location, goods quantity and quality specifications, payment terms and method, dispute resolution mechanism, force majeure clauses (what circumstances excuse non-performance).

**Quality Verification.** Pre-delivery inspection confirming goods match contract specifications. Fabric is sampled and compared to agreed quality. Food items are taste-tested and assessed for spoilage. Disputes over quality are reduced by advance verification.

**Relationship Management.** Long-term merchants develop reputation with trusted suppliers and customers, creating incentive for good-faith performance avoiding disputes. One-off transactions between strangers have higher dispute risk because neither party's reputation creates incentive for fairness.

**Payment Security.** Rather than full payment on delivery, merchants use: deposit (10-20% paid upfront ensuring buyer seriousness), payment-on-inspection (full payment after goods verified at destination), or escrow (neutral third party holds payment until both parties confirm satisfactory completion).

**Insurance and Guarantees.** Merchants contract with guild offices for performance guarantees (if supplier fails to deliver, guild compensates buyer for damages). Guarantees cost 2-5% of contract value but reduce enforcement risk substantially.

## Implications

Guild Arbitration is one of Terravelle's advantages in cross-border commerce. Merchants trust Arbitration Bench because arbitrators are industry specialists understanding commercial realities. Courts, by comparison, are slower, more expensive, and less specialized. This specialized justice system is infrastructure enabling Terravelle merchant dominance in many trade domains despite kingdom's smaller formal institutional capacity compared to Auralis.
