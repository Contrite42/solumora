# Maren Timber Collective

The Maren Timber Collective is the largest coordinated logging operation in [[Terravelle]], managing sustainable timber harvest across distributed forest territories in the [[Maren Valley]] uplands. The Collective represents guild-coordinated forestry practice: approximately 80-120 active workers organized through cooperative decision-making, shared profit distribution, and multi-generational knowledge transmission. Operations contrast sharply with [[Auralis]] institutional logging, emphasizing long-term forest health over maximum extraction rates.

The Collective formed sixty-eight years ago when independent family logging operations recognized coordination benefits — shared equipment, coordinated territory management, collective merchant negotiation improved economic viability for all participants while preventing competitive over-harvesting. Current lead coordinator is [[Marn Drest]], age 54, managing operations for past eleven years.

## Territory and Forest Management

**Geographic Scope:** Distributed harvest territories spanning 120 square kilometers [[Maren Valley]] eastern uplands, elevation 400-900 meters. Territories divided into rotating harvest zones: one-fifth actively harvested annually while remaining zones regenerate. Rotation cycle: twenty-five years per zone allowing sustainable yield without long-term depletion.

**Forest Composition:** Mixed hardwood-softwood forests. Primary species: oak (premium shipbuilding timber), ash (construction and specialty applications), maple (furniture and finishing work), pine (general construction), fir (bulk applications). Natural regeneration supported by selective-cutting practice maintaining forest-floor light levels encouraging seedling growth.

**Territory Allocation:** Families hold traditional harvest rights to specific zones based on generational occupation and collective recognition. Rights are not ownership (land remains commons) but use-priority. New families enter system through marriage, apprenticeship, or collective invitation following multi-year probationary period demonstrating competence and commitment to sustainable practice.

**Harvest Scheduling:** Collective governance meetings (held quarterly) establish annual harvest allocation by territory, species priority, and crew assignment. Decisions emerge through consensus discussion balancing family needs, merchant contract obligations, and forest-health assessment. Disputed allocations resolved through experienced-elder arbitration rather than majority voting.

## Operations and Workflow

**Selective Harvest Method:** Standard practice targets mature trees (oak 60+ years, ash 50+ years, pine 35+ years) while leaving younger growth. Crews assess forest sections, mark harvest candidates, coordinate cutting timing (typically March-November, avoiding winter conditions). Annual Collective output: 2000-2500 cubic meters processed timber, sustainable yield maintaining forest productivity across rotation cycle.

**Felling and Processing:** Two-to-three-person teams perform cutting using combination of traditional hand tools and [[Control Tier|T1-T2]] Flux-enhanced precision equipment (reducing waste and improving cut quality). Branches removed on-site; logs transported to processing locations via oxen-drawn sleds or temporary roadways. Some territories near rivers utilize log flotation transport.

**Sawmill Processing:** Collective maintains three sawmills converting raw logs to dimensional lumber. Sawmills represent major capital investment (12,000-15,000 [[Watts]] per facility including structure, cutting equipment, and [[Flux]]-enhancement infrastructure). Sawmill workers are Collective members receiving standard profit-share regardless of field versus processing role assignment.

**Quality Grading:** Experienced assessors evaluate output quality determining pricing tiers. Premium-grade oak (clear grain, minimal defects) for shipbuilding commands 55-65 Watts per cubic meter. Standard construction lumber: 20-30 Watts per cubic meter. Damaged or lower-quality material processed for fuel or sold at reduced pricing.

## Labor Structure and Compensation

**Field Workers:** 60-70 active loggers performing harvest operations. Mostly [[Control Tier|T0-T2]]. Compensation through profit-sharing: Collective revenue distributed proportionally based on labor contribution (calculated quarterly through time accountability system). Average earnings: 22-28 Watts weekly, higher than wage-based logging operations elsewhere.

**Processing Workers:** 15-20 sawmill operators and timber-treatment specialists. Similar profit-share compensation structure. Average: 25-32 Watts weekly.

**Coordination and Management:** [[Marn Drest]] and 4-5 assistant coordinators manage merchant relationships, schedule coordination, equipment maintenance, and conflict resolution. Compensation: 35-40 Watts weekly base plus profit-share (total approximately 65-75 Watts weekly).

**Apprentices and Family Members:** Younger participants (age 15-25) learn forestry practice through direct participation. Receive reduced profit-share during apprenticeship period (50-70% of full-member allocation) advancing to full membership after demonstrated competence (typically 5-7 years).

## Economic Model and Market Relations

**Merchant Relationships:** Collective maintains long-term contracts with three [[Trade Guilds|merchant houses]] purchasing bulk timber for distribution to [[Valdenmoor]], [[Ashford]], and coastal shipbuilding operations. Contract pricing negotiated annually based on market conditions and projected output. Long-term relationships provide pricing stability and reduce transaction-search costs.

**Premium Market Access:** Highest-quality oak and specialty hardwoods sold directly to shipbuilding operations (primarily [[Keln Harbor]]) commanding premium prices. Direct sales require Collective delivering processed lumber to buyer locations — higher transport cost offset by superior pricing.

**Equipment Investment:** Collective maintains shared-capital fund (currently 8,000 Watts) for equipment purchases, sawmill maintenance, and emergency support. Fund sustained through 8-10% revenue allocation and occasional assessments when major expenditures required. Democratic governance determines fund utilization.

**Profit Distribution:** Remaining revenue (after operational costs, equipment fund allocation, merchant transport fees) distributed quarterly to members based on documented labor contribution. Transparency maintained through public record-keeping — all members can review financial accounts.

## Contemporary Wartime Pressure

**Military Contract Demands:** [[Auralis]] military procurement approached Collective eighteen months ago requesting accelerated output (40-50% increase) for fortification construction. Collective declined citing sustainability limits and unwillingness to compromise long-term forest health. [[Auralis]] response: attempts to purchase timber from individual Collective members at premium rates (economic pressure undermining collective solidarity).

**Labor Conscription:** Military service conscription removed approximately 15-20 younger workers from Collective operations. Remaining workforce absorbs additional labor but cannot fully compensate. Output declining 15-20% despite remaining workers extending hours.

**Price Pressure:** Market timber prices rising due to wartime scarcity. Collective benefits from higher pricing but faces ethical tension: profiting from war-driven demand creates guilt among members who oppose military acceleration. Internal debate unresolved — some members advocate expanding harvest to capitalize on pricing; others insist maintaining sustainable limits regardless of profit opportunity.

**Institutional Threat:** [[Terravelle Administration]] has not (yet) seized forest territories or mandated institutional control. However, [[Auralis]] precedent (direct [[The Council of Auralis|Council]] control over logging operations) creates concern that [[Terravelle]] might adopt similar policies if military pressure escalates. Collective leadership quietly plans resistance strategies should seizure attempts occur.

**Environmental Stewardship Versus Economic Survival:** Core tension. Maintaining sustainable harvest limits reduces short-term income during period when member families face inflation and economic uncertainty. Some members privately question whether long-term sustainability matters if military conflict produces near-term catastrophic outcomes. Collective solidarity strained but holding.

## Cultural Identity and Values

Collective members identify strongly with forestry practice and long-term territorial stewardship. Multi-generational families view forest health as personal responsibility — depleting territories would be betrayal of ancestors who maintained sustainable practice and children who will inherit diminished resource base.

This cultural identity differentiates Collective from wage-labor logging operations. Members are not employees extracting resources for institutional profit — they are stakeholders managing commons for collective benefit across generations. Pride in this identity helps maintain solidarity under wartime economic pressure.

Collective governance emphasizes consensus, transparency, and mutual aid. When member families face hardship (illness, accident, economic difficulty), Collective provides support through shared-capital fund or labor assistance. This mutual aid reinforces collective bonds and reduces individual vulnerability to economic shocks that might otherwise force members toward short-term exploitative decisions.

## Future Considerations

If [[Terravelle]] avoids institutional seizure and military conflict concludes without catastrophic outcomes, Maren Timber Collective likely survives as viable model demonstrating sustainable forestry practice. Post-war timber scarcity (resulting from other operations' over-harvesting) would strengthen Collective's market position — maintaining healthy forests while others depleted theirs becomes competitive advantage.

If institutional seizure occurs or economic pressure forces Collective dissolution, distributed sustainable forestry practice across [[Terravelle]] collapses. Replacement system likely resembles [[Auralis]] institutional model: centralized control, accelerated extraction, long-term environmental consequences.

[[Marn Drest]] recognizes Collective represents cultural and economic experiment. Success requires maintaining member solidarity, resisting institutional pressure, and demonstrating that long-term sustainability produces better outcomes than short-term extraction maximization. Whether experiment survives wartime stress remains uncertain.
