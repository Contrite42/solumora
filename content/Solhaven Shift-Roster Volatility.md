In [[Solhaven]], steady employment is less common than steady availability. Most harbor and lower-terrace workers are hired through rotating weekly and daily rosters tied to cargo arrival, warehouse backlog, and municipal repair bursts.

## How Shift Volatility Works

The city publishes no single schedule. Instead, labor is fragmented across dock masters, warehouse foremen, contractor scribes, and guild-affiliated dispatch clerks. Workers often queue in two or three places before dawn to secure one shift. A worker may get three consecutive days at 12 to 14 [[Watts]] daily, then lose two days to low cargo volume.

Roster stability is lowest in waterfront handling, sanitation clearing, and emergency terrace maintenance. It is highest in guild-certified technical roles where employers require known credentials and repeat reliability.

## Household Effects

Shift volatility pushes households toward layered income strategies:

- one member takes unstable dock work,
- one member takes lower-wage but predictable neighborhood service work,
- older children or elders handle queue tasks, fuel purchase, and food timing.

When two adults in the same household depend on variable shifts, rent payment timing becomes the central risk. Families with no buffer miss rent by three to seven days in bad weeks, triggering fee multipliers from private landlords.

## Credential Barrier Link

Workers without recognized guild references are moved to the end of preferred rosters even when they are physically capable and experienced. This creates a cycle: unstable work prevents savings, and lack of savings prevents buying into training pathways that unlock more stable rosters.

## Crisis Pattern

During transport disruption windows, shift volatility initially rises (surge unloading, emergency sorting), then collapses (empty berths, reduced inflow). Households that borrowed against expected harbor wages are exposed first.
