Seasonal labor cycles around [[Maren Valley]] and river trade create a rotating tenancy pattern across [[Valdenmoor]] that shapes rent, neighborhood turnover, and household planning.

## Rotation Structure

Three tenant groups rotate through the same housing stock:

- permanent guild households with long-term leases,
- seasonal workers tied to harvest and transport peaks,
- short-stay trainees awaiting certification or placement.

Landlords optimize for whichever group yields the best short-term return in a given season. In high-demand windows, month-to-month rents rise and long-standing informal agreements are re-priced.

## Pressure Windows

Rotation pressure peaks during:

- pre-harvest labor inflow,
- river-volume surges requiring extra handlers,
- post-winter repair seasons when temporary crews arrive.

In these windows, shared-room occupancy increases, lease certainty declines, and eviction-by-renewal-denial becomes more common than formal eviction.

## Social Effects

Neighborhood institutions (guild halls, local markets, mutual aid circles) lose continuity when too many households are temporary. Schooling routines and childcare exchanges become unstable, which then feeds back into worker attendance reliability.

## Household Survival Strategies

Families respond by diversifying tenure risk: one member keeps guild-linked lease access while another uses temporary beds near work zones. This lowers commute failure but increases household fragmentation and care burden.

Where kin networks are weak, families depend on boarding-house keepers as informal coordinators for work notices, food credit, and bed transitions.
