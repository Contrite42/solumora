# Logging Operations

Logging operations harvest timber from forest regions accessible to established settlements, producing construction lumber, specialized woods for metalwork and craftswork, and fuel stock that supplies heating and industrial applications. The sector operates on seasonal cycles coordinated with transportation and processing capacity, employing temporary labor during peak seasons and core workforce during off-seasons.

## Forest Management and Harvesting

**Accessible timber zones** surround major settlements — regions within 2-5 days travel where logging operations can extract timber sustainably without exhausting transport capacity. [[Maren Valley]] settlements harvest from extensive valley forests; coastal settlements draw from riparian zones and foothill timber; northern settlements access subalpine timber with specialized cold-climate species.

**Forest management** at established operations follows multi-year rotation cycles: designated timber zones are harvested over 2-3 year periods, then allowed 15-20 year recovery before re-harvesting. This rotation system maintains timber supply consistency while theoretically permitting forest regeneration.

Sustainable example: [[Maren Timber Collective]] ([[Terravelle]] guild-coordinated cooperative) operates 25-year rotation cycles under [[Marn Drest|T2 lead coordinator]], deliberately limiting output to preserve long-term forest health despite institutional pressure for accelerated production. Represents alternative cooperative management model resisting institutional consolidation.

**Tree selection** for harvesting follows timber-type requirements: hardwoods (oak, ash, birch) for construction and tool-handle materials; specialized woods (cherry, walnut) for fine craftswork; softwoods (pine, fir) for bulk construction and fuel. Tree size, quality, and species guide harvesting decisions.

**Felling** is performed by experienced timber workers using hand tools (specialized axes, saws with team operation requirements). Felling a single large tree requires 1-2 days of skilled work and careful directional control to prevent costly damage.

**Primary processing** happens at logging camps: debranching, rough cutting into transportable logs (8-20 foot lengths), quality grading. Secondary processing (final milling, planing, specialized shaping) occurs at destination settlements where equipment and skilled craftspeople are concentrated.

## Operational Cycle

**Winter recruitment (November-December)** — Logging operations hire temporary labor for spring-through-autumn intensive harvest season. Typical wages: 15-25 Watts weekly for team members, 25-35 Watts weekly for skilled supervisors. Housing (often crude bunkhouse-style) is typically provided on-site, with food often deducted from wages.

**Spring (March-May)** — Accessible timber zones are cleared of winter debris, equipment (saws, axes, transport sleds) is moved into position, camps are established. Intensive harvest begins as weather permits. Spring harvest concentrates on highest-priority timber zones with best access.

**Summer (June-August)** — Peak harvest season. Teams operate at maximum capacity, working 6 days weekly with single rest day. Log volume accumulates at camps awaiting transport. Processing capacity becomes bottleneck as raw logs exceed transport and milling capacity.

**Autumn (September-October)** — Transition to completion of summer harvest and transport preparation. Final logging pushes attempt to clear priority zones before winter. Transport of accumulated logs begins as water flow and trail conditions stabilize.

**Winter (January-February)** — Minimal active harvest; focus shifts toward equipment maintenance and planning. Some operations maintain reduced activity if deep snow provides excellent sledding conditions for log transport (snow-based sleds can move larger logs than wheeled transport).

## Labor Requirements

**Field crews** (4-10 person teams) handle felling, debranching, and log movement. Team-based structure permits work coordination and safety monitoring. Crew members are typically T0-T2 practitioners or non-practitioners with seasonal employment experience. Physical demands are high; injury risk is constant.

**Transport specialists** manage log movement from harvest sites to camps and from camps to river-transport or wagon-transport points. This work requires route knowledge, equipment maintenance, and coordination of multiple teams. Physical demands vary by transport method (river driving is particularly hazardous).

**Processing workers** (at camps and destination mills) sort, grade, and perform secondary processing. Skills range from general labor (moving raw logs) to skilled work (milling, quality control). T1-T3 practitioners typically supervise; T0-T1 workers perform field tasks.

**Supervisory staff** (T2-T3 practitioners) manage crew workflow, equipment maintenance, safety enforcement, and production coordination. Consistent employment contracts run year-round even during off-seasons, reflecting supervisor criticality to operation continuity.

Typical logging operation employs 30-50 core year-round staff plus 50-100 seasonal workers during peak season.

## Transport and Processing

**Log transport** uses waterways where available (rivers permit huge volume movement but water seasons are short), wagon/cart systems where terrain supports it, or sledding over snow in winter regions. Transport bottleneck frequently constrains harvest rate — more logs are harvested than can immediately move out.

**Milling** is concentrated at permanent settlement installations where equipment (sawmills powered by Flux or water wheels), skilled operators, and market access are established. Logs often spend 1-3 months in transit and queue waiting for milling capacity.

**Seasoning** (moisture content reduction) requires 6-12 months for high-quality construction timber. Operations maintain seasoning racks at permanent locations where milled timber dries naturally. Rush processing (Flux-enhanced drying) accelerates timeline but consumes resources.

##Contemporary Pressure and Unsustainability

**Harvest acceleration** is stripping accessible timber zones faster than rotation cycles permit recovery. Operations that historically harvested at 60-70% capacity are now operating at 100-120% capacity (harvesting zones that should be resting, reducing area rotations below theoretical sustainability threshold). Regional deforestation is visible: previously densely-forested zones show obvious timber depletion.

**Labor conscription** is removing experienced timber workers for military operations. Camps report 15-25% workforce reductions in past 12 months from conscription, forcing operations to either reduce output or hire replacement workers with minimal training. Replacement workers suffer higher injury rates and produce lower-quality output.

**Transport bottleneck** is becoming severe. Log volume exceeds transport capacity, creating backlogs at camps and causing wet-season milling queues lasting 4-6 months. Institutions are pressuring operations to solve transport through Flux-intensive solutions (magical transport, Flux-enhanced heavy loading), consuming resources that civilian operations cannot afford.

**Equipment degradation** — Tools require constant maintenance and periodic replacement. Supply chains for replacement tools are increasingly constrained, forcing operations to maintain equipment beyond safe operational periods. Equipment failure risks increase injury rates and reduce production efficiency.

**Secondary sustainability concerns** — Deforestation creates erosion, stream sedimentation, and habitat loss for game species. Operations harvest timber without replanting in many regions, converting sustainable rotation-based management into extraction-based resource depletion.

Logging is transitioning from sustainable multi-generational enterprise to short-term extraction operation optimized for current military material demands without regard for long-term forest health.
