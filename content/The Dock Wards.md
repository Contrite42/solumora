The Dock Wards are [[Valdenmoor]]'s river-facing commercial district — the stretch of the city's western bank where the [[Maren River]] receives its traffic and converts it into the goods and supply chains that feed the rest of [[Terravelle]]. They are the city's functional center of gravity, and they smell like river and old timber and whatever is being unloaded this week.

## Layout

The Dock Wards run for roughly two miles along the Maren's western bank and extend back inland three or four blocks before the city's character shifts from waterfront commercial to the interior trades and residences. The waterfront itself is a working surface: permanent pier structures, craned loading platforms, covered warehousing that runs right up to the water's edge, and the constant negotiation of space between boats, carts, laborers, and the administrators who track what goes where.

Behind the immediate waterfront are the warehousing streets — two- and three-story buildings that hold everything in transit: goods waiting for the next stage of the journey east toward [[Ashford]], goods that have arrived from upriver communities and need distribution into Valdenmoor's own market, seasonal agricultural surplus that has been purchased forward and is waiting for the price to settle. The smell of grain is constant; in late summer, the district's air carries a particular sweetness when the harvest deliveries are arriving continuously.

Further back are the offices: freight companies, bonded storage operators, freight brokers who coordinate between ship schedules and land transport, and the administrative guild offices that maintain the [[Maren Freight Chain]]'s dispatch records. These streets are quieter and somewhat more formal than the waterfront, and the people in them are managing numbers and schedules rather than physical loads.

## Economy

The Dock Wards' economy is volume and coordination. Individual transactions are small; the aggregate is enormous. A single good harvest from the Maren Valley farm communities means weeks of continuous delivery — boats running continuously, warehouse staff working extended hours, freight brokers updating schedules daily. A poor harvest, or a flood that disrupts the river navigation in early spring, produces the reverse: reduced incoming volume, idle labor, and the particular economic tension that comes from a city of half a million adjusting to temporarily reduced throughput.

The ferry crossings are part of the district's economy as well. Valdenmoor spans both river banks, and the crossings between them are constant — people, livestock, equipment, and small cargo that does not justify the delay of the main dock logistics chain. The ferry operators are a distinct professional community from the dock workers, with their own guild track and their own deep knowledge of the river's seasonal behavior.

[[Caven Torst]] has run one of the main crossings for fifteen years. His knowledge of the Maren's behavior at that specific point — the current patterns that change between spring snowmelt and late summer low water, the exact wind behavior that makes a loaded crossing dangerous in certain conditions — is the kind of knowledge that a tier assessment does not measure and that guild certification does not formally recognize but that the people who use his crossing consider as valuable as any documented credential.

## Social Character

The Dock Wards are the most economically diverse part of Valdenmoor. Day laborers from rural communities come in for seasonal work and leave; permanent dock workers have guild standing and reasonable wages; freight administrators earn professional-tier income; successful freight brokers and warehouse operators accumulate capital that eventually lets them move into other districts without quite leaving the Dock Wards behind.

Tier matters here but in a narrower way than in most of Valdenmoor. Physical dock work does not require Flux capacity, so T0 and T1 workers are common on the waterfront itself. The specialized work — maintaining the flood-control [[Sigils]] on the river walls, running the preservation spells on grain storage, doing the cargo documentation that requires Copymark authentication — is T2-T3 work and is paid accordingly. A T0 dock foreman with fifteen years of experience earns roughly the same as a T2 sigil worker doing routine ward maintenance, because the guild wage structures account for practical expertise alongside tier capacity.

The Covenant of Measure is practiced here without much formal organization. There is no temple specifically in the Dock Wards — the nearest guild halls that host Covenant ceremonies are one block into the Guild Quarter proper. But the Covenant's practical ethic — proportionate effort, honest dealing, waste as a moral failure — is how the Dock Wards actually operate, because waste in a logistics context is also economic failure, and the culture has aligned the ethical and the practical.

## Notable Features

The **North Dock Market** operates from early morning as the day labor pool for dock work — workers gather, foremen assess needs, assignments are made. It is not an auction; the guild rates apply. It is a daily coordination mechanism, and it functions with the kind of efficiency that comes from the same interaction happening every morning for decades.

The **River Wall Maintenance Posts** are the station points for the guild-certified T2-T3 casters who maintain the flood-control sigil system. The original sigil work on the Valdenmoor river walls is centuries old; each generation's maintenance has added overlapping layers that senior practitioners describe as the most complex accumulated preservation structure in [[Terravelle]]. The work requires understanding what the original casters were doing well enough to maintain it without disrupting the underlying logic, which is a form of historical scholarship practiced by people who consider themselves maintenance workers.
