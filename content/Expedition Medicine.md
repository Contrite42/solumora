Expedition medicine exists because ordinary clinic assumptions fail in the crossing economy around [[Equatorial Desert]] and [[Halveth]].

A city clinic assumes stable shelter, repeat visits, and access to follow-up supplies. A crossing team has heat stress, contaminated water risk, unstable sleep, and injury in places where evacuation may be impossible for days. Medical planning is therefore integrated into route planning, weight planning, and command discipline.

## Pre-Departure Medical Planning

Serious expeditions in [[Halveth]] budget medicine before they budget optional equipment.

Standard pre-departure planning includes:

- screening for prior heat injury and unresolved infection;
- assigning one treatment lead and one backup;
- purchasing staged supply kits by route duration;
- defining hard return thresholds before departure.

Teams that skip this work often improvise later, and improvisation under heat stress has a high failure rate.

## Field Treatment Priorities

In the field, treatment priority is:

1. Stop bleeding and stabilize breathing.
2. Control contamination and dehydration.
3. Preserve mobility and navigation capacity.
4. Decide fast whether to continue or withdraw.

The fourth decision is where most teams fail. Leaders delay withdrawal because sunk cost feels operationally rational. Medically, delay often turns recoverable injuries into fatal ones.

## Supply Architecture

Medical kits are packed in layers so teams can lose one pack without losing all capability.

- **Immediate layer:** bleeding control, pain suppression, basic antimicrobial treatment.
- **Sustain layer:** rehydration salts, fever management, wound re-cleaning supplies.
- **Contingency layer:** splinting, emergency sedatives, anti-shock compounds, signaling tools.

Supply degradation is expected. Heat shortens stability windows for several compounds, so experienced crews rotate stock by season and never trust old inventory labels.

## Evacuation Protocols

Evacuation is a logistics problem first and a medical problem second.

Teams use three evacuation modes:

- controlled return under reduced pace;
- relay transfer to guide stations;
- emergency extraction with asset abandonment.

Emergency extraction saves lives but destroys expedition economics. Many teams hesitate too long because losing cargo can end a sponsor relationship. This is one reason death rates remain higher than official crossing literature suggests.

## Typical Field Injuries

Most expedition injuries are not dramatic combat wounds.

- severe dehydration and electrolyte collapse;
- infected minor cuts that worsen quickly in heat;
- stress fractures and joint failure from load imbalance;
- acute panic episodes during rift-adjacent events;
- Flux destabilization in teams using heavy sigil support.

Teams that train for ordinary injuries outperform teams that only plan for rare catastrophic events.

## Desert-Edge Referral Pattern

When teams return to [[Halveth]], referrals split:

- routine recovery handled locally by T1-T2 practitioners;
- complicated trauma sent toward [[Ashford]] or [[Solhaven]];
- pathway or anomaly-related cases escalated to specialized [[Flux Medicine]] practitioners.

The delay between field injury and specialist intake is the critical mortality factor. Even good field stabilization can fail if transfer time exceeds tissue viability or infection control windows.

## Practical Constraint

Expedition medicine is judged by one blunt metric: who comes back alive with recoverable function. Everything else, including purity of protocol, is secondary.
