The Outer Coast Packet Line is the long-distance coastal shipping circuit that connects northern settlements near [[Wolfpoint]] with the eastern export basin around [[Ashford]] and, through contracted transfer ports, selected southern buyers tied to [[Auralis]]. It is slow, seasonal, and economically essential.

Packet captains run smaller, faster-hull vessels than bulk freighters, optimized for mixed cargo and document reliability rather than maximum tonnage. Typical loads include preserved fish, treated cloth, replacement tools, medicinal compounds, and sealed correspondence that cannot wait for overland relay chains. A full circuit can take weeks depending on weather windows and inspection delays.

The line matters because it links places that do not share institutions. Wolfpoint does not operate on either kingdom's timetable. Ashford operates on trade volume. Auralis coastal buyers operate on political caution. Packet operators bridge those mismatched systems with contract language, redundant manifests, and a reputation economy where one bad run can end a captain's access for a year.

Risk is managed, not removed. Northern fog banks can erase visibility for hours, and eastern patrol pressure can turn routine checks into day-long holds. The usual mitigation is redundancy: duplicate ledger entries, parallel packet dispatches for critical documents, and predefined fallback ports when a planned handoff fails.

Everyone in the chain knows the same rule: if the packet line stops moving, information lags first and shortages follow. Keeping it moving is cheaper than repairing what happens after a break.

## Coastal Settlement Integration

The Line integrates with [[Terravelle]]'s western coastal settlement network, making stops at maritime communities that provide distributed services the packet circuit depends on: fishing operations, provisions acquisition, storm intelligence, and emergency repair access.

[[Brend Point]] is a mid-route stopping point along the northern section, positioned where packet ships operating between [[Wolfpoint]] and southern [[Terravelle]] ports can access northern coastal resources. Ships stop here for:

- Preserved fish for provisions (cold-water species that provide sustained nutrition during extended transits)
- Storm intelligence from Veln Tors' weather coordination network (critical for planning northern fog-zone operations)
- Fresh water and supply replenishment before entering remote northern sections with limited settlement infrastructure
- Message transport serving [[Brend Point]]'s collective operations and connecting them to broader [[Terravelle]] economic networks

Stops at [[Brend Point]] typically occur during northbound or southbound transits when weather windows allow and supply needs justify the time cost. Unlike the high-frequency [[Hedun-Solhaven Coastal Run]], the Outer Coast Line operates on longer cycles with greater schedule flexibility, which means coastal stops are integrated opportunistically rather than through fixed timetables.

This flexibility reflects the Line's operational reality: northern coastal conditions are severe enough that rigid schedules fail more often than adaptive routing. Packet captains maintain relationships with coastal settlements like [[Brend Point]], know which settlements provide which services, and adjust stopping patterns based on current weather, supply status, and cargo priorities.

The distributed settlement network along [[Terravelle]]'s western coast — [[Brend Point]] and similar communities northward — creates operational infrastructure that makes the packet line viable in zones where major port cities do not exist. Without these settlements providing provisions, storm warnings, and emergency support, packet operations in northern coastal zones would require significantly longer transit times, higher operational costs, and worse reliability.

The relationship is economically reciprocal: packet ships provide coastal settlements with market access for preserved fish, connection to inland trade networks, message transport, and intelligence about broader maritime conditions. Settlements provide packet operations with distributed services that improve safety, reduce costs, and enable sustained circuit operations across challenging coastal zones.

Together, the formal packet-line infrastructure and informal [[Coastal Settlement Network]] create a maritime system that functions across Solumora's western coastlines despite limited institutional coordination and the absence of comprehensive port city infrastructure in remote northern zones.

## Seasonal Operations and Weather Pressure

The Outer Coast Packet Line operates according to a seasonal schedule determined by weather patterns that vary predictably across the year. Packet schedules are not fixed — they adjust monthly in response to storm probability, storm intensity, seasonal wind patterns, and settlement-side supply availability.

**Optimal Window (Highdry–Harvestfirst, 10-12 weeks):** Northern fog density is lower and storm frequency is minimal. Packet captains prefer to schedule major runs during this window when crossing visibility is higher and unexpected storm encounters are uncommon. 60-70% of annual packet volume concentrates in this optimal window. Both northbound (with supply goods and correspondence for [[Wolfpoint]]) and southbound (with intelligence, northern-origin exports, and priority documents) traffic peaks during optimal-window weeks. Coastal settlement provisioning reaches maximum capacity — [[Brend Point]] and similar stops experience surge demand for preserved provisions and require advance notice of high-traffic periods.

**Transition Periods (Brightward–early Highdry and Peaklite–early Harvestfall, 4–6 weeks each):** Storm frequency increases and fog patterns become unpredictable, but operations remain viable. Packet schedules accept longer transit times and higher delay probability. Single-run reliability declines from 85-90% in optimal window to 70-75% in transition periods. Captains accept margin pressure during transitions: a two-week delay at [[Brend Point]] waiting for a storm window is routine. Commercial operators using the line accept transition-period delivery windows as standard contract terms and price cargo accordingly.

**High-Risk Season (Harvestfall–Coolward, 8-10 weeks):** Storm intensity climbs and seasonal wind patterns produce unpredictable conditions. Storm frequency reaches 40-50% of operating days. Packet scheduling becomes episodic rather than consistent — captains wait for multi-day weather windows instead of maintaining regular departure patterns. Transit time stretches from 10-14 days to 18-25 days. Routine delays accumulate into significant lags. Cargo insurance premiums increase 30-50%. Some packet runs are explicitly cancelled if cargo priority does not justify the extended delay risk. The line continues operating, but at reduced efficiency and elevated cost. A shipment of routine supplies scheduled for fourteen-day transit might instead require thirty days.

**Winter Isolation (Colden–Thawmark, 8-10 weeks):** Coastal conditions shift into operational difficulty. Northern fog obscures navigation entirely during extended periods. Storm frequency remains high. Extreme cold damages equipment and provisioning. Packet scheduling drops to reactive operations — captains only depart when critical cargo requires movement or weather windows open unexpectedly. Volume collapses to 10-15% of optimal-window traffic. [[Wolfpoint]] settlement receives minimal packet service; critical correspondence and urgent supplies arrive monthly at best. Isolated settlements deplete reserves during winter isolation; supply shortages that were manageable if packets arrived weekly become critical if packets arrive monthly.

**Unpredictable Spring (Firstgreen–Plantmoon, 5-6 weeks):** River melt affects coastal current patterns. Spring storms are fierce but brief. Ice remnants complicate northern navigation. Packet scheduling is chaotic — weather windows are variable and often brief. Captains plan single-run transits instead of circuits. Transit times are unpredictable (10-20 day variance is normal). The line functions during spring, but provides unreliable service. Settlement provisioning plans cannot depend on spring packet arrivals.

**Schedule Impact:** The 10-12 week optimal window combined with transition periods provides approximately 18-20 weeks of reliable packet service. Winter isolation and unpredictable spring compromise 16-18 weeks of high-delay operations. Commercial expectations align with this reality: critical cargo is routed to depart during optimal windows or accepts multi-week delays; routine commerce depends on consistent packet schedules and experiences disruption during winter isolation; urgent communication uses parallel systems (overland couriers, institutional channels) when packet service is unreliable.

**War Pressure Cascade:** Current conscription and material requisitions disrupt the packet line's economics. Military supply priority seizes packet capacity during optimal windows, reducing merchant volume. [[Auralis]] coastal patrol increases create longer inspection delays. Equipment maintenance is deferred (clockmakers, riggers conscripted). Packet crew recruitment becomes difficult. The result: packets that normally depart three times per month during optimal-window peaks are departing once monthly or cancelled entirely. Winter isolation — already a service bottleneck — becomes near-total.
