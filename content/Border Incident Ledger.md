# Border Incident Ledger

The **Border Incident Ledger** is a shared minimal record format used by bilateral liaisons to keep high-friction border incidents on a common factual baseline before political narratives diverge.

It is not a treaty instrument. It is an operational tool used through [[Border Coordination Offices]] and customs coordinators in [[Ashford]], [[Halveth]], and related corridor offices.

## Purpose

The ledger exists to solve one practical problem: incidents escalate faster than policy clarification.

Without a shared record, each side logs different facts and then treats disagreement itself as evidence of bad faith. The ledger narrows that gap by forcing early alignment on basic event structure.

## Minimum Record Fields

Each ledger entry requires only fields that can be verified quickly:

- incident timestamp and location
- cargo or packet class (not full sensitive details)
- trigger condition used by enforcement
- hold/seizure scope (specific packet vs full batch)
- review authority and release authority
- status at 24h, 48h, and closure

This keeps entries short enough for real operations while preserving enough structure for later review.

## When It Activates

Operators open a ledger entry when incidents cross agreed severity thresholds:

- off-checkpoint interception of licensed carriers
- full-batch seizure for mixed-risk cargo
- holds exceeding corridor norms
- any event receiving public narrative framing before technical adjudication

These are the same trigger patterns identified in the [[Near-War Incident Ladder]] transition from Stage 3 to Stage 4.

## Operational Value

The ledger does three useful things even under tension:

- creates shared chronology for de-escalation channels
- limits rumor expansion in [[Cross-Border Information Flow]]
- supports after-action corrections to [[Border De-Escalation Protocols]]

It does not prevent escalation by itself. It makes escalation harder to justify using contradictory timelines.

## Failure Modes

The ledger fails in predictable ways:

- entries are created late, after public framing hardens
- local offices omit hold-scope details to preserve domestic discretion
- updates stop at "pending review" and never reach closure annotation

When this happens, the ledger still exists but loses correction power and becomes administrative theater.

## Current Use Pattern

Current corridor reporting suggests increased ledger activation but uneven quality.

In practical terms, offices are logging more events but closing fewer entries with synchronized final status. That pattern indicates rising incident volume and reduced resolution capacity rather than improved transparency.
