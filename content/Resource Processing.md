# Resource Processing

Resource processing transforms raw extraction materials into refined products suitable for manufacturing, consumption, or institutional use. Processing operations range from simple mechanical separation (ore sorting, timber milling) to complex chemical and Flux-intensive refinement (metal smelting, creature venom extraction). The sector bridges extraction and manufacturing, representing 8-12% of economic activity but facing severe capacity constraints from military demand and labor conscription.

## Processing Categories

**Ore processing and smelting** converts mined ore into refined metal stock through crushing, ore-concentration separation, and thermal smelting. Smelting capacity determines extraction viability — raw ore has limited market value; smelted metal commands commodity pricing. Institutional smelting operations achieve economies of scale; distributed small foundries lack efficiency. Contemporary military demand is concentrating smelting capacity toward institutional control, reducing independent operators' market access.

**Timber milling** converts raw logs into finished dimensional lumber through mechanical sawing, drying, and finishing. Mill location determines timely processing — transport constraints often force milling at regional hubs where logs accumulate. Specialized milling (exotic woods, precision work) commands premium pricing. Automation (water-powered or Flux-powered mills) improves efficiency but requires capital investment most independent operators cannot sustain.

**Creature-product processing** extracts and refines venom, hides, bone, and organ materials from harvested creatures. Specialized protocols preserve fragile materials; timing is critical. High-value products (Bloodshrike venom, preserved organs) require expert handling; mistakes cause total material loss. Flux-intensive extraction (venom isolation under controlled Flux conditions) commands premium prices but consumes resources.

**Food preservation processing** (salting, smoking, drying) converts perishable harvest into stable products. [[Mountain Game Collective]]'s preserved-meat operation exemplifies scaled preservation; household-level food preservation is widespread but informal. Commercial preservation operations occupy middle ground between household and institutional processing.

**Textile processing** converts harvested wool/fiber into finished textile products through cleaning, carding, spinning, and weaving. [[Wolfpoint]]'s textile processing is incomplete — raw wool is carded locally, spun and woven elsewhere. Terravelle textile guilds maintain integrated operations; Auralis operations are fragmented.

**Salvage material processing** recovers and refines recovered pre-collapse components: separating recoverable materials from degradation, decontamination protocols for materials showing Flux anomalies, structural restoration where integrity permits.

## Labor Tier Structure

**Unskilled labor** (T0-T1) performs physically demanding but repetitive work — ore crushing, log movement, hide processing. Wages: 8-15 Watts weekly. High workplace injury risk; minimal career advancement.

**Skilled operators** (T2-T3) manage specialized processes: mill operation requiring precision, smelting-furnace management balancing heat control and material flow, creature-venom extraction requiring Flux sensitivity. Wages: 20-35 Watts weekly. Requires training; progression to supervisor roles possible.

**Master craftspeople/specialists** (T3-T4) design process parameters, troubleshoot failures, manage quality control. Deeply embedded skill development; responsible for operation consistency and product quality. Wages: 40-60+ Watts weekly; often profit-sharing arrangements. Conscription of this tier creates operational cascades.

**Management and logistics** (T2-T4) manage production scheduling, supply coordination, market distribution. Often overlapping with craftspeople; specialized role in larger operations. Conscription profoundly affects operational capacity.

## Contemporary Pressure

**Capacity bottleneck** — Raw material extraction is increasing faster than processing capacity can handle. Mining operations are producing ore faster than smelting can process; logging operations are harvesting timber faster than milling can process. This creates raw-material inventory buildup and forces prioritization decisions.

**Specialist conscription** — Master craftspeople and skilled-operator tier are disproportionately conscripted for military operations. Their technical skills transfer to military Flux applications and equipment manufacturing. Replacement training is inadequate, creating cascading skill loss across processing operations.

**Flux resource allocation** — Flux-intensive processing (metal smelting, venom extraction, accelerated drying) is increasingly diverted toward military applications. Civilian processing operations face Flux availability constraints; military production takes priority. This is forcing civilian operations toward less-efficient processes, increasing cost and reducing output.

**Capital equipment degradation** — Mills, smelting furnaces, specialized extraction equipment require maintenance and periodic replacement. Equipment supply chains are constrained; operations are maintaining equipment beyond safe service life, increasing mechanical failure risk and reducing efficiency.

**Market consolidation pressure** — Institutional buyers are securing exclusive contracts with large processing operations, reducing independent operators' market access. Smaller mills, smelters, and processing facilities are facing margin compression and consolidation pressure.

**Environmental impact acceleration** — Processing operations generate byproducts and waste: ore-mining tailings creating run-off contamination, timber-milling sawdust/bark accumulation, creature-processing organic waste. Simultaneously with capacity increases, environmental remediation is being deprioritized as resources are diverted toward military production.

## Operational Efficiency and Sustainability

**Processing efficiency** directly determines economic viability. A smelting operation achieving 85% ore-to-metal conversion is profitable; one achieving 70% is marginal. Contemporary pressure to increase output is forcing efficiency reduction — prioritizing speed over refinement, accepting higher scrap rates, operating equipment beyond design parameters.

**Economies of scale** — Large institutional operations achieve efficiencies impossible for smaller operators: equipment utilization rates, bulk material purchasing, labor distribution across multiple products. Military-driven institutional consolidation is accelerating scale advantages, systematically favoring institutional consolidation over independent operations.

**Sustainability pressure** — Processing at current acceleration rates is approaching sustainability boundaries in multiple categories. Timber processing is depleting accessible timber supply; creature-product processing is exceeding harvest sustainability; ore refining creates environmental degradation. Continued acceleration without extractive-sector restraint will transition sustainability concerns into hard resource constraints within 3-5 years.

Processing is transitioning from diverse independent-operator sector to consolidated institutional-dominant system optimized for military production rather than economically balanced commercial operations.
