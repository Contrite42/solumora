Selected letters from [[Ilke Vann]], trader, coastal circuit [[Ashford]]–[[Halveth]], to a correspondent in Halveth. Fifteen years of correspondence; excerpts arranged by season and subject.

---

**On the midpoint cape, spring**

The cape looks different in spring than any other time. I know you've seen it from the land side and I know you think the land view is the better one. The land view is wrong. From the water, when the spring current is running south and you're working against it through the cape passage, the cliff face is close enough that you can see the individual ledges where the birds nest. The birds are nesting now. There are thousands of them. The noise they make is not something I can describe in a way that will be useful to you. It is simply a wall of sound that the *Reasonable Margin* passes through over about four minutes, and then the cape is behind you and the sound drops off and the coast opens up again to the south.

I think about this every spring and then I am there and it is always more than I thought.

---

**On a passenger, autumn**

I took a passenger on the northern leg last month. She needed to reach Ashford without using the inland roads and did not want to explain why. I did not ask. She sat in the forward section for most of the run and watched the water and occasionally asked me practical questions about coastal navigation that were better than the questions most passengers ask.

On the second day she said: "You watch the water the way someone watches a person they're trying to understand."

I said that was accurate.

She said: "What have you understood?"

I told her I'd been working on it for eighteen years and was not finished.

She got off in Ashford, paid fairly, and that was the end of it. I've thought about her question twice since, which is less than I expected.

---

**On the bar at Halveth harbor, any season**

The bar shifts. I know you know this. I know you have watched at least two operators come into the harbor incorrectly because they did not account for the seasonal drift and one of them had to be pulled off the sandbar with lines from the dock, which I witnessed from the water and found informative. What I want you to understand is that the bar is not the problem. The problem is that the bar looks the same regardless of where it actually is. The surface reads the same. The color reads the same. The problem is a reading problem, not a navigation problem, and the solution is to check your last known position and correct from that rather than from what the water looks like.

I am telling you this because the second operator who came in wrong was using a chart that was accurate six months prior and had not accounted for drift. Accurate charts become inaccurate. This is the primary lesson of fifteen years on this coast and I have not found a way to make it more interesting than that.

---

**On winter, on the water**

Winter on this coast is not dramatic the way people who have not sailed it imagine. The storms come and I wait them out in coves I have mapped over fifteen years, and the *Reasonable Margin* holds, and I read or sleep or watch the water change color as the weather moves through. The dramatic part is in the decision-making before the storm, not in the storm itself.

The coast in winter light is something I have tried to describe to you before and not quite managed. It is low and grey and very specific. The color the water turns in winter is not a color that has a name I'm aware of. It is the color the water turns. I will try again when I am back.
