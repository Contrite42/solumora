# Economy

The [[Solumora]] economy is an integrated system of resource extraction, production, commerce, currency, credit mechanisms, and institutional oversight that collectively enable the two kingdoms and their distributed settlements to function as economically coherent entities despite geographic separation and institutional independence. The system is simultaneously robust (distributed across multiple institutional structures, merchants, and operational loci) and fragile (dependent on institutional relationships that are visible strain under war preparation pressures).

## Core Economic Functions

**Resource Production** — Primary extraction and production of goods that form the foundation of economic activity:

- Agricultural production ([[Maren Valley]] grain farming, {{Equatorial Desert]] irrigation settlements, coastal fisheries)
- Resource extraction (mineral operations, logging, salt harvest, creature-product harvesting)
- Craftwork production (metalwork, textile manufacturing, grimoire copying, equipment fabrication)
- Knowledge services (healing practitioners, educational institutions, training services, specialized expertise)

Contemporary pressure: Military requisitions and conscription reducing agricultural labor availability, creature-product demand overwhelming sustainable harvesting levels, craftwork labor competing with military conscription.

**Currency and Financial Infrastructure** — Systems enabling value-exchange and capital allocation:

- [[Watts as Currency|Watts-denominated currency system]] providing unified economic measurement across both kingdoms
- [[Banking Institutions|Institutional banking]] (Auralis centralized, Terravelle distributed) managing accounts, authentication, credit provision
- [[Cross-Kingdom Financial Exchange|Cross-kingdom settlement mechanisms]] enabling inter-kingdom commerce despite institutional separation
- [[Informal Lending Systems|Informal lending networks]] providing capital to population segments institutional banking cannot serve

Contemporary pressure: Institutional currency instability (2-3% inflation), lending compression toward military priorities, authentication practitioner scarcity increasing verification costs.

**Commerce Infrastructure** — Physical and organizational mechanisms enabling goods movement and merchant operations:

- [[Trade & Travel|Trade routes and transportation systems]] (land caravan routes, maritime shipping, courier services)
- [[Merchant Houses|Merchant house networks]] providing wholesale distribution, capital aggregation, information networks
- [[Trade Guilds]] (Terravelle) setting standards, resolving disputes, maintaining apprenticeship systems
- [[The Advancement Corps]] (Auralis) providing institutional purchasing power and logistics infrastructure

Contemporary pressure: Military logistics competing for transport capacity, border settlements experiencing military encroachment, trading routes experiencing security degradation.

**Institutional Risk Management** — Structures theoretically designed to manage economic disruption and maintain stability:

- [[Council of Auralis]] institutional oversight, policy direction, centralized reserves
- [[Trade Guilds|Guild Federation]] coordination, mutual-assistance funds, dispute resolution
- [[Covenant of Measure|Covenant]] philosophical structure (proportional measure, capability-based assessment) theoretically constraining exploitative economic behavior
- Institutional insurance mechanisms (limited; primarily mutual-assistance structures rather than formal insurance)

Contemporary pressure: Institutional reserves depleting for military financing, mutual-assistance capacity strained by simultaneous institutional strain across multiple sectors.

## Economic Sectors

**Agricultural Sector** — Primary production of grains, preserved foods, and basic agricultural products:

- Production centers: [[Maren Valley]] (massive grain farming), [[Equatorial Fauna|equatorial settlements]] (irrigation agriculture), coastal regions (fishing, salt preservation)
- Market structure: Small-scale family farming (60-70% of production), medium-scale commercial farming (20-25%), institutional agricultural operations (5-10%)
- Supply chain: From farmer → regional aggregation (local markets, merchant collection points) → regional distribution (Ashford, Solhaven, Valdenmoor) → retail/institutional consumption
- Economic contribution: Estimated 30-40% of total economic activity (by Watt volume), 50%+ of employed population
- Contemporary pressure: Military conscription reducing available labor, requisition quotas forcing farmers to sell at below-market prices, increased food demand to supply armies, extreme weather affecting crop yields

**Craft and Manufacturing Sector** — Production of goods beyond basic agricultural output:

- Metalwork (weaponry, tools, precision instruments), textile production, equipment fabrication, grimoire copying, food preservation
- Market structure: Guild-affiliated craftspeople (Terravelle) providing certified production, independent craftspeople in parallel market, institutional production for military supply
- Production scale: Small-scale individual craftspeople (40-50%), medium-scale workshops (30-40%), large institutional operations (10-15%)
- Economic contribution: Estimated 20-30% of economic activity, concentrated in major settlements
- Contemporary pressure: Labor conscription affecting workshop capacity, material scarcity (particularly Flux-enhanced materials), institutional contracting for military equipment, craftspeople skill transition to military operations

**Service Sector** — Provision of knowledge, specialization, and capability services:

- Healing practitioners, education, entertainment, specialized services (cartography, route guidance, equipment repair, skill training)
- Market scaling: Individual practitioners (60-70%), institutional service provision (20-30%), informal services (10%)
- Economic contribution: 10-15% of economic activity by Watt volume, higher cultural contribution
- Contemporary pressure: Practitioner conscription for military operations, institutional service prioritization toward military support, informal service growth where institutional service becomes unavailable

**Resource Extraction Sector** — Harvesting of raw materials (minerals, salvaged materials, creature products):

- Mining operations, logging, salt harvesting, hunting/creature harvesting, ruin salvage
- Production scale: Small-scale individual efforts (40%), medium-scale operations (40%), large institutional extraction (20%)
- Market structure: Specialty commodity markets (creature products command premium pricing, salvaged materials highly variable), institutional purchasing for military operations
- Economic contribution: 5-10% of Watt volume but critical material supply for other sectors
- Contemporary pressure: Military demand overwhelming sustainable supply levels, environmental degradation from extraction acceleration, institutional monopolization of high-value materials (Flux-reactive minerals, creature venom)

**Financial Services Sector** — Banking, currency management, lending, investment facilitation:

- Institutional banking (Auralis Council Banking, Terravelle Guild banking houses), informal lending networks, merchant-house credit services
- Market participation: Institutional services (60-70% of high-volume transactions), merchant-house intermediaries (15-20%), informal lending (10-15%)
- Economic contribution: 2-5% of Watt volume (by lending, fees, transaction costs) but critical infrastructure enabling other sectors' operation
- Contemporary pressure: Institutional consolidation reducing competition, military lending priority compressing civilian credit supply, informal lending acceleration, currency instability from military financing

## Economic Inequality and Tier Integration

Economic activity stratifies by [[Control Tier|tier]], creating fundamental inequality:

**High-Tier Practitioner Access** — T3+ practitioners have access to economic opportunities unavailable to lower-tier populations:

- Higher-paying specialized services (T4-T5 healing, high-tier spell services, institutional expertise)
- Credit access advantages (banks provide better rates to T3+ borrowers, institutional relationships enable preferential terms)
- Employment opportunities in institutional work (Council positions, guild authorization, high-skilled craftwork)
- Capital accumulation advantages (higher income, preferential lending, institutional employment stability)

**Mid-Tier Professional Class** — T2-T3 practitioners representing the bulk of commercial workforce:

- Skilled craftwork, teaching, merchant operations, service provision
- Moderate credit access through institutional banking or established merchant relationships
- Guild membership (Terravelle) or institutional affiliation (Auralis) providing professional structure
- Economic vulnerability to institutional disruption (conscription affecting service provision, credit compression affecting business capital availability)

**Low-Tier Population** — T0-T1 practitioners and non-practitioners:

- Limited to low-skill labor, agricultural work, basic service provision
- Minimal institutional credit access; dependent on informal lending at higher rates
- Economic vulnerability to institutional policy (agricultural requisitions, labor conscription)
- Subsistence-level income for most of this population; advancement to mid-tier professional status difficult due to capital scarcity and educational access barriers

**Institutional Pressure on Tier Integration** — Contemporary war preparation has accelerated tier stratification:

- Military conscription disproportionately affects T1-T3 practitioners (providing skilled labor), removing skilled craft and service providers from civilian economy
- Credit compression disproportionately affects marginal borrowers (T0-T1 tier), reducing entrepreneurship and small-business formation in lower-tier population
- Institutional employment prioritization for T3+ practitioners, reducing mid-tier availability for non-institutional work
- Informal lending growth serving T0-T1 population increasingly uses predatory mechanisms, structurally disadvantaging lower-tier borrowers

## Economic Sustainability and Risk

The economy faces multiple sustainability challenges, many accelerating due to war preparation:

**Agricultural Sustainability** — Crop production capacity relies on soil health, water availability, and seasonal labor availability. Military conscription and resource requisitions are reducing labor availability while increasing food demand. Crop yields in heavily requisitioned regions show 10-15% decline, creating emerging food scarcity risk. Institutional authorities attempt management through grain requisition reductions and labor exemptions, but inadequately address fundamental sustainability questions.

**Resource Extraction Sustainability** — Creature harvesting is approaching unsustainable levels (estimated 20-30% above sustainable yield in creatures like Bloodshrike and Flux Scorpion), lumber harvesting is concentrating in remaining accessible forests, and salt-harvest operations show emerging degradation. Institutional demand for military materials (particularly Flux-reactive materials and creature venom) is overwhelming sustainable supply levels.

**Financial System Stability** — Currency inflation, institutional capital drain toward military operations, and informal lending system growth are creating financial system fragility. Banking institutions increasingly dependent on institutional backing rather than merchant capital deposits. If military operations escalate further or if any significant banking institution experiences disruption, cascade failures could destroy institutional credit capacity.

**Labor Force Sustainability** — Military conscription is removing approximately 10-15% of working-age population from civilian economy in active military-preparation regions (Auralis southern settlements, Ashford). This level of labor removal is reducing production capacity across all economic sectors. Simultaneous military conscription in multiple sectors (craftwork, service provision, agricultural work) suggests military planners are attempting to prepare force structures without regard for civilian economic sustainability.

**Cross-Kingdom Relationship Sustainability** — Trade relationships between kingdoms depend on mutual benefit and reasonable tariff/trade cost structures. Contemporary war preparation has created structural misalignment: Auralis military operations create security risk for [[Terravelle]] border settlements, while Terravelle resources (grain, minerals, craftwork) are increasingly requisitioned by Auralis for military support. If political relationship between kingdoms deteriorates significantly, trade relationships would suffer.

## Institutional Response to Economic Pressure

Institutional authorities have implemented multiple policy responses:

**Auralis Council Responses:**
- Increased taxation (5-10% average increase) to fund military operations and institutional deficit spending
- Institutional borrowing from internal reserves to fund military operations (depleting reserves at estimated 8-12% annually)
- Institutional lending prioritization toward military ventures and essential civilian services
- Currency supply increases (printed Flux-marked currency to increase available capital, creating 2-3% inflation)
- Labor conscription and agricultural requisition quotas to direct resources toward military preparation

**Terravelle Guild Responses:**
- Trade guild mutual-assistance fund increases to support economically stressed merchants and craftspeople
- Guild apprenticeship program expansion attempting to train replacement workforce for conscripted skilled workers
- Rural assistance programs providing emergency grain supplies to communities experiencing crop failure
- Inter-guild coordination attempting to manage resource allocation priorities and prevent sector-specific collapse

**Coordinated Responses:**
- Informal institutional agreement between kingdoms regarding trade route protection (military patrols reducing banditry, enabling continued commerce)
- Currency coordination maintaining Watt-based trade settlement across kingdom borders
- Limited emergency lending coordination (institutions occasionally providing emergency liquidity support to maintain critical trade relationships)

## Post-War Economic Scenarios

The economic system faces fundamental uncertainty about post-war stabilization:

**Optimistic Scenario** — Military operations conclude, institutional authorities reduce military spending, institutions redirect capital toward economic reconstruction, labor conscription ends, practitioners and craftspeople return to civilian roles. In this scenario, economy would recover gradually over 2-3 years, with permanent changes in tier distribution and institutional relationships but generally restoring pre-war economic structure.

**Pessimistic Scenario** — Military escalation continues or expands, institutional reserves deplete, civilian credit system collapses, agricultural production fails to meet population needs, resource extraction becomes unsustainable. In this scenario, economic collapse would create cascading failures across multiple sectors, with multi-year recovery period and permanent structural reorganization of economic system.

**Institutional Integration Scenario** — Contemporary war operations accelerate institutional integration of civilian economy, with institutional authorities gradually assuming direct control over resource allocation, labor deployment, and credit provision. In this scenario, post-war economy would remain institutionally controlled with substantially reduced merchant-class independence and centralized allocation replacing market-based distribution.

**Political Fragmentation Scenario** — War operations create regional political fragmentation, with Auralis military control expanding in southern regions while Terravelle maintains independence in northern areas. In this scenario, unified economy would fracture into regional economies with reduced trade and separate currency systems.

The economy's contemporary trajectory suggests one of these scenarios will manifest within 2-4 years. The specific outcome depends on military escalation decisions and institutional policy choices currently being made by political authorities. Merchants, practitioners, and institutional operators are simultaneously attempting to plan for continuity while recognizing that fundamental economic transformation appears probable.
