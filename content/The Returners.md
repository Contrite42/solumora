The Returners are a fringe religious tradition — found mainly in communities near [[Ancient Ruins|ruins sites]], in the desert-edge territories of [[Halveth]] and [[Emberfall]], and in the older quarters of certain southern [[Auralis]] settlements. The tradition holds that the civilization that preceded both kingdoms did not vanish but withdrew: that it persists in some form, present in the ruins, accessible through the right approach, and that contact is possible for those with the patience and understanding to attempt it.

## Core Doctrine

The Returners' central claim is interpretive, not prophetic. They are not waiting for a return from outside. They believe the civilization is already here, embedded in the ruins, in the anomalous Flux behavior around certain sites, in the inscriptions that do not conform to any known sigil standard. The vanishing was not death; it was a change of state. The civilization is present in the way a signal is present in noise — not absent, but requiring the right instrument to receive.

What would "contact" mean? Returners disagree on the details. Most practice around the ruins sites is receptive rather than active: visits, quiet observation, careful documentation of what they perceive near sites. A minority holds that specific inscription patterns are intended as interface points — that the civilization left instructions for communication that the current world has not yet read correctly. These practitioners work from partial translations of anomalous inscriptions, which are neither verified nor verifiable.

The [[The Culmination Faction|Culmination Faction]]'s academic arm and certain Returner communities overlap awkwardly: [[Aldric Mourne]]'s ruins interpretation shares enough surface features with Returner doctrine that both sides find the similarity uncomfortable. The Culmination Faction's position is academic and institutional; the Returners' position is religious and community-based. Neither claims the other as kin.

## Rituals

Returner practice centers on ruins-site presence. Visits are quiet, careful, and oriented toward receiving rather than doing. Practitioners bring personal objects — particularly objects associated with [[Flux]] work or a life's craft — and leave them near ruins sites for a period before reclaiming them. The theory is ambiguous: the objects are either acquiring something from the site's Flux presence or demonstrating intent, depending on which Returner community you ask.

The major taboo is noise. Loud activity near ruins sites, particularly during the pre-dawn hours that most Returner communities treat as liminal, is considered a failure of the practice's fundamental orientation. You cannot receive what you are too busy making to hear.

## Political Relationship to Grimoires

Returners maintain their own records — not grimoires in the standard sense, but collections of observations, partial inscription copies, and community documents recording what practitioners have perceived at specific sites. These records circulate within the tradition and are not published through any official channel. They are not the kind of documentation the [[The Advancement Corps]] would recognize as rigorous, and the Corps regards Returner communities near ruins sites as a management problem rather than a research partnership.

The Returners regard the Corps's presence at ruins sites with something between unease and contempt. The Corps's approach is extractive; the Returner's approach is receptive. From a Returner's perspective, the Corps is trying to take something that requires listening, using methods designed to take rather than listen, and failing to notice that this is why they keep getting incomplete results.

## Common Practice

For most people who identify with the Returners in any loose sense, the practice is much lighter than the committed communities near ruins sites. It is an interpretive frame for the ruins — a way of thinking about them that makes them feel less like evidence of catastrophic failure and more like evidence of something else. In communities that live near ruins and have for generations, the Returner tradition is the way people have made peace with what surrounds them.
