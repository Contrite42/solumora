# Ossan Vert

Ossan Vert is a commission researcher at the [[Valdenmoor Institute for Applied Sigil Study]], age 38, [[Control Tier|T3]], who specializes in field fault diagnosis and spell salvage.

## Appearance

Ossan is a man in his late thirties with the practical, slightly disheveled appearance of someone who spends more time working with damaged materials than attending formal research presentations. He has brown hair that tends toward unruly, often pushed back absently while examining corrupted spell documents. He wears the Institute's standard researcher attire but his clothing tends to show the signs of his work — ink stains, chemical residue stains from various preservation treatments, small burns from testing spell failures.

He has remarkably steady hands despite years of working with unstable corrupted spells, and keeps his workspace meticulously organized despite the chaos of his appearance. His eyes show the slight strain of someone who does extensive close work examining damaged sigil inscriptions under varied lighting conditions. He habitually wears protective glasses when working with potentially unstable spell materials, and colleagues can tell how dangerous a particular salvage attempt is by how many additional safety precautions he takes.

He moves through the Institute with the preoccupied air of someone mentally working through complex diagnostic problems. He has developed the habit of unconsciously sketching sigil patterns in the air with his finger while thinking, a trait his colleagues find endearing if occasionally distracting during meetings. His face shows genuine enthusiasm when discussing interesting corruption patterns, displaying the excitement of someone who finds beauty in understanding how magical systems fail.

## Current Project: Corruption Mapping and Salvage Methodology

Ossan's work focuses on understanding what happens when spells are partially corrupted — when sigil composition is incomplete, damaged, or intentionally altered from original specifications — and whether components of corrupted spells can be salvaged or repurposed.

The practical motivation comes from practitioners in the field who encounter damaged grimoires, incomplete spell copies, or spells that have been modified by previous owners or corrupted during transport. Standard procedure is to discard the corrupted spell and source a clean copy. Ossan's research asks whether a T2 practitioner with proper guidance could assess a corrupted spell, identify which components are still valid, and reconstruct enough of the original to make it functional.

## Applications

If successful, this work would:
- Reduce the cost of spell acquisition (corrupted copies could be recovered instead of discarded)
- Increase resilience for practitioners operating in field conditions where resupply is difficult
- Create new economic opportunities for specialized practitioners who can diagnose and salvage corrupted materials

## Challenges

The work is difficult because spell corruption is highly variable. Thermal damage, water exposure, chemical contamination, intentional alteration, and simple transcription errors all produce different failure modes. Understanding the space of possible corruptions and developing diagnostic tools that work across the full range requires iterative testing with actual damaged materials, which limits experiment velocity.

Additionally, the research sometimes produces results that make practitioners uncomfortable: occasionally, corrupted spells can be modified into something functional that is not the original spell. This creates questions about whether the practitioner should attempt salvage or pursue a known-good alternative. Ossan's work does not answer this question — it simply illuminates the technical possibility space.
