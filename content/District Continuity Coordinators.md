# District Continuity Coordinators

**District Continuity Coordinators** are local operators who synchronize queue, shelter, food, and clinic decisions when regular institutions are all functioning but overloaded.

They are usually mid-level administrators, guild clerks, or temple logistics leads with enough local authority to sequence decisions across systems that do not share a command chain.

## Core Function

Their core job is timing alignment.

Most service failures under strain are not total failures. They are sequencing failures: clinic slots open after food windows close, shelter renewals expire before wage confirmation, or ration updates arrive after queue token issuance.

Coordinators reduce these misalignments through daily cross-office scheduling.

## Practical Responsibilities

Typical responsibilities include:

- reconciling queue priority rules across service offices
- assigning escalation lanes for high-friction household cases
- coordinating with [[Queue Management Offices]] on rollover logic
- routing shelter overflow through [[Temporary Housing Pressure]] protocols
- feeding incident summaries into district review meetings

Their decisions are operational rather than judicial; they keep systems moving, not adjudicate fault.

## Authority Profile

Coordinator authority is mostly procedural:

- they can reprioritize sequence windows
- they can request temporary exemptions
- they can broker inter-office swaps of daily capacity

They usually cannot change total district resource levels. That means they manage distribution quality, not scarcity origin.

## Failure Points

Coordinator systems fail when:

- policy updates arrive faster than local communication loops
- office heads ignore shared sequencing rules
- data quality degrades across manual ledgers

Under those conditions, coordinators become reactive firebreaks instead of continuity planners.

## Relationship to Household Stability

For households, coordinators are often the difference between repeated collapse and fragile continuity.

A single sequencing correction can preserve three linked outcomes: shelter renewal, ration eligibility, and follow-up care attendance. Without that correction, the household re-enters multiple queues from zero.

## Current Use Pattern

Districts in both kingdoms report increasing reliance on coordinator functions, especially where service queues are now permanent rather than episodic.

That pattern indicates systems are adapting competently but operating near load limits for extended periods.
