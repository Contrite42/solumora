**Auralis Enforcement Bureau** manages criminal investigation, patrol operations, and enforcement across [[Auralis]]'s settled territories. Operating under formal civic mandate, the Bureau handles everything from routine theft to complex [[Flux]] crimes requiring specialized investigation. It is understaffed, under-resourced, and constantly triaging which crimes receive investigation priority.

## Structure

The Bureau divides operations into three functional branches:

**Patrol Division** provides visible security presence in urban areas, responds to immediate incidents, and handles routine law enforcement. Patrol officers are typically [[Control Tier|T1-T2]] practitioners with basic detection and containment training. They manage theft, assault, public disturbances, and low-tier Flux violations. Most patrol work involves deterrence and initial response rather than complex investigation.

**Investigation Division** handles cases requiring evidence gathering, witness interviews, and sustained inquiry. Investigators are typically T2-T3 practitioners with specialized training in forensic technique, interview methods, and evidence documentation. They work cases brought by Patrol Division or directly reported by victims. Investigators gather evidence for [[Auralis Civic Courts]] prosecution but do not determine guilt — courts make final rulings.

**Specialist Units** address high-tier Flux crimes, organized threats, and cases requiring capabilities beyond standard investigators. Specialist investigators are T3-T4 practitioners with advanced Flux detection training and authority to contract external high-tier practitioners when cases exceed Bureau capabilities. These units are small (20-30 personnel for all of Auralis) and handle only the highest-priority cases.

## Personnel and Training

Bureau personnel undergo 6-month training covering:
- Legal authority boundaries and arrest procedures
- Evidence collection and documentation standards
- Flux detection basics (T1-T2 level sigil recognition, residue identification)
- Witness interview techniques
- Use of force policies and containment protocols
- Court testimony procedures

Training prioritizes practical competence over theoretical depth. Bureau personnel are taught what they need to do the job, not comprehensive Flux theory. A patrol officer learns to recognize when a sigil has been cast recently; they do not need to understand the full variable structure of that sigil.

Advancement requires field experience and demonstrated competence. A patrol officer who handles incidents effectively and documents evidence properly can advance to Investigation Division. An investigator who successfully closes complex cases can advance to Specialist Units. Advancement correlates with [[Control Tier]] capacity but is not solely determined by it — a T2 investigator with excellent interview skills outperforms a T4 investigator with no interpersonal competence.

## Resource Constraints

The Bureau operates under permanent capacity constraints. [[Auralis]] has perhaps 800-1,000 Bureau personnel for a kingdom of ~100 million people. Most reported crimes never receive investigation. The Bureau triages based on:

- **Severity:** Murder, major theft, violent assault receive priority over minor property crimes
- **Evidence viability:** Cases with physical evidence, witnesses, or clear leads receive priority over cases with no investigative starting point
- **Political pressure:** High-profile cases or crimes against influential citizens receive priority regardless of objective severity
- **Resource availability:** Cases requiring specialist intervention often wait months for specialists to become available

A merchant robbed of 2,000 [[Watts]] of goods will file a report, receive a case number, and likely never hear from an investigator. Patrol officers document the incident, but Investigation Division cannot allocate resources to routine theft when murders and high-tier Flux crimes demand attention.

This triage is not secret. Citizens understand that Bureau capacity is limited and that most crimes go unprosecuted. Some accept this as inevitable resource allocation. Others view it as institutional failure justifying private enforcement or community justice outside formal systems.

## Flux Crime Investigation

Investigating Flux-enabled crimes requires capabilities most Bureau personnel lack. A T2 investigator cannot detect whether a T4 practitioner destroyed evidence chemically, manipulated witness memory, or used concealed sigils to commit crimes remotely.

**Detection Limitations:** Bureau investigators can detect low-tier Flux manipulation (recent sigil use, obvious residue, crude evidence destruction). They cannot reliably detect high-tier manipulation that leaves minimal traces or uses sophisticated concealment. This creates systematic bias: the Bureau successfully investigates incompetent criminals and fails to investigate competent ones.

**Specialist Escalation:** When investigators encounter evidence of high-tier Flux involvement, they escalate to Specialist Units. Specialists have authority to contract external [[Flux Users|practitioners]] — often T5+ [[Flux Users#Channelers|Channelers]] or experienced [[Flux Users#Scholars|Scholars]] — to conduct detection assessments. These contracted practitioners are expensive (5,000-20,000 Watts per case), limited in availability, and sometimes unreliable (contracted practitioners may have conflicts of interest or refuse cases for professional reasons).

**Evidence Standards:** Even when Specialists detect Flux manipulation, proving it in [[Auralis Civic Courts]] requires documentation that courts will accept as credible. Expert testimony from contracted practitioners can be challenged by defense attorneys. Physical evidence that was destroyed cannot be produced. Courts require evidence *of the crime*, not merely evidence *that evidence was destroyed*. This standard, while protecting defendants from unfounded accusations, also protects competent criminals who leave no prosecutable trail.

The practical result: Bureau successfully prosecutes low-tier Flux crimes (T1-T2 offenders who leave obvious evidence) and fails to prosecute most high-tier Flux crimes (T4+ offenders who destroy evidence competently).

## Enforcement Tactics

Bureau personnel use standardized tactics for different incident types:

**Routine Patrol:** Officers walk or ride assigned routes, maintain visible presence, respond to reported incidents within their patrol zone. Most patrol work involves deterrence — criminals who see patrol officers nearby choose not to act.

**Incident Response:** When crimes occur, patrol responds first, secures the scene, provides immediate aid to victims, and documents basic facts. Investigators follow up if the case meets triage thresholds.

**Arrest Operations:** When evidence justifies arrest, Bureau personnel issue warrants and conduct arrests using containment sigils, physical restraint, and backup officers as needed. High-tier arrest operations require Specialist involvement and pre-planned tactics because high-tier suspects can resist or flee using capabilities patrol officers cannot counter.

**Evidence Gathering:** Investigators photograph scenes, collect physical evidence, interview witnesses, and document everything for court presentation. Evidence chain-of-custody is strictly maintained because defense attorneys challenge any procedural irregularity.

## Corruption and Misconduct

Bureau personnel operate under institutional oversight, but corruption exists. Common forms:

**Bribery:** Wealthy defendants or their representatives offer payment to officers or investigators to lose evidence, delay arrests, or provide favorable testimony. Most Bureau personnel refuse, but some accept when payments exceed yearly salaries.

**Political Interference:** [[The Council of Auralis]] occasionally directs Bureau to prioritize or deprioritize specific cases for political reasons. Bureau leadership formally resists but practically complies because Council controls funding and appointments.

**Excessive Force:** Some officers use more force than necessary during arrests, particularly against low-tier or non-Flux citizens who cannot resist effectively. Oversight exists but inconsistently enforces use-of-force policies.

**Evidence Fabrication:** Rare but documented cases where investigators fabricate evidence to secure convictions in cases where actual evidence is insufficient. This typically occurs under pressure to close high-profile cases.

Bureau misconduct is investigated by Internal Standards Division, a small unit within the Bureau tasked with investigating personnel complaints. Internal investigation creates obvious conflict of interest, and critics argue Bureau personnel investigating other Bureau personnel produces inadequate accountability.

## Relationship with Courts

Bureau operates as investigative and enforcement arm for [[Auralis Civic Courts]]. Bureau gathers evidence; courts determine guilt. This division of responsibility creates both clarity and tension.

**Clarity:** Bureau personnel do not decide who is guilty. They gather evidence and present it to courts. Courts make final determinations based on evidence quality and legal standards. This separation prevents enforcement personnel from acting as judge and executioner.

**Tension:** Bureau personnel sometimes believe they have gathered sufficient evidence to prove guilt, only to see courts acquit because evidence does not meet legal standards. Investigators frustrated by court acquittals may become cynical about procedural formality, viewing it as obstacle rather than safeguard.

Some Bureau personnel advocate for streamlined prosecution procedures allowing faster convictions without extensive court review. Most judges resist, arguing procedural protections prevent wrongful convictions and that Bureau frustration does not justify reducing defendant rights.

## Public Perception

Common citizens view Bureau as necessary but distant. In urban areas, visible patrol presence provides reassurance. In rural areas, Bureau personnel are rarely seen, and citizens rely more on community justice than formal enforcement.

Wealthy citizens view Bureau as servants — they expect responsive investigation when they report crimes and become frustrated when Bureau prioritizes other cases. Poor citizens view Bureau with suspicion, having experienced or witnessed heavy-handed enforcement, delayed responses, or outright dismissal of reported crimes.

High-tier practitioners view Bureau as irrelevant to their professional lives unless they commit crimes so egregious that Specialist Units become involved. Most high-tier practitioners never interact with Bureau personnel.

## Key Personnel

**[[Renna Drost]]** leads Specialist Units focusing on high-tier Flux crimes, with authority to contract external practitioners and coordinate cross-division operations for complex cases.

**Korst Vennal** directs Investigation Division in [[Solhaven]], managing 40 investigators handling the capital's criminal caseload.

**Tess Aldren** commands Patrol Division in [[Valdenmoor]], supervising 80 patrol officers across the city's districts.

## Limitations

The Bureau cannot:
- Investigate all reported crimes (volume exceeds capacity)
- Detect all high-tier Flux manipulation (tier ceilings among personnel create gaps)
- Operate outside Auralis jurisdiction (no authority in [[Terravelle]] or [[Equatorial Desert|Desert Zakros]])
- Compel testimony from uncooperative witnesses unless courts issue formal orders
- Prevent crimes before they occur (reactive enforcement model, not predictive)
- Function independently of political influence (Council controls funding and appointments)

Bureau personnel work within these constraints, prioritizing cases they can plausibly solve, accepting that most crimes go unprosecuted, and managing public expectations that formal enforcement will address citizen security concerns. The system functions but does not provide comprehensive justice. It provides institutional enforcement within resource limits and capability boundaries.
