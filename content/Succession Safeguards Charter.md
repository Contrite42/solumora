# Succession Safeguards Charter

The Succession Safeguards Charter (SSC) is a cross-institutional continuity framework adopted one year after the first casualty to prevent rollback of post-revelation protections during leadership turnover.

Its core premise is procedural: coexistence safeguards fail most often not during peak crisis but during administrative succession when institutional memory fragments and political incentives re-open old pathways.

## Adoption Context

After six months of relative corridor stability, three risk indicators rose simultaneously:
- retirement and reassignment of first-wave crisis administrators,
- electoral pressure campaigns framing safeguards as temporary overreach,
- declining public recall of pre-stabilization casualty risk.

The SSC was negotiated to keep anti-persecution and technical-governance commitments from becoming personality-dependent.

## Charter Objectives

1. Preserve hard constraints on persecution-pattern mechanisms across leadership cycles.
2. Protect technical institutions from partisan capture during appointments.
3. Require formal memory-transfer procedures when key offices rotate.
4. Keep public transparency standards stable under administrative change.

## Core Provisions

### Article A - Continuity Trigger Protocol
- Mandatory 30-day overlap for outgoing and incoming officials in designated high-risk offices.
- Structured handoff dossiers: active risk maps, pending compliance cases, unresolved exception logs.
- Public certification that handoff package was received and reviewed.

### Article B - Non-Derogation Floor
- New administrations may tighten safeguards but cannot lower baseline protections without supermajority + public hearing cycle.
- Prohibits unilateral suspension of anti-rumor detention standards.
- Requires explicit historical-impact statement for any proposed rollback.

### Article C - Technical Independence Guardrails
- Fixed-term protections for key JRMC and APCT technical seats.
- Appointment review includes competence and conflict-of-interest screen.
- Emergency replacement authority limited to 21 days without ratification.

### Article D - Memory Preservation Requirements
- Annual publication of "near-collapse casebook" excerpts from first-year operations.
- Mandatory briefing for incoming officials on [[The Lantern Decrees]] and related recurrence patterns.
- Schools and civic academies receive standardized continuity modules.

### Article E - Public Alert Integrity
- Risk bulletin language standards cannot be altered by political office without technical concurrence.
- Any deviation from established alert lexicon must be docketed and justified publicly.

## Enforcement Architecture

The Charter is enforced through linked institutions:
- [[Anti-Persecution Compliance Tribunal]] for civil-safeguard violations,
- [[Ashford Coexistence Oversight Commission]] for procedural breaches,
- [[Joint Rift Monitoring Cell]] for technical alert integrity and risk-language disputes.

Repeated non-compliance can trigger treaty-level conference under the [[Corridor Stabilization Treaty]].

## Strategic Significance

The SSC shifts coexistence governance from crisis charisma to institutional memory discipline. It acknowledges that stability is not self-sustaining and must be actively re-internalized by each leadership generation.

Its success condition is quiet: fewer preventable reversions during ordinary transitions.
