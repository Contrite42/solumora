# Campaign Coexistence Doctrine

The **Campaign Coexistence Doctrine** establishes operational guidelines for political campaigns navigating [[Electoral Coexistence Safeguards Framework|coexistence governance]] during electoral competition. Developed by [[Terravelle]] and [[Auralis]] electoral administrators in consultation with campaign professionals, the doctrine functions as shared practical convention rather than binding law — candidatestechnically can ignore it, but campaigns that do face tactical disadvantages and public censure.

## Doctrinal Foundation

The doctrine emerged from Year 4 post-revelation electoral cycle preparation, when campaign managers from both kingdoms recognized they needed clearer guidance on what constituted permissible coexistence-related messaging. The [[Electoral Coexistence Safeguards Framework]] established legal boundaries; the Campaign Doctrine provides tactical roadmap for operating within those boundaries while maintaining competitive campaigns.

Primary principle: *Coexistence governance is policy infrastructure, not wedge issue arena.* Campaigns compete on operational competence and resource allocation rather than paradigm legitimacy.

## Permissible Campaign Positions

### Implementation Critique

Candidates may campaign on improving coexistence framework operations without challenging frameworks' right to exist:

- **Enforcement Efficiency:** "Current oversight procedures create unnecessary delays; I will streamline review timelines while maintaining protection standards."
- **Resource Allocation:** "Protected Observation zones receive disproportionate funding relative to Full Integration districts; I will rebalance allocations to reflect population distribution."
- **Regional Adaptation:** "One-size enforcement doesn't work. Border settlements need different procedures than core cities; I will expand regional variance authority."

These positions critique *how* coexistence operates without questioning *whether* it should operate. Doctrine guidelines emphasize concrete operational proposals rather than abstract opposition.

### Cross-Kingdom Coordination

Candidates may criticize coordination failures or treaty implementation gaps:

- **Response Time Critique:** "[[Joint Rift Monitoring Cell]] took forty-eight hours to coordinate emergency response. I will establish faster communication protocols."
- **Treaty Compliance:** "Auralis routinely delays information sharing required under [[Corridor Stabilization Treaty]]. I will demand stricter compliance enforcement or renegotiate terms."
- **Jurisdiction Clarity:** "Current frameworks create overlapping authority that slows crisis response. I will negotiate clearer jurisdictional boundaries."

These positions target coordination mechanics rather than coexistence principle. Doctrine explicitly permits criticism of treaty partner performance while maintaining commitment to treaty framework.

### Accountability Standards

Candidates may demand higher oversight transparency or faster failure correction:

- **Commission Performance:** "[[Ashford Coexistence Oversight Commission]] missed error-correction timelines in three documented cases. I will require quarterly public reporting."
- **Enforcement Review:** "Current appeal procedures take too long to resolve wrongful detention claims. I will mandate forty-eight-hour preliminary review standards."
- **Historical Documentation:** "We lack systematic tracking of framework implementation costs. I will require annual fiscal reports with line-item breakdowns."

These positions demand better institutional performance without questioning institutional legitimacy. Doctrine encourages competence-based competition rather than existence-based combat.

## Prohibited Campaign Positions

The doctrine clarifies what constitutes safeguard violations that trigger electoral review:

### Paradigm Rollback Advocacy

- **Direct Repeal:** "I will dismantle coexistence frameworks and return to traditional enforcement."
- **Status Removal:** "High Demons should not have legal protections. I will remove their protected status."
- **Prosecution Revival:** "We should resume systematic hunts of immortal beings as institutional policy."

These positions advocate systematic violence and violate [[Anti-Persecution Compliance Tribunal]] precedent. Campaigns using this language face electoral disqualification procedures.

### Evidence-Free Threat Claims

- **Conspiracy Theories:** "High Demons secretly control our government and manipulate policy."
- **Fabricated Incidents:** "Protected populations commit crimes at higher rates" without documented evidence.
- **Panic Mobilization:** "Coexistence threatens our children's safety" without specific operational failures to cite.

These positions fail evidence standards and constitute misinformation grounds for censure. Doctrine requires claims about coexistence failures to cite documented incidents verifiable through oversight commission records.

### 'Treaty Violation Threats

- **Unilateral Withdrawal:** "I will withdraw from [[Corridor Stabilization Treaty]] regardless of consequences."
- **Cross-Border Hostility:** "I will restrict all cross-kingdom coordination and resume strategic competition."
- **Bad Faith Breach:** "I will honor treaty obligations only when politically convenient."

These positions threaten international framework stability and constitute grounds for pre-election review intervention. Doctrine permits treaty renegotiation advocacy but prohibits deliberate breach threats.

## Tactical Guidance for Campaign Operations

### Messaging Framework

Campaign professionals receive specific guidance on framing coexistence-related positions:

**Instead of:** "Coexistence has failed — we need new approach."  
**Use:** "Current coexistence operations have documented inefficiencies. I will improve implementation while maintaining protection commitments."

**Instead of:** "High Demons are dangerous — we can't trust them."  
**Use:** "Oversight procedures need better transparency. I will require more frequent public reporting from monitoring commissions."

**Instead of:** "My opponent supports coexistence; I oppose it."  
**Use:** "My opponent's approach creates excessive bureaucracy. Mine prioritizes operational efficiency with better outcomes."

The tactical shift: frame opposition as *administration incompetence* rather than *paradigm wrongness*.

### Opposition Research Standards

Campaigns may use opponents' coexistence positions as electoral material only within doctrine boundaries:

- **Permissible:** "My opponent voted to reduce oversight funding, making frameworks less effective."
- **Prohibited:** "My opponent supports dangerous coexistence policies that threaten our families."

The distinction: critique voting records and administrative choices, not paradigm alignment. Candidates who violate this standard provide their opponents with ready-made safeguard violation documentation.

### Debate Preparation

Doctrine recommends debate strategies for handling coexistence questions:

1. **Acknowledge framework legitimacy** ("Coexistence governance is necessary and I'm committed to maintaining it")
2. **Identify specific operational failures** ("But current implementation has documented problems")
3. **Propose concrete improvements** ("Here's my plan to fix those problems while maintaining protections")

This sequence maintains safeguard compliance while allowing competitive differentiation. Candidates who skip step 1 risk safeguard review; candidates who skip steps 2-3 appear to lack policy substance.

## Electoral Administrator Enforcement

Electoral administrators use the doctrine as review guideline when assessing whether campaign materials violate [[Electoral Coexistence Safeguards Framework]] provisions. Materials clearly within doctrine boundaries rarely face review challenges; materials in grey areas receive advance guidance; materials clearly outside boundaries trigger formal violation notices.

The doctrine's practical value: campaigns that follow it avoid enforcement uncertainty and opposition-research vulnerabilities. Most campaigns comply not because they philosophically agree but because non-compliance creates tactical disadvantage.

## Documented Use Patterns

### Year 4-5 Electoral Cycles

Campaign analysis from initial framework testing shows doctrine adoption patterns:

- **Established Politicians:** High compliance rates (85%+). Experienced candidates understood tactical value of staying within boundaries.
- **Challenger Candidates:** Lower initial compliance (60-70%) with rapid learning curves. Challengers tested boundaries early, faced review consequences, and quickly adapted messaging.
- **Fringe Candidates:** Deliberate non-compliance as brand positioning (~10% of candidate pool). These candidates used safeguard violations as proof of "refusing to be silenced" — most failed to gain traction beyond base supporters.

Electoral administrators noted that doctrine's real enforcement came from *opposition campaigns* rather than review bodies. Opponents weaponized safeguard violations more effectively than administrators could, creating peer-pressure compliance dynamics.

### Regional Variation

Border settlement campaigns showed higher rates of implementation criticism and resource allocation challenges — constituents experienced direct coexistence operational costs and demanded responsive messaging. Core city campaigns focused more on cross-kingdom coordination and treaty compliance — constituents prioritized stability and predictability over operational flexibility.

The doctrine accommodated both patterns without triggering safeguard concerns because both remained within permissible critique boundaries.

## Integration with Historical Precedent

The doctrine's emphasis on evidence-based claims and concrete operational critique reflects [[The Three Near-Wars#Historical Foundations: The Persecution Era (~400-200 Years Ago)|persecution-era]] lessons about institutional mobilization. Persecution succeeded when governments coordinated organized violence through official channels; it failed when magistrates demanded evidence before authorizing hunts.

Campaign doctrine requires candidates to *act like magistrates reviewing enforcement cases* rather than activists mobilizing panic. This procedural constraint slows political weaponization of coexistence governance even when popular sentiment remains skeptical.

## Practical Limitations

The doctrine cannot prevent:

- **Coded Messaging:** Sophisticated campaigns use language that technically complies with doctrine while signaling anti-coexistence positions to receptive audiences.
- **Non-Campaign Actors:** Advocacy groups, independent organizations, and private citizens can make prohibited claims without facing electoral sanctions.
- **Long-Term Strategy:** Candidates who comply during campaigns can, once elected, work within office to gradually weaken frameworks through legitimate policy changes.

Oversight commissioners note that doctrine's value lies in *delaying and diffusing* political challenges to coexistence rather than permanently preventing them. Frameworks survive because doctrine buys time for institutional durability to strengthen and because most elected officials discover operational dependencies that make rollback impractical.
