# Fifty-Eight Days After the First Casualty - Corridor Model

Merra stops calling it a warning and starts calling it a timetable.

By day fifty-eight, she, Jess, and Kess have cross-checked the corridor forecast against every source they can reach without formal authorization: caravan delay logs, warehouse throughput records, military requisition volumes, ruins-site micro-distortion reports, and three years of weather variance data for the central desert lanes. The models disagree on details and agree on one thing.

If both kingdoms execute current deployment schedules, the corridor hardens into cascade conditions inside five weeks.

Jess writes the conclusion in block letters at the top of a ledger page:

**FAILURE WINDOW: 33-41 DAYS**

Then she underlines it twice.

Kess lays out inscription fragments from Site Seventeen beside Jess's numbers. Her translations map threshold behavior in ancient stabilization arrays: flux load stays manageable until overlapping high-tier discharge events exceed structural bleed capacity. After that, failure is not linear. It is abrupt.

"It does not ramp," Kess says. "It snaps."

Merra traces the overlapping route lines with her thumb. "Can we tell which deployment triggers first snap?"

Jess flips to the military movement assumptions they have reconstructed from procurement and transit patterns. "Terravelle heavy ward units to the northern band on a ten-day cadence. Auralis dimension specialists to staging flats on staggered twelve-day rotation. First dangerous overlap is day sixty-seven if nothing slips. Day sixty-four if command accelerates."

"Command will accelerate," Merra says.

No one argues.

They build three presentation versions in one night.

Version one: technical. Full tables, assumptions, confidence ranges, caveats.

Version two: operational. "If you do X on this date, you get breach cascade by this date."

Version three: political. "If this happens, your war stops being a war and becomes a regional extinction event."

Jess hates version three. Kess says version three is the only one people read when they are scared.

By dawn, they have a package solid enough to survive hostile review and clear enough to survive executive boredom. Merra seals the packet for Border Coordination and another for Terravelle military planning.

There is no packet for Auralis.

Kess sees Merra looking at the empty third envelope and says, "Auralis won't take this from me. They won't take it from you. They might take it from Cassia if she can reach the right room."

Merra nods without committing. She has not yet asked Cassia for anything. She has also run out of time to avoid that conversation.

Before they leave, Jess adds one final page titled **Actions That Reduce Cascade Probability**.

1. Delay high-tier deployments through the corridor by twenty days.
2. Shift supply corridors west to lower-load bands.
3. Suspend all nonessential high-flux testing near ruins clusters.
4. Stand up joint rift monitoring with shared real-time reporting.

"It won't save everything," Jess says. "It buys days. Days are all we've got left to buy."

Merra takes the packets and steps into morning rain over Ashford stone.

For the first time since Kellvost, the problem has shape.

For the first time, shape means they can be ignored on purpose.
