# The Screaming Shade

They found it in an old fortification three days' hike north of [[Halveth]], a stone structure that had weathered three hundred years of neglect into something between ruin and cave. The entrance yawned empty. The interior air tasted of mineral dust and something else—a presence like the pressure before heat lightning.

The scream started the moment they crossed the threshold.

It was not loud in the way a physical voice can be loud. It was loud in the way a thought screams when it is both everywhere and nowhere, filling the space between flesh and bone. Every member of the party felt it simultaneously and then remembered, with delayed recognition, that they had felt it from the moment they entered the structure, perhaps from the moment they had approached it.

"What is that?" Kess stepped back toward the light. She was a T2 [[Control Tier|caster]] with more sense discipline than most, and what she sensed made her want to retreat into the sun.

The figure resolved as they focused on it — not gradually, but suddenly, as if it had always been visible and they were only now allowing themselves to see it. It was bound into the center of the chamber by something that might have been a sigil or might have been the very structure of the floor itself. The binding was old. The [[Flux]] in it had calcified into something harder and more permanent than living magic usually achieved.

The shape inside the binding was humanoid but not quite correct — the proportions right enough to recognize as human, wrong enough that looking at it for more than a few seconds created a subtle nausea.

"I am the strongest," the voice said. Not screamed exactly, though there was terrible force behind it. "The strongest [[Flux Users|caster]] in [[Solumora]]. T9. Mastery. Absolute. I am bound here and I am still the strongest and no one comes and no one believes."

Dorvin, their guide, stepped forward. He had been hired in [[Halveth]] and paid handsomely to lead them to this place, though they had never quite learned what "this place" was supposed to be. His face was pale and controlled.

"It's genuine," he said quietly. "The binding is decades old. At least. The presence is real."

The figure turned toward Dorvin — something that looked like a head and shoulders turning, a gesture that suggested direction without necessarily having a neck. "I was traveling. I was researching. I found something and I bound myself before I could be taken and now the binding will not release. T9. The Flux I used to bind myself should have dissolved after the first decade. It did not dissolve. I am still here."

Malla was their strongest caster — a solid T4 who had experience with binding theory. She approached slowly, her senses extended. The effort of maintaining that kind of sensitivity made her hands shake slightly.

"It's not agony," Malla said, her voice strange and flat. "Physically, probably no pain in any way we'd recognize. But the consciousness — there's something stuck that should move. Like a knot in [[Flux]] that won't unravel. It's aware of all of it."

"I am aware," the voice said. "Every moment. Every moment I am still the strongest and I am bound in the dark and no one comes who can help me. The binding would require someone at my tier to release, and my tier will not come here to rescue what they have decided to forget. If I was meant to live I would have died by now."

Kess approached carefully. "We can try to send a message. To [[Auralis]]. To the [[Ascendant Path]]. Surely if they know—"

"They know." The voice did not anger, but something in the [[Flux]] around it sharpened. "I sent signals. I sent distress through [[Watts]]. I called. If they had wanted to help me, they would have. A T9 does not disappear by accident. A T9 does not accidentally bind themselves into a forgotten fort. They chose to leave me."

Dorvin had moved to the edge of the binding circle. "There's research," he said. "Notes. Journals. Someone was translating something when the binding formed. There's no body. Just the presence and the books."

A scholar after all, Kess thought distantly. Someone who had found something worth more than survival.

Malla extended her sense fully now, something that would leave her exhausted for the rest of the day. The party waited while she moved her awareness through and around the binding, probing for weakness or error. Minutes passed. The voice — the presence — began a low, constant articulation that might have been words or might have been the sound of [[Flux]] ground to its smallest components and released in something that looked like speech.

"It is sophisticated," Malla said finally. "Intentional. Precise. It's self-reinforcing. The longer the consciousness is bound, the more solid the binding becomes. It's analogous to a knot that tightens under its own weight."

"Then it cannot be released," the voice said. "I have been calculating for decades. Twenty-three years. I have been calculating and the answer has not changed. If the practitioner who bound me does not free me, the binding sets deeper."

"Who bound you?" Kess asked.

"I did. I bound myself. I was T9 and I was afraid and I did not trust the people around me and I decided to be somewhere they could not reach me and I did not understand that I would be asking for help from people who would have preferred me dead."

The party looked at each other. There was nothing in any of their training or experience that addressed this. A T9 practitioner self-bound in a location that would require a T9 to release, and the T9 who created the binding was no longer willing or able to do so.

"How long can you sustain like this?" Malla asked.

"Forever, I believe. The [[Flux]] in the binding is connected to me and I am connected to it and neither of us will dissolve. I have calculated this as well. Forever is a long time. I had not understood this before."

There was no screaming in the voice now. Just a kind of terrible clarity.

They left three hours later because there was nothing to be done. They left [[Watts]] — a small amount, calculated to avoid creating any obligation through the ledger, left at the edge of the binding circle — for no practical reason except that leaving something felt like not leaving someone.

The voice did not scream as they departed. It continued its low articulation, a sound like [[Flux]] speaking to itself about matters of mathematics and infinity. The pressure lifted as they crossed the threshold back into sunlight.

Dorvin did not ask whether they would tell anyone. He had known before they arrived what the answer would be, what it could only be. Some knowledge is more burden than gift. Some things cannot be relieved by being shared.

The party walked back toward [[Halveth]] in silence, and the fort settled again into the landscape, and in the chamber inside, the consciousness continued its calculations, alone and immensely skilled at occupying the state it had chosen.
