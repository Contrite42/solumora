# The Salvage Team That Succeeded Too Well

The collapsed caravan created a problem for the regional trade infrastructure: forty tons of goods stranded in the eastern approach to Zakros, with the recovery team's Flux budget nearly exhausted and the primary rescue coordinator severely injured. The situation was salvageable if — and only if — someone could extract a specific subset of the cargo within the window before either the goods degraded or the merchant who owned them ran out of resources to fund the recovery.

Specialist recovery teams exist for exactly these situations. Salven Kort's team had extracted eight stranded caravans from hostile terrain. They specialized in speed-oriented assessment and selective retrieval. They operated on margin, which meant successful recoveries covered their overhead and unsuccessful ones cost them sharply.

The goods manifest in the collapsed caravan included three containers of [[Flux]] medicinal extracts, eighteen bars of trade-standard gold, and documentation of intellectual property for a specific [[Alchemy]] compression technique. The merchant had insured the documentation but not the extracts. The gold was significant but replaceable. The extracts, however, were produced in a specialized facility in the north and were worth three times the merchant's current willingness to pay for the recovery. If Salven could extract them, the recovery would be wildly profitable.

Salven assessed the site: the goods were 4.2 kilometers into Zakros. The rift incursion pattern was stable at time of survey. The route had been traversed seventeen times in the past three cycles without major incidents. The caravan had collapsed due to equipment failure and driver error, not environmental hostility. On paper, the recovery was high-confidence.

The team moved in at dawn. Four practitioners, two support crew, one guide, two logistics handlers. Standard recovery team composition. They had a time window of approximately seventeen hours before the encroaching rift zone would make the recovery zone inaccessible without substantially escalating Flux expenditure.

The approach took eight hours rather than projected six. Equipment repositioning in sandy terrain consumed more effort than the pre-assessment had accounted for. At midpoint, Salven made a choice: push forward on the current timeline and reduce buffer margins, or retreat and reschedule for a secondary attempt. The guides recommended retreat.

Salven noted: if they retreated, three months would pass before the merchant could fund a second attempt. The goods would degrade. The insurance wouldn't cover unrecovered cargo. The merchant would likely go bankrupt. The extracts — valuable and needed in southern cities that had limited pharmaceutical supply — would be lost. But the team would have maximum safety margins.

If they continued: substantially reduced buffer, increased risk of minor Flux miscalculations becoming critical, but substantial probability of complete success.

Salven continued. The team reached the caravan site with nine hours until the window closed. They began controlled extraction of the three containers. The methodology was sound. The practitioners' casting was precise. Fifty-four minutes into the extraction, the lead practitioner, who had been managing the environmental stabilization of the corrosion barrier on Container Three, made a judgment call: the barrier was degrading faster than the timeline should allow.

She briefly paused the extraction to reinforce the barrier. Standard procedure. Correct response.

The rift, which had been stable fifteen minutes before, began its expansion cycle three minutes later.

The expansion was moderate. The team was outside the immediate danger zone. But the expanding rift created a secondary effect: a narrow band of accelerated temperature flux that ran vertically through the air, intersecting the team's current position. Not a rift incursion. Not catastrophic. But a phenomenon that none of the team members had encountered in a recovery scenario.

The team deployed defensive casting. They got three containers loaded and began moving toward the exit route. The temperature band dissipated after forty-three seconds. The rift stabilized again after ninety seconds. The team exited with maximum speed and minimal additional harm.

Two handlers suffered minor burns. One practitioner's Flux channels showed temporary strain signatures consistent with heat exposure. The three medicinal extract containers remained intact and successfully transported back to Solumora.

The recovery was a technical success. The merchant's goods were saved. The financial margins were substantial. Salven's team had solved a complex problem.

The problem was that the temperature band had intersected their exit route — and yet no instrument reading or environmental assessment had predicted it would. Which meant the rift activity patterns were more complex than the twenty-year base data that regional teams relied on. Which meant the seventeen successful recoveries that had preceded this one had succeeded despite operating in a zone with inadequately mapped environmental behavior.

Salven published findings. The regional recovery guild incorporated the new information into their pre-assessment protocols. But the recovery had revealed something systemic: the margin of safety that recovery teams operated at included unmapped risk factors that had not yet become critical for experienced teams.

The next unscheduled team that left a recovery attempt might not have the margin that Salven's decision-making had generated.
