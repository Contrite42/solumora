# The Salvage Team That Succeeded Too Well

When the caravan broke, it did not break cleanly.

It sheared a wheel, rolled half-sideways in hot sand, and spilled forty tons of trade into the eastern Zakros approach like a merchant's nightmare made physical: burst casks, split crates, precious cargo half-buried and cooking in reflected heat.

By the time Salven Kort arrived, the first rescue team had already spent most of their [[Flux]] just keeping people alive.

"What can we still save?" the owner asked, voice dry enough to crack.

Salven knelt by the manifest board and did the math.

Gold bars: replaceable.
Alchemy patents: insured.
Medicinal extracts: irreplaceable at this scale.

"These three containers," he said, tapping the list. "Anything else is luxury." 

---

Salven's crew had a reputation for ugly wins.

Four practitioners. Two support handlers. One guide. Two logistics lifters with more scars than patience. They specialized in selective salvage under bad timing, which meant they lived and died by margin discipline.

Site assessment looked almost comforting:

- 4.2 kilometers in,
- historically stable corridor,
- no active hostile incursion at survey start,
- seventeen prior traversals with no severe anomaly reports.

On paper, high confidence.

In real terrain, the approach ran slow. Sand took equipment deeper than expected. A cooling rig jammed once. They lost two hours before reaching the wreck.

At midpoint, guide recommended withdrawal.

"Too thin on exit buffer," she said.

Salven looked at the owner, who had gone pale enough to match the salt crust on his sleeves.

If they withdrew, second attempt would be months away, maybe never funded. The extracts would cook, degrade, and vanish from any meaningful value.

If they pushed, they could still make it.

Could.

"We continue," Salven said.

No one smiled.

---

At wreck site, the work was beautiful in the practical way.

Cut straps.
Stabilize crate angles.
Deploy corrosion barriers.
Lift, brace, move.

The team flowed like a machine built from hard lessons.

Fifty-four minutes into extraction, Practitioner Vel reached Container Three and hissed through her teeth.

"Barrier's decaying fast. Faster than profile."

"Patch it," Salven said.

She did. Correct call. Correct method.

Three minutes later, reality changed.

The nearby rift edge, previously stable, flexed into an expansion pulse. Not a full breach. Worse, in a way: a narrow vertical ribbon of accelerated thermal flux sliced through their planned exit path like invisible fire.

"Down!" Salven shouted.

Defensive casts snapped up in overlapping arcs. One handler screamed as heat licked through his sleeve. Another dropped to one knee, eyes watering from flash-scorch.

Forty-three seconds of chaos.

Then the ribbon collapsed, leaving scorched sand and the metallic smell of heated dust.

"Move now," Vel said, voice hoarse.

They moved.

No speeches. No second guessing. Just extraction discipline at maximum speed while the window still existed.

Ninety seconds after the pulse, local behavior normalized enough for full retreat.

They reached safe range with all three extract containers intact.

Two minor burn cases. One practitioner channel strain. Everyone breathing.

By guild language: successful recovery.

---

The payout was excellent. The merchant stayed solvent. Southern hospitals got their medicine.

Everyone called it a win.

Salven filed his after-action report with one uncomfortable emphasis: the thermal ribbon was not predicted by any existing pre-assessment model used for that corridor.

Which meant their previous confidence wasn't wrong exactly. It was incomplete.

A month later, at a recovery guild briefing, Salven stood before thirty route leads and said:

"We did not survive because the system is safe. We survived because our team had enough reserve competence to absorb a model failure in real time. Do not confuse those two facts."

The room went very quiet.

Protocols changed after that. New anomaly categories. Wider mandatory exit buffers. Stronger language around unknown interactions during rift expansion pulses.

Years later, people still cited the mission as a model salvage operation.

Salven disliked that phrasing.

"It was a near-miss wearing a success report," he told trainees. "Learn from the report, not the payout."
