# Nineteen Survived

The [[Halveth Guide Bureau|Bureau]] coordinator read the report twice before calling for Seln Vorra.

**Expedition #447, departed Firstgreen 14, returned Plantmoon 3. Planned: 22 personnel. Returned: 19 personnel. Casualties: 3. Cause: [[Rift Incursions|rift]] encounter, fauna complications, dehydration cascade.**

The numbers were ugly but not unprecedented. Three deaths on a 22-person expedition was a 14% casualty rate. The [[Equatorial Desert|Zakros]] crossing average for Plantmoon — the secondary window, elevated risk but not failure season — ran 12-15%. Expedition #447 had landed exactly in expected range.

That's what made it worth examining. Expected casualties weren't acceptable casualties. They were prediction-accurate casualties, which meant the system was working as documented but not working well enough to improve.

Seln Vorra arrived thirty minutes later. She was 51, [[Control Tier|T3]], and had guided seventeen crossings with a personal record of 91% return rate. The Bureau trusted her assessments because she wrote them accurately regardless of whether accuracy made her look good.

The coordinator handed her the report. "Read it. Tell me what happened."

Seln read. "Standard failure cascade. They hit a rift zone day nine, deviated from planned route to avoid expansion zone, deviation added two days to crossing timeline, water margins became inadequate, one person collapsed from dehydration, response to medical emergency consumed additional supplies, group ran deficit, second collapse day sixteen, third person died day eighteen from accumulated exposure. The nineteen who survived reached settlement with critically low reserves."

"That's what the report says. Is it what happened?"

Seln looked up. "You're asking if the guide made mistakes or if the situation was unwinnable."

"I'm asking whether this was preventable or intrinsic."

"Both." Seln set the report down. "The rift encounter was intrinsic — Plantmoon rift frequency is 2-3x summer levels, encounter probability approaches 40% over fourteen-day crossing. The route deviation was correct response. The supply margin failure was preventable."

"How?"

"The expedition launched with supply margins calculated for summer-window timeline. Plantmoon timelines need 20% additional contingency buffer. The guide either didn't know that or the client didn't want to pay for it."

The coordinator pulled the expedition contract file. Reviewed the supply manifest. Noted the provisions purchased. Compared against Bureau recommended standards for Plantmoon crossings.

The expedition had bought summer-standard supplies.

"The guide advised them?" the coordinator asked.

"Report doesn't say. If they had, the advice would be documented in the contract notes. It's not."

"So the guide failed to advise, or advised and the client ignored it, or advised and didn't document it."

"Correct."

The coordinator wrote a note for the guide's file and moved to the next question. "The three who died. Could they have been saved if the expedition had correct margins?"

Seln considered. "First casualty, yes. Standard dehydration collapse with adequate water reserve doesn't become fatal. Second casualty, probably. Third casualty was accumulated exposure — that one's borderline. Even with supplies, the person might not have recovered. But the first two were preventable with correct margins."

"So we're looking at two preventable deaths and one probable."

"That's my assessment."

The coordinator closed the file and looked at Seln. "How do we fix it?"

"You can't mandate clients buy adequate supplies. You can document that Bureau-recommended margins exist, what they are, and that deviation from recommendations voids certain contractual protections. You can require guides to document supply advisory conversations explicitly. You can track which guides consistently launch expeditions with inadequate margins and decline to certify them."

"That's regulatory tightening. Clients will resist."

"Clients resist costs until costs become casualties. Then they complain that the Bureau didn't warn them adequately." Seln tapped the report. "Expedition #447 purchased minimum margins, encountered expected complications, and experienced expected casualties. The system is working exactly as the economic incentives create. If you want different outcomes, change the incentives."

The coordinator made notes. "I'll recommend supply advisory documentation becomes mandatory contract language. That puts the decision on record and makes client liability explicit."

"That's a start."

"What else?"

"The guide's return rate was 86%. Bureau average is 89%. That's not disqualifying but it's below standard. I'd recommend the guide gets advisory notice and additional mentoring before next certification renewal."

The coordinator added the note to the guide's file. "Done. Anything else?"

"The three casualties have families?"

"One does. Spouse, two children. The other two were unattached."

"The Bureau's making the casualty payment?"

"Contractual obligation. Two thousand Watts to the family, five hundred each to the other casualties' designated recipients."

Seln nodded. "That's appropriate. Make sure the family understands the payment isn't compensation, it's acknowledgment. Zakros deaths can't be compensated."

The coordinator filed the expedition report, noted the recommendations, and moved to the next case in the review queue. Expedition #448 had returned with zero casualties and a commendation for the guide. Expedition #449 hadn't returned yet — still six days out from projected arrival, well within normal variance.

The statistics tracked cleanly. Plantmoon expeditions averaged 12-15% casualties. Summer-window expeditions averaged 5-10%. Failure-season expeditions that launched despite advisory warnings averaged 25-35%. The numbers were documented, stable, and predictable.

The Bureau's role was to minimize casualties within the economic constraints of what clients would pay for and what guides could deliver. Perfect safety didn't exist. Adequate safety cost more than most clients wanted to spend until they experienced a crossing where adequate margins would have saved someone they knew.

Nineteen survived. Three didn't. The expedition was a success by contractual definition and a failure by moral evaluation and an expected outcome by statistical prediction. All three assessments were accurate simultaneously.

The coordinator filed the reports, updated the casualty database, and prepared the next season's advisory bulletins. The recommendations would be published. Some clients would follow them. Others wouldn't. Casualties would continue occurring at rates proportional to the gap between what the desert required and what people were willing to prepare for.

The system worked. It didn't work well enough. It would continue not working well enough until something changed the economic calculation that made inadequate margins cheaper than adequate ones right up until the moment they weren't.

Seln left the Bureau office and returned to her next expedition preparation. She would advise the clients explicitly, document the conversation, and explain what Plantmoon margins required. They would either pay for adequate supplies or they wouldn't. She would guide them either way, because that was the contract.

Some would survive. Some wouldn't. The ones who survived because she'd given them correct advice would tell other people. The ones who died because they'd ignored correct advice wouldn't.

That was the work. Nineteen survived. She'd try to make it twenty next time. The desert would decide whether that was possible.
