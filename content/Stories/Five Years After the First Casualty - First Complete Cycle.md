# Five Years After the First Casualty - First Complete Cycle

_Part of the electoral durability testing story arc documenting coexistence framework survival through routine political transitions._

---

The [[Ashford Coexistence Oversight Commission|Commission]] analyst reviewed the post-election assessment report one section at a time. Five years after [[The Three Near-Wars#The Kellvost Incursion|Rift Catastrophe Revelation]], both [[Terravelle]] and [[Auralis]] had completed full electoral cycles under [[Electoral Coexistence Safeguards Framework|safeguard frameworks]]. The assessment documented whether coexistence governance had survived routine political transition or merely delayed inevitable rollback.

The data suggested survival — but survival with complications.

**Electoral Participation:** 78% of eligible voters participated in contested elections. No significant drop from pre-revelation baseline patterns. Coexistence governance had not suppressed democratic engagement.

**Campaign Compliance:** 847 candidates filed campaign materials for safeguard review. 89% received clearance without revision. 8% received clearance after modification. 3% faced formal violation notices.

The analyst made a note: the 3% violation rate was concentrated among fringe candidates who'd deliberately positioned non-compliance as brand identity. Most mainstream campaigns had learned that doctrine compliance provided tactical advantage over safeguard violation risks.

**Electoral Outcomes:** Mixed results, which was precisely what healthy democracy looked like. Some pro-coexistence incumbents won reelection. Some lost to competence-critique challengers. Some districts elected candidates who'd campaigned on boundary adjustment reform. Some districts elected candidates who'd campaigned on strengthening oversight.

The system had accommodated electoral variance without collapsing into paradigm warfare.

**Post-Election Implementation:** The ninety-day delay provisions had functioned as designed. New legislators discovered that coexistence governance was operationally embedded infrastructure rather than reversible policy. Most post-delay implementation focused on optimization rather than rollback.

Seven documented cases of new legislators attempting framework dismantlement during their first terms. All seven attempts had failed — not because safeguards blocked them, but because treaty obligations, operational dependencies, and constituent interests made dismantlement impractical.

The analyst added that finding to the summary section. Frameworks survived elections not primarily through legal constraints but through operational entrenchment. After five years, coexistence governance had become normal administrative infrastructure that communities relied on for trade, labor coordination, and civic order.

---

The Commission director reviewed the assessment during the quarterly coordination session with [[Terravelle]] and [[Auralis]] oversight liaisons.

"Five-year electoral durability test: passed," she summarized. "Frameworks survived competitive elections, peaceful power transfers, and implementation of competence-critique reforms without paradigm rollback."

"But?" one Terravelle liaison prompted. The director's tone suggested complications.

"But we've now transitioned from founder-led implementation to successor operations across approximately forty percent of oversight roles. Commission coordinators who built the frameworks are retiring or rotating out. New administrators inherit operational procedures without necessarily understanding historical precedent."

She pulled up the documented error cases. "Three instances in the past year where new oversight personnel misapplied [[Campaign Coexistence Doctrine|doctrine guidelines]] — overreach in one case, under-enforcement in two others. All three were corrected through internal review, but the pattern indicates that institutional memory transfer is incomplete."

"Natural transition friction," an Auralis liaison suggested. "Any complex system faces learning curves during personnel turnover."

"Natural friction, yes. But coexistence governance depends on precise understanding of historical precedent —why certain safeguards exist, what [[The Three Near-Wars#Historical Foundations: The Persecution Era (~400-200 Years Ago)|persecution-era]] patterns frameworks prevent, which operational compromises maintain political viability. New administrators applying procedures without understanding context create procedural compliance without strategic coherence."

"Training problem?"

"Partly. Also generational knowledge problem. The administrators who built frameworks remember why every provision exists. Their successors implement procedures because procedures exist in the manual."

The director moved to the next assessment section. "Related concern: [[Intergenerational Coexistence Curriculum|curriculum provisions]] are functioning in educational institutions, but we're five years into a twenty-year generational transition. First cohort educated entirely under post-revelation paradigm won't reach voting age for another decade. Current electorate still consists primarily of adults who remember persecution-era fears or pre-revelation assumptions."

The liaisons absorbed this timeline soberly. Electoral durability testing had confirmed five-year survival, but meaningful generational transition required fifteen more years of operational continuity. The frameworks needed to maintain legitimacy and effectiveness _more_ during the awkward middle period of institutional maturity.

"What's the assessment recommendation?" asked a Terravelle liaison.

"Accelerate succession training protocols. Expand [[Succession Safeguards Charter|succession planning]] beyond commission leadership to include mid-level administrative roles. Develop institutional memory documentation that preserves rationale context, not just procedural guidelines."

"Which is administrative burden on top of operational duties," noted an Auralis liaison. "Resources?"

"That's the political question. Five-year assessment gives us evidence to request sustained funding for coordination infrastructure rather than treating frameworks as temporary crisis response. Frameworks are permanent governance now — they need permanent institutional support."

---

The Commission analyst returned to her desk after the coordination session and pulled up the final assessment metric: public sentiment tracking.

The data showed slow, uneven shift. Support for coexistence protections had increased from 54% (one year post-revelation) to 61% (five years post-revelation) across combined kingdom populations. Opposition had decreased from 28% to 21%. Undecided/ambivalent had remained relatively stableat 18%.

The trend indicated normalization — coexistence becoming ordinary governance rather than contested emergency policy. But 21% opposition was not negligible. Roughly one in five citizens would prefer frameworks didn't exist.

The analyst added this finding to the final summary: "Five-year electoral durability does not equal universal acceptance. Frameworks survive because 61% support and operational dependencies create sufficient political coalition to resist rollback. Sustained opposition means framework maintenance requires ongoing political attention, not complacent assumption of permanence."

She flagged the finding for director review. This was the assessment's core message: coexistence had survived its first complete electoral cycle, but survival required continuous operational competence, institutional adaptation, and political engagement. Durability was achievement, not guarantee.

---

Three weeks later, the Commission published the five-year assessment publicly. The document received modest coverage in guild bulletins and trade journals, limited attention in general population newspapers.

Which was, in its own way, a measure of success. Coexistence governance had become routine enough that assessment reports were administrative documentation rather than political controversy.

One editorial in a northern [[Terravelle]] publication caught the analyst's attention:

> "The Five-Year Assessment confirms what border communities already know: coexistence works when competently administered, struggles when poorly managed, and survives because we've built operational lives that depend on it. Perfect? No. Better than alternatives? Yes. Worth defending? Increasingly."

She clipped the editorial for her reference file. Not enthusiastic endorsement. Not celebration. Just pragmatic acceptance that frameworks functioned and should continue functioning.

After five years, that counted as durability.

---

The Commission director filed the assessment with archived documentation and moved forward with implementation recommendations. Succession training expansion. Institutional memory protocols. Sustained funding requests.

Coexistence governance had passed its first electoral cycle test. Now came the harder work: maintaining operational effectiveness through the messy middle decades of generational transition, institutionalnormalization, and routine political competition.

The frameworks worked because competent people had built them carefully. They would continue working if competent people maintained them carefully.

Which was less dramatic than crisis revelation but more important for determining whether coexistence persisted beyond founder generation emergency fuel.

Five years after [[The Three Near-Wars#The Kellvost Incursion|the first casualty]], coexistence had become ordinary governance.

The next twenty years would determine whether ordinary governance could remain competent enough to avoid becoming forgotten precedent.
