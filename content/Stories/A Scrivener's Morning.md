# A Scrivener's Morning

The morning light in the [[Valdenmoor]] scriptorum was the exact color of old honey, and Maren Kel took that as a blessing.

Good paper. Steady lamp heat. Quiet desks. No tremor in her hands.

On days like this, copying felt less like labor and more like tuning an instrument no one would ever clap for.

She had done it for fourteen years under her own seal, nineteen if she counted childhood afternoons at her mother's elbow learning how to breathe between strokes.

She was [[Control Tier|T1]], which meant she did not improvise. She transmitted.

Faithfully.

Exactly.

With a precision that had once failed only once and cost someone everything.

That memory lived at the edge of her vision like a stain she could not wash out. She did not speak of it. She did not need to. It shaped every line she laid.

---

Today's first assignment was "Warmbloom," ordinary [[Heat]] utility from the [[Common Grimoire]]. Four pages. Familiar cadence. A spell so common apprentices joked they could copy it blind.

Maren did not joke about common spells. Common spells are the ones used by tired people in bad weather, and tired people in bad weather deserve accuracy more than anyone.

She was halfway through page three when a T2 practitioner entered, coat still dusted from road grit.

"Any "Ghostpath" in stock?" he asked the apprentice at intake.

The apprentice checked shelves, then checked again.

"None available today."

The practitioner nodded in that polite, disappointed way professionals do when they cannot afford to show frustration in public.

He left.

Ten minutes later, the apprentice returned to Maren's desk with a high-priority requisition and an overage note.

"Specific request," she said. "From Halveth. They asked for your hand by name."

Maren looked at the form.

She had never been to [[Life in Halveth|Halveth]]. Did not know the requester. Did not know whether they were brilliant, reckless, patient, cruel, devout, drunk, desperate, or all seven in sequence.

She knew only that someone far away trusted her work enough to ask for it specifically.

She set "Warmbloom" aside and pulled out eight blank sheets.

"Ghostpath" was harder: denser notation, tighter failure margins, less forgiving to rhythm slips. The sort of spell that punished vanity.

Maren dipped her nib, checked alignment, and began.

---

As the first page filled, the room around her blurred into sounds she had known most of her life: scrape of chair leg, soft cough, page turn, someone muttering fractions under breath.

Her mother used to say, _Copying is a kind of promise made to strangers._

Maren understood that better now than when she was young enough to think craft was about pride.

The practitioner in Halveth would receive this in three weeks if roads held. They would cast in a moment Maren would never see. They might live because of one correctly placed glyph. They might fail for reasons beyond ink and line.

All she controlled was this desk, this page, this stroke.

When she reached the midpoint knotwork on page five, she paused, flexed her fingers once, then continued with deliberate calm.

By noon, she had six pages complete and no corrections in the margin.

At day close, all eight were dry, sealed, and bound for courier transit.

The apprentice asked if it felt strange, being known in a city she had never seen.

Maren smiled without looking up from cleanup.

"It's better than being known in this one," she said. "Fewer interruptions." 

Then, after a beat: "Pack it with care."

Because in a world built on routes, faith, and error margins, sometimes the truest story of power was a woman at a desk making sure a stranger's spell arrived exactly as intended.
