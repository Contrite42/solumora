# Thirty Months After the First Casualty - Veto Count

The tally board in crown office used three chalk colors: confirmed support, confirmed opposition, and "performative undecided." Tonight most marks sat in the third column.

[[Merra Veld]] watched [[Cassia]] redraw the same district list for the second time.

"You keep moving two names between columns," Merra said.

"Because they keep moving in real time," Cassia replied. "Publicly pro-rollback in morning interviews, privately asking whether veto override math survives if turnout shifts in academy wards."

On the table lay two documents:
- Final passage text of Bill 47-C, narrower than original but still stripping key safeguards
- Draft veto memorandum invoking the [[Framework Retrenchment Defense Protocol]] and requesting mandatory harm forecast publication before reintroduction

[[Kess Tarren]] arrived late from Tribunal floor review with rain on her coat and no patience left for optimism.

"Field leads think they can absorb the rollback for one quarter," she said. "They're wrong. First crisis incident and we lose correction visibility again."

"Can we hold without veto?" Cassia asked.

Kess answered immediately. "No."

Merra pointed at the tally board. "Then the question is not whether to veto. It's whether we can survive override attempt."

At 22:10, final whip count came in: veto override likely short by one if two undecideds stayed absent. Likely. Not certain.

Cassia read the memorandum once more, then signed.

The veto announcement went public at 23:00.

By midnight, reaction fractured as predicted:
- Core city reform blocs praised constitutional restraint
- Security hardliners called it governance paralysis
- Border districts asked whether protocols would now trigger automatic dual-jurisdiction hearings

In Ashford, Jess's teams posted early-warning guidance for noncooperation signals before dawn.

At 08:00, override session opened.

At 10:14, first undecided voted to override.
At 10:21, second voted to sustain veto.

The final count held. Veto sustained by one.

No cheering in chamber. No collapse either. Just clerks entering numbers into ledger systems that would decide next quarter's legal terrain.

After session, Cassia stood in the side corridor where older stone still carried faint marks from prior-generation hearings.

"One vote," she said.

Merra shrugged. "Most durable systems are built on one-vote margins repeated long enough to become boring."

Kess joined them with implementation packet already marked for circulation: retrenchment harm forecast template, dual-jurisdiction hearing notice, mandatory publication schedule.

"They'll bring it back," she said.

"I know," Cassia replied.

"Good," Kess said. "Then we keep meeting it in procedure instead of panic."

Outside, rain cleared and traffic resumed. Dock manifests moved, academy bells rang, cross-border permits processed in uneven batches. Ordinary life did not care about rhetoric; it cared whether systems stayed legible.

Thirty months after revelation, coexistence was no longer defended by shock or charisma.

It was defended by veto counts, publication schedules, and people willing to spend their nights protecting paperwork from becoming amnesia.
