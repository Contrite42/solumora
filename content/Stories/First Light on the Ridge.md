# First Light on the Ridge

The ridge came into view three hours after dawn, exactly where [[Maren Koss]]'s survey map said it would be. [[Lenna Vrell]] checked the sightline twice, confirmed the elevation matched the projection, and allowed herself a brief moment of satisfaction before returning to the immediate work of not dying on the approach.

The [[Wolfpoint]] highlands didn't tolerate carelessness. The terrain forty miles northwest of the settlement consisted of broken rock, thin air, and weather that changed from clear to lethal faster than you could pack your gear. Lenna's expedition — herself, [[Control Tier|T3]], two researchers, and a logistics coordinator — had spent six days moving through country that might not have seen human presence in a generation.

Now they were eight hundred meters from an unmapped ridge that might contain [[Ancient Ruins|pre-Contact ruins]], or geological anomalies, or absolutely nothing worth the cost of reaching it. Lenna's job was to find out which.

---

**Ridge approach.** The eastern face was climbable — barely. [[Seln Torra]], the junior researcher, had [[Control Tier|T1]] capacity and basic [[Grimoires|grimoire]] training, which meant she could manage light [[Flux]] work but nothing weight-bearing. [[Vren Mast]], the senior researcher, was [[Control Tier|T2]] and experienced, which gave them one reliable caster for structural support if the climb required it.

Lenna herself was T3 and had been doing mountain work for eleven years. That meant she went first.

The rock was solid — old granite, weathered but stable. She placed anchors every twenty meters, tested each one with full body weight before committing, and signaled the team forward only after the route was confirmed safe. Three hours of methodical climbing brought them to the ridgeline.

The ruins were there.

Not dramatic — no towers, no intact structures, nothing that would justify an artist's rendering. But there: a foundation perimeter carved into the granite, channels that had once carried water or [[Flux]] conduits, and a central platform with markings that matched the pre-Contact style documented at [[The Solden Archive]].

Vren dropped his pack and pulled the survey tools. "We're recording this correctly."

Lenna nodded. "Full documentation. Three days minimum."

Seln was already photographing the channel system with a portable imaging grimoire, the kind that cost more than most independent expeditions could afford. This expedition wasn't independent. [[Wolfpoint]] had funded it directly, which meant they had real equipment and real expectations.

Lenna walked the perimeter while Vren set up the measurement grid. The foundation was fifty meters across — large enough to have supported a significant structure, but not monumental. The condition was remarkable: minimal erosion, no vegetation intrusion, and the carved channels still showed precision edges after however many centuries had passed.

"Why's it preserved?" Seln asked, looking up from the camera work.

"Elevation, probably," Vren said. "Cold inhibits organic decay. No soil accumulation means no root intrusion."

"Or it's warded," Lenna added. She knelt beside the central platform and ran her hand above the surface without touching it. No [[Flux]] response — or none she could detect. "Either way, we document what we see, not what we guess."

---

**Three days of work.** They mapped every channel, every marking, every dimension. Vren's measurements went into the primary log. Seln's images captured detail invisible to direct observation. Lenna maintained camp operations, managed the [[Flux]]-sustained heating system, and ensured they had enough consumables to finish the survey without cutting corners.

On the morning of the fourth day, they descended.

The climb down took four hours. The hike back to the staging camp took two days. The report filing took another three days after they returned to Wolfpoint proper. When it was done, Lenna delivered the full documentation to [[Sera Voss]]'s archival office and waited.

Sera reviewed the imaging grimoire plates first, then the measurement logs, then the written observations. She didn't speak for six minutes. Finally: "This matches the Solden construction pattern but predates it by positioning. We didn't know they built this far north."

"They did," Lenna said.

"You're certain the survey's complete?"

"As complete as one expedition can make it. If you want deeper analysis, send a full research team. We gave you the foundation."

Sera closed the documentation folder. "You gave us more than that. This changes the northern settlement range theory." She paused. "The Council will fund follow-up. Are you available?"

Lenna considered. She'd been in the field for two months before this expedition. Her gear needed maintenance. Her savings needed replenishment. And she was tired in the way that only sustained high-altitude work created.

"Not immediately," she said. "Three months from now, maybe."

"That's reasonable." Sera made a note. "I'll route the payment authorization today. Full negotiated rate plus the documentation bonus."

Lenna left the archival office and walked back through [[Wolfpoint]]'s narrow streets to the boarding house where she'd been staying. The payment would clear in two days. The documentation bonus was more than she'd expected. The follow-up contract was almost certain.

She'd spent six days in brutal terrain, risked the climb, and done methodical work under conditions that made methodical work difficult. The ruins had been there. The survey was sound. The outcome was exactly what it was supposed to be.

That didn't happen every time. When it did, you documented it carefully, took the payment, and prepared for the next expedition where it might not.

Lenna reached her room, dropped her pack, and sat on the edge of the narrow bed. Through the small window she could see the northwestern peaks — distant, cold, and full of places nobody had mapped yet.

In three months she'd go back. Not to the same ridge, probably, but to something like it. There was always more ground to cover, and the ruins didn't document themselves.

For now, though, she had a bed and a city and a brief window where survival wasn't the dominant concern. She intended to use it for rest, equipment repair, and ignoring mountains.

The peaks would still be there in three months. They always were.
