# Fifty-Eight Days After the First Casualty - Closed Door Briefing

The briefing room in Border Coordination has no windows and too many chairs.

Merra arrives with Jess and one sealed packet. Kess is denied entry at the second checkpoint on grounds of "current non-cooperation status with Auralis research authorities." The guard uses the phrase like policy. Merra hears it as strategy.

Inside, six people sit around the table: two logistics officers, one legal advisor, one military liaison, one civilian deputy from Terravelle's executive office, and a recorder who never looks up.

Jess begins with numbers.

She walks them through load assumptions, corridor overlap, and the failure window. She explains exactly where confidence is high, where error bars widen, and why uncertainty still points in one direction. She names the point at which war planning becomes irrelevant because infrastructure and dimensional integrity fail together.

The military liaison interrupts at minute twelve.

"Your model assumes Auralis movement timing with no direct intelligence access."

Jess answers without heat. "Correct. We inferred from requisition flow and staging throughput. If your intelligence says their cadence is slower, this gets better. If your intelligence says they are accelerating, this gets worse."

"We do not discuss intelligence cadence in this room."

"Then discuss tonnage," Jess says. "Tonnage does not classify itself."

The legal advisor asks whether any of this analysis has been peer-reviewed by Council-certified ruins specialists.

Merra slides the second packet forward. "It was built with Kess Tarren's inscription analysis. She's outside because your checkpoint doesn't like her employer history."

The deputy from the executive office rubs his eyes and asks the only honest question in the room.

"What do you need from us by end of day?"

Merra reads from Jess's action sheet.

"Delay corridor deployment twenty days. Shift heavy-flux movement west. Suspend nonessential testing near ruins clusters. Authorize shared rift reporting with Auralis."

Silence.

Then process.

The military liaison says delay signals weakness.
The legal advisor says shared reporting requires reciprocal treaty language.
The logistics officers say reroute capacity is possible but expensive.
The deputy asks what happens if they do nothing.

Jess answers, "You get a cascade window between days sixty-four and sixty-eight if acceleration continues."

The recorder writes for the first time in ten minutes.

At the end, the deputy signs receipt of the briefing and says the package will be elevated "through emergency channels."

Merra has heard that phrase before. It means there is now a document proving someone was warned.

Outside, in the corridor, Kess waits with her maps against her chest.

"How bad?" she asks.

Merra hands her the signed receipt.

"Best case, we bought a meeting. Worst case, we bought a paper trail."

Jess checks the time and closes her ledger.

"Then we run parallel track," she says. "If command won't move fast enough, we find someone who can act without command."

Kess looks at Merra. "You mean your anonymous friend."

Merra does not deny it.

"I mean whoever sent us the truth before institutions did."
