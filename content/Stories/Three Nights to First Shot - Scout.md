# Three Nights to First Shot - Scout

Rennic Vale had learned to count silence in weights.

Light silence meant a patrol line was doing its work. Men and women checked markers, rotated watch, sent ordinary updates nobody would read twice.

Heavy silence meant someone had decided not to send something.

Tonight was heavy.

He stood on the northern ridge above the checkpoint lights and watched the southern side through a lens older than he was. [[Auralis]] patrols were visible only in fragments: one lamp moving wrong, one supply cart where no cart should be, one signal blink cut short before full pattern.

None of it violated standing posture. All of it changed the shape of risk.

---

At second bell, his junior scout handed him the slate.

"No courier handoff from the south," she said. "Third night."

He wrote the line into the field log and hated how normal his handwriting looked.

No handoff could mean weather, injury, broken axle, bad timing. It could also mean [[The Communication Severance]] had moved from policy language into habit.

Once habit formed, people stopped expecting clarification. After that, they started planning around fear.

He sent a coded note to [[Border Coordination Offices]] anyway.

No claims. No accusation.

Just sequence: three nights, no handoff, mirrored lantern drift, extra wagon at south marker C.

Evidence first. Narrative later, if possible never.

---

Near dawn, movement began at the low road.

Not an advance. Not a retreat. A relocation that made tactical sense if you're afraid of being seen as weak and political sense if you're afraid of being seen as eager.

Rennic had seen that shape before in training files tied to the [[Near-War Incident Ladder]].

Both sides called it readiness adjustment.

Both sides read it as intent.

He marked the map with a thin charcoal line and thought about how often history turned on lines that could be erased with one wet thumb.

---

When his relief arrived, Rennic gave instructions twice.

"If anything moves fast, we report movement, not motive."

"If signal fails, we default to observation, not interception."

The second instruction was unpopular. Interception felt like action. Observation felt like waiting to be blamed.

But [[Border De-Escalation Protocols]] were built for exactly this hour: when everyone wanted certainty and none existed.

As he walked back toward the temporary barracks, the morning wind shifted warm from the south.

He did not believe in omens.

He believed in exhaustion, bad assumptions, and distance.

Three nights of heavy silence had made all three worse.
