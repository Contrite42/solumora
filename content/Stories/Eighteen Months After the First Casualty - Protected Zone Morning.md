# Eighteen Months After the First Casualty - Protected Zone Morning

The [[Ashford Coexistence Oversight Commission|Commission]] observer arrived in [[Celn Vard]] at dawn, as agreed. Not because dawn was required, [[Merra Veld]] had learned, but because remote settlements wanted to make clear you were being watched back.

She stood at the village boundary marker while the elder—Torren Keld, seventy-three, third-generation after the regional [[The Persecution Era|persecution sweeps]]—approached with deliberate slowness. The village behind him looked orderly. Thirty houses, one meeting hall, terraced grain fields climbing the hillside. No public spaces large enough for the [[Intergenerational Coexistence Curriculum|integration curriculum]] the cities now mandated.

"Protected Observation Zone," Torren said, not as greeting. "Charter says we don't have to do anything."

"Charter says the Commission observes to prevent active persecution," Merra agreed. "And that you're not required to implement integration measures if your community isn't ready."

"We're not ready."

"I'm here to confirm that. Not to change your mind."

Torren studied her for a long moment. The morning sun caught the grain fields in that particular angle that made you understand why people defended remote places. "You're the one from the stories. From the war that wasn't."

"I helped stop it, yes."

"With one of _them_."

Merra didn't correct the pronoun. The [[Regional Enforcement Variance Doctrine|Variance Doctrine]] had taught her that forcing language changes before communities were ready just made people better liars. "Yes."

"He still alive?"

"[[Teren Voss|Verath]]'s public now. Works with the [[Joint Rift Monitoring Cell|Monitoring Cell]]. You can look him up on the official registers if you want."

Torren's expression didn't change. "My grandmother used to tell stories about the hunting. Lantern formations. How you had to watch the way shadows moved because they could look like anyone until they didn't."

"I've read the [[Ashford Witness Amnesty Records|Amnesty Records]]."

"Records." He said it like the word itself was inadequate. "She died six years ago. Last thing she told me: 'Don't let them pretend it's forgiven because it's over.'"

Merra let the silence hold. The morning wind moved through the grain fields with a sound like water.

"The Commission doesn't want to pretend," she said finally. "That's why Protected Observation exists. Your grandmother was right—forgiveness isn't something anyone can demand. The Charter only asks that the hunting stay stopped."

"And if we keep our children out of your integrated schools? Keep our own histories?"

"Then you're in compliance with Protected Observation status. As long as there's no active persecution—no violence, no organized exclusion if someone moves here who doesn't share your views—the Commission marks you green and leaves."

Torren looked past her, toward the road that connected Celn Vard to the regional network. "The [[Anti-Persecution Compliance Tribunal|Tribunal]]'s not going to accept that forever."

"The Tribunal's already understaffed for the core cities. They don't have capacity to enforce integration everywhere, even if they wanted to."

"But they want to."

"Some of them do," Merra acknowledged. "[[Kess Tarren]] thinks Protected Observation is moral cowardice. She's not wrong from her perspective."

Torren's eyes came back to her. "You're very honest."

"Lying to you wastes both our time. The Commission's mandate is preventing another [[The Persecution Era|persecution cycle]]—not forcing cultural integration on a timeline your grandmother wouldn't have believed possible. If those are the same thing, we'll find out. If they're not, Protected Observation gives us twenty years to figure out which matters more."

"Twenty years."

"Generation and a half. Long enough for children who learn the integrated curriculum in the cities to have children of their own. Long enough to see if this framework holds when the people who remember the war are gone."

Torren was quiet for a long time. Behind him, smoke rose from morning cooking fires. The village was waking. Normal life, with the strangeness held at the boundary.

"I'll show you the village records," he said finally. "Population register, trade contacts, last year's harvest documentation. You can verify no one's been excluded or pressured to leave. Then you go back and mark us green."

"That's all I need."

"But you're going to come back. Every six months, the Charter said."

"Every six months," Merra confirmed. "To verify the hunting stays stopped. Not to convince you of anything else."

Torren nodded slowly. "My grandmother would have hated everything about this. The Commission, the Charter, the official registers. All of it."

"But?"

"But she'd have hated hunts more." He turned toward the village. "Come on. Records are in the meeting hall. You can be gone by midday if you don't waste time."

Merra followed him past the boundary marker. The grain fields continued their quiet rustling. Protected Observation meant living with this tension—communities that would teach their own histories, keep their own distance, hold their grandmothers' warnings alive. Not forgiven. Not integrated.

But not hunting.

The Commission counted that as progress. Whether it counted as justice was a question for people with longer timeframes than eighteen months.
