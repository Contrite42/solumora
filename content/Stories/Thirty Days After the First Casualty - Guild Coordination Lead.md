# Thirty Days After the First Casualty — Guild Coordination Lead

Esten Morv represents the Terravelle Guild Alliance on the Escalation Response Council. She has been in this role for fourteen years, but the past thirty days feel like the true duration of her appointment. Everything before the casualty reports was training for this exact meeting cycle.

The Council meets three times weekly now, instead of monthly. Attendance is mandatory, and the agenda expands before each session. Week one: casualty verification and supply continuity. Week two: military supply requests and civilian rationing frameworks. Week three: military supply requests and displaced person population modeling. Week four: everything simultaneously, with the addition of economic stabilization measurements.

On day twenty-nine, Esten receives a request from the Treasury for the Guild Alliance to voluntarily moderate its pricing on essential goods. This is not mandatory. It is also not a request. The Treasury representative explains that public confidence requires visible restraint, and the guilds' restraint would signal "institutional coordination in crisis" rather than "price panic." She agrees that this is true, and she understands that refusing would signal the opposite.

Esten has seventeen hours to present this to her constituent guilds. She sends a message outlining the framework and receives, within an hour, three responses asking whether voluntary restraint will be reciprocated with government contracts. Within two hours, two guilds decline to participate unless contracts are formally guaranteed. By evening, four guilds have requested clarity on the term length of this cooperation.

Esten sends a follow-up message: "Restraint term: thirty days from day thirty. Reciprocal: Provisioning officers may prioritize guild merchants for rapid procurement. Formal: Temporary trade exemptions for essential goods until thirty-day term expires." She knows, while writing this, that the contracts will never be formalized, that the exemptions will become permanent, and that she has just agreed to redefine the guild's operational structure without formal authority.

Five guilds sign by day thirty. The remaining four will sign within a week, once their competitors secure the provisional contracts.

Esten attends the day-thirty Council meeting and reports that the Guild Alliance is implementing voluntary pricing restraint through day sixty. The Council minutes record this as "Guild Alliance voluntarily coordinating pricing in support of crisis stability." The minutes do not record that Esten negotiated away thirty years of pricing autonomy in exchange for contracts that the government cannot afford to honor while maintaining military operations.

This is the difference between being part of an institutional response and being part of the institutional response. Esten has moved from the former to the latter. She is constructing the fiction that merchant interests and government interests are compatible over the next thirty days. On day sixty-one, that fiction will require rebuilding.

She makes notes for the next meeting: "Guild Alliance positioning stable. No public confidence loss. Pricing restraint framework operational. Watch for merchant guild erosion when contract terms expire. Watch for government credibility loss if contracts cannot be honored. Watch for institutional failure cascade when everyone simultaneously realizes the fiction is ending."

She has written this note before. She will write it again on day sixty.
