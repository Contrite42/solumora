# Thirty Days After the First Casualty — Banking Coordinator

Shen Delmar has not slept more than four hours a night since the casualty reports began crossing her desk. She runs the Banking Institutions' coordination node in Auralis Central—the office where the Treasury Department, the grain collateral system, and three major lending houses converge. For thirty days, they have all been asking the same question in different dialects: *Will we get paid back?*

The first week, the answer was silence. The second week, the answer was "probably." Now, on day thirty-one, the Treasury has released a loan guarantee framework. It guarantees nothing.

Shen reads the document for the seventh time. "War-contingent collateral restructuring" means the lending houses can call their grain-backed instruments early if military outflows exceed 11 percent of monthly fiscal flow. "Early" means the farmers have forty days to repay, not the contracted eighteen—which is mathematically impossible across three northern districts. The guarantee covers the difference up to 30 percent of lost principal. Above that, the banks absorb the loss.

She puts the document down and remembers, very clearly, the conversation with House Meldren's representative on day eighteen. "We need certainty, Shen. Our depositors are asking whether they should move money south. Certainty helps more than the best terms ever will." He had been correct, of course. But the Treasury can only guarantee what the Treasury can predict, and predictions have stopped having decimal places.

Shen's job is now to translate the Treasury framework into lending house language. She will tell them that this is the best guarantee available in a situation where "available" is itself a war-contingent term. They will accept it because the alternative is calling all remaining loans simultaneously and discovering that the northern agricultural districts cannot pay. That would be honesty, and banking institutions are not equipped for honesty when it intersects with scale.

By afternoon, two of the three lending houses have signed. The third—House Kess—sends a message asking for verbal clarification. Shen schedules a call and understands, before the call begins, that she will be asked whether she personally believes the war will exceed eleven percent of fiscal flow. She will explain that "personal belief" is not data. They will press. She will suggest they consult their internal actuaries. They will accept this as confirmation.

She has moved past the illusion that her job is banking. Now she understands it clearly: her job is managing the fiction that financial systems can predict military outcomes. As long as the fiction holds, the farmers keep their land. The day the fiction breaks, the institutions begin consuming themselves from the interior.

Shen makes notes for day thirty-one. "All loan frameworks restructured to war-contingency model. No additional clarity available from institutional sources. Waiting for next casualty report to determine next restructure window."

The next casualty report is due tomorrow.
