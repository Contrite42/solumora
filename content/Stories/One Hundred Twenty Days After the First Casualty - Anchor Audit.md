# One Hundred Twenty Days After the First Casualty - Anchor Audit

Kess Tarren hates ceremonial site visits. They are usually for photos, not for truth.

This one might be different.

The Joint Rift Monitoring Cell has flagged low-frequency instability creep at two reinforced corridor anchors. Nothing acute. Nothing headline-worthy. Exactly the kind of pattern institutions ignore until it becomes urgent.

Merra, Kess, and Verath travel the route with a six-person mixed audit team and a rule set the Commission negotiated last week: every intervention logged, every decision traceable, every exception publicly reviewable.

At Anchor Point D, the readings are worse than the bulletin suggested. Ward drift at 14 percent over baseline, seam tension rising during routine freight windows. Verath can stabilize it in minutes. Under new protocol, he does not act first.

Kess runs the sequence.

"Low-tier reinforcement pass. Confirm drift source. If we can correct with human-team rotation, we do that before high-load intervention."

Verath nods and steps back.

For thirty-one minutes, human teams execute corrective cycles while Jess's remote load model updates through relay. Drift drops from 14 to 8, then stalls.

Merra checks time windows against freight schedules. "If we hold at eight, we roll risk into night transfer and lose margin."

Kess looks to Verath. "Targeted dampening, no spillover, two-minute cap."

He moves with the same quiet precision as Ashford standstill week, except this time the sequence is institutional, not improvised. Intervention logged, observers present, constraints predeclared.

Drift drops to 2.1 percent.

No panic, no hero narrative, no secrecy.

Just governance functioning under stress.

On return, Kess files the audit packet to both the JRMC and ACOC dockets with full parameter logs attached. The packet is public by evening.

Auralis hardline commentators call it strategic dependence theater.
Terravelle skeptics call it normalization risk.
Freight operators call it lane stability and keep shipping food.

Merra reads the comments once and closes the slate.

"People can argue classification tomorrow," she says. "Today the anchor held."

Verath, standing by the map table, answers without irony.

"I have waited centuries to hear someone describe this as ordinary work."

Kess signs final line item:

**Audit complete. Corrective action lawful. System stable pending next cycle.**

That sentence may be the most radical thing any of them has written this year.
