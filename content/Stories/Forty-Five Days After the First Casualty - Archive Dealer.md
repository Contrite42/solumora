# Forty-Five Days After the First Casualty - Archive Dealer

Teren Voss closes the shop at sunset and waits exactly twelve minutes before taking the floorboard key from its false drawer. Twelve minutes is long enough for regular customers to clear the lane and too short for tails to settle into patient confidence.

He has been patient for one hundred eighty-seven years.

In the basement, behind shelves of damp paper and binding thread, sits the ledger he has not opened in thirty years. The cover is plain gray cloth, a clerk's notebook by appearance. Inside are names, dates, and places where dimensional lines bent too far and almost broke: salt flats north of Emberfall, a caravan route near Zakros, a mining cut west of Solhaven where the stone bled blue light for three weeks.

Kellvost joins the list in careful handwriting.

Verath does not like writing in this ledger. Every entry means the same thing: the world is walking toward a wound it has made before.

He reads the latest page from memory anyway. Military flux concentration forecasts based on border mobilization patterns. Predicted overlap windows. Estimated cascade probability if both kingdoms deploy high-tier discipline units across unstable desert bands.

The number has climbed again.

If he does nothing, the pattern completes. If he intervenes visibly, the world learns what he is.

He chooses a third option first: place the right humans in the same room.

Merra Veld because she recognizes failure signatures before they become obituary counts. Jess Maren because she can translate panic into tonnage and route time. Kess Tarren because she reads ruins as systems, not mythology. Three variables with independent competence and no institutional loyalty strong enough to blind them.

Verath writes the note in Teren's hand. He does not sign it. He includes the Messon deviation detail as proof of access and to ensure Merra understands this is not random blackmail.

Then he prepares the second packet, the one they will not see tonight.

It contains a copied inscription segment from Site Seventeen, annotated in Kess's own early notation style from before the Council edited the archive trail. The line she translated as **anchor array integrity thresholds**. The line the Council published as **ceremonial gate balance rites**.

The difference between those two translations is the difference between warning people and burying them.

He wraps the packet in oilcloth and seals it with wax impressed by an obsolete trade stamp. If the meeting fails, he burns it. If the meeting succeeds, he leaves it where Kess will find it and believe, against her better judgment, that someone inside the lie still wants the truth to survive.

Near midnight, he walks to the roof and watches Ashford's warehouse district breathe in narrow bands of lamp light. He can feel the pressure in the air where dimensional seams are already thin. Not enough for humans to notice yet. Enough for him.

He thinks of the other four survivors and the rule they agreed to when the persecutions ended: no public interventions, no signatures, no displays. Live quietly or do not live long.

Quiet has bought nearly two centuries.

Quiet will not buy this one.

Verath returns downstairs, locks the ledger, and writes one final line on a scrap he will destroy before dawn.

"If they choose action, I will step into the story."

He burns the scrap in a brass dish and watches the ash curl into nothing.
