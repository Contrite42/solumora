# Two Years After the First Casualty - Sweep Night

The alert reached [[Kess Tarren]] at second bell: coordinated enforcement action, Aldrane lower districts, thirty-two addresses, suspected organized non-compliance network sheltering unregistered High Demon contacts.

By third bell, the numbers had doubled.

"Who authorized expansion from thirty-two to sixty-eight?" Kess asked as she crossed the Tribunal operations floor.

No one answered quickly enough.

The duty coordinator finally said, "District command interpreted rolling probable-cause updates as dynamic authority under the emergency clause."

"The emergency clause requires dual-signature confirmation after the first expansion threshold."

"They said the signatures were pending."

Kess stopped walking. Around her, clerks were processing incoming detention manifests in stacks that looked wrong on sight: too fast, too clean, too many identical rationale phrases.

"Get me raw logs from field channels and the names of every authorizing officer," she said. "Now."

By fourth bell, she had enough to know the operation had outrun procedure. By fifth, she had enough to know it had outrun reality.

Detained list included:
- Two registered [[Joint Rift Monitoring Cell]] analysts with active city credentials
- One Commission liaison assigned to the academy district
- Eight residents flagged by outdated street registries from prior quarter
- Multiple T0 service workers pulled from night shifts with no linkage to the target warrant set

"This is a false-positive cascade," Kess said flatly. "Issue immediate hold on further pickups."

"District command says stopping now signals weakness and will trigger coordinated resistance," the coordinator replied.

"Continuing is illegal," Kess said. "Transmit hold order under Tribunal authority."

The coordinator hesitated for one breath too long before relaying it.

That was the moment Kess understood the problem was bigger than one bad night. Staff trusted momentum more than protocol because momentum felt like control.

By dawn, sixty-one people had been detained. Eighteen were released before sunrise. By midday, the release count hit forty-three.

Outside the Tribunal annex, crowds gathered with printed names and handwritten signs: _You Can't Repair Trust With Locked Doors._ _Legal Does Not Mean Right._ _We Cooperated and You Swept Anyway._

Kess read the first public brief from district command. It described the operation as "precision enforcement under exceptional risk conditions." She looked at the detention correction file on her desk and marked the phrase in red.

At noon, [[Cassia]] requested immediate status. At twelve-fifteen, [[Merra Veld]] sent one line from Commission channels: _If you call this routine correction, we lose the city._

Kess called emergency hearing authority for 14:00 and requested independent observers before she had formal clearance to do so. She'd ask forgiveness later if needed.

At 14:00, the hearing room overflowed. Families, operators, students, merchants, and field staff filled every seat and both hallways. Kess opened the session with the line she had rewritten three times:

"Tonight's enforcement action failed legal standards and public duty obligations. We are not here to defend that failure. We are here to account for it in the open and prevent recurrence."

The room stayed silent for a long beat, as if testing whether this was script or truth.

Then the questions started.

By evening, Kess had signed temporary suspension orders on mass dynamic expansions, requested activation criteria review for what would become the [[Coexistence Enforcement Failure Review Board]], and authorized immediate record correction support for every improperly detained resident.

It wasn't enough. Not yet. But it was movement away from denial.

When she finally left the building after midnight, the square was mostly empty. One remaining sign lay against the steps in chalk-stained board:

_If your framework can fail this hard, prove it can admit that._

Kess stood there longer than she meant to. Two years into coexistence, she had believed the danger was backlash from outside institutions. Sweep Night taught her the greater risk: institutions mistaking urgency for accuracy and calling the damage collateral.

Tomorrow would begin repair. Tonight had to be named what it was.

Failure.
