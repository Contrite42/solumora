# Five Days Before the Breakpoint - Merchant

Cavel Dorst had rewritten the same contract three times in two hours and none of the versions were honest.

The first pretended inspections were still predictable.

The second pretended delays were random.

The third admitted, in language no court would reject, that delivery windows now depended on administrative weather no merchant could forecast.

He set the page aside and looked out over Ashford where cranes moved cargo as if routine still existed.

Routine existed. It just no longer protected margin.

---

The buyer in Solhaven wanted fixed price and fixed delivery. Of course they did.

Cavel wanted variable windows and dispute-safe language tied to inspection escalation tiers. Of course he did.

They met in the middle by lying politely.

"Three to five days in corridor processing," he said.

"Acceptable if documentation is complete," the buyer replied.

Both men had read this week's notices. Both knew "complete" was now a moving target.

Cavel added a rider for administrative variance and called it a technical clarifier. The buyer requested a premium cap and called it continuity protection. Neither clause solved anything. Both clauses would later be used to prove the other side had acted in bad faith.

---

By evening he had six open files tied to the same pattern:

- higher costs from [[Crossing Risk Underwriters]]
- slower release through corridor reviews
- clients demanding certainty in an uncertainty market
- couriers charging scarcity rates for reliability that no longer existed

He could still make money. That was not the question.

The question was how long a house could keep promising stability while every institution it relied on was optimizing for self-protection.

---

At the dock office, his junior clerk brought in a flagged packet.

"Seal looks right," the clerk said, "but the provenance path doesn't line up with the route window."

Cavel checked it once and set it aside for hold review.

"Good catch," he said.

"Do we report it?"

He smiled without humor. "We report everything now. That's the era."

The clerk hesitated. "And if reporting slows us enough to lose the contract?"

Cavel tapped the packet. "Then we lose the contract honestly instead of losing the house dishonestly."

He did not add that honest losses were becoming easier to explain than honest profits.

---

Night settled over Ashford and the ledger room filled with the soft noise of pages turning.

He finalized the contract with contingency language dense enough to survive review and thin enough to pass first read.

At the bottom he added the sentence he had resisted all day:

"Settlement timing subject to adjudication conditions outside merchant control."

The sentence would anger the buyer, confuse their legal desk, and probably save both parties from pretending later.

He sanded the ink, sealed the copy, and marked dispatch for dawn.

Five days, he thought, might be enough to deliver this and still call it trade instead of posture.

He did not believe it.

He filed it anyway.
