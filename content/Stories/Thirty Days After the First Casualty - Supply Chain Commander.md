# Thirty Days After the First Casualty — Supply Chain Commander

Voris Hent commands the Maren Freight Chain logistics hub in Ashford. For thirty days, he has been running seventeen different distribution networks through the same physical bottleneck: the Ashford warehouse complex, now operating under what everyone calls "emergency stack" protocols. The protocols were designed for natural disaster coordination. They are handling something different—a sustained state where normalcy is no longer an attainable endstate.

The first week, Voris prioritized by institutional need: medical supplies first, then food reserves, then fuel, then everything else. The second week, the priority shifted because the medical system had absorbed enough spare capacity to stop the panic. Now, week five, the system has developed a new hierarchy: items that will rot get moved first, then military logistics requests (which were denied for the first two weeks but are now being discreetly processed), then household food, then everything else.

Voris has three people he answers to. The Ashford Dock Compact representative asks about civilian supply continuity. The Border Guards command asks about warehouse security and potential rerouting to military positions. The Treasury logistics office asks about cost management and prevention of "unnecessary duplicative holding." All three are correct that Voris is not sufficiently prioritizing their domain.

On day twenty-eight, a military quartermaster arrives unannounced and asks Voris whether the warehouse can accommodate a thirty-day forward supply cache for 200 soldiers. Voris says yes, technically. The quartermaster asks when. Voris says "not before day thirty-seven, if I empty seven civilian categories." The quartermaster leaves and files a request for warehouse authority restructuring.

By day thirty, the authority restructuring has not been approved, but Voris has received three more military requests for supply staging. He has begun moving items without formal authorization—a decision that is technically not a crime but is legally undefensive if the supply chain fails.

He meets with the Dock Compact representative on day thirty and tells her, directly, that the civilian supply continuity she is asking about is now a secondary priority. She understands what he is not saying. By evening, three household food contracts are being rerouted to a secondary warehouse outside Ashford, reducing load on the primary complex by 12 percent. This is technically a supply diversification. It is actually the beginning of institutional fragmentation.

Voris makes notes for day thirty. "Primary warehouse is now operating at 94 percent capacity with mixed-priority staging. Security posture is degraded. Cannot accommodate both civilian continuity and military forward-staging without authority restructuring. If restructuring proceeds, civilian supply deteriorates within fourteen days."

He has written a version of this note for twenty-two days in a row. The document now reads like autobiography.
