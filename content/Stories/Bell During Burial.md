# Bell During Burial

Korven Tess was seventeen minutes into the burial when the bell sounded.

He stopped mid-prayer. Everyone stopped. In [[Equatorial Desert|Zakros]], you stopped for bells.

The sound came from the south — high, clear, repeating at five-second intervals. Emergency signal. Someone was in immediate danger and close enough for the sound to reach the way station.

Korven looked at the expedition lead, a [[Control Tier|T4]] named Merra Sols who'd done twenty-three crossings and understood exactly what the bell meant.

Merra's expression didn't change. "Finish the burial. Fast."

Korven spoke the final prayer sequence in abbreviated form, completing the ritual in two minutes instead of the traditional fifteen. The body they were burying — Teln Vass, age 34, died from [[Rift Incursions|rift]]-contamination complications four hours ago — deserved full ceremony. Teln would get abbreviated ceremony because someone alive needed help more urgently than someone dead needed complete ritual.

They covered the grave, marked it with stones, and documented the location. Then Korven, Merra, and two other T3 practitioners organized response.

The bell was still ringing. That meant whoever was signaling had enough [[Flux]] reserve to sustain the casting or enough desperation to burn themselves dry trying to get help.

---

**Response protocol:** Bells rang for equipment failure, medical emergency, rift encounter, or fauna attack. Whatever the cause, response required practitioners with reserves, medical supplies, and combat capability if the situation was still active.

Merra assigned roles. "Korven, you're medical. Seln, you're combat reserve. Vorn, you're supplies. I'm command. We move fast but we don't run. Running in Zakros gets you killed as often as slow response does."

They moved south. The bell continued. The five-second interval was standard emergency pattern — faster meant immediate life-threat, slower meant urgent-but-stable. Five-second meant "come soon or someone dies but they're not dying right now."

Thirty minutes of travel. The bell stopped.

That meant either the situation resolved, or the person ringing it ran out of Flux, or they collapsed, or they died.

Merra didn't slow down. They continued.

Fifteen minutes later they found the source.

A solo traveler — young, mid-twenties, T2 by the look of their gear — sitting propped against a rock with a tourniquet on their left leg and a bell-casting [[Grimoires|grimoire]] open beside them. The grimoire pages showed burn marks where sustained emergency casting had damaged the sigil work.

The traveler was conscious. "Took you long enough."

Korven assessed the injury. Deep laceration, probable fauna attack, significant blood loss but tourniquet applied correctly. "You're alive. That means we got here in time."

"Barely."

"Barely counts." Korven opened the medical kit and began cleaning the wound. "What happened?"

"[[Bloodshrike]]. Came out of nowhere, went for my leg. I burned Flux getting clear, hit it with a concussive spell, it retreated. I got the tourniquet on and started signaling."

"How long ago?"

"Two hours. Maybe three. I'm not sure."

Two hours of sustained bell-casting at T2 Flux capacity meant the traveler had burned nearly their entire reserve. The grimoire damage confirmed it — sustained emergency spellwork degraded the sigil matrix faster than normal casting.

Merra examined the traveler's gear. "You're solo."

"I know the risks."

"Apparently not well enough. Solo travelers die at three times the rate of expedition groups."

"I couldn't afford a convoy slot."

Merra didn't argue the point. Economics determined risk tolerance as much as judgment did. "You're lucky we were close. If we'd been another hour south you'd have bled out before we found you."

"I'm aware."

Korven finished cleaning the wound and began suturing. No anesthetic — medical supplies in Zakros prioritized function over comfort. The traveler absorbed the pain without complaint, which meant they'd done painful things before or they had enough self-control to not waste energy screaming.

The suturing took twenty minutes. Korven dressed the wound, checked the tourniquet, and monitored for shock indicators. The blood loss was significant but not catastrophic. With rest and proper care, the injury would heal. Without intervention, the traveler would have died within another hour.

Merra made the operational call. "You're coming back to the way station with us. You can't continue solo with that injury. We'll get you to [[Halveth]] when the next south-bound group comes through."

"I need to reach [[Auralis]]. I have a contract."

"Your contract is void if you're dead. You have a contract with us now — we saved your life, you owe us passage debt. You work it off or you pay it off, but you're coming with us."

The traveler's jaw tightened. Passage debt was legitimate under Zakros emergency convention — rescue wasn't free, especially when rescue consumed supplies and practitioner time. But it also wasn't exploitation. The debt would be calculated fairly based on documented costs.

"How much?" the traveler asked.

"We'll calculate it at the way station. Medical supplies, practitioner time, lost travel time. Probably three hundred Watts."

The traveler nodded slowly. "I can work it off. I'm a copyist. If you've got [[Grimoires|grimoire]] work, I'll do it."

"We have grimoire work. The way station always has grimoire work."

They helped the traveler stand. The leg held weight with support. Good enough to travel slowly.

The group returned to the way station, arriving ninety minutes after they'd left. The burial they'd interrupted was complete but abbreviated. Teln Vass was in the ground with proper marking but without full ceremony.

Korven logged the rescue in the way station record. **Emergency bell response, solo traveler, fauna injury, medical intervention, passage debt incurred, traveler remains at station for recovery.**

The traveler spent three days at the way station doing grimoire copying work — maintenance transcription on damaged expedition records, sigil repair preparation, inventory documentation. The work was legitimate and productive. Passage debt wasn't punishment, it was resource exchange. The traveler owed the expedition for the supplies and time spent on rescue. The expedition received useful labor in return.

On the fourth day, a south-bound group came through. The traveler negotiated passage with them, paying the remainder of their debt to Merra's expedition in coin. The groups exchanged documentation, updated their contracts, and continued in opposite directions.

Korven returned to the daily work of way station maintenance. The emergency bell sat in the equipment cache, ready for the next crisis. It would ring again. Someone would respond or they wouldn't. The desert would decide outcomes based on preparation, timing, and proximity to help.

Teln Vass had received abbreviated ceremony because someone alive needed help. That was the correct priority. The dead didn't object to shortened prayers. The living objected to dying when help was seventeen minutes away and no one came.

The bell rang for emergencies. People responded because someday they would be the one ringing it, hoping someone else was close enough to hear.
