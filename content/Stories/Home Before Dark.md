# Home Before Dark

[[Rett Soln]] led the survey party back to the [[Halveth]] staging point forty minutes before sunset, which meant they'd finish equipment breakdown and be inside the settlement proper before full dark. That was the standard. That was how it was supposed to work.

The survey had been routine: six hours north mapping [[Rift Incursions|rift activity]] patterns along the established safe corridors, no close encounters, no diversions, three data points collected that would feed into next season's route coordination. [[Mira Keld]] and [[Vren Soss]], both [[Control Tier|T2]], had handled documentation while Rett maintained watch.

"Clean run," Mira said, securing her [[Grimoires|grimoire]] satchel.

"Clean enough," Rett agreed. He didn't call runs clean until they were back inside settlement perimeter. Too many things could happen in the last forty minutes.

But forty minutes passed without incident. They crossed the staging threshold, confirmed return with the watch coordinator, and began breakdown.

---

Rett's equipment check took twenty minutes: primary grimoire inspected for binding integrity, backup grimoire confirmed accessible, water reserves measured against expected usage (matched within normal variance), [[Flux]] capacity assessed (depleted to seventy percent baseline, which was fine for day work). Everything within tolerance.

Mira and Vren completed their own checks. All equipment functional. No damage requiring immediate repair.

"Tomorrow's schedule?" Vren asked.

"Same route, different survey points. Departure at dawn unless coordination changes it."

"I'll be here."

The three of them split at the staging gate. Mira went south toward the residential quarter. Vren went east toward the Trade Guild lodging. Rett went north toward the small house he rented three blocks from the settlement perimeter — close enough to staging that emergency calls reached him quickly, far enough that he wasn't living on top of the work.

---

The house was empty. That was expected. Rett's household consisted of himself, an aging stove that worked when properly maintained, and a collection of expedition gear in various states of repair cycle.

He stored the day's equipment in the dedicated closet, separated items needing maintenance from items ready for immediate reuse, and made notes in his personal log about the grimoire binding that was showing early wear. Not urgent yet, but worth monitoring.

Dinner was simple: preserved vegetables, bread from yesterday's purchase, water boiled for safety even though Halveth's supply was generally reliable. He ate at the small table by the window and watched the settlement transition from day activity to evening routine.

Most people who lived in [[Halveth]] either worked directly in [[Equatorial Desert|desert]] coordination or supported the people who did. The settlement's economy was built on crossing facilitation, and that meant most households maintained similar schedules: early starts, equipment-dependent operations, return before dark when possible.

The street outside reflected that pattern. People coming home, securing gear, preparing for the next day. No emergencies visible. No unusual activity. Just the ordinary rhythm of a settlement where most residents had learned that survival depended on routine competence more than dramatic intervention.

Rett finished dinner, cleaned the dishes, and reviewed tomorrow's route plan. Same general corridor as today, slightly different survey points, adjusted for seasonal rift pattern shifts that the morning coordination briefing would detail.

Straightforward work. The kind of work that kept people alive because it was done correctly day after day until the accumulated data made route coordination accurate enough that most crossings succeeded.

Most. Not all. But the gap between most and all was where guides like Rett operated — closing margin, improving odds, reducing the percentage of expeditions that ended in casualty.

---

Night fell. Rett lit the single Flux-sustained lamp that provided evening light — low-tier casting, minimal drain, the kind of everyday [[Flux]] use that [[Control Tier|T3]] practitioners could maintain indefinitely if they managed it correctly.

He'd been doing this work for eleven years. Day surveys, route coordination, occasional guide contracts for actual crossings, training sessions for apprentices who thought they wanted desert work until they understood what desert work actually required.

The work wasn't dramatic. It was detailed, methodical, and built on the assumption that preventing problems was more valuable than solving them after they emerged.

Tomorrow he'd do another six-hour survey. Then another. Then another. Occasionally those surveys would identify pattern shifts that required route adjustments. Occasionally something unexpected would happen and he'd have to demonstrate the crisis-response skills that justified his [[Control Tier|T3]] status and eleven years of experience.

But most days were like today: out at dawn, work through afternoon, home before dark, prepare for tomorrow.

That was the job. That was what it looked like when it worked correctly.

Rett extinguished the lamp and settled into the evening routine. Morning would arrive early. The desert would still be there. The routes would still need surveying.

And he'd still be one of the people who went out every day to make sure that when someone crossed, they crossed with the most accurate information available.

That was enough.
