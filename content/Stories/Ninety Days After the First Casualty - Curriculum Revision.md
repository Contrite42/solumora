# Ninety Days After the First Casualty - Curriculum Revision

Registrar Iven Corsk never expected to rewrite tier education standards in a year when he still had roof leaks in the west classroom.

Yet on day ninety after the first casualty, he stands in front of a chalkboard at the district educators' council, crossing out one sentence every school in the region has taught for thirty years:

**"Tier progression defines practical relevance in crisis."**

He replaces it with:

**"Crisis response requires mixed competencies: logistics, interpretation, restraint, and power."**

No one argues the wording. They argue the implication.

"You're teaching children that a Tier 0 can outrank a Tier 6 in a live emergency," one senior instructor says.

Iven gestures toward the printed case packet on every desk: Ashford standstill logs, stabilization sequence notes, and public transcripts from the Fifth Bell declaration.

"I'm teaching children what kept us alive this season," he says.

The revised curriculum adds three mandatory modules for upper-level civic education:

- **Structural Risk Literacy:** how institutional systems fail and why early warning matters;
- **Operational Ethics Under Uncertainty:** when legal authority and survival timelines conflict;
- **Cross-Tier Coordination:** role discipline in mixed-capacity response teams.

For the first time, lesson plans cite [[Jess Maren]] and [[Merra Veld]] alongside high-tier theorists.

Parents split fast. Some call the change practical and overdue. Others call it anti-merit propaganda aimed at humiliating high-tier households. Auralis commentators accuse Terravelle districts of political pedagogy under crisis cover. Terravelle commentators answer that facts are not anti-magic.

Iven has no taste for ideology fights. He has attendance sheets and staffing ratios.

What he does know is that children absorbed the old sentence too well. They learned to confuse output with judgment, rank with responsibility, theory with field survival.

He watched adults do the same.

In week one after publication, a twelve-year-old asks him whether the High Demon in the transcript is "the strongest person in the room."

Iven answers, "Strongest at one thing. Not strongest at every thing."

The student nods and writes it down.

By evening, the district board approves the revisions 8-3 with emergency implementation language. Textbook updates will take months; teachers start with stapled packets and copied diagrams.

Iven returns to his office, sets the vote record beside his old attendance logs, and marks the day in his notebook:

**Policy changed before memory faded.**

In Solumora, that counts as rare luck.
