# A Cost That Makes Sense

The first mistake Lera made that morning was small enough to fit between two breaths.

She had been copying shape-field equations for four hours straight—a common grimoire utility spell that paid poorly but was intellectually neutral enough that she could work on autopilot while her mind wandered to her mother's letter, unopened on her desk at home. Three weeks without contact, and now this. Lera's hands moved steadily through the familiar sigils, muscle memory carrying her through curves and brackets.

She copied one line of the equation, skipped two, then continued cleanly down the page as if nothing had happened. The ink looked beautiful. The sigils were perfectly centered. The margins were exact. Anyone looking at the copy would think she had done everything right.

The client was a practical carpenter from [[Life in Valdenmoor|lower Valdenmoor]], a woman with calloused hands who paid on time and asked no philosophical questions. Lera had apprenticed under Ossam for three years and had learned that clients like this were the spine of the trade: not glamorous, not famous, but reliable.

She delivered the pages on schedule.

Three days later, the client came back.

She didn't look angry. Worse—she looked puzzled.

"The cast won't work right," she said, spreading the page across Ossam's desk with a kind of resigned frustration. "I tested it this way and that way. Cost jumped from what you said to almost double."

Ossam, who had been reading inventory manifests, set them aside without hurrying. He was old enough to have seen every variety of copying mishap, and young scribes always misread something about his patience. They thought it meant he was kind. It actually meant he was interested.

He examined the page in silence for a long moment.

"Thank you," he told the client. "You caught something important. I'll handle correction."

The client left, paid double for the time wasted, and promised to provide more business for other jobs. Ossam waited until the door closed before he turned to Lera.

By noon, the failed page sat on Ossam's desk in [[Valdenmoor]], weighted at the corners with brass clips as if it might run away.

Ossam did not raise his voice. He never did.

"Walk me through it," he said.

Lera's throat felt narrow. She had been copying for five years, and in all that time, her work had been near-invisible to anyone but other scribes. The thought that a mistake of hers actually changed the shape of someone's life—made a spell cost double—made her feel slightly sick.

"I checked source alignment," she said, her voice smaller than she meant. "I verified line breaks. I copied the first segment. Then..." She touched the page where the transition crossed from one sheet to the next. "Then I looked up and went forward. I was thinking about something else."

"What were you thinking about?"

"My mother wrote. I hadn't heard—" She stopped. "That's not relevant."

"It's actually very relevant," Ossam said. "You were tired. Your eye took the jump. Standard fatigue error."

Lera waited for it: the punishment every scribe feared. Docked pay. Suspension from the desk. Maybe reassignment to inventory scraping where no one trusted her with live text. It was the natural consequence. It was fair.

Instead, Ossam reached into a side drawer and withdrew a small slate and chalk.

He slid both across to her.

"Go calculate the world where this is correct."

She frowned. "Master?"

"You wrote a different spell. So tell me what that spell actually does in the hands of a real [[Flux Users|practitioner]]. Go on. The library is yours. Take as long as you need."

---

She spent the afternoon with a chalk board, two reference grimoires, and a headache that felt like a nail being driven between her eyes.

At the base cost of 18 [[Watts]], the spell lived in T1 territory: repeatable, safe, useful for workshops and farm sheds. Any competent practitioner could cast it without special equipment. A carpenter could commission one copy and use it for years.

At 42 Watts—the cost the transformed version demanded—the territory changed entirely. Now you were in T2 planning: larger Flux reservoirs, stricter sequencing, higher risk if cast during fatigue or physical stress. The caster would need specific training. The equipment would need certification. Mistakes would not be forgiven.

It was no longer a convenience. It was a commitment.

The more Lera worked, the less the page looked like failure and the more it looked like branching paths.

In one world—the world she intended—a small workshop buys six copies and keeps rain off their roof all season. A carpenter works without fear. A child stays dry during storms.

In another world—the one her mistake created—a caravan foreman commissions two expensive castings to stabilize a temporary depot in hostile weather. A T2 practitioner accepts the contract and pushes themselves toward exhaustion. Someone lives because of that expensive casting. Or someone dies because a tired caster made an error with expensive magic.

Same sigils. Different lives.

When Ossam returned at dusk, she had covered three slates with numbers. Her handwriting was shaky with the weight of what she'd calculated.

"Well?" he asked.

"I didn't just make it wrong," she said quietly. "I changed who could use it. I changed who could afford to be helped by it. I moved it from 'basic utility' to 'luxury high-risk work.' The same person who could commission six of the real ones can't even get one copy of this version. But a caravan with a real Flux budget could justify it."

Ossam nodded once and sat down across from her.

"Now you understand what we actually copy," he said. "And why it matters that you pay attention."

He didn't say "I forgive you." He didn't need to. The entire lesson was the forgiveness.

---

Three days later, Lera brought him a new question during a quiet morning when the shop was empty of clients.

"Could someone order the 42-Watt version on purpose?"

"Yes."

"Would we sell it?"

"If they spec it honestly, and if safety notes are explicit, yes."

She sat with that answer for a long moment.

"So my mistake is..."

"A bad commission," Ossam said, leaning back in his chair. The wood creaked. After forty years of scrivening, he had a scholar's curve to his spine. "Not nonsense. Not chaos. A bad commission filed under a good one. The difference between an accident and a crime is documentation."

That answer changed her more than the correction itself.

Over the next weeks, Lera started reading every spell like a contract between people: what was it asking, who could pay, who could survive using it, who got locked out by one changed number. She began noticing things her older colleagues barely acknowledged—the way a tiny variation in a healing formula changed whether it could be purchased by a settlement healer or only by a wealthy merchant, the way cost escalation naturally created a ladder where some people couldn't climb high enough to access safety.

Her error rate dropped to nearly zero over the next quarter, but not because she became afraid of making mistakes.

Because she became interested in what mistakes meant.

---

Five years later, when Lera had her own small shop and three apprentices of her own, she kept the failed page in a thin cedar frame above her station. The page was yellowed, the brass clips had tarnished to green, and new scribes assumed it was a warning.

It was, but not in the way they thought.

When a nervous apprentice asked why she kept a visible mistake on display, she said:

"This is what happens when you forget that [[Spells]] are not decoration. They are specifications. Every line moves money, risk, and possibility through someone's life. If you miscopy, you do not create nothing. You create a different world and hand it to someone who trusts you."

Then she handed them chalk and a slate.

"Now. Show me the world where your mistake is correct. I want to see what you actually made."

The apprentices always hated that task. It forced them to understand that mistakes were not abstract failures—they were alternate futures, some of which were kinder and some of which were not.

But every scribe who learned under her method never forgot to double-check a seam again.
