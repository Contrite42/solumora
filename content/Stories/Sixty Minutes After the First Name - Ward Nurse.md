# Sixty Minutes After the First Name - Ward Nurse

Lessa Orrn learned the news from a child asking the wrong question.

"Is it true the border is closed now?"

The girl stood in clinic line three with a fever token and a cracked water flask. She was maybe ten, old enough to repeat what adults whispered, too young to know which whisper mattered.

Lessa checked the board. No closure notice.

No transport freeze.

No emergency class shift.

Still, she felt the room tighten.

Rooms always tightened before official text arrived.

---

At minute nineteen, the clinic received a caution packet from district logistics:

"Possible border casualty event. Expect rumor surge. Preserve routine where possible."

Preserve routine where possible was institutional language for hold the walls while nobody above you agrees what happened.

Lessa moved to triage desk and changed nothing on paper.

Then she changed everything in practice.

She pulled one nurse from non-urgent checks to queue management. She told intake to separate panic from symptoms. She asked kitchen support for an extra tea boiler because frightened people stood longer and fought less when their hands were warm.

---

At minute thirty-seven, two parents arrived arguing over transport permits.

One said they needed to leave [[Ashford]] before roads locked.

The other said no roads were locked and leaving would make things worse.

Both were right enough to be dangerous.

Lessa sat them down beside the vitals shelf and spoke in the same tone she used for blood-loss families.

"No closure notice yet. If that changes, we process your paperwork here. Until then, your child gets seen first, and fear waits its turn."

They stopped arguing because someone gave them instructions that sounded like a path.

---

At minute fifty-three, official notice finally reached the ward:

"One confirmed casualty near Marker C. Cause under review."

One line. No names.

The ward went quiet in the way places do when everyone starts counting who they know on the road.

Lessa pinned the notice beside the ration schedule and wrote underneath it:

"Clinic operations normal. Bring children by sequence number."

Then she walked back to line three and called the next patient.

A world did not break all at once.

It frayed by interruptions, missed doses, skipped meals, and people deciding rules no longer applied.

Her work, for this hour, was to keep one room from fraying.
