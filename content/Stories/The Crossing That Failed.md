# The Crossing That Failed

The group had been planning the [[Equatorial Desert|Zakros]] crossing for eight months. Three [[Control Tier|T4]] practitioners with substantial reserves. Two T3 support specialists. Seventeen sealed containers of provisions designed to withstand heat and Flux interference. A route prepared by a crossing guide who had been through seven times before.

Everything was prepared. Everything was correct. The [[Standard Guided Crossing|guide]] was experienced. The practitioners' cost estimates were conservative. The timeline built in recovery buffers. They had done everything a crossing expedition is supposed to do.

The [[Rift Incursions|rift incursion]] started on day six. Not a large one. Not even unusual by Zakros standards. The incursion zone was three kilometers to the east. The group's planned route bisected the danger radius, but the calculations said they could move through the edge of the zone  faster than the rift was expanding.

The lead T4 practitioner, who had been running point on environmental sensing, began unraveling on day seven. Not all at once. First, confusion about whether they had marked waypoints correctly. Then hypervigilance about the team's bearing. Then attempts to recalculate Flux reserve estimates mid-journey, which broke concentration and began draining resources faster than planned.

By day nine, the practitioner couldn't sleep, couldn't stop casting sensing spells redundantly, couldn't articulate why the sense of immediate threat felt so overwhelming. The team made emergency camp and waited for clarity that didn't come. The practitioner was T4, had been through crossings before. This was not normal.

The guide said, quietly, to the senior coordinator: "Rift contamination. It happens."

"Contamination" is the technical term for what happens when a practitioner's Flux channel is partially intersected by Rift dimensions. The symptoms include heightened anxiety, rapid cognitive degradation, and catastrophic judgment failure. The condition is not immediately lethal. It is lethal in the specific context of being 8 days into a desert crossing with no option to retreat quickly.

The team voted. Majority to push forward slowly. One dissent from the practitioner who was contaminated — they wanted to retreat, which meant moving backward through the zone they had barely passed. That vote didn't carry weight. Contaminated practitioners don't get good votes.

On day ten, they found the first body. A practitioner from a different crossing attempt, probably three weeks old based on Zakros decomposition patterns. The team marked the location and continued. The guide said, "Not our concern," which was correct procedure but didn't make the moment feel any less like looking at their own future.

On day twelve, five kilometers from the desert edge and back toward settlement, the contaminated practitioner walked into a rift that had not been there twenty minutes before. The team watched a person they knew, liked, had been traveling with for two weeks simply stop existing. Rift incursions don't leave bodies behind.

The remaining four practitioners and the guide emerged from Zakros nine days after entering it. They had Watts left, which was technically success — they had miscalculated nothing critical enough to die from. They had succeeded at crossing.

The team filed incident reports. They noted rift zone activity, the exposure window, the team composition decisions now understood as mistakes. They noted that crossing guide experience and practitioner competence and conservative resource management did not prevent one contingency: a rift that intersects a Flux channel, contaminating a mind, destroying judgment at exactly the moment judgment mattered most.

The question the team never answered, because there is no answer: how many crossings fail silently, in ways that become unrecoverable during the crossing itself, before anyone thinks to call them failures? The contaminated practitioner was identified in the report. The team knew what happened. The next team that encounters one moment of cascade failure, where the first error creates conditions for the second error, creating conditions for the third to become fatal — nobody will ever fully know what happened to them.

The guide did not take another crossing contract for eight months.
