# Ninety Days After the First Casualty - Corridor Market Morning

At first light, the Ashford corridor market reopens lane C for mixed-kingdom vendors under JRMC clearance conditions.

It is a small thing by war standards: twelve stalls, two inspection tables, one shared notice board, and a queue line painted by hand because no one wanted to wait for procurement.

By ordinary-life standards, it is enormous.

Merra arrives for supply pickup and finds the line already split in familiar ways. Terravelle families near the grain stall. Auralis craft traders near the hardware carts. Children drifting between both because children ignore maps adults enforce.

Above the lane entrance hangs the new sign:

**ACTIVE UNDER CEASEFIRE TECHNICAL CONDITIONS - COMPLY WITH RED-BAND NOTICES**

Jess stands near the board with two clerks, translating JRMC risk bulletin language into market closure windows people can actually use.

"Green until second bell, amber after due to corridor maintenance cycle," she says to a baker who only wants to know whether flour shipment will arrive before noon.

"So... yes?" the baker asks.

"Yes, if your driver reads signs."

Kess is three stalls down explaining to a metalsmith why lane D remains closed.

"Because the ground there is not metaphorically unstable," she says. "It is physically unstable."

He nods, unconvinced but compliant.

Near the center table, a boy no older than ten asks his mother whether the "bookstore demon" will be here today. The mother says maybe, and that if he is, the boy will keep both hands to himself and use respectful language.

Verath does not appear.

His absence is almost as important as his presence was. The market runs because institutions and civilians now possess procedures that do not require a Tier 9 entity to stand in every lane.

At second bell, one courier arrives late with a sealed packet from Auralis courts: provisional hearing language ratified with minor amendments. Jess pins the summary to the board next to shipment times and weather warnings.

No one in line reads it immediately. They read prices first.

That, Merra thinks, is the real indicator.

Panic economies read proclamations before bread.
Recovering economies read bread first and policy second.

By midday, lane C has processed forty-two transactions without incident and one argument over inspection priority resolved by the same queue protocol both sides cursed three months ago.

Merra shoulders her supplies and watches children chalking hop marks along the curb beside red-band warning symbols they have learned not to cross.

This is not peace.
It is governance learning new reflexes while old instincts still twitch.

For now, that is enough to call the morning a success.
