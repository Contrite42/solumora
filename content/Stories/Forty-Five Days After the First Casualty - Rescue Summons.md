# Forty-Five Days After the First Casualty - Rescue Summons

Merra Veld receives three requests before sunrise and rejects all three by habit. One asks her to escort a private Auralis family convoy south through a route she marked "military-active" last week. One asks for a body recovery beyond the still-hot edge of the Kellvost quarantine where no recovery crew can stand for more than forty minutes. One asks for the impossible: "quiet extraction" of a Council courier who knows too much.

She sets those requests into the metal tray on the left side of her desk, the one labeled **No** in charcoal. Then she opens the fourth packet.

No seal. No signature. Handwritten in tight, practical script.

"If both kingdoms mobilize at current pace, dimensional instability reaches cascade threshold in 38-52 days. You have documented this pattern in expedition failures. I have documentation that confirms it. Bring someone who understands tonnage and someone who can read old lies. Ashford, dry warehouse nine, second bell after dusk. Come alone if possible."

The note includes one detail no outsider should know: the Messon collapse route deviation she logged twelve years ago and never published.

Merra reads it twice, then walks to the back room where she keeps old ledgers and an emergency water map pinned with rusting nails. Her first instinct is to burn the note and keep doing what she has always done: rescue who she can, bury who she cannot, avoid people who speak in thresholds and destinies.

Her second instinct wins.

She takes the note to Jess Maren.

Jess is already awake, sitting at a warehouse table under bad light with two ledgers open and a third balanced on her knee. She does not look up when Merra enters.

"If this is another council memo asking me to make thirty carts do the work of fifty, they can eat paper," Jess says.

Merra slides the note across. Jess reads it once, then flips it over, checks the paper grain, and holds it near the lamp.

"Whoever wrote this had archive stock," Jess says. "Not market paper. And they know the same numbers I do."

"You trust it?"

"I trust math. I trust your face right now. I do not trust anonymous invitations in dry warehouses, but that's a separate category."

Merra nods. "Need a scholar. Someone who reads the old inscription material without lying for the Council."

Jess snorts. "There's one. Which is why everyone in Auralis hates her."

They spend the afternoon assembling a cover story no one will believe but everyone will pretend to accept: Merra is evaluating route viability for displaced families; Jess is auditing warehouse throughput; both need a ruins specialist to validate structural safety at old storage sites. It is transparent fiction, but transparent fictions are the only ones institutions permit during panic.

At dusk, Kess Tarren arrives at Ashford with one satchel, three annotated site maps, and no patience.

"If this is another committee that wants me to say the collapse is unlikely," she says before sitting down, "you can save the tea."

Merra places the note between them.

Kess reads, freezes at the Messon reference, then traces the final line with her thumb as if testing whether ink can lie by texture.

"This is not Council language," Kess says quietly. "And it is not wrong."

Jess taps the timetable she has already started sketching. "If the number is real, we are late."

Merra folds the note and pockets it. "Then we stop waiting for official permission."

Outside, Ashford's second bell rings through warehouse steel and river fog. Inside, three people who have never sat at one table before begin planning as if no one else is coming to help.

Because no one else is.
