# The Third Room at Milepost Nine

The way station at Milepost Nine had three rooms.

The first room was public space: equipment storage, supply inventory, water cistern, cooking area, common sleeping platforms for expeditions passing through. Standard infrastructure for Zakros crossing support maintained by the [[Halveth Cooperative|Cooperative]] and funded through transit fees.

The second room was records: expedition logs, supply manifests, maintenance schedules, casualty documentation, [[Grimoires|grimoire]] repair workspace. The paper records were stored in sealed cases rated for desert heat and protected by preservation sigils that [[Control Tier|T3]] practitioners renewed quarterly.

The third room was where people went when they weren't going to make it.

---

Selt Morna knew the third room well. She'd been managing Milepost Nine for six years, which meant she'd seen forty-three crossings use the station, logged thirty-one casualties, and supervised nineteen uses of the third room.

Not everyone who entered the third room died there. Twelve people had recovered enough to continue their crossing. Seven had died despite every intervention available. Those numbers — twelve survived, seven didn't — summarized the third room's purpose: it was a place where people who were failing had one final chance to not-fail before the failure became permanent.

Today, the third room held a [[Control Tier|T2]] practitioner named Vorn Kess who had [[Rift Incursions|rift]]-contamination sickness and maybe two days left to live without intervention.

Selt checked on Vorn every three hours. The contamination was progressing predictably: elevated temperature, Flux-circulation disruption, increasing disorientation. Standard progression. Vorn was at the midpoint — stable enough to survive short-term, degraded enough that continuation without treatment would lead to cascade failure.

The expedition Vorn had been traveling with was six days ahead, unaware one of their members had fallen behind. That happened. Zakros expeditions moved at the speed of their slowest member until the slowest member couldn't keep up, and then the expedition faced a choice: slow down and risk everyone, or leave the failing member behind with supplies and hope.

Vorn had been left with hope. The hope was running out.

---

**Day Two.** Selt administered the standard rift-contamination treatment protocol: Flux-stabilization mixture (herbal preparation that didn't cure contamination but slowed progression), enforced rest (letting the body's natural Flux regulation attempt recovery), temperature management (keeping fever from becoming brain damage), hydration maintenance (contamination dehydrated people faster than normal desert exposure).

None of it was curative. All of it bought time.

Vorn remained coherent through the morning, became confused by midday, and spent the evening semi-lucid. That was standard progression. The contamination was disrupting their Flux circulation, which disrupted their nervous system function, which would eventually disrupt functions essential to staying alive.

The treatment protocol could extend survival by three to five days. It couldn't reverse contamination. That required either high-tier intervention (T5+ practitioner with specialized contamination-reversal training, which Milepost Nine didn't have) or slow natural recovery (body purges contamination over weeks or months, which only happened if the contamination level stayed below the threshold where cascade failure started).

Vorn's contamination level was borderline. They might recover naturally. They might not. The probability was somewhere around 40-60%, which meant Selt couldn't predict the outcome but could provide conditions that maximized the 40% chance.

---

**Day Four.** Vorn's fever broke.

That was the decision point. Fever breaking meant either the body had started winning the fight against contamination (good outcome track) or the contamination had progressed past the stage where fever was the primary symptom (bad outcome track).

Selt monitored Vorn's Flux circulation using a diagnostic grimoire. The readings showed... stabilization. Not recovery, but stabilization. The contamination wasn't getting worse.

That was the best outcome available without high-tier intervention. Vorn would spend another week at Milepost Nine recovering before they could travel. The expedition they'd been part of was gone. Vorn would need to join a return-direction group or wait for the next south-bound expedition with space for recovering personnel.

Selt logged the outcome in the third room record. **Rift contamination, day-four stabilization, recovery track probable, extended stay required.**

Vorn would survive. They would leave the third room, spend time in the first room recovering, and eventually continue their journey. The third room would be cleaned, resupplied, and made ready for the next person who needed it.

---

**Day Seven.** A different expedition arrived at Milepost Nine. Twelve people, standard supplies, making good time. They paid the transit fee, resupplied water, and asked about route conditions ahead.

Selt provided current intelligence: no major rift activity reported in the past week, two expeditions had passed through going south without incident, water cache status at the next three mileposts was adequate.

The expedition prepared to continue. Then one of them asked, "You have someone in recovery here?"

"Yes. Rift contamination. Stabilized but not ready to travel yet."

"How long before they can move?"

"Another week, maybe two."

The practitioner considered. "We have space. If they can travel slow, we can take them south. We're not in a rush."

Selt checked with Vorn. They could travel with support. The expedition agreed to slow their pace and accepted Vorn into their group. The additional person meant slightly slower crossing time and shared supplies, but expeditions helped each other when they could because next time they might be the ones needing help.

Vorn left Milepost Nine with the new expedition. The third room was empty again.

---

**Day Nine.** Selt cleaned the third room, replaced the used medical supplies, and re-sealed the preservation sigils on the treatment protocols.

The room was four meters by three meters. It held one sleeping platform, one medical supply cache, one waste management system, one water reserve, one small grimoire library with treatment documentation. Everything necessary to keep someone alive while their body decided whether to recover or fail.

Nineteen people had used this room during Selt's tenure. Twelve survived, seven didn't. The room had a 63% survival rate, which was better than [[Equatorial Desert|Zakros]] exposure survival rate (approximately 40% for unassisted rift contamination), and worse than high-tier medical facility survival rate (approximately 85% with T5+ intervention).

The room wasn't a hospital. It was a place where people who were failing had adequate resources to attempt not-failing. Sometimes adequate resources were enough. Sometimes they weren't.

Selt filed the supply requisition for used materials and updated the station log. Next supply delivery would restock the medical cache. The third room would be ready for the next person who needed it.

That was the work. Maintaining the infrastructure that gave people chances. The chances succeeded or they didn't, but without the infrastructure the chances didn't exist at all.

Milepost Nine had three rooms. The first room kept people moving. The second room kept records accurate. The third room kept people alive long enough for their body to decide if survival was possible.

Sixty-three percent was better than nothing. Selt would maintain the room, stock the supplies, and provide the protocols. The desert would send people who needed them. Some would leave the third room recovered. Others wouldn't leave it at all.

That was Zakros. That was the work.
