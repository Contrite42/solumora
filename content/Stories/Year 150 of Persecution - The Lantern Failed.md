# Year 150 of Persecution - The Lantern Failed

The expedition leader crouched in the dry wash with maybe thirty soldiers looking to her for direction, and behind them the sound of chaos from what was supposed to be a simple sweep and capture.

The lantern formations had worked. They'd flushed two dozen refugees from the mountain cache sites and herded them into the designated valley. Clean operation. Textbook coordination between Auralis sweepers and Terravelle flank support.

Then the refugees didn't run separate. They moved together. Ran like coordinated unit rather than panicked individuals. Split into two groups moving toward separate ridge lines, one clearly heading for territory already swept (seeming suicidal until you understood the magistrate would claim success regardless), and the other moving toward border country where jurisdiction became ambiguous.

Now the commander was watching her best soldiers being picked apart by what had to be coordinated resistance from mountain communities. Not open combat. Nothing you could report as direct engagement. Just rocks from unexpected angles, disabled equipment, strategic terrain use that most refugees shouldn't have accessible knowledge of.

"They're trained," one of her officers said flat.

"They're desperate," she replied, but she was watching too. Desperation didn't create this kind of coordination. Desperation didn't map the terrain well enough to use it against professional soldiers. Desperation didn't include mounted riders—actual horses, which meant resources, which meant community backing.

"Do we push?" 

The commander closed her eyes for maybe three seconds. Thought about the report she'd have to file. Thought about the success metrics she was supposed to achieve this cycle. Thought about the fact that thirty of her soldiers were now scattered across mountain territory that would kill half of them just through exhaustion and altitude with no need for actual combat.

"We fall back to declared territory," she said. "Report successful sweep and relocation of suspected refugee population. Mark all resistance points as 'cleared during extended hunt operations.'"

"That's not what happened."

"That's what we'll report happened."

The officer held her gaze for one second too long, then nodded and relayed orders for retreat.

Walking back down the mountain felt like failure. Actually reporting it as success felt worse—like the institution understood that mountains and community protection and coordinated resistance made hunts into performance more than operation.

The refugees would survive this one. They survived most of them by now.

The question that would consume commanders for the next fifty years was whether this meant the institution should hunt harder or accept that hunting didn't work anymore.

Most answers came back: perform the hunts more visibly while accepting they achieved nothing.
