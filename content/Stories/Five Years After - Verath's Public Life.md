# Five Years After - Verath's Public Life

_Post-crisis account of what "normal" means for a High Demon who spent four centuries in hiding and then became publicly known institutional founder._

---

Teren Voss — Verath, he reminded himself for the hundredth time that week — reviewed the [[Joint Rift Monitoring Cell]] quarterly coordination report with practiced focus. Five years after [[The Three Near-Wars#The Kellvost Incursion|public revelation]], he still occasionally defaulted to his maintained identity out of four-hundred-year habit.

The coordination meeting included representatives from both [[Terravelle]] and [[Auralis]] oversight bodies, academic rift researchers, and field monitoring personnel. All of them knew what he was. Most of them had adapted to working alongside someone who'd lived longer than their kingdoms' recorded institutional histories.

Some still found it deeply uncomfortable.

"The eastern corridor rift activity shows seasonal intensification consistent with historical patterns," one Auralis researcher reported, carefully not making eye contact with Verath while presenting data. "Recommend maintaining current monitoring intensity through winter cycle."

Verath nodded. "Historical data from approximately two hundred years ago supports that assessment. Similar intensification occurred during comparable climate patterns. No intervention required unless activity exceeds documented variance thresholds."

The researcher absorbed this information with visible tension. Verath had learned to recognize the cognitive dissonance: intellectually accepting that High Demons existed and possessed valuable historical knowledge did not immediately resolve emotional discomfort about working alongside immortal beings.

He didn't fault the researchers. His existence challenged fundamental assumptions about mortality, power, and social structure. Five years was insufficient time for complete cultural adaptation.

---

After the coordination meeting, [[Jess Maren|Jess]] caught up with him in the corridor. "Still getting the nervous sideways looks?"

"Consistently," Verath confirmed. "Less than year one, more than pre-revelation baseline of complete invisibility."

"You miss the hiding?"

He considered carefully. This was complicated question. "I miss the simplicity. Four centuries of maintained identity provided predictable operational framework. Public existence removes that predictability."

"But?"

"But sustained hiding was isolation that felt increasingly untenable. I'd spent four hundred years documenting pattern recognition with no one to share observations with beyond four other immortals equally isolated. Public existence allows intellectual engagement andcontribution to institutional frameworks I'd only been able to observe passively before."

They walked through [[Ashford-Halveth Courier Road|Ashford's administrative quarter]] toward evening meal meeting point. Five years of coexistence governance meant regular coordination dinners with other revelation group members — partly logistical necessity, partly mutual support network for people processing unusual shared experience.

"You're getting recognized in public now," Jess noted. "I saw someone point at you and whisper to their companion."

"Frequently," Verath confirmed. "[[Public Status of High Demons|Legal protections]] prevent direct harassment, but social curiosity creates constant low-level awareness that I'm being observed and assessed."

"How does that compare to pre-revelation hypervigilance?"

"Different flavor, comparable intensity. Hiding required constant monitoring for exposure risks. Public existence requires constant monitoring for social boundary violations and threat assessment. The vigilance never actually stops — it just changed focus."

---

The evening coordination dinner included [[Merra Veld|Merra]], [[Kess Tarren|Kess]], and [[Cassia]], though Cassia sent regrets — Council session running late. The remaining four founders gathering had become informal tradition.

"Five-year retrospective assessment," Jess proposed while they waited for food. "What's better than expected, what's worse?"

Kess answered first: "Better: Frameworks actually working through first complete electoral cycle. Worse: Amount of ongoing institutional maintenance required. I'd hoped systems would become self-sustaining faster."

Merra: "Better: Desert crossing routes fully operational without military interference. Worse: Being recognized during routine rescue operations and asked to provide historical commentary while people are actively dying of dehydration."

Verath considered his response. "Better: Intellectual engagement with rift monitoring and historical documentation after four centuries of isolated observation. Worse: Persistent social discomfort from populations processing my public existence."

"You're the only one of us whose existence fundamentally challenges species assumptions," Merra noted. "We're founders who helped prevent war. You're immortal being who shouldn't exist according to prior cultural frameworks. The recognition burden is qualitatively different."

"Yes," Verath agreed. "And I cannot retreat from it. [[Persecution Era - The Voss Survival Line|My lineage]] spent two centuries in hiding after first revelation failed catastrophically. Second revelation required public commitment. I cannot rebuild maintained identity without undermining frameworks that depend on transparent High Demon participation."

"Do you regret that?" Kess asked.

"Regret public revelation? No. Regret that comfortable hiding is permanently unavailable? Yes. Both can be simultaneously true."

---

The dinner conversation shifted to operational updates — Merra's recent rescue extractions, Jess's logistics coordination challenges, Kess's ongoing ruins documentation work. For several hours, they weren't founders of coexistence paradigm. They were professionals discussing shop talk with colleagues who understood shared context.

Until serving staff recognized Verath and requested, very politely, whether he'd be willing to answer "just one question" about what four hundred years of life experience felt like.

He declined politely, explaining that reducing centuries of accumulated experience to dinner-conversation soundbite would be intellectually dishonest.

The server retreated apologetically. Merra shook her head. "Constant low-level intrusion."

"Constant," Verath confirmed. "But manageable. Pre-revelation existence was isolation. Post-revelation existence is exposure. Neither is comfortable. Both are survivable."

---

Three weeks later, Verath participated in his first formal academic lecture series about High Demon historical perspective on kingdom development. The lecture hall held two hundred attendees. Half appeared genuinely interested in historical content. Half appeared present primarily to observe High Demon in academic setting.

He presented documented observations from two centuries of cross-border relations development, persecution-era institutional patterns, and contemporary coexistence framework precedents. The content was professional, thoroughly sourced, and deliberately free of sensationalist "immortal perspective" framing.

The question period inevitably focused less on historical content and more on personal experience questions. "What does it feel like to know you'll outlive everyone here?" someone asked.

"Isolating," Verath answered honestly. "Generational human mortality means relationships continuously cycle through formation, depth, and loss. I've adapted to that pattern over centuries, but adaptation doesn't eliminate the underlying grief of outlasting the companionship."

"Do you resent humans for dying?"

"No. I resent time for making mortality inevitable. Humans don't choose death. Biological reality imposes it. Resentment seems misapplied."

The questions continued in that register— personal rather than historical, emotional rather than analytical. Verath had prepared for this. Public existence meant his lived experience became educational material whether he preferred that or not.

---

Six months after that lecture, [[Intergenerational Coexistence Curriculum|educational curriculum developers]] requested his participation in developing historical documentation for next-generation instruction. The explicit goal: Help future students understand that High Demons are people with comprehensible motivations rather than incomprehensible supernatural entities.

Verath agreed despite professional preference for rift monitoring over public education. The work mattered. Persecution-era violence emerged partly from cultural narratives treating High Demons as inhuman threats. Contemporary frameworks survived partly because populations gradually updated those narratives through direct exposure to High Demons operating as competent institutional contributors rather than lurking dangers.

If his public participation helped prevent persecution revival for future generations, exposure discomfort was operationally justified cost.

---

Five years after revelation, Verath had transitioned from four centuries of hiding to permanent public existence. The transition remained uncomfortable. He missed anonymous observation. He processed ongoing grief about losing anonymity that had defined his adult existence.

He also contributed meaningfully to rift monitoring frameworks, historical documentation, and coexistence governance maintenance that hiding had never permitted.

His assessment: Public existence was professionally rewarding and personally exhausting.

His role in founders framework: The visible proof that High Demons could function as institutional assets rather than existential threats.

His private perspective: Being historical example was burden he'd accepted as necessary rather than role he'd chosen as desired.

But four hundred years of pattern observation had taught him: Necessary work often mattered more than comfortable work.

Post-revelation life was necessary. He'd learned to survive discomfort centuries ago.

This was just new variety of survival requiring adaptation.
