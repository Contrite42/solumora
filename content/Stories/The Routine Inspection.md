# The Routine Inspection

Vren Kolst spread the harness across the workbench and ran his fingers along the reinforcement stitching. Third time this season the same customer — [[Tella Morn]], independent scout, [[Control Tier|T1]] — brought him gear showing this exact wear pattern. He'd fixed it twice. This time he was going to tell her why it kept happening.

The morning light through the workshop window hit the leather at the right angle to show the stress lines. Eight years doing this work had taught him what failure looked like before it arrived. The harness wasn't torn yet. It would be, probably two days into her next expedition if she kept loading it the way she was.

He marked the weak points with chalk and laid out the replacement sections. Good leather — not the cheapest available, but not luxury either. Tella paid on time and didn't negotiate. She'd started with bottom-tier gear three years ago and worked her way up to mid-quality equipment, which meant she was succeeding often enough to stay solvent but not so dramatically that she could afford waste.

The stitching repair would take three hours. The conversation about why she needed it would take five minutes and would cost him nothing except the risk that she'd take her business to someone who didn't ask questions.

---

**Ten days later.** Tella returned for the harness mid-afternoon, paid without examining the work, and moved to leave.

"Wait," Vren said.

She turned, one hand already on the door. "Problem?"

"Not with the repair." He gestured at the harness. "Third time. Same failure point. I need to know how you're loading it."

Her expression flattened. "It holds what I need it to hold."

"For how long?"

"Long enough."

Vren pulled a length of test strap from the hook beside the bench and handed it to her. "Show me."

She looked at the strap, then at him. "Why?"

"Because you're paying me to fix the same problem repeatedly and I'd rather fix the actual problem once."

Tella's jaw shifted slightly — not quite agreement, not quite irritation. She took the strap. "I carry a backup water container on the left side. Sixteen liters. Clipped here." She indicated the stress point he'd marked. "Terrain's rough south of [[Valdenmoor]]. Crossing streams, climbing over rockfall. The container swings."

"How much does it weigh when full?"

"Eighteen, nineteen kilograms."

Vren nodded slowly. "And you're clipping it to a load point designed for eight kilograms maximum."

"It's the only attachment point that keeps it accessible."

"It's the only attachment point you're using." He pulled the harness back toward the bench and pointed at the secondary reinforcement loops she'd been ignoring. "These distribute weight across the back panel. You run a stabilizer strap through here and here, the container doesn't swing, the load spreads, and the stitching doesn't shear."

Tella studied the harness. "I didn't know those were for that."

"Most people don't." He pulled a roll of stabilizer strap from the storage rack. "I'm going to add this. No charge. You're going to use it. Next time this harness comes back damaged, I'm going to assume you ignored what I just told you and I won't fix it."

She met his eyes. "That's direct."

"Third time's the teaching moment. Fourth time's your choice."

Tella took the modified harness, tested the new strap configuration, and slung it over her shoulder. "If this works, I'll tell people."

"If it works," Vren said, "you'll stop paying me to fix the same failure. I'll take the reduced revenue."

She left. Vren returned to the next repair in the queue — a rope harness with obvious overload damage, customer anonymous, no return address. He marked it for basic structural repair and moved on.

Two months later, a different scout came in with a harness identical to Tella's and specified that he wanted "the load distribution setup Morn's been using." Vren added the stabilizer straps, explained the attachment points, and charged for the strap and labor.

The scout paid and left. Vren updated his supply order for stabilizer strap. Teaching moments paid eventual dividends, but only if the lesson was accurate and the recipient stayed alive long enough to repeat it.

He returned to the workbench and picked up the next piece — a [[Grimoires|grimoire]] satchel with a failing closure sigil. The canvas was sound but the [[Sigils|sigil]] ink was degrading, which meant either the customer had bought cheap or someone had sold them fraudulent [[Control Tier|T2]]-grade ink. He'd know which after he tested it.

The workshop smelled like leather and metal polish. Afternoon light crossed the bench in the same diagonal it had that morning. The queue of repairs hadn't shortened. It wouldn't. That was the nature of the work. Equipment failed. People used it wrong, or they used it right under conditions it wasn't designed for, or they bought cheap because expensive wasn't available when they needed it.

Vren's job wasn't to prevent failure. It was to recognize the pattern before the failure became catastrophic and do something about it when a customer gave him the opportunity.

Some listened. Some didn't. The ones who did came back with referrals. The ones who didn't came back with the same failures until they either learned or stopped coming back at all.

He'd been doing this for eight years. He expected to keep doing it as long as people kept traveling into places that tested their equipment past the point of comfort. That meant he'd keep doing it for the rest of his working life.

The grimoire satchel closure sigil was degraded but not fraudulent — legitimate [[Control Tier|T2]] ink exposed to environmental conditions it wasn't rated for. Vren made a note for the customer about storage conditions and moved the satchel to the sigil-repair section of the queue.

The next item was a pack frame with a stress fracture. He marked it, measured the replacement rod, and began the work.

Outside, the city continued. Inside, the repairs continued. The work was ordinary. That's why it mattered.
