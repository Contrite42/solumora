# One Hundred Eighty Days After the First Casualty - Winter Convoy

Winter arrives with practical cruelty: shorter light, slower roads, narrower margins.

The first major cross-kingdom winter convoy under treaty conditions lines up before dawn at Ashford lane C. Food concentrates, lamp oil, insulation cloth, medical salts. Nothing dramatic. Everything necessary.

Merra leads route sequencing with two junior coordinators who were still students when Kellvost happened. Jess runs load timing against amber-window forecasts from the JRMC. Kess checks anchor drift updates and marks one lane as no-go until noon reinforcement.

Verath does not ride with the convoy.

He remains at the corridor edge advisory post with rotating ward teams, exactly where treaty protocol places non-human high-load intervention: available, constrained, auditable.

Halfway through loading, an Auralis driver and Terravelle inspector argue over manifest discrepancy on fuel barrels. Voices rise. Queue stalls. Old instincts sharpen.

Merra steps between them with the same tone she used in standstill week.
"Count, verify, adjust, move. Nobody wins a winter argument at a frozen dock."

Jess resolves the discrepancy in three minutes: duplicate entry from relay lag, no fraud, corrected manifest published immediately.

By second bell, the convoy rolls in staggered intervals through green-to-amber windows.

Children watch from the curb as marked carts move past red-band warnings that now function less like symbols of dread and more like ordinary hazard signs people know how to read.

At midday, snowfall starts. Lane visibility drops. One rear cart breaks axle pin near checkpoint four. Under old command culture, the delay would trigger blame and rumors.

Under current protocol, it triggers a repair subroutine.

Replacement pin dispatched, queue resequenced, no closure needed.

By dusk, 87 percent of planned winter loads reach scheduled depots on first cycle. The remaining 13 percent are delayed, not lost.

Merra logs completion status and closes the slate.

At the tea stand, Bren asks how bad it was.

"Winter bad," Merra says. "Not collapse bad."

Bren nods like that is all anyone should ask from a season like this.

The convoy's paperwork will read as routine logistics.

Routine is the point.

Six months ago, this corridor was a probable cascade trigger.
Tonight it is a place where people argue about manifests, fix broken pins, and deliver oil before night freeze.

That is what survival looks like after institutions finally learn to be boring on purpose.
