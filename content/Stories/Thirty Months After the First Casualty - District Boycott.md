# Thirty Months After the First Casualty - District Boycott

By week two of the filing boycott, the licensing queue in [[Ashford]] had collapsed into paper stacks and frustrated silence. No one had damaged offices. No one had threatened staff. People just stopped filing normal updates while still demanding services continue.

[[Jess Maren]] pinned the participation chart to the dock board where everyone could see it.

"Stage 2," she said to the gathered district coordinators. "Structured refusal. We are not in enforcement posture. We are in repair posture."

A guild registrar folded his arms. "Repair for what? They boycotted us, not the other way around."

"They boycotted because they believe we only listen when process breaks," Jess said. "So now process is broken. We can be offended later."

The [[Civic Noncooperation Recovery Ladder]] required a public acknowledgment session before any sanction step. By noon, the hall was full: fishery cooperatives, caravan schedulers, academy parents, transit clerks, and people who had no title except resident.

First speaker, a route accountant, said what many had been circling for days: "We file, you lose records, then tell us to trust corrections. We skip filing, suddenly everyone shows up with urgency. You taught us which lever works."

No one argued.

Jess opened the temporary continuity desk with a mixed team: Commission mediator, Tribunal records clerk, two local administrators selected by resident groups. Every case handled in open log format with resolution deadline posted publicly.

By end of day one:
- 67 frozen permit updates restored
- 24 school reporting errors corrected
- 11 grievance cases reopened with independent note-takers

Day two brought harder cases: a cooperative flagged for noncooperation while its filings sat unprocessed in an internal transfer queue; two families denied travel endorsements due to mismatched district codes after policy template updates.

"None of this is malicious," one clerk said, exhausted.

"Most institutional harm isn't," Jess answered. "It's still harm."

At sunset on day three, participation climbed above continuity floor but remained fragile. The boycott committee offered partial resumption under one condition: weekly dashboard publication showing unresolved-case age and agency owner.

Old reflexes in the room pushed back immediately. Too much transparency, too much exposure, too much chance for political theater.

Jess signed the dashboard order anyway.

"If we're right, the numbers will show it," she said. "If we're wrong, the numbers should show that too."

By day nine, full filing resumed in three districts and partial filing in two. Not because trust had returned, but because residents believed refusal no longer had to be the only language institutions heard.

As teams packed down the temporary desks, a dock foreman handed Jess a folded note with no signature.

_You keep calling this repair. Good. Keep calling it that after election season._

Jess slid the note into her field binder beside the Ladder checklist.

Boycotts were quieter than riots and slower than hearings. They also told the truth about legitimacy faster than most official metrics.
