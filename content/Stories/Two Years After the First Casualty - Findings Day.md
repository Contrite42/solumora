# Two Years After the First Casualty - Findings Day

The hearing chamber in [[Auralis Civic Courts]] filled before dawn. By rule, the [[Coexistence Enforcement Failure Review Board]] published findings in public session with full evidentiary annex available same-day. By custom, people arrived early enough to test whether "public" meant real access this time.

[[Merra Veld]] stood with the Commission delegation near the rear aisle, not at the front table. [[Kess Tarren]] sat at counsel position for Tribunal response. [[Cassia]] attended without ceremonial guard formation. [[Teren Voss|Verath]] remained in gallery seating, present but silent.

At first bell, Board Chair Liren Oss opened the docket.

"Case EF-2A, Aldrane Sweep Incident," she said. "Thresholds met for scope, legitimacy failure, and cross-district destabilization. Findings are binding under Board charter sections three and five."

No preamble. No rhetorical softening.

The chronology took forty minutes. Every expansion order, every signature gap, every channel message where staff interpreted pending authorization as granted authority. The Board projected the sequence in plain timelines, then overlaid false-positive rates that climbed with each expansion wave.

By the third chart, the room had gone quiet in the way institutions fear most: not outrage, just recognition.

Finding One: dynamic expansion controls were treated as advisory in practice despite mandatory language in Tribunal protocol.

Finding Two: district command pressure reporting rewarded action volume over verification quality.

Finding Three: correction pathways existed but were staffed below surge minimums, converting legal reversal into social delay.

Finding Four: public brief language obscured known errors during first twelve hours, intensifying trust collapse.

Then came directives.

"Directive A," Chair Oss read. "Mass dynamic expansion authority suspended pending dual-signature hard gate implementation and external observer confirmation. Effective immediately."

"Directive B. Emergency Reintegration deployment mandatory for any incident where corrected actions exceed twenty-five percent within forty-eight hours."

"Directive C. False-positive index published monthly by district, with remediation lag metrics. No redactions except active witness safety exceptions."

"Directive D. Leadership retraining and recertification for implicated command staff before return to field authority."

Kess took notes without expression. Merra watched the gallery instead: students, dock workers, district clerks, survivors from persecution-lineage communities, operators from both kingdoms. No applause. No visible triumph. Just people checking whether consequences were real.

At noon, response window opened.

Kess stood first. "Tribunal accepts findings and directives in full. Implementation schedule submitted with no reservation language."

It mattered that she did not defend the institution before accepting correction.

Cassia followed. "Crown office will publish compliance tracking independent of district brief channels. This framework survives only if accountability is boringly routine, not exceptional theater."

A student delegate from [[Aldrane Academy]] asked from public microphone: "How do we know this isn't one correction before everything snaps back?"

Merra requested speaking time.

"You don't know yet," she said. "You verify over time. You read monthly index reports. You track remediation lag. You check whether districts that fail lose authority until they earn it back. Trust isn't restored by today's words; it's restored by whether next quarter's numbers prove these directives are enforced even when attention moves on."

The student nodded slowly, dissatisfied but not dismissive.

When Verath finally spoke, it was from gallery microphone, voice carrying without amplification strain.

"Two centuries of [[The Persecution Era|persecution]] taught one lesson clearly," he said. "Unreviewable authority always finds reasons to expand itself. Today matters because your institutions just accepted review they cannot control. Keep that feature. Lose it, and everything else is temporary."

By late afternoon, the Board published full annex files and machine-readable audit tables. Reporters, guild delegates, and neighborhood coordinators copied them before leaving the chamber. The process felt administrative, not dramatic.

That was the point.

On Findings Day, coexistence did not prove it was morally complete. It proved something narrower and maybe more important: when the framework failed publicly, it could also correct publicly, with enough force to change operator behavior before the next crisis.

For year two, that counted as durability.
