# Seven Days After the First Casualty - School Registrar

Iven Corsk spent the first week after the casualty notices rewriting attendance codes.

Not because the school had new students.

Because old reasons for absence no longer fit.

In the registrar book, there had been clean categories:

illness, labor duty, weather, household emergency.

By day seven, he added three temporary marks:

road delay,

permit hold,

caretaker redeployment.

Half his job was now naming disruptions accurately enough that future arguments would have less room to lie.

---

Morning assembly at the valley school was shorter than usual.

Teachers had stopped giving long civic lectures because children repeated fragments at home and fragments grew teeth.

Instead, Iven read one practical bulletin:

Routes open with delay.

Classes continue.

Midday meal service protected.

If pickup fails, children remain on site under roster supervision.

No one applauded. Parents nodded once and kept standing by the gate two minutes longer than before.

---

By noon, three households requested provisional transfer papers to relatives in safer districts.

Iven approved two and held one for missing guardian signatures.

The holding decision would make him unpopular by evening.

He made it anyway.

In fast-crisis weeks, bad paperwork became permanent family damage.

The school could survive angry parents.

It could not survive disappearing children.

---

At 15:30, a teacher reported rising hallway fights tied to border rumors.

Iven coordinated with the meal coordinator and moved dismissal ten minutes earlier to reduce crowd compression at the lane exit.

Small change, measurable effect: fewer bodies, fewer collisions, fewer accusations that "their side" started it.

He logged the adjustment and sent copy to district education office with a second note to [[Care Triage Workflows]] contacts in case meal demand rose.

---

When the building emptied, Iven archived the day sheet under a new folder title:

"Week One - Continuity Under Security Shock."

He disliked the phrase.

It sounded like policy writing.

It was still true.

Schools in a war-edge week were not only for learning.

They were daytime shelter networks with pencils.
