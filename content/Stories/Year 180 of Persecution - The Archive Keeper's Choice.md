# Year 180 of Persecution - The Archive Keeper's Choice

The High Demon called himself Kale in the administrative records, which was close enough to the old name that [[Teren Voss|he]] could recognize himself in documents without claiming it openly.

For sixty years, his primary role had been maintaining the city archive where [[Auralis]]'s institutional records lived. Births, deaths, property transfers, legal proceedings, everything that proved society's continuity.

The problem was the Lantern Decrees. Every legal persecution action was now a record. Every family marked as suspected sympathizers created paper. Every refugee was documented and then had that documentation destroyed by magistrate order, leaving contradictory records of people who simultaneously existed and didn't exist.

The archive had become a monument to institutional incoherence.

Year 180, a young clerk asked for guidance on filing contradiction: a family marked as sympathizers had their records destroyed by Decree, but the property registry still showed their holdings purchased legitimately five years before destruction order. How was he supposed to file actual property transfer when the family's legal existence had been administratively erased?

Kale understood the trap. The institution was generating so many contradictions that honest record-keeping was impossible. You either lied by commission (falsifying records) or lied by omission (destroying contradictory information).

Most archivists chose omission. They destroyed uncomfortable records. The contradiction dissolved.

Kale made different choice.

He began creating detailed duplicate records. Shadow archives, hidden in archive walls, in locked books that appeared ordinary to inspection. Documented everything: every destroyed record, every false prosecution, every family that got administratively erased.

It took twenty years to build the complete parallel. He created cross-indexing systems, chronologies, descriptions of what had been erased and why.

By year 200, when persecution formally ended, Kale made his choice known. Revealed the shadow archives to the next magistrate administration.

Offered the records to the future with simple statement: "If we erase what we did, we ensure we repeat it."

The records were sealed, preserved, and eventually became the foundation for what historians now call the Ashford Witness Amnesty documentation.

Kale retired after that, disappeared from administrative position in a way that suggested he was more than human even then. No one asked directly. Institutional benefit of doubt: maybe he'd simply aged out. Maybe he'd left the city.

He'd done what survival sometimes required: stayed visible long enough to be useful, then faded before questions became dangerous.
