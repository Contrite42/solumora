# Forty-Five Days After the First Casualty - Dry Warehouse Nine

The warehouse smells like old grain and rain-damp rope. Merra arrives first, checks three exits, and chooses the table nearest the side door. Jess arrives second with ledgers under one arm and a tin cup in the other. Kess arrives third carrying maps and the posture of someone who expects to be lied to.

No one arrives fourth.

They wait through second bell. Through third.

"We got played," Jess says.

Kess unfolds her maps anyway. "Then let's steal what we can from the setup."

Merra spreads a district chart across the table and marks recent rift incidents with charcoal: Kellvost, the Halveth perimeter flare, two unreported farm-edge distortions from caravan rumors she trusts more than official bulletins. Kess adds ruins concentrations in red ink. Jess overlays supply corridors and military staging projections.

The overlap is ugly.

Rift incidents, active ruins bands, and planned troop movement converge in a diagonal corridor running from north of Ashford toward the interior desert staging flats.

Jess does not soften her voice. "If both sides push high-tier units through this corridor, they stack flux load on unstable lines. Infrastructure fails first, then people."

Kess taps one ruins marker. "These are not decorative sites. They are anchors. If enough stress hits the network, you don't get one breach. You get linked failure."

Merra studies the map like a route she has to walk blind. "How long?"

Jess answers before Kess can. "Best case, two months. Worst case, six weeks."

A sound comes from the back of the warehouse: one crate settling under its own weight, or a footstep too careful to be accidental.

Merra is on her feet instantly. "Who's there?"

Silence, then a parcel slides from shadow into lantern light. Oilcloth wrap, old trade wax, no note.

Kess kneels, breaks the seal, and goes still as she reads the first page.

"This is Site Seventeen archival copy," she says. "Pre-edit. My old notation." She flips to the margin marks and laughs once, short and bitter. "They replaced 'anchor threshold' with 'ritual balance' in the public record. I knew they edited it, but not this cleanly."

Jess leans over. "Can you prove this to people who matter?"

"To people who care about proof, yes."

"Do any of those people currently matter?" Jess asks.

Kess does not answer.

Merra picks up the final page in the parcel. A single line in spare script: **You are late, not wrong. Move before the corridor hardens.**

No signature.

Jess exhales through her teeth. "Anonymous prophet routine. Great."

"Not a prophet," Kess says. "Someone with access. Someone terrified enough to leak this."

Merra folds the page and tucks it into her jacket. "Then we stop waiting for institutions to notice they're standing on a fault line."

Jess closes her ledger. "What do we actually do?"

Merra answers like a rescue brief.

"Step one: verify the corridor model independently so no one can dismiss it as one scholar's panic. Step two: identify which deployments trigger threshold first. Step three: force that data in front of people who still control movement orders. If they ignore it, we move to step four."

"Which is?" Kess asks.

Merra looks at the shadowed back wall where no one now stands.

"Find whoever sent the note. If they know this much, they know what we missed."

Outside, Ashford's fourth bell rings. Inside Dry Warehouse Nine, the meeting no institution authorized ends with three people agreeing to act as if the world can still be prevented from breaking.

For now, that is enough.
