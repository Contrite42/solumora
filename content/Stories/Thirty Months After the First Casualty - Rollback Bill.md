# Thirty Months After the First Casualty - Rollback Bill

The chamber clock in [[Auralis Civic Courts]] still ran seven minutes fast, a habit from blockade-era scheduling when everyone wanted hearings to finish before tempers peaked. [[Cassia]] watched the minute hand jump and thought about how many institutions were now built on people pretending clocks could solve trust.

"Bill 47-C," the clerk announced. "Administrative Efficiency Restoration Act."

The title drew a few tired laughs from gallery seats. Everyone in the room knew what it actually did.

[[Kess Tarren]] leaned toward Cassia without looking at her. "Section 12 removes public false-positive reporting. Section 18 turns observer requirements into discretionary guidance. Section 22 narrows review standing to directly detained parties only."

"All the boring pieces that keep the framework honest," Cassia said.

"Yes."

The sponsor rose and began the practiced argument: crisis-era safeguards were overbuilt, frontline operators were paralyzed by paperwork, districts needed flexibility to respond to fast threats. Several members nodded. Efficiency was an easy word when your district hadn't been swept by mistake.

Cassia requested speaking time as constitutional witness.

"This bill is lawful to propose," she began, voice level. "It is not harmless to enact. The sections presented as technical cleanup are exactly the sections that made post-sweep correction visible and enforceable. Remove visibility, and you do not remove errors. You remove evidence of errors."

From gallery benches, district delegates took notes. A student from [[Aldrane Academy]] held up an annotated packet with three columns: _claim_, _impact_, _who pays_.

Kess followed with Tribunal position: "Field strain is real. So are false-positive cascades. Bill 47-C solves strain by deleting accountability requirements, not by improving operational throughput. That trade restores speed at the cost of legitimacy."

A member from northern districts replied, "Legitimacy is already collapsing in my towns because people believe institutions are weak."

Kess nodded once. "Weakness and constraint are not synonyms."

The debate ran six hours. By dusk, compromise amendments had reduced none of the core rollbacks. The vote failed by two.

Temporary relief passed through the chamber like weather.

Cassia didn't celebrate. "Two votes means this comes back next session with better messaging," she said while aides packed brief folders.

"Then we use the [[Framework Retrenchment Defense Protocol]]," Kess replied. "Delay lock, harm forecast, dual-jurisdiction review. Make them argue in daylight with numbers."

Cassia glanced toward the open public gallery doors where people were already leaving, arguing in low voices about process and fear and what counted as security.

"Thirty months in," she said quietly, "and we're still one bill away from pretending none of this happened."

"Thirty months in," Kess said, "and we're one vote away from proving we remember."

Outside, the evening rain started hard against courthouse stone. Reporters ran for awnings, citizens stood in lines to file public comments before deadline, and clerks wheeled boxes of hearing records to archive storage.

It didn't look heroic. It looked procedural.

That was the point.
