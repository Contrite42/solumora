# Cache Mark Seven

Meln Korst found the cache seventeen meters north of where the map said it should be.

The displacement wasn't unusual. [[Equatorial Desert|Zakros]] shifted. Sand moved, reference rocks buried themselves, and the markers people left behind became suggestions rather than guarantees. Seventeen meters was close enough to call the cache found, which meant the expedition wouldn't need to ration water for the next three days.

He cleared the sand from the sealed lid and checked the integrity sigil carved into the protective casing. Still active. Good. That meant the cache had been properly maintained within the last six months. He opened it.

Twelve water bladders, each holding ten liters. Six nutrition packs rated for desert heat. Two replacement rope coils. One medical resupply kit. One emergency [[Grimoires|grimoire]] with basic environmental protection spells. Everything listed on the cache manifest, preserved exactly as the [[Halveth Guide Bureau|Bureau]] protocol specified.

Meln pulled four water bladders for immediate expedition use and resealed the cache. The other eight bladders stayed. Someone else would need them, probably within the next two weeks if the expedition schedule Halveth was running held to pattern. Supply caches worked because people took what they needed and left what remained. The system failed when someone took everything.

He marked the cache coordinates on his own map, noting the seventeen-meter offset. When he returned to [[Halveth]], he'd report the displacement to the Bureau. They'd update the master cache maps. The expedition after next would find the cache location accurate again until the desert shifted it somewhere else.

---

**Four days later.** The expedition — eleven people, mixed [[Control Tier|T2]] and T3 practitioners, commercial crossing ferrying [[Grimoires|grimoire]] trade materials — made standard progress. Meln's job was simple: verify cache locations, manage resupply timing, and keep the expedition from running deficits that would require emergency rationing.

Cache Mark Seven had been routine. Cache Mark Eight, three days further into the crossing, was not.

He reached the coordinates and found nothing.

No disturbed sand indicating recent excavation. No marker stones. No partial sigil traces. The cache was gone, or it had never been here, or the maps were wrong by more than search radius could compensate for.

Meln spent two hours searching a hundred-meter radius. Nothing. He returned to the expedition and reported.

The lead practitioner, a woman named Torra Veln who'd done six crossings and knew how margins worked, recalculated. "We have enough water to reach Cache Mark Nine if we ration starting now. That's four days without finding Mark Eight."

"Mark Nine should be there," Meln said. "It was verified two months ago."

"So was Mark Eight, presumably."

"Mark Eight was verified four months ago. It's possible someone raided it completely. It's possible the records were wrong. It's possible a [[Rift Incursions|rift]] event displaced it beyond search range."

"Which means we proceed on the assumption we're not finding it." Torra looked at the rest of the expedition. "Rationing starts now. Personal water allotments drop to sixty percent. We reach Cache Mark Nine with reserves or we reach it with problems."

No one objected. The expedition had signed contracts that included rationing clauses. Everyone understood that desert crossings operated on contingency planning and that contingency planning meant accepting reduced comfort before reduced survival became the alternative.

Meln adjusted his own water consumption and continued. The job wasn't to prevent problems. It was to identify problems early enough that solutions remained available.

---

**Six days later.** They reached Cache Mark Nine.

Meln found it thirty-two meters south of the map coordinates, which was worse displacement than usual but still within search tolerance. He cleared the sand, checked the sigil, and opened the cache.

Eight water bladders. Four nutrition packs. One rope coil. One medical kit. No grimoire.

The cache was half-empty. It had been raided, or it had never been fully stocked, or the previous expedition had taken more than protocol allowed and no one had reported it.

Meln pulled three bladders and resealed the cache, leaving five. The nutrition packs he took all four — the expedition was running deficit on food now, water had been the urgent concern. He marked the reduced inventory on his map and returned to the expedition.

Torra reviewed the resupply and recalculated again. "We're back to adequate margins if the crossing stays standard-complications from here. If we hit delays, we're rationing again."

"Cache Mark Ten is six days out," Meln said. "It was verified five weeks ago. It should be fully stocked."

"Should be."

"Yes."

The expedition continued. Meln watched the water consumption rates and tracked the remaining distance. The margins were thin but functional. Thin margins completed crossings as often as comfortable ones. The difference was that comfortable margins allowed mistakes and thin margins did not.

---

**Eight days later.** Cache Mark Ten was exactly where the map said it would be, fully stocked, sigil intact.

Meln resupplied the expedition to normal operational levels and filed the cache locations in his memory. When he returned to Halveth, he would report: Cache Seven displaced seventeen meters, Cache Eight missing entirely, Cache Nine half-stocked, Cache Ten perfect.

The Bureau would note the discrepancies, investigate what could be investigated, update the reliability ratings for the cache sequence, and continue operations. Some caches failed. That was the operational reality. The system worked because enough caches succeeded that expeditions could route around failures without dying.

The expedition reached [[Auralis]]-side settlement fourteen days after Cache Mark Ten. Everyone survived. No one was injured. The crossing was a success by every contractual and operational standard.

Meln collected his payment, rested for three days, and accepted the next expedition contract. The work was ordinary. That's what made it valuable. Supply chain maintenance didn't create dramatic successes. It prevented dramatic failures, which was more important and less visible and exactly as essential.

He would cross Zakros four more times before the season ended. He would find twelve of fifteen caches exactly where they should be. Two would be displaced but recoverable. One would be missing. The expeditions would complete because the system was designed to tolerate exactly that failure rate.

The caches were maintained by the Bureau, which was funded by expedition fees, which were paid by people who needed to cross the desert for reasons that mattered more than the cost. The system worked because everyone involved understood that redundancy wasn't waste, it was the minimum requirement for survival when the environment did not tolerate single points of failure.

Meln marked his maps, reported his findings, and prepared for the next crossing. The caches would be where they were. The desert would shift them. The expeditions would adapt. That was the work.
