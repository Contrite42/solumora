# Eighteen Months After the First Casualty - Border Market

The [[Ashford]] spring market operated under the [[Border Settlement Coexistence Protocol|Border Protocol]]'s dual observer system, which meant [[Jess Maren]] spent her morning mediating disputes that no single kingdom wanted to claim jurisdiction over.

"The price was set at the winter rate," the [[Terravelle]] grain merchant insisted. "Eight watts per bushel, agreed in writing."

"Before the [[Corridor Stabilization Treaty|Treaty]] provisions about cross-border rift monitoring," the [[Auralis]] expedition outfitter countered. "Now you're required to disclose if High Demon expertise was used in cultivation. Were your yield projections—"

"Stop," Jess said. Both merchants turned to her. The market square was crowded with spring expedition preparation—supply crates being assembled, guide contracts being negotiated, the normal chaos of [[Ashford]] coming back to life after winter. Three months ago, this place had been categorized as Transitional Compliance. Two weeks ago, with expedition crews flooding in, it had shifted to Full Integration. The Protocol scaled with population.

"Expertise disclosure applies to institutional research sharing," Jess continued. "Not to private cultivation on registered farmland. If the merchant consulted with [[Joint Rift Monitoring Cell|Monitoring Cell]] staff about seasonal Flux patterns, that's public information available to anyone. Not competitive advantage."

The outfitter's expression suggested he knew he was overreaching. "But if—"

"You're trying to renegotiate winter contracts because spring prices are higher and you want a legal justification." Jess glanced at her notes. "Third time this month someone's tried the expertise-disclosure claim. The Protocol doesn't give you that."

The merchant relaxed slightly. "So the contract stands?"

"Unless you both want to escalate to the joint Commission panel. Which will take six weeks and cost you three watts in administrative fees."

Both merchants exchanged looks. "Contract stands," the outfitter muttered.

"Good." Jess made a notation. "Next."

The morning proceeded with variations on the same theme: people testing the Protocol's boundaries, looking for advantages in the jurisdictional complexity, trying to figure out what the new rules actually meant in practice. By noon, Jess had mediated twelve disputes, issued four compliance warnings, and escalated exactly zero cases to the joint panel.

The Protocol was working. Mostly.

"Busy morning." [[Teren Voss]] approached from the [[Joint Rift Monitoring Cell|Cell]] station, carrying two tin cups of what passed for coffee in spring Ashford. He moved through the market crowd with the ease of someone who'd spent eighteen months proving he wasn't a threat. Some people still watched him carefully. Most just nodded and kept moving.

"Spring always is," Jess accepted the coffee. "How's the rift forecast model?"

"We're integrating the [[Auralis]] Council's historical data with [[Terravelle]] guild observation networks. Both kingdoms pretending they always planned to cooperate on this." He gestured toward the market. "Your dual observer counterpart's late again."

"Relk's not late. He's establishing that [[Terravelle]] won't jump when [[Auralis]] schedules meetings." Jess took a sip of terrible coffee. "Which I'm supposed to ignore unless it becomes a pattern of non-compliance, per the Protocol's 'jurisdictional flexibility' clause."

"Politics."

"Welcome to border governance." She watched the market's flow. [[Merra Veld]] was  visible near the expedition coordination board, talking to a crew leader about desert routes. [[Kess Tarren]] had arrived yesterday from the [[Anti-Persecution Compliance Tribunal|Tribunal]] for a Protocol review inspection—her current expression as she observed the market suggested she was compiling a list of everything wrong with flexible enforcement.

"The [[Regional Enforcement Variance Doctrine|Variance Doctrine]] evaluation's due next week," Verath said quietly. "You're reporting to the [[Ashford Coexistence Oversight Commission|Commission]]?"

"That Ashford's dual observer system has mediated eighty-three disputes, escalated zero to diplomatic crisis, and enabled functional cross-border cooperation." Jess paused. "Also that we're running compliance theater half the time and most merchants have figured out how to game the jurisdictional gaps."

"Both true."

"Both true," Jess agreed. "Question is which matters more—that the framework's messy, or that we're forecasting rifts instead of preparing for war."

Verath's expression shifted slightly. "Eighteen months ago, I was choosing between letting [[Auralis]] and [[Terravelle]] collapse into war or revealing what I am to stop it. This market—this Protocol, this jurisdictional mess—is what that revelation bought. I'll take imperfect coexistence over perfect combat any cycle."

Jess nodded. The market continued its chaotic exchange around them. Spring expedition season meant Ashford bloomed with that particular energy of people preparing to risk the desert—guides comparing route notes, merchants calculating profits, crews assembling from both kingdoms and not caring much about which one claimed jurisdiction as long as the contracts held.

"That's him." Verath indicated a figure entering the market square—the [[Terravelle]] observer, Relk Tonn, moving with deliberate visibility toward the coordination point. "Right on schedule to be exactly fifteen minutes late."

"Politics," Jess said again. She finished her coffee. "Let's go establish that [[Auralis]] will tolerate minor scheduling disrespect in exchange for functional cooperation on everything that matters."

"The Protocol in one sentence."

They crossed the market square together. Around them, merchants negotiated, crews planned, and the spring season began its work of proving that border settlements could maintain coexistence—not because the frameworks were elegant, but because the alternative was abandoning the desert crossing entirely.

The [[Border Settlement Coexistence Protocol|Protocol]] called that Transitional Compliance shifting to seasonal Full Integration. Jess called it making things work with duct tape and compromise. 

Both were true. In Ashford, they learned to count that as success.
