# Three Nights to First Shot - Courier

Mera Seln rode without colors.

No guild sash. No city badge. No kingdom crest. Just a plain coat, dust-weight satchel, and a permit so old the corners had gone soft.

In cleaner years, that would have been enough.

Tonight, every checkpoint wanted a flag before a name.

---

The first stop was routine inspection dressed as suspicion.

The second stop was suspicion dressed as routine.

At the third, a young officer asked her who she worked for and then asked the same question three different ways, hoping one answer would sound like treason.

Mera gave him paperwork and silence.

He found the message class marker and frowned.

"Technical reconciliation packet?"

"Yes."

"Between which offices?"

"The ones still trying not to be famous."

He almost smiled, then remembered his uniform and waved her through.

---

By midnight she reached the interim exchange shed outside [[Ashford]], where couriers used to hand off under mixed supervision.

Used to.

Now there were separate tables, separate logbooks, separate guards staring at the same wall from opposite sides.

Mera placed her satchel on the neutral plank and opened it slowly.

No cipher drama. No spycraft flourish.

Just route corrections, incident timestamps, and a draft checklist for [[Border De-Escalation Protocols]] execution if patrol spacing narrowed further tomorrow.

No one wanted those documents.

Everyone needed them.

---

The receiving clerk from the southern side arrived late and breathing hard.

"We weren't told this exchange was still authorized," he said.

"It's not," Mera said. "It's not forbidden either, if nobody writes the wrong title on the form."

He laughed once, short and unhappy.

"You know this doesn't hold if they decide to close the road."

"I know," she said. "That's why I came tonight and not tomorrow."

She waited while he checked each sheet and signed with a hand that shook only at the last stroke.

---

When she left the shed, dawn had started to flatten the horizon into pale metal.

She had one outbound packet left, addressed to a coordinator she'd never met in [[Terravelle]] and stamped urgent without priority code.

Bad sign.

Urgent without code meant people knew time mattered and had stopped agreeing why.

Mera mounted up and chose the long route with better sightlines.

If the road stayed open one more day, that would be because a handful of bored, underpaid, stubborn people kept carrying paper no one wanted to read until it was too late.

She had built a life on that kind of stubborn.
