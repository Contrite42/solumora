# Twenty-Four Hours After the First Casualty - Grain Broker

Sela Vorr opened her ledger before sunrise and changed three numbers she had sworn never to change in the same week.

Purchase floor.

Delivery premium.

Late-risk surcharge.

By midmorning, customers would call it gouging.

By evening, the same customers would call it inevitable.

Both judgments were true enough to hurt.

---

The first casualty had not closed markets.

It had changed confidence.

Confidence moved faster than carts.

Two warehouse partners withheld stock "pending route clarity." Three bakers attempted double-ordering at old rates. One district buyer tried to convert a monthly contract to cash-on-delivery in case permits froze.

Sela denied the conversion and offered staged release instead.

"You are shifting risk to us," the buyer said.

"I am pricing the risk already here," she answered.

---

At noon, she met with a [[Queue Management Offices]] coordinator and a clinic runner requesting protected allotment for household staples.

Sela moved two reserve pallets into controlled release, tagged for care sites and large-family ration lines.

It would reduce her weekly margin by a third.

It would also keep line violence down when evening shortages became visible.

She signed the release herself so no junior clerk would carry blame.

---

At 16:20, a rival broker posted lower rates across south market and pulled a crowd immediately.

By 17:05, his staff began refusing fulfillment beyond tiny lot sizes.

Cheap headline. Empty sacks.

Sela sent an assistant to copy the board and file it into her dispute stack for next week's arbitration.

She had no time to be angry now. Anger did not move grain.

---

At close, she recalculated tomorrow's open.

If roads held, prices stabilized in three days.

If roads failed, paper prices stopped mattering because there would be no paper left worth reading.

She wrote one line on the front page of her ledger for apprentices to read at dawn:

"In crisis, fairness is not one price for all. Fairness is making sure everyone still eats."
