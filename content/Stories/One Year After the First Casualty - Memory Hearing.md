# One Year After the First Casualty - Memory Hearing

The hearing notice says "Annual Public Memory Session." Half the city calls it theater before it starts.

By evening, the hall is full anyway.

Under the [[Succession Safeguards Charter]], institutions must present one transparent review of near-collapse events each year with public questions permitted and answers docketed. No classified shorthand. No summary euphemisms.

Kess opens with the technical timeline: Kellvost, corridor convergence, standstill windows, first joint anchor operation, treaty codification.

Jess follows with logistics numbers most officials once tried not to publish: projected casualty bands under failed intervention scenarios, supply-collapse thresholds, documented panic loops.

Then the floor opens.

A merchant asks why the city should keep funding oversight bodies now that "nothing catastrophic happened this winter."

Jess replies, "Nothing catastrophic happened this winter because the funding existed before winter."

A parent asks whether children are being taught fear through the new curriculum.

Kess answers, "They're being taught mechanism. Fear is what happens when mechanism is hidden."

A retired officer asks Verath directly whether demons will eventually demand governing power.

Verath's answer is shorter than expected.

"I demand survivable systems, not your throne."

No one claps. People write that line down.

The final question comes from a student in the back row wearing a district academy pin.

"If institutions can forget this fast, how do we stop forgetting when the people who lived it are gone?"

Cassia takes that one.

"By making memory procedural, not sentimental," she says. "Records, audits, curricula, and hearings that continue when we are not in the room."

After adjournment, the docket clerk posts all questions and responses before midnight as required.

Outside, arguments continue in clusters under rainlight. No consensus. No collapse.

In this era, that counts as a functioning memory system.
