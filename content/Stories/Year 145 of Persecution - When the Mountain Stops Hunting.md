# Year 145 of Persecution - When the Mountain Stops Hunting

The town had received warning nine days before the sweep: Lantern formation would be moving through border territory in coordinated action. Usual pattern. Move people to safety. Clear the market of merchants from territories where they might be questioned.

This time something felt different to Elder Celn. After seventy years of these cycles, you developed instinct for institutional rhythm.

She spent the warning period doing something unusual: staying visible. Walking markets. Greeting people by name. Taking careful inventory of which merchants' carts were loaded for rapid evacuation, which families had decided to stay.

The Lantern formation arrived at dusk, which meant night sweeps. Good tactically—harder for refugees to move in darkness. Good psychologically—terror management through visibility and noise and flickering light.

But the town was ready in ways the formation didn't expect.

The roads didn't exist where maps said they did. The storage facilities had been deliberately reorganized so documented refugee holding areas were empty. The merchant caravans moved outward on routes the formation had already swept, becoming invisible through redundancy.

And the young people of the town, all of them, moved through formation operations with the absolute composure of people who'd rehearsed responses to state violence.

What should have been hour-long sweep took three hours. What should have been forty to sixty refugees located ended with formation commanders filing reports claiming three hundred sweep-ground distance covered, sixty-eight individuals questioned, thirty-seven "relocated for further institutional processing" (meaning the magistrate would release them in a week when political attention shifted).

Actually: thirty-seven refugees moved to different refugee sites. Nobody relocated.

When the formation left, Elder Celn was standing in the town center. The commander made direct eye contact.

"Your town is sheltering refugees," she said. Factual statement, not interrogation.

"Our town provides hospitality," Elder Celn replied. Same facts, different language.

"I should arrest you."

"You should report successful sweep completion. Which you can do truthfully: you swept, we provided no resistance. What you don't find isn't your responsibility."

The commander held her gaze for long moment. Then nodded once. "Your magistrate will be contacted about your cooperation."

Which was magistrate language for: "I'm going to file a report that your town was difficult but ultimately compliant, and the sympathetic magistrate will interpret that as validation for continued protection."

After the formation left, one of the town council—younger than Celn, modern generation—asked directly: "How long can this continue?"

"Until institutions change," Celn said. "Until kingdoms stop hunting and we stop hiding. Until someone makes different choice at scale."

"That could be generations."

"Yes. Learn how to sustain this. Teach your children. This is now what community means."

The mountain town had stopped hunting for refuge-residents. They'd made that change three decades prior. Now institutions were performing hunts they understood were theater.

Long-term coexistence was being built by communities before kingdoms even admitted it was possible.
