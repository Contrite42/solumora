# Year 95 of Persecution - The Merchant's Family Erasure

The records said Kolm did not exist.

He'd been a spice merchant for thirty-seven years—documented in trade guild registries, property deeds, marriage certificates. Raised two children in the city. Paid taxes. Served on neighborhood council committees.

Year 92 of persecution, someone reported his daughter to the magistrate. The report wasn't specific. Just something about her being unusually long-lived, unusually healthy, looking "not quite right for her age."

The daughter was forty-three. Looked forty-three. This was the institutional paranoia functioning: if someone survived to forty-three without obvious visible aging, they might be immortal. Or they might be sheltering immortals. Or they might have ancestors who'd sheltered immortals.

Suspicion was sufficient. The magistrate's office issued Document Destruction Order against the family line: Kolm, Wife, Daughter, Son, all grandchildren. All civil records confiscated, destroyed, struck from registry.

Kolm stood in the magistrate's office and watched them do it. Watched clerks pull his birth certificate, his marriage license, his merchant registration. Watched them cut his daughter's name from family registers. Watched his fifty years of documented existence being unmade.

"You'll need to leave the city," the magistrate said afterward. He sounded apologetic. That was the worst part. He actually felt bad about it.

"I can't leave," Kolm said. "I own property. My family is here."

"You don't own property anymore. Records confirm you never did. Your family name has been corrected out of historical documentation."

The property seizure was legal because the confiscated records meant he couldn't prove original ownership. Without documentation, he had no claim.

Without documentation, he couldn;t work merchant trades. Without documentation, his wife couldn't access their bank accounts. Without documentation, his children couldn't attend school, couldn't enter guild apprenticeships, couldn't marry legally.

They had 48 hours to leave the city before resident status expiration made them technically trespassers.

## Three Years Later

Kolm's daughter found the refuge network through chance—a merchant fleeing the city who recognized her from pre-erasure markets, introduced her to someone who knew someone. By year 95, she was in mountain communities helping other erased families.

Kolm himself never left the city, it turned out. He simply disappeared into the servant class—worked without documentation, paid under the table, lived in spaces between legal categories. His wife did the same.

The institution's document erasure had accomplished something almost impossible: creating hundreds of unemployable, uncheckable people living in plain sight. They couldn't prove existence, but they also couldn't be tracked through census. They were ghosts with bodies requiring food.

The refugee networks expanded to accommodate erased families alongside High Demons. By year 150, erased-family population exceeded documented refugee population.

Persecution had intended to eliminate immortal beings. It had created an entire shadow population of legally non-existent humans.

If you asked Kolm whether he was living in a refuge, he would have said no. He'd say he was living in his city, just without the possibility of documentation. It was functionally the same thing as refuge: survival depending on community protection and sympathy magistrates.
