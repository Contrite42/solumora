# The Copy That Stayed Cheap

Tarin Vess knew the argument before the ledger even opened.

The request came from a lower-terrace workshop cooperative in [[Valdenmoor]]: twelve weatherproofing spell copies from a [[Common Grimoire]], needed before the rain turn cycle that would make half the city temporarily unworkable, total cost cap 96 [[Watts]].

It was legitimate work from legitimate clients. The cooperative comprised six workshops that shared resources—textile dyeing, leather working, metal fabrication. They had a collective roof that did not belong to any single entity, which meant the cost had to be split across six ways, which meant they genuinely could not afford expensive magic.

Her supervisor glanced at the figures and sniffed once, with the dismissive sound of someone who had been making profit decisions for forty years.

"Add fifteen percent," he said. "Standard markup for rush jobs."

"They can't carry fifteen percent," Tarin replied carefully, trying to keep the conversation from becoming adversarial. She had been scripting for about eight years and had learned not to sound like she was arguing directly against her supervisor's judgment. "These clients cut volume if we raise price. Half the roofs get covered, or they commission work elsewhere."

"That is not our problem," her supervisor said, his tone suggesting this was a settled matter.

Tarin looked down at the ledger for a long moment. She could feel the impending rain. The city weather patterns were reliable enough that everyone could sense when storm season would crunch down, and this year the season was arriving early.

"It becomes our problem when they stop ordering because we priced utility like luxury," she said quietly.

Her supervisor closed the grimoire he'd been consulting and gave her forty minutes to prove an alternative solution. Forty minutes was either generous or dismissive depending on which direction the math went.

---

She spent the full time at a back desk, with three other grimoires pulled for reference, calculating every variable she could think of.

What if they shared prep batch work instead of each copy having independent startup costs?

What if she grouped variable checks together—verified once for a cluster instead of once for every iteration?

What if verification went from one review per page to one deep verification for every three pages of uniform work?

What if she accepted slightly longer copying sequences to reduce active practitioner supervision time, trading her own labor hours for less Flux-user hours?

No quality loss through any of these sequences. Just systematization. Just a willingness to work less elegantly in the interest of affordability.

She attached six pages of notes to the proposal:

- past-quarter error rates demonstrating low-risk profile,
- expected labor drag calculations showing she could absorb the extra time,
- a note in plain language that if these copies were priced correctly, the district remained dry enough to keep ordering services next season instead of developing internal spell-copying competence or switching suppliers.

Her supervisor read the packet in silence long enough for Tarin to hear her own pulse. He was the kind of man who moved slowly through decisions because he actually thought rather than just reacting.

Then he signed the original price line without the markup.

"Don't make me regret this," he said, not looking up.

"I won't," she answered.

---

The cooperative sent runners every evening to collect finished sheets still warm from the drying racks.

On delivery night, the forewoman—a woman named Sten with grey threading through her hair and the careful way of moving that came from old joint damage—gripped Tarin's forearm.

"If this holds," Sten said quietly, "we keep our machine room open through storm quarter instead of shutting down temporary. Eight people keep their regular hours instead of shifting to lower-wage seasonal work."

Tarin nodded. She had understood the stakes from reading the ledger. Eight people at reduced hours meant eight families under additional financial pressure during the expensive season when heating and warm food cost more.

Two weeks later, the sky cracked open for three days of brutal rain.

The roofs held.

No dramatic announcements. No city notice. No story worth telling in tea halls. Just

 less water in workrooms, fewer ruined materials, fewer families forced into emergency borrowing because damp had chewed through stock and work had become impossible.

It was invisible success. It was the kind of intervention that never generated legend.

The order did disappear into accounting history like modest decisions do.

But at the desk level, people noticed. Apprentices heard the story. Foremen started asking different questions. By winter, Tarin's sequencing model became a standard option for utility jobs across three affiliate desks. Trainees were taught to ask one extra question before quoting high:

_What fails downstream if this gets too expensive? Who doesn't get served? What breaks if we price for maximum margin?_

---

A year later, a newly certified apprentice scribe asked Tarin why she kept taking jobs that were "important but not prestigious or profitable."

Tarin set her pen down precisely. She was forty years old. She had another twenty, maybe twenty-five years of professional work left before age made copying difficult. She knew already that she would not become famous. There would be no major commissions with her name inscribed in colophons. There would be no legendary manuscripts that bore her hand.

"Prestige doesn't keep rain out," she said finally.

The apprentice laughed, then realized from Tarin's expression that she wasn't joking.

"Listen," Tarin continued, turning a grimoire page so the apprentice could see the cost equations clearly. "If a spell is flawless and unaffordable, then for most people it's broken. Price is part of function. You write a perfect spell that costs too much and you've written a spell for wealthy people only. That's not a spell. That's a luxury good masquerading as protection."

The apprentice wrote that down quickly, pretending it was for a different class, pretending she wasn't storing a fundamental principle away in the back of her mind.

Tarin pretended not to notice.

Outside the scriptorum window, storm clouds were already gathering at the edge of the district, moving in with the seasonal inevitability that weather possessed. Somewhere in the lower terraces, six workshops with a collective roof were about to receive water management protection that worked specifically because someone had chosen to fight over a single line item rather than accepting that scarcity and cost escalation were natural laws instead of design decisions.

By spring, those workshops would keep running. Eight people would remain employed at full wages. Next season, they'd commission again.

That was what staying cheap accomplished. Not poverty. Not compromise. Just the steady work of making sure ordinary people could access the things they needed without becoming indebted to systems that preferred them perpetually obligated.
