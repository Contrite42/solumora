# Five Days Before the Breakpoint - Household

Mira Keld counted bread by half-days now.

Not because they were starving. Not yet. Because schedules had stopped lining up.

Her partner's shift changed twice this week, the clinic follow-up moved once, and the shelter renewal window was now on the same morning as ration verification. The city still called these services available. The city was correct in the way ledgers are correct.

---

At dawn she took token 47 from the queue office and was told to return by midday for confirmation.

At midday she was told the lane had rolled to tomorrow because two districts had merged intake due to a water interruption.

By afternoon she was at the clinic anyway, carrying her son's fever chart and the old referral slip with the corners softened from use.

The nurse looked at the slip and said the right words in the wrong order.

"You're still in the system."

"I need him seen today."

"I know."

The nurse refiled them to the next slot and whispered the part nobody said loudly: "Try not to lose your place in housing while you wait."

---

That evening the boarding house posted new rotation terms.

Two nights guaranteed, one night contingent, re-entry subject to queue verification.

Mira read it twice and felt the quiet math tighten around her chest.

One missed verification meant one missed bed. One missed bed meant no sleep. No sleep meant missed shift. Missed shift meant no wage proof. No wage proof meant lower queue priority.

Collapse came as sequence, not explosion.

---

On the fourth day she found a coordinator at a side desk who looked tired enough to be honest.

He reviewed the packet and wrote three notes in the margin:

- "clinic continuity priority"
- "dependent household"
- "temporary housing rollover"

"This will keep you from resetting to zero," he said.

"For how long?"

"Long enough to make next week your problem instead of today."

She thanked him because gratitude was cheaper than argument and sometimes more effective.

---

That night her son slept through without coughing for the first time in six days.

Mira sat by the window with the ration card and shelter slip side by side, planning routes between offices as if route planning were now her profession.

Outside, people crossed the courtyard carrying water containers and folded blankets, all of them performing the same private arithmetic.

No sirens. No collapse. No headline.

Just a city staying functional one exhausted sequence at a time.

She folded the papers, set the alarm before first light, and told herself what everyone told themselves now:

If tomorrow holds, we hold.
