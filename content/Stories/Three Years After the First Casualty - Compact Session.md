# Three Years After the First Casualty - Compact Session

The gallery benches were full before first bell in [[Auralis Civic Courts]], not with protest signs this time but with notebooks, guild badges, and district packets highlighted in three colors. People had learned the ritual: if a framework changed, you showed up early and read the footnotes.

[[Cassia]] stood at witness table beside the joint delegation from [[Terravelle]]. [[Merra Veld]] sat one row behind with implementation maps and a field binder thick enough to count as blunt force.

"Interkingdom Accountability Compact, final session," the clerk announced.

The sponsor from Terravelle opened: "This does not require us to agree on political philosophy. It requires us to agree on what must be disclosed when shared institutions fail."

Auralis opposition speakers called it sovereignty drift. One used the phrase "foreign oversight by paperwork" twice in three minutes.

Cassia answered without changing tone. "When institutions are shared, secrecy is not sovereignty. It is risk export."

The line held in the chamber like a struck bell.

Questions moved quickly: redaction boundaries, petition standing for non-citizen workers, conflict windows between domestic law and shared disclosure clock. None of it was simple, which was exactly why the hearing mattered.

At noon, [[Kess Tarren]] testified for the Tribunal.

"Our review authority failed once because momentum outran verification," she said. "Compact language forces chronology publication even when publication is politically expensive. That is not anti-sovereign. It is anti-amnesia."

From public microphone, an academy student asked the question every late-stage framework eventually faces: "How do we know this survives leadership change?"

Merra requested response time.

"You don't know in advance," she said. "You design for it. Shared clocks, reciprocal audit access, equivalency rules. Then you test them under leaders who didn't build the system."

Vote call came at 16:40.

Passed in Auralis by four.
Passed in Terravelle by six.

No cheering. Just the sound of clerks marking two ledgers with the same timestamp.

Outside the court, rain threatened and failed. Reporters chased quotes about constitutional precedent. Dock delegations headed back to evening convoys. Students compared notes on standing clauses and redaction exceptions like it was exam prep.

Cassia closed her folder and exhaled once.

"Three years," she said.

"Three years and still arguing over publication tables," Merra replied.

Cassia gave a tired half-smile. "That's better than arguing over mobilization tables."

They walked down the courthouse steps into ordinary traffic, where governance had to become mundane to remain durable.
