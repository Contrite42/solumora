# Two Years After the First Casualty - Repair Window

[[Jess Maren]] had spent enough years in [[Ashford]] to know when a district stopped speaking to institutions. It wasn't loud. It was procedural silence: fewer permit renewals, fewer grievance filings, fewer cooperative reports. People didn't protest; they routed around you.

Three days after Sweep Night in [[Auralis]], Ashford's East Dock district posted the same signals.

"They're not rejecting the framework," Jess told the emergency call with Commission and Tribunal channels. "They're rejecting your promise that process protects them."

"East Dock wasn't the incident site," a Tribunal clerk objected.

"Trust doesn't respect jurisdiction maps," Jess said. "If core-city operators can sweep compliant residents, border communities assume we're next."

By protocol, this was the point where local offices waited for formal directives. Under the new [[Emergency Reintegration Mandate]], waiting was prohibited.

At 09:00, the seventy-two-hour repair window opened.

By 10:30, reintegration teams had a temporary station in the dock cooperative hall: one Commission mediator, one Tribunal records clerk, one guild registrar, one transit office dispatcher. No uniforms. No enforcement insignia. Just desks, correction forms, and a board listing common remediation requests.

The first resident in line was a cargo scheduler whose travel credential had been flagged during a synchronization error pushed from Auralis records after the sweep correction cycle.

"I wasn't detained," she said, handing over her paperwork. "But my route clearances vanished this morning. If I miss two convoy windows, I lose my contract."

The Tribunal clerk scanned her file, winced, and started immediate restoration. "System inherited a false-positive marker cluster. You're right; this should never have propagated."

"Shouldn't," she repeated. "Still did."

Jess watched the exchange and wrote the case number on the public log board. Visibility mattered as much as correction.

By midday, the queue stretched into the square:
- Three apprentices blocked from academy attendance due to cross-system status mismatch
- Seven dock workers with suspended permit endorsements
- One guide cooperative flagged for "association risk" after hosting a Monitoring Cell briefing
- Two families whose housing transfer requests froze when district risk scores spiked

None were direct victims of Sweep Night. All were downstream casualties of institutional panic.

At 14:00, Jess chaired the open acknowledgment hearing required by Mandate activation. Attendance exceeded fire limits; speakers rotated in fifteen-minute blocks.

A retired route captain spoke first: "You keep saying the system corrected itself. We don't live in system language. We live in missed shifts, blocked school entries, and whether your clerk answers honestly when the error is yours."

A Tribunal representative stood and gave the prepared line about extraordinary circumstances. The room chilled immediately.

Jess cut in before the tone hardened. "No exceptional-circumstances framing today. This is repair session, not institutional defense. If the process harmed you, we log it, fix it, and publish the correction timeline."

The room warmed by one degree. Not trust. Just willingness to continue talking.

At 18:00, [[Merra Veld]] joined by remote link from Commission central channels and delivered the only statement that landed cleanly:

"If we cannot restore ordinary life faster than we disrupted it, we do not deserve compliance."

By day three, the station had processed 219 cases:
- 187 resolved
- 26 in active escalation
- 6 requiring Board-threshold review for structural failures

Participation metrics in East Dock rose from crisis floor to transitional baseline. Not recovered, but moving.

Jess closed the temporary station at 20:00 on the third day and signed the first weekly trust-rebuild report. She added a handwritten line below the official summary:

_Repair is not apology in paragraph form. It is labor with receipts._

The [[Emergency Reintegration Mandate]] called the phase complete when metrics crossed threshold. Jess called it complete when the line outside the station disappeared because people believed regular channels would function tomorrow.

That took another nine days.
