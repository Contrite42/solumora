# One Year After the First Casualty - New Cohort Drill

The drill yard at dawn looks like any other training cycle until you read the exercise boards.

No duel ladders. No output rankings. No faction colors.

Scenario title: **Mixed Corridor Failure - Amber to Red Transition**.

Participants: twenty-four trainees from civic academy, logistics apprenticeships, ward programs, and public registry tracks. Half were children when the first casualty happened. None held authority during the crisis they are now expected to understand.

Iven Corsk and two instructors run the session under the [[Intergenerational Coexistence Curriculum]] advanced module set.

The rules are simple and uncomfortable:
- no team can pass by maximizing a single capability,
- rumor reports are injected as noise and penalized if treated as evidence,
- every action must cite procedural basis.

At 08:15, the simulation injects a false non-human sabotage claim from a market witness.

Three trainees push immediate detention recommendation.
Two challenge evidence basis.
One asks for bulletin-state confirmation before any coercive action.

Iven marks the board:

**Correct pathway initiated: verification before force.**

At 09:40, the scenario forces resource scarcity. Teams can either maintain lane stability or preserve political optics. The best-performing group chooses lane stability and accepts temporary reputational penalty in simulation scoring.

An older observer mutters that this generation is being trained to apologize instead of command.

Iven answers without turning.

"They're being trained not to turn fear into paperwork against the wrong people."

Final assessment panel includes one logistics evaluator, one legal evaluator, and one technical evaluator. Highest scores go to teams with moderate output, strong documentation, and low coercive error rates.

At closing, trainees sign reflection sheets that ask one question:

**Which mechanism failed first in your worst run?**

Not who failed. Which mechanism.

By noon, the cohort disperses into ordinary classes while instructors compile the drill report for annual curriculum update.

No one calls the drill heroic. No one should.

It is the kind of boring practice societies invent when they finally understand that collapse is usually procedural before it is dramatic.
