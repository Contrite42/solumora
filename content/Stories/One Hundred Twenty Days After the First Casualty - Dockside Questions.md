# One Hundred Twenty Days After the First Casualty - Dockside Questions

The questions start before sunrise and do not stop all day.

At the Ashford dock tea stand, people no longer ask whether High Demons are real. They ask whether their children should register tier assessments earlier, whether anti-detention safeguards will survive the next election cycle, whether corridor closures will hit winter grain prices, whether cooperation means surrender.

Bren Ossve has sold tea at this corner for nineteen years and has developed a useful civic theory: if people ask practical questions, society is still repairable.

By midmorning, three table conversations overlap under fog and gull noise.

At table one, two warehouse clerks argue about Commission legitimacy. One says it is too soft. One says it is the first office in years that publishes complete dockets.

At table two, a courier apprentice asks whether Verath counts as "citizen, ally, or emergency equipment."

At table three, a school caretaker says her students now learn cross-tier coordination before advanced spell modules and that this is either wisdom or decline depending on which parent meeting you attend.

Bren pours tea and listens.

Around noon, Jess stops by for ten minutes between hearings, drinks in silence, and leaves after pinning the latest corridor bulletin to the public post.

The bulletin reads green-to-amber through evening. No closure expected.

That single line changes the mood more than any speech.

Later, Merra appears with supply receipts and the tired walk of someone who has spent months converting urgency into procedure. She reads the same bulletin, exhales, and finally sits long enough to finish a cup before moving on.

"How bad today?" Bren asks.

"Ordinary bad," Merra says. "Best category available."

By dusk, someone chalks a new line on the curb beside old red-band markings:

**ASK BETTER QUESTIONS THAN FEAR CAN ANSWER**

No one claims ownership.

Bren closes the stall after last bell and writes one sentence in his ledger, the same place he keeps pricing notes and weather records.

**Public panic has become public argument. Argument is slower than panic and kinder than silence.**

Tomorrow the questions will return.

That is the point.
