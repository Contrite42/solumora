_(Moral Philosophy, Crisis Governance)_

**Final-Generation Ethics of Sacrifice** summarizes the moral framework inferred from late-era directives of [[The Ancient Civilization Final Generation]], especially how leaders justified selective protection when universal rescue became impossible. It is relevant to the current war arc because modern institutions are approaching similar allocation dilemmas while still pretending total protection is achievable.

## Core Ethical Shift

Earlier records emphasize universal civic obligation. Late records shift to constrained ethics:

- Preserve the largest viable future, not every present life
- Prioritize capabilities that multiply later survival
- Avoid symbolic heroics that consume irreplaceable specialists

This is not compassionless utilitarianism; it is structured triage under acknowledged system limits.

## Categories of Priority

Late directives appear to rank protection targets by continuity value:

- Medical and maintenance specialists
- Archive custodians and routing coordinators
- Infrastructure operators for water, energy, and transit
- Dependent populations with no self-evacuation capacity

Military prestige roles are not consistently prioritized in surviving fragments.

## Why It Matters Now

In the current escalation, officials still publicly promise full safety while private planning already uses implicit sacrifice logic. The ancient record warns that hidden triage erodes legitimacy faster than explicit hard choices. Systems survive longer when sacrifice rules are declared, contested, and documented.
