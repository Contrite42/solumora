_(King of Terravelle)_

Eddan is the King of [[Terravelle]], a man in his mid-sixties who has held the throne long enough that the weight of it has stopped showing on his face. He is not a dramatic ruler. He does not make speeches about destiny or legacy. He runs his kingdom the way [[Terravelle]] runs its magic — at the lowest effective cost, with nothing wasted, and nothing displayed that doesn't need to be.

He is, quietly, a [[Control Tier|Tier 3 or 4]] caster. Nobody outside a very small circle knows this with certainty. He has never seen a reason to advertise it, and in Terravelle that restraint reads not as weakness but as exactly the kind of character a king should have.

## Reign

Eddan has been king long enough to have watched [[Auralis]] cycle through two queens and a regent before its current ruler settled in. He has outlasted advisors, survived three separate attempts to destabilize Terravelle's eastern trade routes, and maintained a cold peace with the south through patience and a willingness to absorb small provocations without response. His reputation in diplomatic circles is that he is very difficult to provoke into a mistake.

This is accurate. It is also the product of considerable personal cost.

## The Council

Eddan governs with a council he does not fully trust. This is not naivety — he is aware of their limitations and has mapped most of their competing interests with reasonable accuracy. Several have Auralis-adjacent sympathies, whether ideological or financial. Others are simply self-interested in ways that make their behavior unpredictable under pressure. He manages them carefully, feeds them the information they need and not much more, and makes the decisions that matter alone or with one or two people whose loyalty he is more confident of.

He has no illusions about this arrangement. A council you cannot trust is still more useful than no council at all, provided you never forget what it is.

## Family

Eddan's wife died some years ago. He has not remarried and shows no inclination to.

His elder son — the crown prince, the one who had been shaped for rulership from childhood — died several years back during a territorial expansion campaign east of the capital. It was not a battle death. The campaign was successful. The death was an accident of the kind that happens when men push into difficult terrain, and it left no one to blame and nothing to resolve. Eddan grieved it, set it down, and kept working. That is what he does.

His younger son, [[Cael]], is nineteen. He was never prepared for what he now is. He spends his evenings at parties, keeps company that does not challenge him, and has so far declined every serious attempt to begin his education in governance. Eddan watches this with something more complicated than frustration — a boy who watched his brother leave for a campaign and come back in a box has earned some time before being asked to become that brother. The question is how much time [[Solumora]] is going to allow.

The succession question sits at the back of everything Eddan does. He is sixty-something years old, in reasonable health, managing a kingdom against a patient adversary, with an heir who is not ready. He does not discuss this openly. He thinks about it constantly.

## Relationship with Auralis

Eddan's assessment of [[Cassia|Queen Cassia of Auralis]] is respectful and, he believes, accurate: she is patient, she is competent, and she wants things that are incompatible with [[Terravelle]]'s continued independence. What he does not know — what he has no reason to know — is that the patience and the ambition belong primarily to [[The Council of Auralis]] rather than to Cassia herself. He does not hate her. He does not underestimate her. He intends to outlast her current position, and if he cannot do that, he intends to leave Cael a kingdom that is still defensible.

Whether Cael will be able to defend it is the question he has no good answer to.

## The Border Problem

Eddan is aware of the [[Rift Incursions|incursion situation]] on Terravelle's southern border. He knows more about the scale of it than Auralis believes he does, and he has been managing it quietly — border deployments justified through other documentation, resources allocated without formal acknowledgment of what they are for. He cannot escalate formally without Auralis's cooperation, which requires Auralis to admit a problem exists that they have decided not to admit.

He has not found a solution to this. He has found ways to manage it. He is not certain those are the same thing, and the gap between them bothers him more than he shows.
