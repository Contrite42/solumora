[[The Ancestral Current]] is a folk tradition, not an organized religion, and this is both its defining characteristic and its primary source of friction with formal religious institutions. The Current has no clergy, no theology, no central authority, and no interest in institutional recognition. It persists through family practice and community custom, and it does not argue with the formal religions of [[Solumora]] because it operates in spaces those religions have not claimed.

This indifference is mutual until the institutions decide it should not be.

## The Core Practice

The Ancestral Current holds that a practitioner's [[Flux]] capacity does not vanish at death but dissipates gradually into the places where that person worked. The strongest taboo in the tradition is disturbing a practitioner's workspace in the first year after their death — the period when dissipation is most active. After that year, the workspace can be reorganized, the tools can be passed on, and life continues.

The practice is maintained through family routines rather than ceremony. You do not pray to your ancestors; you maintain their spaces. You do not build shrines; you preserve tools. You do not conduct elaborate rituals; you observe a year of non-interference and then move on.

This is not dramatic. It is not theologically ambitious. It is a set of habits around how to treat the places where serious work happened, and most families practicing the Current would resist calling it religion at all.

## The Ascendant Path's Official Position

The [[Ascendant Path]]'s formal theology does not have a clear doctrine on what happens to Flux capacity after death. The Path focuses on the living practitioner's advancement, not on what persists afterward. This means the Path has no theological grounds to condemn the Ancestral Current directly, but it also has no framework to accommodate it.

The [[Flux Users#The Luminants|Luminants]]' official position is studied ambiguity: the Ancestral Current is not incompatible with Path theology, but it has not been harmonized with Path principles, and practitioners should be cautious about adopting folk practices that lack formal doctrinal support. This is not condemnation. It is also not endorsement. It is the Path's standard approach to traditions it cannot suppress but does not want to legitimize.

In practice, this means Path-aligned families in rural [[Auralis]] often practice the Ancestral Current alongside Path observance without anyone framing it as contradiction. The Path officiates at births, tier assessments, and marriages; the Current governs what happens in the year after a practitioner's death. The two traditions address different questions and do not compete.

The tension emerges when the Path tries to extend its authority into spaces the Current already occupies. If a Luminant suggests that a family's Current-based practices are theologically questionable, the family's response is usually some version of: we have been doing this longer than the Path has existed, and we are not interested in Path approval. The Luminant cannot easily counter this, because the Current's claim to precedence is accurate.

## Covenant Practitioners and the Current

The [[Covenant of Measure]] and the Ancestral Current coexist in [[Terravelle]] without friction, because the Covenant is an ethics of living and the Current is a practice around dying. The Covenant teaches proportionality in Flux use; the Current teaches respect for the places where Flux work accumulated. These are not competing claims.

Covenant practitioners who also observe Current practices see no contradiction. The Covenant governs how you work; the Current governs how you honor the work of those who came before. A rural family in Terravelle might be devout in their Covenant restraint while also maintaining strict Current taboos around a grandparent's workshop. Neither tradition objects, because neither tradition claims exclusive authority over what practitioners do.

The rare tension between Covenant and Current practice arises when economic necessity conflicts with Current taboos. A family business that needs to reorganize a workspace before the first year is up faces a choice: violate the Current's primary taboo or delay necessary work. Covenant elders, when consulted, usually advise finding a compromise — reorganizing only what is absolutely necessary, marking the disturbed space ritually, acknowledging the violation openly. This is not a theological ruling. It is practical guidance from people who understand that survival sometimes requires bending traditions that were designed for more stable circumstances.

## The Institutional Problem

The Ancestral Current's primary friction with institutional religion is not theological. It is jurisdictional. The Current operates in a space — the treatment of practitioners' workspaces, the handling of death, the question of what persists — that formal institutions would prefer to control, and the Current does not recognize institutional authority in that space.

When a high-tier practitioner dies in a city with strong Path presence, the Luminants officiate at the funeral and frame the practitioner's life in Path terms: the journey walked, the advancement achieved, the example set. This is the Path's role. The Ancestral Current does not contest it.

What happens after the funeral is not the Path's role, and this is where friction appears. If the practitioner's family maintains Current practices — leaving the workspace undisturbed, treating the practitioner's tools with ritual care, observing the year-long taboo — the Luminants sometimes frame this as folk superstition that distracts from the Path's theological focus on the living. The family's response, when they bother to articulate one, is that the Luminants are welcome to their theology, and the family will continue doing what families have always done.

The Path cannot suppress this. The Current is too decentralized, too embedded in family practice, and too indifferent to institutional approval. What the Path can do, and does, is frame Current practices as backward, rural, insufficiently sophisticated — cultural residue that educated practitioners should outgrow.

This framing works in cities, where Path influence is strong and younger practitioners are more likely to adopt Path norms. It does not work in rural communities, where the Current predates the Path by centuries and where family memory of how things are done is stronger than institutional instruction on how things should be done.

## The High-Tier Problem

The Ancestral Current's most uncomfortable institutional friction involves very high-tier practitioners. If Flux dissipates gradually, a [[Control Tier|T7 or T8]] practitioner's workspace would hold more significant presence for longer. Certain rural communities with histories of high-tier practitioners treat those sites accordingly — with more caution, more maintenance, more care around the taboo period.

Institutional representatives — both Path Luminants and Terravelle guild coordinators — sometimes regard this heightened attention as ancestor worship, which carries different theological and political implications than simple folk practice. Ancestor worship suggests belief in persistent agency, ongoing influence, and the possibility of contact. The Current's actual position is more modest: high-tier practitioners leave stronger traces, and those traces take longer to dissipate, but they are not conscious, not responsive, and not entities you can communicate with.

The distinction matters to institutions. Folk practice around dissipating Flux presence is one thing. A practice that implies high-tier practitioners continue influencing the world after death is another, because it challenges the Path's doctrinal monopoly on what advancement means and the Covenant's practical focus on living practitioners only.

The Current does not argue this point. Families practicing the tradition know what they observe, and they do not need institutional validation. The institutions cannot easily dismiss the observation — enough practitioners have reported sensing something in spaces where high-tier work accumulated that outright denial looks like willful ignorance. The result is an uneasy standoff: the institutions do not endorse the Current's interpretation, the Current does not seek institutional endorsement, and both sides avoid explicit confrontation because neither has a mechanism to resolve it.

## Wren and the Halveth Exception

[[Wren]]'s presence in [[Halveth]] for thirty years has done something to the border road that experienced practitioners notice. Wren is not dead, so the Ancestral Current's framework does not technically apply, but the folk intuition that sustained high-tier Flux work accumulates in a place is, in Halveth's case, accurate in ways that are hard to dismiss as superstition.

The Ancestral Current's practitioners regard Halveth as confirmation that their understanding of Flux presence is correct. High-tier work leaves traces that persist, accumulate, and can be sensed by people who know what to pay attention to. Wren's case is just the most extreme version of something that happens wherever practitioners work seriously for extended periods.

Institutional representatives do not have a clean counter to this. The Halveth effect is real enough that denying it would require dismissing the observations of too many credible practitioners. The compromise position is to acknowledge that Halveth is unusual without endorsing the Current's broader framework: Wren is a T9, Halveth is a unique case, and extrapolating from edge cases to general principles is not sound reasoning.

The Current's response, when it bothers to respond, is that the entire tradition is built on observations of what actually happens in the world, and if institutions are uncomfortable with those observations, that is the institutions' problem, not the Current's.

## The Persistence Problem

The fundamental friction between the Ancestral Current and institutional religion is that the Current does not need institutions. It persists through family transmission, community recognition of what works, and direct observation of Flux presence in spaces where work happened. No clergy required, no theology required, no institutional validation required.

This makes it nearly impossible to control, nearly impossible to suppress, and nearly impossible to co-opt. The Path cannot absorb the Current because the Current does not want to be absorbed. The Covenant cannot redirect the Current because the Current is not trying to go anywhere. The tradition simply continues, generation after generation, in the same decentralized, informal, stubbornly persistent way it always has.

Institutional representatives sometimes describe the Ancestral Current as "pre-theological" — a stage of religious development that more organized traditions have moved beyond. The Current's practitioners would not contest this. They are not interested in theology. They are interested in maintaining the spaces where their grandparents worked, observing a year of non-interference when someone dies, and passing those practices to their children. If institutions want to call that pre-theological, they are welcome to. It does not change what families do.

The Current is not winning an argument with institutional religion. It is declining to have the argument in the first place, and this refusal is its most effective form of resistance.
