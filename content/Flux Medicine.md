Healing via [[Flux]] is not a separate discipline from other [[Flux]] practice — it uses the same sigil structures, the same casting mechanics, the same [[Control Tier|tier-dependent]] capability limits. What makes it a distinct field is the domain it applies to: the body, its systems, and the Flux pathways that run through both.

The body interacts with Flux in ways that are distinct from inert materials. Living tissue has its own ambient Flux signature that shifts with health and illness. Flux pathways — the channels through which a person's casting ability flows — are structural features that can be assessed, damaged, and in some cases repaired. The relationship between Flux and biology is complex enough that practitioners who specialize in it tend to spend careers on it, and the gap between a general practitioner and a specialist is significant.

## Diagnostic vs. Therapeutic Practice

Flux medicine separates into two related but distinct capabilities: diagnosis and treatment.

**Diagnostic** work is the reading of a body's Flux signature, tissue state, and pathway structure to identify what is wrong. This is fundamentally an assessment discipline — the practitioner is gathering information rather than intervening. Diagnostic accuracy scales with [[Control Tier|tier]]: a T2 practitioner can read surface states and general signatures, detect heat abnormalities and the broad patterns of illness, and identify standard presentations reliably. A T4 practitioner can read below the surface into tissue and pathway structure, identifying presentations that look normal at the surface but show abnormality at depth. Higher tiers extend this sensitivity further, down to Flux pathway states that require T6 or above to read with precision.

**Therapeutic** work is intervention — actively using Flux to address what diagnosis has identified. This also scales with tier, and the two skills do not always come together in the same practitioner. A high-tier diagnostician who has not developed the specific technique for a given intervention may be able to identify a problem precisely while lacking the ability to address it. The specialized nature of high-tier medical work means that even practitioners with the theoretical tier ceiling for a procedure may lack the years of specific practice it requires.

## Tier and Practice

The following general ranges describe what is accessible at each tier level. Individual practitioners vary significantly based on specialization and experience.

**T1–T2**: The range of general walk-in practice. Stabilization of common injuries, treatment of standard infections and inflammatory states, bone setting with Flux support, chemical filtration for toxic exposures. Diagnostic work at this level identifies most common conditions accurately. Cases that fall outside this range can usually be identified as requiring higher-level care, even if the practitioner cannot diagnose the specific problem. Most of the population in both kingdoms receives medical care at this level or below.

**T3**: The working grade for most guild-certified medical practitioners. Broader diagnostic range, more precise interventions, capability for complex wound treatment and some surgical applications. At T3, a practitioner can begin to detect the Flux pathway irregularities that indicate certain neurological and systemic conditions.

**T4**: The threshold for what is called deep diagnostic work — assessment of Flux pathway structure that cannot be read at lower tiers. Conditions that present normally at the surface but have structural origins below it require T4 diagnostic capability to accurately identify. T4 also enables direct intervention in Flux pathway damage, which T3 cannot do. This is the level of the certified chapter's senior practitioners in major cities, and it is the standard for practice in high-stakes applications — civil injuries, unusual presentations, cases referred up from general practitioners.

**T5 and above**: Rare and highly specialized. At these tiers, practitioners can address structural collapses in Flux pathways that T4 cannot resolve, work on cases that have been assessed as untreatable at lower grades, and perform interventions whose complexity exceeds what the guild certification system formally describes. Most people will never require this level of care. The practitioners who provide it are few enough that access is a significant variable in outcomes.

## The Certified and the Informal

Both kingdoms have formal medical certification structures — in [[Terravelle]], through the [[Trade Guilds|guild system]], with tier-graded certification standards and documentation requirements; in [[Auralis]], through the [[Ascendant Path]]'s institutional network, which integrates medical practice into the advancement framework.

The certified system covers most of the formal population in both kingdoms. It has documentation requirements, referral networks, and the institutional infrastructure of established practice. It also has gaps.

The documentation requirements and the formal intake process exclude populations who cannot or will not engage with them. Undocumented workers, people with complicated status, people with conditions that would attract official attention — these populations often access care through informal practitioners who have the same technical capabilities as certified practitioners but operate outside the formal structure. This is not technically legal in most jurisdictions, but it is consistent and practiced enough that the distinction is administrative rather than practical.

Rural populations in both kingdoms are served primarily by informal healers — practitioners who learned their skills through apprenticeship or self-training, work at the tier they have, and address the presentations they can address. In [[Terravelle]]'s rural areas, informal healers are a normal feature of community life rather than an exception to it. A settlement two days from the nearest certified practitioner does not wait two days for care that a local T2 can provide.

## Flux Pathway Damage

Flux pathway damage is the category of medical condition most specific to Flux medicine. Ordinary illness and injury can be addressed by healers who understand biology; pathway damage requires a practitioner who can read and work within the Flux dimension of a person's physical structure.

Pathway damage has multiple causes: overextension during casting, exposure to certain categories of Flux anomaly, specific types of injury in the vicinity of active sigil work, and congenital variations in pathway structure that produce instability. The symptoms range from reduced casting capacity to neurological effects that a practitioner without Flux diagnostic capability would misidentify entirely. Cases that require above-standard care are most commonly pathway cases — the condition that looks like something a T2 can handle but is not.
