# Marn Drest

**Marn Drest** is lead coordinator of [[Maren Timber Collective]], [[Terravelle]]'s largest cooperative logging operation. He is fifty-four years old, [[Control Tier|T2]], and has managed Collective operations for eleven years following three decades as field logger and sawmill operator. His role represents facilitative leadership rather than hierarchical control — he coordinates rather than commands, builds consensus rather than issuing directives, and views long-term forest stewardship as moral obligation transcending immediate economic considerations.

Marn came to forestry through family tradition. His grandfather helped found the Collective sixty-eight years ago; his father worked Collective territories for forty-three years; Marn joined operations at sixteen and never considered alternative employment. This multi-generational continuity shapes his perspective — forests he maintains will be harvested by his children and grandchildren. Depleting present resources for short-term profit constitutes family betrayal, not merely economic miscalculation.

## Position and Responsibilities

**Operational Coordination:** Schedule harvest operations, allocate crews to territories, coordinate sawmill processing, and manage merchant contracts. Unlike institutional management (hierarchical authority), Collective coordination operates through consensus-building. Marn proposes schedules based on forest-health assessment and merchant commitments; Collective membership discusses, modifies, and approves through quarterly governance meetings.

**Merchant Relationship Management:** Negotiate annual contracts with three [[Trade Guilds|merchant houses]] purchasing bulk timber, manage direct sales to premium customers (shipbuilding operations), and coordinate transport logistics. Relationship management emphasizes long-term stability over maximum short-term pricing — Collective prefers predictable contracted revenue to volatile spot-market transactions.

**Forest-Health Assessment:** Lead annual forest surveys assessing regeneration rates, identifying harvest-ready territories, and detecting ecological stress indicators (disease, pest infestation, drought impact). Assessment combines traditional knowledge (visual assessment, historical comparison, intuitive judgment developed through decades of observation) with emerging quantitative measurement (growth-rate tracking, species-composition analysis). Marn synthesizes assessment data into harvest recommendations balancing immediate production needs against long-term sustainability.

**Conflict Resolution:** Mediate territorial disputes, address compensation disagreements, and facilitate consensus when Collective members disagree about harvest schedules or policy changes. Resolution emphasizes dialogue, transparency, and appeal to shared values (long-term sustainability, collective benefit, mutual aid) rather than imposed arbitration. Success depends on members recognizing Marn's legitimacy derives from experience and commitment to Collective welfare rather than formal authority.

**External Relations:** Interface with [[Terravelle Administration]], respond to military procurement approaches, and coordinate with other guild operations. Recent challenge: [[Auralis]] military representatives attempting to negotiate accelerated timber contracts. Marn's response: polite refusal citing sustainability limits and unwillingness to over-harvest despite premium pricing offered. This refusal creates potential future conflict if institutional pressure escalates.

## Leadership Philosophy

Marn views leadership as service to collective-defined objectives rather than exercise of personal authority. His role facilitates Collective functioning rather than imposing vision. This philosophy reflects Collective organizational culture and Marn's personality (uncomfortable with hierarchical power, skeptical of concentrated decision-making authority).

**Consensus Building:** Major decisions emerge through discussion rather than decree. Marn identifies issues requiring collective choice, facilitates meeting discussions exploring options, and works toward member agreement. When consensus proves elusive, he brings in experienced elders for mediation or defers decisions until additional information clarifies choice. Rarely uses formal voting — preference for discussion continuing until shared understanding emerges.

**Transparency:** Collective finances, harvest data, merchant contracts, and operational challenges openly shared with membership. Marn maintains comprehensive records accessible to any member requesting review. Transparency serves multiple functions: prevents corruption, builds trust, and enables informed member participation in governance discussions.

**Long-Term Orientation:** Decisions evaluated against multi-generational timeline rather than annual profit cycles. Marn frequently references "what forests will look like when my grandchildren harvest here" as decision framework. This orientation frustrates some younger members seeking higher immediate income but resonates with multi-generational families viewing themselves as forest stewards rather than resource extractors.

## Contemporary Wartime Challenges

**Resisting Institutional Acceleration Pressure:** [[Auralis]] military procurement attempted negotiating 40-50% output increase at premium pricing. Marn led Collective deliberation concluding acceleration contradicts sustainability mandates regardless of economic benefit. Refusal creates institutional resentment — military representatives view Collective as uncooperative during national emergency.

Marn recognizes refusal carries risk: precedent exists for institutional seizure of productive resources under military-necessity justification. If [[Terravelle]] adopts similar policies or [[Auralis]] directly coerces compliance, Collective loses autonomy. His calculation: maintaining sustainability principles worth institutional conflict risk. Whether membership continues supporting this position as economic pressure escalates remains uncertain.

**Managing Labor-Conscription Impact:** Military service removed 15-20 younger workers producing 15-20% output decline. Remaining members extend hours attempting compensation but cannot fully replace lost capacity. Marn coordinates schedule adjustments distributing additional labor across membership and negotiates merchant-contract modifications reducing committed volumes.

More concerning: conscripted members may not return. If military casualties are substantial, Collective loses next-generation workers whose family knowledge would sustain operations. Marn has no authority preventing conscription — can only document losses and plan operations around reduced capacity.

**Navigating Profit-Versus-Principle Tension:** Wartime timber scarcity drives market prices upward. Collective benefits from higher pricing but faces internal debate: some members advocate expanding harvest capitalizing on pricing opportunity; others insist maintaining sustainable limits regardless of profit. Marn facilitates discussion without imposing resolution.

His personal position: environmental stewardship transcends short-term economics. Depleting forests for war-driven profit constitutes moral failure. However, he recognizes member families face economic hardship from inflation and reduced purchasing power. Dismissing economic concerns as mere greed oversimplifies legitimate tension. Resolution requires finding balance his facilitative leadership role cannot dictate.

**Maintaining Collective Solidarity:** External pressure and internal economic stress strain collective bonds. Some members privately question whether long-term sustainability matters if near-term military conflict produces catastrophic outcomes. Others resent foregoing profit opportunities while watching independent operators accelerate extraction capturing higher market pricing.

Marn addresses solidarity erosion through reinforcing shared identity — Collective members are not employees but co-owners participating in multi-generational enterprise. Depleting forests for immediate gain betrays children who inherit degraded resource base. Appeals to identity help maintain cohesion but effectiveness depends on members valuing continuity over short-term advantage.

## Personal Values and World

 View

Marn's core conviction: humans are temporary stewards of resources they did not create and do not own. Forests existed before Collective; will exist after. Current generation's responsibility: maintain forest health enabling future generations equivalent opportunities.

This stewardship philosophy frequently conflicts with market logic treating forests as capital to be liquidated for maximum return. Marn is not anti-profit — Collective members require income surviving economically. But profit pursuit cannot overwhelm sustainability mandates without transforming stewardship into exploitation.

He observes [[Auralis]] institutional extraction operations (clear-cutting, accelerated schedules, environmental degradation) with dismay. Those operations maximize short-term output producing long-term resource depletion and ecological damage. Justifications appeal to military necessity and national emergency — Marn considers these rationalizations for exploitation rather than legitimate priorities.

War does not suspend ecological limits. Forests clear-cut today require 60-100 years regeneration regardless of military outcomes. Post-war societies will face consequences of present acceleration decisions. Marn views his role as maintaining sustainable alternative demonstrating long-term thinking produces better outcomes than short-term extraction maximization.

## Personal Life and Relationships

Marn lives in small cabin adjacent to Collective sawmill complex. Widower (wife died six years ago from prolonged illness); two adult children both active in Collective operations. Maintains close relationships with core Collective families through shared meals, informal gatherings, and mutual-aid participation.

Social life entirely embedded within Collective community. Marn does not participate in regional social events, rarely travels beyond Collective territories except for merchant negotiations, and has minimal contact with urban [[Terravelle]] culture. This insularity reflects both personality (preference for familiar community over diverse social engagement) and commitment (Collective operations require sustained attention leaving limited time for external activities).

Colleagues describe him as patient, deliberate, unflappable in crisis, and occasionally frustrating when consensus-building extends decisions beyond what others consider reasonable timeline. His facilitative approach sometimes interpreted as indecisiveness though those working with him longer recognize he deliberately avoids imposing personal judgment in place of collective wisdom.

## Future Trajectory

If Collective survives wartime pressure without institutional seizure or economic collapse, Marn likely continues coordination another 5-10 years before retiring. Succession planning informal but emerging — his daughter and 2-3 senior members developing facilitator skills through increasing responsibility delegation.

If institutional seizure occurs transforming Collective into hierarchical operation, Marn will likely resign rather than manage under external control. Whether resignation constitutes principled stand or abandonment of members during crisis depends on circumstances and personal interpretation. Marn has not decided how he would respond — hopes situation never requires choosing.

His aspiration: Retire knowing forests remain healthy, Collective survives as viable cooperative model, and next generation inherits functioning system demonstrating sustainable forestry practice works economically and ecologically. Whether wartime conditions permit realizing this aspiration remains uncertain.

What is certain: Marn will continue advocating long-term sustainability over short-term extraction regardless of institutional pressure or economic incentives. This is not heroism — it is commitment to values embedded through multi-generational participation in forestry community viewing itself as stewards rather than owners.
