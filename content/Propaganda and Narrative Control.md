**Propaganda and Narrative Control** are the systematic efforts by [[Auralis]] and [[Terravelle]] to shape how their populations understand events, interpret policy, and perceive the opposing kingdom. Neither kingdom operates centralized propaganda ministries. Instead, both use distributed influence through institutional channels, selective disclosure, and narrative framing to ensure their preferred interpretation reaches settlements before competing versions do.

This is not about lying. Most propaganda in Solumora is based on true information, framed to emphasize specific interpretations while minimizing others. The goal is not to fabricate reality but to control which aspects of reality people notice and how they understand those aspects.

## Methods

### Selective Disclosure

Both kingdoms control what information becomes public knowledge and when disclosure occurs.

**[[Auralis]] Approach:**
- [[The Council of Auralis]] decides what events are announced publicly, what details are included, and how long delays occur before disclosure
- Positive developments (research breakthroughs, successful expeditions, economic growth) are announced quickly with detailed explanations
- Negative developments (enforcement failures, [[Rift Incursions]], diplomatic setbacks) are announced late, with minimal detail, often framed as "under investigation" or "being addressed"
- Crisis information is released on schedules that minimize public reaction — tax increases announced after market fairs, enforcement incidents disclosed when attention is diverted elsewhere

**[[Terravelle]] Approach:**
- [[Trade Guilds]] control information flow through commercial networks, deciding what gets communicated to settlements and how urgently
- Information that supports guild interests (trade opportunities, competitor failures, policy changes benefiting commerce) spreads rapidly through merchant gossip
- Information that challenges guild authority (internal disputes, corruption allegations, failed ventures) is suppressed or delayed until it can be contextualized to minimize damage
- [[Terravelle Administration]] coordinates with guilds to ensure official announcements align with merchant messaging, creating consistent narrative

The practical difference: Auralis controls timing through administrative authority (government decides when to post notices). Terravelle controls timing through commercial coordination (guilds decide what merchants emphasize when talking to customers and partners).

### Framing

The same event can be described in ways that lead to opposite conclusions. Propaganda works by ensuring the preferred framing reaches populations first and most persuasively.

**Example: Border Customs Dispute**

A shipment of [[Grimoires]] is delayed at [[Halveth]] customs for three days. The cargo includes [[Rare Grimoire]] texts purchased legally in [[Auralis]] by [[Terravelle]] buyers.

**Auralis Framing:** "Terravelle customs authorities detained a legitimate trade shipment for politically motivated inspection, disrupting commerce and demonstrating their unwillingness to honor trade agreements. This is part of a broader pattern of Terravelle obstruction designed to undermine Auralis economic interests."

**Terravelle Framing:** "Customs personnel identified irregularities in grimoire authentication documentation, requiring additional verification to prevent black market materials from entering our kingdom. The delay protected Terravelle's regulatory standards and demonstrated our commitment to preventing Auralis from exploiting trade channels to circumvent our tier-access restrictions."

Both descriptions refer to the same event. Both emphasize factually accurate details. But Auralis frames the event as obstruction, while Terravelle frames it as prudent oversight. Populations in each kingdom receive their government's framing first, through official notices and merchant networks aligned with institutional interests. By the time competing narratives arrive (if they arrive at all), initial framing has already shaped perception.

### Omission

Information that does not fit preferred narratives is not actively suppressed — it is simply not mentioned. Over time, unreported events disappear from public awareness.

**[[Flux]] Crime Underreporting:** Both kingdoms underreport Flux-based crime to avoid creating perception that enforcement is failing. A T4 practitioner using Flux to manipulate commercial contracts gets prosecuted quietly, with no public announcement. Settlements never learn the crime occurred. Result: populations believe Flux crime is rarer than it actually is, reducing political pressure for expanded enforcement while maintaining the appearance of control.

**Expedition Disasters:** Failed expeditions in the [[Equatorial Desert]] are not systematically disclosed. A crossing that results in multiple deaths due to guide negligence or inadequate preparation might be noted in official records but never announced publicly. Guides like [[Avel Coss]] know about these failures through professional networks. General public does not. Result: populations underestimate desert crossing risks, allowing continued expedition activity without political pressure for stricter regulation.

**Diplomatic Failures:** Negotiations between [[Auralis]] and [[Terravelle]] that collapse without reaching agreement are not announced. The public learns only about negotiations that succeed or situations that escalate to the point where concealment is no longer possible. Result: populations do not realize how often their governments attempt cooperation and fail, maintaining the narrative that the opposing kingdom is categorically uncooperative.

### Amplification

Information that supports institutional interests is deliberately amplified through repetition, official endorsement, and distribution across multiple channels.

**[[Auralis]] Research Successes:** When [[Flux]] research programs produce results, [[The Council of Auralis]] ensures announcements reach every settlement. Notices are posted, merchants are briefed, couriers are encouraged to mention it in their gossip. By the time the information finishes circulating, it has been reinforced dozens of times: "Auralis continues to lead in Flux advancement."

**[[Terravelle]] Economic Stability:** When [[Maren Freight Chain]] logistics achieve smooth operation, [[Trade Guilds]] ensure settlement populations hear about it. Merchants emphasize reliability, guild administrators cite successful cargo delivery statistics, public houses circulate stories about seamless trade operations. Message: "Terravelle's pragmatic governance delivers consistent results."

Amplification works because repetition creates familiarity, and familiarity is often mistaken for truth. A claim heard from five different sources seems more credible than a claim heard once, even if all five sources originate from the same institutional directive.

## Target Audiences

Propaganda is not uniform. Different messages are crafted for different populations, based on what those populations already believe and what behavior the kingdom wants to encourage.

### Urban Populations (Solhaven, Valdenmoor, Emberfall, Ashford)

Urban centers have diverse information sources: merchants, couriers, travelers, guild members. Populations are harder to control because competing narratives arrive quickly. Propaganda focuses on:

- Reinforcing institutional legitimacy ("Your government is competent and working in your interests")
- Framing the opposing kingdom as dangerous, unreasonable, or untrustworthy
- Emphasizing economic success, research progress, social stability

### Rural Populations

Rural settlements have fewer information sources. News arrives late and incomplete. Propaganda focuses on:

- Providing simple, memorable narratives that require no additional context
- Positioning central government as protector of rural interests against external threats
- Emphasizing continuity and stability (minimize mention of policy changes unless absolutely necessary)

### [[Control Tier|High-Tier Flux Users]]

T3-T5 practitioners are politically important, well-informed, and skeptical of obvious manipulation. Propaganda focuses on:

- Framing policy in terms of Flux advancement and research priorities
- Emphasizing alignment between institutional goals and practitioner interests
- Providing detailed technical justifications for decisions (high-tier users dismiss simplistic narratives)

### Border Populations (Halveth, Ashford, frontier settlements)

Border communities interact with both kingdoms and have direct exposure to cross-border tensions. Propaganda focuses on:

- Justifying security measures and customs procedures as necessary protection
- Framing the opposing kingdom as source of risk (smuggling, instability, hostile intent)
- Emphasizing local importance ("Your settlement is critical to kingdom security")

## Propaganda vs. Misinformation

Propaganda is institutionally-directed narrative shaping. Misinformation is uncontrolled false or misleading information that spreads through social networks without central coordination.

Governments distinguish between the two:
- Propaganda (their own messaging) is "strategic communication" or "public information"
- Misinformation (competing narratives or grassroots rumors) is "destabilizing falsehood" or "malicious distortion"

In practice, both are forms of narrative control. The difference is who controls the narrative.

### Government-Generated Misinformation

Both kingdoms occasionally seed false information to serve strategic goals:

**Concealing Capabilities:** [[Auralis]] has, on at least three documented occasions, publicly downplayed advances in [[Flux]]-based detection methods while privately deploying those methods at customs posts. This misleads [[Terravelle]] about Auralis's enforcement capabilities, allowing more effective interdiction of smuggled [[Grimoires]].

**Misdirection During Crises:** When [[Rift Incursions]] threaten settlements, both kingdoms have released incomplete or slightly inaccurate information to prevent panic while organizing response. A Rift appearing near housing districts might be publicly described as being "under monitoring" when in fact evacuation is being planned. This prevents premature evacuation that could cause chaos, but also misleads populations about actual risk.

**Discrediting Opposition:** When individuals criticize government policy publicly, institutional responses sometimes include releasing information (true or exaggerated) that harms the critic's credibility. A merchant who complains about trade restrictions might suddenly face publicized investigations into tax compliance, whether or not actual violations exist.

### Merchant-Generated Misinformation

Commercial operators spread false or exaggerated information to manipulate prices, undermine competitors, or create market opportunities:

- Rumors about supply shortages to justify price increases
- False reports about competitor product failures or unsafe practices
- Exaggerated accounts of route disruptions to redirect trade traffic

[[Merchant House Intelligence Network]] participants exploit this actively, seeding misinformation through trusted channels while using their own intelligence to know the truth and act counter to market panic they created.

### Grassroots Misinformation

Ordinary people repeat and distort information based on incomplete understanding, fear, or desire to be the first to share dramatic news:

- [[Flux Demons|Flux Demon]] sightings reported in locations where no Flux activity occurred (misidentification of natural phenomena)
- Exaggerated accounts of crimes or violence (retold stories where details shift in each retelling)
- Conspiracy theories about government actions (interpreting incompetence or bureaucratic delay as evidence of hidden plots)

This misinformation spreads faster than corrections can reach affected populations, creating persistent false beliefs that governments struggle to counter.

## Counter-Propaganda

Both kingdoms attempt to undermine the other's messaging, though their methods differ.

**[[Auralis]] Approach:**
- Publicly dispute [[Terravelle]] claims through official notices and diplomatic channels
- Use merchant contacts in [[Terravelle]] to spread alternative narratives within Terravelle territory
- Emphasize Terravelle policy failures and internal contradictions
- Frame Terravelle restraint as cowardice, inefficiency, or fear of Auralis superiority

**[[Terravelle]] Approach:**
- Allow Auralis propaganda to circulate, then provide detailed rebuttals through [[Trade Guilds|guild]] channels
- Use [[Merchant House Intelligence Network]] to identify which Auralis claims are gaining traction, focusing counter-messaging on those
- Emphasize Auralis recklessness, resource waste, and disregard for consequences
- Frame Auralis ambition as dangerous instability threatening both kingdoms

Neither approach is fully effective. Populations already inclined to trust their own government tend to accept official narratives and dismiss counter-propaganda as the other side lying. Border populations, exposed to both kingdoms' messaging, develop more cynical views — they assume both kingdoms are manipulating information and rely on direct observation rather than official claims.

## Practical Impact

**For Government Legitimacy:**
- Populations that believe their government is competent, acting in their interests, and responding effectively to problems are more cooperative and less likely to resist policy
- Loss of narrative control — when governments cannot shape how events are understood — leads to erosion of institutional authority

**For Border Tensions:**
- Both kingdoms' propaganda frames the other as unreasonable, dangerous, or hostile
- Populations in each kingdom genuinely believe their side is responding defensively
- This mutual distrust makes negotiation and coordination harder, even when both governments would benefit from cooperation

**For Information Trade:**
- Intelligence brokers like [[Tolla Rend's Intelligence Exchange]] profit by providing information that contradicts official narratives
- Access to non-propaganda information becomes valuable commodity, sold to those who need accurate assessment more than reassuring messaging

## Contemporary Concerns

**Escalation Cycle:** As [[Auralis]]-[[Terravelle]] relations deteriorate, propaganda intensifies. Each kingdom's messaging becomes more aggressive, framing the other as existential threat. This creates political environment where leaders cannot easily pursue de-escalation without appearing to capitulate to enemy narrative.

**Erosion of Trust:** Populations increasingly aware that official information is shaped for strategic purposes become skeptical of all institutional messaging. This makes crisis communication harder — when governments announce actual emergencies, populations discount warnings as probable exaggeration.

**Information Warfare:** Both kingdoms now treat narrative control as strategic priority comparable to economic or military concerns. Resources allocated to monitoring opposing kingdom's messaging, developing counter-narratives, and coordinating institutional communication have increased significantly over past five years.

## Key Figures

**Bren Alvst**, [[Control Tier|T3]], 46 years old, serves as information coordinator for [[The Council of Auralis]]. His role is advising the Council on public communication strategy: what to announce, how to frame it, when to release information. He is not a propagandist — he views himself as a strategic analyst whose job is ensuring government messaging achieves desired policy outcomes. He has held this role for nine years and has become increasingly concerned that escalating propaganda commitments are limiting Auralis's diplomatic options. He cannot say this publicly without appearing disloyal.

**Vessa Trel**, T0, 52 years old, operates a news aggregation service in [[Valdenmoor]], selling subscriptions to merchants who want compiled reports about trade conditions, policy changes, and cross-border developments. She explicitly markets her service as "unfiltered information" — she includes both [[Terravelle]] official messaging and reports from [[Auralis]] sources, allowing merchants to compare narratives. She has been investigated twice by [[Terravelle]] guilds concerned that she is spreading Auralis propaganda, but both investigations concluded she was providing legitimate service to commercial clients.

**Corvan Selt**, [[Control Tier|T2]], 38 years old, works as a scribe producing pamphlets and notices for [[Auralis]] public distribution. His job is taking Council-approved messaging and converting it into accessible, persuasive language for settlement notice boards. He is good at this — his writing is clear, emotionally resonant, and effective at shaping public perception. He is also aware that much of what he writes is selective framing rather than comprehensive truth. He reconciles this by telling himself that governments must sometimes simplify information for public good.

## Comparative Perspective

[[Auralis]] treats propaganda as necessary governance tool. Populations need clear guidance about what to think and how to understand events. The goal is shaping public opinion to support Council policy, which Auralis views as aligned with kingdom's long-term interests.

[[Terravelle]] treats propaganda as commercial strategy. Information that benefits guild interests gets amplified. Information that challenges guild authority gets minimized. The goal is maintaining guild control over trade and settlement coordination, which Terravelle views as essential to social stability.

Both approaches assume populations cannot be trusted to reach "correct" conclusions without institutional guidance. Both fear that uncontrolled information flow will lead to chaos, panic, or politically destabilizing resistance.

Neither kingdom acknowledges that their propaganda creates problems they claim to be solving. By framing the opposing kingdom as categorically hostile, they ensure populations support policies that increase tensions. By underreporting failures and amplifying successes, they create expectations they cannot meet, leading to disillusionment when reality contradicts official narratives.

The alternative — transparent communication, admitting uncertainty, acknowledging trade-offs — is politically unacceptable to both kingdoms. Leaders who admit complexity lose to competitors who promise simple solutions.
