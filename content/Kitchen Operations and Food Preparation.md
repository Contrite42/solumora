**Kitchen Operations and Food Preparation** documents the labor and infrastructure through which raw food (grain, fish, preserved goods, vegetables) transforms into consumed meals at household and institutional scales. Kitchens are workplaces where T0-T2 labor converts bulk commodities into sustenance through time-intensive, skill-dependent processes. Understanding kitchens reveals both the work invisible in food system economics and the points of failure when labor or fuel supply interrupts.

## Household Food Preparation

The majority of food preparation occurs in households (working-class populations 50-60%, middle-class populations 40-50%), not institutions.

### Working-Class Household Kitchen

Most working-class households rent portions of shared housing (multiple families per building). Kitchen facilities rudimentary: hearth or small cookstove, water access (shared well or public fountain), minimal equipment (pot, plate, basic implements).

**Daily preparation routine:**

- **Early morning (pre-work):** Female household member (typically, though not exclusively) prepares morning meal: light, quick (15-30 minutes). Bread purchased from baker previous day, eaten with cheese or left-over stew if available.
- **Midday:** Cold meal (bread, cheese, salted fish if available) consumed at workplace (workers bring wrapped food).
- **Evening (main meal):** Preparation 30-60 minutes. Grain porridge base (barley or millet boiled in water, salted) with added vegetables (seasonal, preserved, or fresh garden collection), occasionally preserved meat or salted fish. Cooking by female household member or eldest child capable of fire management.

**Labor:** Food preparation is unpaid household labor. Typically 1.5-2.5 hours daily (morning quick meal, evening main meal, some weekly baking if household bakes at home instead of purchasing from bakery). Additional periodic labor: preservation work (autumn vegetable drying, occasional fish salting), storage maintenance.

**Fuel:** Wood or coal for cooking. Cost significant (5-10% of household budget). Scarcity (winter, poor wood supply) forces reduced cooking (cold meals, longer cooking times for same quantity due to reduced heat).

**Water:** Provided by public wells or fountains (most reliable), or purchased from water carriers (cost extra). Water heating requires fuel — major reason most cooking uses water sparingly (one-pot meals boiling rather than heat-cooking).

### Middle-Class Household Kitchen

More spacious kitchen (dedicated room or large shared space), better equipment, water access (household well or internal supply for elite households). More cooking fuel budget.

**Preparation standardization:** More varied meals (meat more frequent, fresh vegetables more common), time investment still substantial but with more cooking comfort (separate kitchen space, better equipment, fuel adequacy). Female household member or servants (wealthy households) manage preparation.

**Servant involvement:** Higher-income households (100+ Watts weekly) employ servants or cooks (part-time or live-in). Cooking becomes professionalized (servant's primary responsibility) rather than household member's additional labor. Wage: live-in cook 20-40 Watts weekly plus food and lodging; part-time cook 10-20 Watts weekly.

**Preservation:** More elaborate preservation operations possible (dedicated storage space, access to salt, preservation equipment). Larger annual preservation work (autumn preservation, ongoing storage management).

## Institutional Food Preparation

Taverns, military barracks, guild houses, [[Ascendant Path]] temples, and institutional kitchens serve 15-25% of food consumption (mostly middle-class and above; working-class occasional).

### Tavern Kitchens

Most common institutional eating space for working-class populations (reliable cheap meals). Tavern kitchen feeds 50-150 daily customers with limited facilities: large cooking pot, hearth, basic equipment.

**Preparation model:** Limited rotation (2-3 base meals: grain porridge base, fish preserved versions, occasional meat). Daily menu determined by ingredient availability and cook's judgment. Meal preparation simultaneous (large-scale batch cooking for multiple consumers rather than individual recipes).

**Labor:** Tavern kitchen cook (8-15 Watts daily) plus 1-2 kitchen helpers (4-8 Watts daily). Volume cooking: one pot feeding 50+ people requires lower ingredient per-serving cost (economies of scale). Meal cost to consumer: 0.3-0.6 Watts (working-class can afford occasional tavern meal, not daily).

**Labor intensity:** Batch cooking less labor-intensive than household preparation (one meal for 50 rather than 50 individual meals). But sustained heat management (fire tending), constant stirring, high-volume washing (dishes for 100 customers daily), requisition and transport logistics.

**Vulnerability:** Tavern kitchen failure (cook injury, equipment failure, fuel shortage) disrupts employment-class food access. Tavern closure even temporarily (1-3 days) creates eating crisis for working-class populations depending on daily tavern meal.

### Military Barracks Kitchens

Military camps and barracks require high-volume food service (3000-5000 soldiers per installation). Centralized kitchen feeding entire installation.

**Preparation scale:** Enormous. Example: 4000-person barracks requires 400-600 pounds daily grain, 100-150 pounds meat/fish, fuel and water for operation. Kitchen infrastructure: multiple massive cooking pots, fire management (multiple hearths), dedicated logistics (requisition, storage, waste management).

**Labor:** 20-30 dedicated kitchen staff per 1000 soldiers (cooks, helpers, logistics workers). Wages modest (10-15 Watts daily) but stable (military employment). Food quality variable (bulk preparation sometimes compromises quality; military nutrition adequate but not palatial).

**Provision sourcing:** Military requisition system (government procurement) supplies ingredients. Direct farmer purchase or institutional supply (grain from [[Basin Grain Allocation Board]], preserved goods from contracted suppliers). Removes civilian market competition (military supply chain separate), but creates civilian supply pressure (requisition removes available civilian market supply).

### Guild and Temple Institutional Kitchens

Larger guilds and [[Ascendant Path]] temples maintain kitchens for feast operations, member events, occasional relief meals. Scale smaller than military but operational infrastructure substantial.

## Fuel Scarcity and Cooking Capacity

Food preparation entirely dependent on cooking fuel availability.

### Fuel Types and Costs

- **Wood:** Primary fuel (7-10 Watts per cord, burning 5-7 days per household). Sourced from forest operations or merchant wood suppliers. Winter shortage (demand high, supply weather-dependent, prices 50%+ above summer).
- **Coal:** Higher cost (10-15 Watts per measure) but burns hotter and longer. Accessible to middle-class populations; working-class occasional (winter supplementing wood).
- **Animal dung/dried plants:** Emergency fuel (low heat, high smoke) used in regions without wood access. Cost nearly free (collected labor) but cooking difficult.

### Scarcity Cascade

Winter fuel shortage creates direct food-preparation capacity reduction. Common example: Colden-Thawmark 618 FE wood shortage (wet fall prevented proper seasoning; supply inadequate for winter demand). Wood prices spike 100-150%. Working-class households reduce cooking fire (shift to cold meals, slower-burning fuel, reduced cooking frequency). Mid-winter: measurable dietary shift (less cooked food, more raw/cold prepared food, reduced nutrient extraction from grains through cooking).

**Health impact:** Reduced cooking (particularly grain cooking reducing digestibility) correlates with nutritional status decline. Documented: winter malnutrition in years with fuel shortage more severe than years with food supply shortage.

## Cooking Equipment and Technology

Availability and quality of cooking implements affects both labor intensity and outcome quality.

### Basic Equipment (Working-Class Standard)

- Cooking pot (1-2, shared or individually owned): 5-15 Watts purchase cost
- Plate and utensil set (spoon minimally, knife if owned): 1-5 Watts
- Hearth/cookstove access: inherited (built-in rental property) or improvised

**Cost:** Basic set ~10-20 Watts (significant investment for working-class; typical household takes 2-4 months to accumulate). Equipment breakage (cracked pot, broken spoon) represents serious financial event (must replace or go without).

### Middle-Class Equipment (Enhanced)

- Multiple cooking pots (different sizes, better quality)
- Oven access or owned oven
- Roasting equipment, finer utensils
- Servants equipped with fuller toolkit

Cost: 50-150+ Watts for full household kitchen setup (accessible to established middle-class households).

### Institutional Equipment

Professional kitchens with specialized equipment: large cauldrons (heating 100+ gallons), mechanical stirrers, griddles, roasting infrastructure. Cost enormous (500-2000+ Watts for full institutional kitchen), maintained by institutions with sustained capital availability (guilds, temple, military).

## Preservation and Storage Labor

Preservation work happens alongside daily food preparation.

### Autumn Preservation Surge

Fall (Harvestfall-Coolward) vegetable preservation: drying, salting, fermenting occupies 10-20% of household food-labor capacity. Household members (typically women, sometimes entire family) spend 5-10 hours daily on preservation work for 4-6 weeks. Goal: preserve seasonal vegetables for winter consumption.

**Labor-intensive:** Physically demanding (standing, repetitive hand work, smoke exposure if smoking preservation). Requires space (drying racks, fermentation vessels) and infrastructure (salt access, containers).

**Outcome:** Preserved vegetables provide 5-15% of winter vegetable consumption; insufficient for adequate vegetables without preservation (would eat nearly zero vegetables winter without preservation work).

### Summer Fish Preservation

Coastal and river communities engage fish preservation (salting, smoking) as summer peak-catch processing. Parallel to vegetable preservation: 4-8 weeks of intense preservation labor.

## Failure Modes: When Cooking Capacity Breaks

Food systems can have adequate supply but face consumption failure if cooking capacity inadequate.

### Equipment Failure

Household pot cracks or breaks (common): family must immediately replace (financial emergency) or manage without (cold meals, requiring neighbor borrowing). Institutional kitchen equipment failure (large cauldron breaks, oven fails): capacity drops 20-50% immediately. Multi-day repair or replacement causes institutional feeding disruption.

### Fuel Interruption

Wood supply shortage (weather, transport failure, price spike): household cooking frequency drops. Populations shift to fewer cooked meals (increasing cold preparation, reducing digestible nutrient extraction). Observable dietary quality decline within 2-3 weeks.

### Cook Labor Loss

Household cook illness/injury (temporary): family management capability drops (alternative cook of lesser skill, time-intensive meal preparation). Institutional cook departure (injury, conscription, death): entire facility dependent on replacement finding. Documented barracks disruption (cook death, replacement took 2 weeks to arrive): meals degradation, morale impact, disease increase (inadequate nutrition, hygiene loss).

### Fuel/Water Simultaneous Shortage

Winter water shortage (freezing making water collection difficult) combined with fuel shortage (reduced wood, high prices) creates worst scenario: difficult to cook (limited fuel, low heat), difficult to boil water (slow, fuel-intensive). Result: reliance on cold meals, safety compromise (insufficient water boiling for hygiene).
