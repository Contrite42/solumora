**Terravelle Reputation Justice** operates through social consequences rather than formal legal penalties. In [[Terravelle]]'s guild-organized professional networks, a practitioner's reputation determines access to clients, professional standing, and economic viability. Behavior that damages reputation — misconduct, incompetence, contract breaches, or refusal to accept [[Terravelle Guild Mediation|guild mediation]] outcomes — triggers cascading social consequences that often exceed formal legal punishment in severity.

## How Reputation Functions

Professional reputation in Terravelle is maintained through three interconnected systems:

**Client Referral Networks:** Most [[Flux Users|practitioners]] acquire contracts through referrals from existing clients, guild members, or professional contacts. A practitioner with good reputation receives steady referrals. A practitioner with damaged reputation stops receiving referrals and contracts dry up. Without contracts, income collapses.

**Guild Standing:** Guild membership provides certification, workshop access, collective resources, and formal professional identity. Good standing enables full participation. Reduced standing means restricted access and fewer opportunities. Guild expulsion eliminates professional infrastructure entirely.

**Community Knowledge:** Terravelle's professional communities are smaller and more interconnected than [[Auralis]]'s urban markets. Information about practitioner behavior spreads through personal networks, guild meetings, and client conversations. A reputation for competence or misconduct propagates quickly and persists for years.

These systems create environment where reputation is survival. A practitioner cannot function professionally without good reputation, regardless of [[Control Tier]] capacity or technical skill.

## Reputation Collapse Triggers

Common triggers that damage professional reputation:

**Contract Breach:** Failing to deliver promised work, abandoning projects mid-completion, or producing substandard results that require expensive corrections. Even single serious breach can trigger reputation collapse if clients publicize the failure.

**Guild Expulsion:** Being expelled from professional guild is permanent reputation mark. Other guilds will not accept expelled members. Clients avoid practitioners without guild standing. Expulsion effectively ends careers.

**Mediation Refusal:** Refusing to participate in guild mediation when dispute arises signals unwillingness to operate within professional norms. Practitioners who refuse mediation are viewed as untrustworthy and excluded from professional networks.

**Public Misconduct:** Visible misbehavior — public intoxication, aggressive confrontations, reckless [[Flux]] use that endangers bystanders — damages reputation even when not formally prosecuted. Community members remember and avoid practitioners who demonstrate poor judgment.

**Dishonesty:** Being caught in professional lies — misrepresenting qualifications, falsifying work completion, lying about prior contracts — destroys professional trust immediately. Practitioners can recover from incompetence through supervised improvement. They rarely recover from documented dishonesty.

## Reputation Recovery

Recovering from damaged reputation is possible but difficult and time-consuming:

**Supervised Work:** A practitioner with damaged reputation but guild standing intact can work under supervision, demonstrating improved competence or reformed behavior. Successful supervised work for 6-12 months can gradually rebuild reputation.

**Client Satisfaction:** Completing contracts successfully for clients willing to hire practitioners with damaged reputations creates positive counter-evidence. If ten clients experience good work after one client experienced poor work, reputation improves incrementally.

**Guild Vouching:** If guild elders publicly vouch for a practitioner's reformed behavior, referral networks begin reopening. Elder vouching carries weight because elders risk their own reputations by endorsing practitioners with known problems.

**Time:** Minor reputation damage fades as community memory moves on. A practitioner who made mistakes five years ago and has performed competently since is evaluated based on recent work, not past failures. Serious reputation damage (expulsion, fraud, major harm) does not fade with time.

**Geographic Relocation:** Moving to settlement or region where reputation has not propagated allows fresh start. However, professional networks overlap between settlements. A practitioner expelled in [[Valdenmoor]] will find that [[Solhaven]] guilds know about the expulsion. True geographic escape requires moving to isolated rural areas with minimal guild presence — which also means limited professional opportunity.

Reputation recovery is not guaranteed. Some practitioners never recover and must exit the profession entirely.

## Comparison with Formal Punishment

Reputation consequences often exceed formal legal penalties in practical severity:

**Imprisonment vs. Expulsion:** A practitioner imprisoned for two years in Auralis formal system serves time and resumes career afterward. A practitioner expelled from Terravelle guild cannot resume career even after punishment period ends. Expulsion is permanent professional death.

**Fines vs. Referral Loss:** Auralis courts impose financial fines as punishment. Terravelle reputation damage causes income loss through referral networks collapsing. A practitioner who loses 50,000 [[Watts]] in annual income due to reputation damage suffers greater financial harm than a practitioner fined 10,000 Watts by courts.

**Documented Conviction vs. Community Knowledge:** Auralis criminal convictions are formal records but may not reach all employers immediately. Terravelle reputation damage spreads through community networks faster and more comprehensively than formal legal records. A practitioner with court conviction might relocate and find work. A practitioner with damaged reputation in guild networks cannot escape community knowledge.

## Cultural Perspective

[[Terravelle]] views reputation justice as more effective than formal legal systems because:

**Immediate Consequences:** Reputation damage happens immediately when misconduct becomes known. Formal legal prosecution takes months or years.

**Proportionate Severity:** Reputation consequences scale with misconduct severity naturally. Minor mistakes cause minor reputation damage that recovers quickly. Serious misconduct causes career-ending reputation collapse. Formal systems impose standardized sentences that may be too lenient for serious crimes or too harsh for minor ones.

**Community Enforcement:** Reputation justice is enforced by the professional community rather than distant institutions. Everyone participates — clients choose who to hire, guilds decide who to include, practitioners decide who to vouch for. This distributed enforcement does not depend on government resources or capacity.

**Restorative Potential:** Reputation can be rebuilt through demonstrated improvement. Formal legal convictions are permanent records. Terravelle's system allows redemption for practitioners who genuinely reform.

[[Auralis]] views reputation justice as arbitrary, inconsistent, and vulnerable to mob dynamics. Reputation can be damaged by false accusations, social manipulation, or community bias. Formal legal systems provide procedural protection that reputation systems lack. [[Terravelle]] practitioners counter that professional communities know members well enough to assess accusations accurately and that false accusation risk is lower than Auralis assumes.

## Limitations

Reputation justice cannot:
- Prevent rich or politically connected practitioners from leveraging influence to minimize reputation damage
- Function in areas where professional networks are weak or absent (isolated rural communities)
- Compel practitioners to change behavior — it only makes misbehavior economically unviable
- Handle crimes against non-guild parties (victims outside professional networks cannot trigger reputation consequences)
- Prevent wealthy practitioners from operating independently without guild or client referrals

These limitations are accepted as trade-offs. Reputation justice works within guild-organized communities and fails outside them. [[Terravelle]] relies on formal courts as backup for cases reputation systems cannot address.

## Intersection with Guild Mediation

[[Terravelle Guild Mediation]] and reputation justice work together as integrated system. Guild mediation resolves disputes through elder panels. Reputation justice enforces mediation outcomes through social consequences.

If a practitioner accepts mediation resolution and complies, reputation remains intact (or improves if they were in the wrong and demonstrated accountability). If a practitioner refuses mediation or violates resolution, reputation collapses and career ends.

This integration makes guild mediation functionally binding despite having no formal legal authority. Mediation works because refusing it triggers reputation consequences that practitioners cannot afford.

## Key Personnel

Reputation justice has no key personnel in the formal sense — it is distributed system maintained through community participation. However, certain practitioners have outsized influence on reputation propagation:

**High-standing guild elders** whose opinions carry weight in professional networks. When elder publicly vouches for or criticizes a practitioner, community listens.

**Major clients** who control substantial contract volume. Their satisfaction or complaints influence whether other clients hire specific practitioners.

**Guild administrators** who manage formal standing records and referral coordination. They do not control reputation directly but facilitate the systems through which reputation operates.

## Practical Example

**Case:** A [[Flux Users#Formulists|Formulist]] completes contract but delivers work three weeks late, causing client significant delay costs. Client publicly complains at guild meeting. Other members hear complaint, note Formulist's name, and become less likely to recommend them for time-sensitive contracts.

**Reputation Impact:** Formulist loses access to high-priority, time-sensitive work (which pays premium rates). They continue receiving routine work with flexible deadlines but at lower income. Over six months, they complete five contracts successfully with no delays. Reputation gradually recovers as evidence of reliability accumulates. After one year, they regain access to time-sensitive work.

**No Formal Punishment:** Guild mediation was not invoked because client only wanted public acknowledgment of delay, not formal resolution. No guild expulsion, no formal sanctions. Yet the Formulist experienced real economic consequences through reputation damage and required year of consistent performance to recover.

This is reputation justice functioning normally — immediate, proportionate, and self-correcting as practitioner demonstrated improvement.
