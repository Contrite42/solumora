_Letters from [[Mell Rask]] to a former colleague in [[Valdenmoor]], responding to questions about [[Wolfpoint]]. Selected extracts._

---

**First letter, responding to an inquiry about conditions:**

You ask what Wolfpoint is like as a place to live. The honest answer is that it depends entirely on what you mean by live.

The physical conditions are straightforward. The settlement is smaller than you are probably imagining — a few hundred permanent residents, more in warmer months when people who winter elsewhere return. The buildings are functional rather than comfortable. There is an adequate supply of everything you need and limited supply of anything you might want. The northern latitude means the winters are serious. You should bring better gear than you think you need and plan to acquire additional gear once you have been here long enough to understand what the winters actually require.

The other conditions are harder to describe briefly. I will try.

There is no institution here. There is an archive, which is a collection of documents rather than an organization with a purpose. There is an informal community of people who are working on related problems, which is not an institution in any useful sense. There are no expectations attached to arrival, no track you are placed on, no evaluation of whether your reasons for being here are sufficient. This is either freeing or terrifying depending on who you are, and I have learned that which one it is becomes clear within about six weeks.

I am telling you this in advance because most people arrive believing they know which category they are in and discover they were wrong.

---

**Third letter, responding to a question about whether the archive is worth the journey:**

Whether the archive is worth the journey depends on what you are bringing to it.

The archive is not a library. It does not answer questions. It contains documents — some fragmentary, some extensive, some in languages that require specialized knowledge to read, some in [[Hypertext]] sequences that require a different kind of knowledge still. What the archive offers is proximity to material that exists nowhere else and that most of the world does not know exists.

What you do with that proximity is entirely your own problem, and the archive will not help you solve it.

I arrived with questions that I thought were specific. I have since learned that my questions were imprecisely phrased approximations of questions I did not know how to ask yet. This is, I have come to believe, the normal experience. The useful version of the question is usually not the one you arrive with. The archive does not tell you this. You figure it out by spending time with material that does not respond to imprecise approximations, and eventually the question revises itself into something you can actually work with.

Whether this is worth the journey is a question you can only answer after you have made the journey, which I recognize is not useful guidance. It is, however, accurate.

---

**Seventh letter, responding to a question about whether to come:**

You have asked me three times now, in different words, whether you should come to Wolfpoint. I have answered the question you actually asked each time. I have not answered the question you are asking.

Come if the work is urgent enough that not doing it is worse than what doing it costs. Do not come if you are still deciding whether the work matters.

The people who stay are not the ones who were most certain when they arrived. They are the ones for whom the question turned out to be unavoidable. You will know which one you are when you know. I cannot tell you before you know.
