**News Distribution Systems** are the informal networks through which information about events, policies, crises, and opportunities reaches ordinary people across [[Solumora]]. There is no institutional press, no centralized news authority, no daily pamphlet distributed across kingdoms. News moves through people talking to each other, merchants sharing reports from recent travels, and settlement authorities posting official notices. Speed, accuracy, and interpretation all depend on who is talking and why they are motivated to share.

## How News Moves

News does not originate in a single location and distribute evenly. It emerges from multiple sources simultaneously, travels through different social networks at different speeds, and arrives in fragmented, contradictory forms that require local interpretation.

**Settlement Notice Boards** — Most settlements maintain public boards where local authorities post official announcements: tax changes, expedition permits, [[Flux]] hazard warnings, enforcement notices. These are considered authoritative within their jurisdiction but carry no weight elsewhere. A notice posted in [[Solhaven]] is ignored in [[Ashford]] unless someone traveling between settlements carries the information and explains why it matters.

**Merchant Gossip Networks** — Traders moving between settlements exchange information as routinely as they exchange cargo. A merchant returning from [[Valdenmoor]] to [[Emberfall]] brings not just goods but reports: grain prices shifted, a guild expelled a member, a [[Control Tier|T4]] practitioner demonstrated new detection methods. Other merchants repeat these reports, add their own observations, and the accumulated narrative becomes "what everyone knows" about conditions in Valdenmoor this season.

**Courier Gossip** — Couriers operating on routes like the [[Ashford-Halveth Courier Road]] or through the [[Meridian Dispatch Network]] carry more than sealed dispatches. They see traffic patterns, observe settlement activity, overhear conversations at relay stations. A courier returning from the [[Equatorial Desert]] edge brings informal reports about expedition disasters, [[Rift Incursions]], or unusual [[Flux Demons|Flux Demon]] activity. These reports are not official — the courier did not witness the events directly — but they are credible enough that people adjust their behavior based on them.

**Tavern Circulation** — Public houses, guild halls, and market taverns are where news gets interpreted and debated. Someone mentions hearing from a courier that a [[High Demons|High Demon]] was spotted near [[Fennick]]. Another person disputes this — they heard it was just unusual [[Flux]] patterns misidentified by a T2 guard. A third person says their cousin lives near Fennick and sent word that nothing unusual happened. By the end of the evening, the settlement has three competing versions of events, and individuals decide which to believe based on who they trust.

**Official Announcements** — [[Auralis]] uses formal announcements distributed through administrative channels when policy changes, new regulations take effect, or crisis response requires coordinated action. [[Terravelle]] relies more heavily on guild networks to distribute official information through trade channels. Both systems assume information will take 1-3 weeks to reach remote settlements, and both plan policy timing accordingly.

## Speed and Reliability

News speed varies dramatically by distance, route, and urgency. Information about a [[Rift Incursions|Rift Incursion]] near [[Halveth]] reaches northern [[Terravelle]] settlements within days via the [[Desert Signal Network]] and [[Ashford-Halveth Courier Road]] courier gossip. By contrast, a policy change in [[Solhaven]] might not reach rural [[Auralis]] settlements for weeks, arriving as second-hand merchant reports rather than official notice.

**Urban Centers (Solhaven, Valdenmoor, Emberfall, Ashford):**
- Major events reach urban populations within 3-7 days via merchant networks and courier gossip
- Official policy changes are posted on notice boards within 1-2 weeks
- Competing narratives emerge quickly as multiple sources report different versions

**Rural Settlements:**
- News arrives 2-4 weeks after urban centers, delivered by traveling merchants or seasonal labor migration
- Official notices may never arrive — settlers rely on travelers to explain what distant authorities decided
- Events are often known only in vague outline ("something happened at the border") with details lost in transmission

**Desert Edge (Halveth, frontier posts):**
- Emergency information (Rift threats, expedition disasters) propagates rapidly via [[Desert Signal Network]] (hours to days)
- Routine news from kingdom interiors arrives slowly and incompletely
- Frontier communities are often better informed about each other than about events in their own kingdom capitals

## Information Quality

News accuracy degrades predictably as it travels. First-hand reports from witnesses are relatively reliable. Second-hand reports introduce misunderstanding and distortion. Third-hand reports often become rumor, where facts, speculation, and fabrication blend together.

People evaluate news quality by assessing source credibility:
- Did the speaker witness events directly, or are they repeating what someone else said?
- Does the speaker have a reason to lie, exaggerate, or minimize?
- Does the report align with other information the listener already considers credible?

This creates persistent information asymmetry. People connected to merchant networks, courier operations, or guild administration have access to more frequent, more detailed information than people who rely on occasional travelers. [[Control Tier|High-tier]] [[Flux Users]] often have better information networks than T0-T1 populations because their work requires coordination across settlements.

## Narrative Distortion

News is not just transmitted — it is interpreted, shaped, and sometimes deliberately manipulated. Merchants emphasize information that benefits their trade interests. Authorities frame policy changes to minimize backlash. Individuals repeat stories that confirm their existing beliefs and ignore stories that challenge them.

**Political Framing** — [[Auralis]] authorities describe [[Terravelle]] actions as "obstructionism" or "restraint failures." [[Terravelle]] authorities describe [[Auralis]] policies as "reckless expansion" or "dangerous experimentation." Both kingdoms know their framing will shape how their populations interpret events, and both invest effort in ensuring their narrative reaches settlements first.

**Commercial Manipulation** — Merchants spread exaggerated reports about supply shortages, competitor failures, or route disruptions when it serves their pricing strategies. A rumor that grain shipments from [[Maren Valley]] are delayed allows Solhaven merchants to justify price increases, even if delays are minor or nonexistent.

**Crisis Amplification** — Stories about [[Flux Demons]], [[Rift Incursions]], or [[High Demons]] get exaggerated in retelling because fear spreads faster than accuracy. A T3 practitioner reporting "unusual Flux patterns near ruins" becomes "a High Demon was spotted attacking a settlement" by the time the story reaches distant listeners. Correcting these exaggerations is difficult because the correction lacks the emotional intensity that made the original story spread.

## Information Control

Both kingdoms attempt to control news flow when it serves their interests, but neither has effective centralized mechanisms for doing so. Control is exercised indirectly through influence over key distribution nodes.

**[[Auralis]] Approach:**
- Ensure official announcements are posted promptly in urban centers
- Use [[The Council of Auralis]] connections to merchant networks to distribute preferred narratives
- Pressure couriers and merchants to avoid spreading "destabilizing rumors" (which usually means information that challenges official policy)
- Rely on administrative authority to shape what gets communicated through formal channels

**[[Terravelle]] Approach:**
- Distribute information through [[Trade Guilds]], which have more direct contact with settlements than central government
- Use guild reputation networks to promote or suppress specific narratives
- Allow competing information to circulate but ensure preferred version has guild endorsement
- Leverage [[Merchant House Intelligence Network]] to monitor what information is spreading and adjust guild messaging accordingly

Neither approach is fully effective. Information spreads through too many channels, involves too many independent actors, and travels faster than authorities can respond. Both kingdoms accept that they can influence news flow but cannot control it.

## Practical Impact

How quickly and accurately people learn about events directly affects their decisions:

**For Merchants:**
- Knowing about supply disruptions, route closures, or policy changes before competitors allows advantageous positioning
- Delayed or inaccurate information leads to mispriced goods, failed shipments, contract violations
- Access to [[Merchant House Intelligence Network]] or relationships with couriers creates competitive advantage

**For Settlers:**
- Learning about [[Rift Incursions]] or [[Flux Demons|Flux Demon]] activity allows evacuation or defense preparation
- Delays in receiving warnings about hazards can be fatal — settlements have been destroyed because warning signals did not arrive in time
- Understanding policy changes (new taxes, expedition restrictions, grimoire regulations) allows compliance or strategic planning

**For Governments:**
- Public response to policy depends partly on how policy is explained and who does the explaining
- Misinformation about crises or threats can create panic that is more costly than the original problem
- Inability to communicate rapidly across kingdom territory limits coordination during emergencies

## Contemporary Concerns

**Border Tension Information Warfare** — As [[Auralis]]-[[Terravelle]] relations deteriorate, both kingdoms increasingly use news distribution to shape perception of the other. Reports of trade disputes, enforcement incidents, or diplomatic failures are framed to make the other side appear unreasonable. This creates information environments where populations in both kingdoms believe their side is responding defensively to the other's aggression.

**Expedition Misinformation** — False reports about [[Equatorial Desert]] conditions, [[Ancient Ruins]] discoveries, or guide availability affect expedition planning and safety. Guides like [[Avel Coss]] periodically publish corrections through courier networks, but misinformation spreads faster than corrections can reach affected parties.

**Flux Crime Reporting** — Detection of [[Flux]]-based crimes is difficult (requires [[Control Tier|T3+]] to detect many effects). Public awareness of Flux crime depends on authorities choosing to announce investigations or convictions. Both kingdoms underreport Flux crime to avoid creating perception that enforcement is failing, which means settlements often have no idea that recent incidents were criminal acts rather than accidents or natural phenomena.

## Key Figures

**Corla Senn**, [[Control Tier|T2]], 43 years old, operates as an information aggregator in [[Solhaven]]. She maintains relationships with incoming merchants, couriers, and travelers, collecting their reports and synthesizing them into coherent summaries that she sells to merchant houses, guild administrators, and occasionally government officials. She does not investigate — she listens, asks clarifying questions, cross-references reports from multiple sources, and produces assessments of what is probably true versus what is likely rumor. Her reputation depends on accuracy, so she is careful to distinguish "confirmed by multiple independent sources" from "single-source report, unverified."

**Trev Dorst**, T0, 56 years old, runs a public house in [[Ashford]] where merchants, couriers, and guild members gather to exchange information. He is not a spy, not a broker, not formally part of any intelligence network. He simply listens. Over twenty-eight years, he has become known as someone who knows what is happening across the trade corridor, who heard what from whom, and which stories are credible versus which are exaggerated. People come to his establishment partly for the ale and partly because they know they will learn more in two hours of conversation than they would in a week of asking questions elsewhere.

**Lenna Korst**, [[Control Tier|T3]], 39 years old, serves as Valdenmoor's official notice administrator. Her job is posting government announcements, ensuring settlement populations are informed about policy changes, and managing public information boards. She has held this role for eleven years and has learned that timing matters as much as content. Posting a tax increase notice the day before a major market fair creates backlash. Posting it two weeks later, after merchants have already left and settlement attention has moved elsewhere, generates less resistance. She does not suppress information — she times it strategically to minimize friction.

## Comparative Perspective

[[Auralis]] treats news distribution as an extension of administrative authority. The goal is to ensure official narratives dominate and that populations understand policy correctly. This works well in urban centers with strong administrative presence but fails in rural areas where government messaging arrives late or not at all.

[[Terravelle]] treats news distribution as a market phenomenon. Information that people need will spread naturally through merchant and guild networks. The goal is to ensure Terravelle guild voices are credible so their framing influences how information is interpreted. This creates more diverse information environments but also more misinformation, since no central authority verifies reports.

Both systems assume that people will talk, merchants will gossip, and couriers will share what they overhear. Neither kingdom attempts to prevent this — both focus on influencing what gets said rather than who says it.
