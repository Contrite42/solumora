**Orre Cavlt** is forty-nine years old and runs a mid-sized trading house in [[Ashford]] that has operated cross-border commerce between [[Terravelle]] and [[Auralis]] for twenty-two years. [[Control Tier|T2]]. He took over the business from his aunt at twenty-seven, after spending his twenties learning the actual work of it — which routes were reliable, which documentation required which handling, which relationships on both sides of the border made the difference between a shipment going through and a shipment sitting at customs for three weeks.

The cross-border trade has been his entire professional life. He has developed an accurate working model of what the political relationship between the two kingdoms means for commercial operations, which is different from what either kingdom's official communications describe. The official communications describe partnership, mutual benefit, and regulatory coordination. What they mean practically: certain goods face more inspection depending on where they are going and why, border crossing wait times vary with political conditions in ways that do not correspond to any formal policy change, and the documentation requirements evolve in ways that require ongoing relationship maintenance with the actual people performing inspections rather than the institutions they represent.

This year has been different from previous years in ways he has not yet been able to model accurately. The informal indicators he normally relies on — wait times, inspection frequency, the tone of his contacts on the Auralis side — have been shifting without a clear pattern. The shift is not obviously bad for his specific business. It is uncertain in a way he finds more difficult to manage than bad. He is good at adjusting to conditions he understands.

He has been trying to determine whether what he is seeing is a temporary fluctuation or the beginning of a structural change. He does not have enough information to decide. He has been in business for twenty-two years, which means he knows that not deciding is also a decision.

## What He Cannot Model

The indicators Orre trusts most are informal: crossing wait times, inspection tone, and whether his contacts on each side of the line are speaking plainly or protecting themselves. Over the last year, those indicators have shifted without a clean pattern.

Two of his longer relationships on the [[Auralis]] side have become careful in ways they were not before. They still answer, but less directly. They still cooperate, but with more documented distance than they used to maintain.

One shipment he expected to clear in one day sat for four without any formal hold being applied. No one called it a problem. No one wrote it as an exception. It simply did not move.

He has been in this business long enough to know the difference between friction and signal. He is not yet sure which this is. But it is not random.
