The Advancement Corps is [[The Council of Auralis|the Council's]] field research institution — the apparatus through which [[Auralis]] conducts its organized inquiry into [[Ancient Ruins|ruins sites]], [[Flux]] anomalies, and the practical limits of Flux application in conditions that cannot be replicated in academic settings. It is staffed, funded, and directed by the Council, and its leadership reports to the Council directly. The institutional independence it presents to the public and to its own operatives is nominal.

The name is deliberate. The [[Ascendant Path]]'s theology of advancement — the belief that Flux mastery is spiritual progress — gives the Corps a vocabulary that makes its work sound like a form of devotion. The name lands differently on different people who hear it. Most Auralis residents understand the Corps as the institution that does the dangerous and important work of expanding the kingdom's knowledge. Most Corps operatives understand that the work is more complicated than this. The Council understands the name as useful.

## What the Corps Does

The Corps's stated mission is the documentation and assessment of Flux-relevant sites across [[Solumora]]: ruins from the civilization that predated the current kingdoms, active anomaly zones, rift-adjacent areas with unusual Flux behavior, and field conditions where controlled academic research is not possible. Site assessors travel to these locations, document what they find, and report back through the regional coordination offices that sit between field operatives and the Council.

This work is genuine. The flux mapping produced by Corps field operations has expanded [[Auralis]]'s scientific knowledge significantly, and some of this knowledge has reached the academic community in forms that are genuinely useful. The ruins cataloguing that Corps teams have completed over decades is the basis for most of what either kingdom understands about the civilization that came before.

The Corps also conducts field experiments with new Flux applications — tests that require remote locations, controlled populations, and conditions that urban academic settings cannot provide. The documentation for this work uses language developed by the Corps's own reporting officers: "environmental stabilization," "community integration," "volunteer participation outcomes." The language is accurate enough to survive review and general enough to describe a range of operations the review process is not designed to examine closely.

## Structure

The Corps operates through regional coordination offices that manage field operatives within a geographic area. The eastern coordination office covers the Zakros-adjacent zones and the ruins networks east of [[Solhaven]]. Other offices handle the western coast, the northern approaches to the [[Equatorial Desert]], and the interior survey circuits.

Field operatives hold grades from Field Assessor through Senior Field Assessor and Field Lead. Senior grades manage their own caseload and submit reports without direct supervision on individual assignments. Field Leads — the most experienced grade — historically managed multi-operative projects and sometimes ran the operations that the Corps's reporting language describes with the most careful phrasing.

The Corps commissions independent contractors for work outside its direct staff capacity: desert crossing guides, specialized Flux practitioners, local consultants in areas where Corps personnel have no established presence. These contractors submit reports to the coordination offices and are paid through the Corps's operational budget. They are not Corps operatives and are not given the full operational picture of the projects they support.

## Reports and Feedback

Field reports travel from operatives through the regional offices to Council-level review. The review process is not transparent to the people who produced the reports.

Operatives occasionally receive acknowledgment that a finding was significant — a site classification elevated, a Flux anomaly flagged for expedition priority, a data point that appears in subsequent briefings as useful. These acknowledgments are genuine. When field work produces something the Council wanted, the operative usually knows.

When field work produces outcomes that are problematic — communities disrupted, experiments with results that do not fit the Corps's stated mission, cases where "volunteer integration outcomes" involved outcomes the operatives who wrote that phrase understood and did not write clearly — the report closes. The operative receives a standard acknowledgment. Further questions find that the file has moved to a review tier they cannot access. The formal record of the operation is complete and accurate and does not describe what happened.

Operatives who have been in the Corps long enough learn to read the pattern. The feedback they receive tells them something. The feedback they do not receive tells them something else.

## The Culmination Faction

The Corps's agenda has been shaped significantly by [[Drest]]'s Culmination Faction and its interest in what the ancient civilization achieved. Ruins work that prior decades conducted primarily as academic documentation has shifted, over the past twenty years, toward active recovery and experimentation. The question the Corps is now functionally oriented around is not "what did they leave behind" but "can we replicate what they knew."

This shift has changed what kinds of field operations get prioritized, what kinds of findings get the selective feedback that signals significance, and what kinds of outcomes are described in the language that the Corps's reporting officers have spent careers developing. The operatives in the field experience this as a change in assignment focus. The Council experiences it as progress.
