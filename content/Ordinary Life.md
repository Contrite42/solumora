Most people in [[Solumora]] are not involved in anything. They are not managing kingdoms, crossing deserts, or developing new casting techniques. They are feeding themselves, raising children, maintaining relationships, and trying to end each year in approximately the position they started it. This is the majority experience of a continent with a quarter billion people, and it is worth describing on its own terms.

## Work

The economy runs on labor and [[Watts]]. In cities, most work is organized through guild structures — formal associations of practitioners in a given trade that set wages, training requirements, and quality standards. Guild membership is not mandatory in either kingdom but is effectively required for steady employment in any skilled trade. A carpenter outside the guild can work, but the major contracts go to guild members, and the protection from client disputes — arbitration, bonding — only exists within the guild framework.

Common trades include construction, food preparation, textile work, animal handling, basic scribing, transport, and every category of maintenance that a city requires to function. Below the guilds is a permanent class of unskilled day laborers who show up at established gathering points each morning and are selected or not selected for work. In [[Valdenmoor]] this market functions efficiently. In [[Solhaven]] the harbor district it is loud, competitive, and not always fair.

A day's unskilled labor in a major city pays enough to eat and sleep in shared accommodation with a small amount left over. Over a year, a reliable worker accumulates modest savings. Skilled guild workers earn significantly more. A journeyman [[Flux Users|Formulist]] with a specialty — preservation, structural assessment, cargo sealing — earns enough to live in private accommodation, eat well, and save for something over a decade.

What that something is varies. Property in major cities is expensive and becoming more so. Most people who accumulate enough buy into a trade, not into land.

## Food

The base diet across both kingdoms is grain, legumes, root vegetables, and preserved protein. The details depend heavily on region. [[Terravelle]]'s upland river valleys produce wheat, barley, and river fish in abundance. [[Auralis]]'s engineered low basin grows rice, leafy plants that prefer warmth and moisture, and saltwater fish from its western and eastern coasts.

Fresh food is cheap where it is produced and expensive where it must be transported. Preserved food — dried, salted, Flux-sealed — travels well and forms the backbone of city diets year-round. A Formulist who specializes in preservation seals has permanent work anywhere there is a market or a warehouse.

Eating well in a city means either earning enough to buy fresh goods daily or knowing someone who produces them. Most people eat adequately. Some eat badly for stretches when money is short. A few eat very well, and the gap between adequate and very well is visible in the same city block in [[Solhaven]] in ways it is not in rural [[Terravelle]].

## Shelter

Most city dwellers rent. Ownership exists and is the aspiration of the successful working class, but a single property transaction in [[Solhaven]] or [[Solhaven|the capital districts]] requires years of saving. Outside cities, rural households typically own the structure they live in — construction labor is cheaper, material is more available, and the land tenure system in both kingdoms favors established rural occupancy.

Shared accommodation — a room in a boarding house, a floor in a work-gang's collective — is the first housing most young adults from outside the city experience. Rooms are small, privacy is limited, and the quality varies from functional to grim. This is not considered unusual. It is considered what you live through until you can do better.

Flux-enhanced housing features — temperature regulation, structural sigils that prevent settling and damp, light fixtures that don't require fuel — exist and are desirable. They are also additional cost. Most ordinary housing has some basic preservation sigils on the food storage areas and not much else.

## Control Tier and What It Means

[[Control Tier]] is determined by capacity, not training, and capacity is present from birth even if not accessible until later childhood. The standard practice in both kingdoms is a formal tier assessment sometime between ages eight and twelve, performed by a certified [[Flux Users|Formulist]] at a guild hall, temple, or equivalent institution. The result is recorded alongside the [[Birth Runes|birth rune]] and becomes part of the individual's formal identity.

For the majority — T0 through T2 — the assessment is a quiet formality. T0 means no channeling capacity at all, which is common enough that it carries no stigma in [[Terravelle]] and mild social weight in [[Auralis]] that most people learn to manage. T1 and T2 casters have ability but not enough to change their economic prospects dramatically unless they train for a trade that uses it specifically.

For the minority who test at T3 or above, the assessment is the first significant event of their life. What happens after that depends entirely on where they live. In Terravelle a T5 child from a farming family gets redirected toward apprenticeship at one of the larger guild structures or toward scholarship. The family is proud and slightly uncertain. In Auralis a T5 child is a social event — the family's position shifts, institution representatives arrive, and the child enters a trajectory that is not precisely chosen.

T9 assessments are rare enough to be generational. T5 and above, across both kingdoms, means something different than the life that was assumed.

## Crime

Watts cannot be counterfeited, and [[Birth Runes|birth rune]] fraud is technically detectable by any trained Scribe. This does not mean theft is impossible — it means it takes different forms. Physical theft is the oldest kind and remains common. Extortion, protection rackets, and coerced transaction consent exist in every city. In [[Ashford]] and [[Hedun]], organized enterprises offer services that are legal in no formal sense but are understood to exist and to serve a function the formal economy does not.

Violence is the universal crime. Cities have guards and varying degrees of will to use them. The guards in major cities are guild-affiliated and inconsistently honest. Rural communities handle violence through community response, which is slower, more public, and often more effective as a deterrent.

Smuggling — moving goods across kingdom lines without documentation or tariff — is a permanent feature of the [[Equatorial Desert|desert border]] economy on both sides.

## Medicine

Trained physicians exist in major cities and larger towns. What they offer is partly expertise in human biology accumulated over generations, and partly [[Flux]] application — injury sealing, infection management, pain suppression for surgical procedures. The Flux component is limited by the practitioner's tier: a T2 field physician does different work than a T4 trauma specialist, and the T4 specialist typically works in [[Solhaven]] or [[Valdenmoor]] and charges accordingly.

Outside cities, medicine is the local herbalist, the experienced midwife, the bone-setter who has been doing it for thirty years, and occasionally a traveling Formulist who knows enough to help. People in rural communities die of things that are survivable in cities. This is treated as the cost of where you live, which does not make it easier for the people it happens to.

## Children and the Shape of a Life

Most children grow up knowing what their parents did for a living, and most of them do something adjacent to it. Guild membership passes through families. Farm work passes through families. The skill of managing particular clients, the knowledge of particular routes, the understanding of how particular institutions actually function — these pass through families too.

Mobility exists. A T5 assessment changes a trajectory. A successful guild journeyman can rise. A merchant with a good decade can buy into property. But the majority experience is that you are born into a world already organized around the assumptions of what you will probably be, and you spend your life either confirming those assumptions or working against them, and the working against them is expensive in time and effort even when it succeeds.

Children who fail tier assessment at T0 in [[Auralis]] know this differently than children who fail it at T0 in [[Terravelle]]. The failure is the same. The world's interpretation of it is not.

## What People Believe

The official religious frameworks — the [[Religion and Belief|Ascendant Path]], the Covenant of Measure, the Quiet Work — shape the categories people use to think about their lives without necessarily governing how those lives are lived day to day. A dockworker in [[Solhaven]]'s harbor district who attends Ascendant Path ceremonies for significant life events and believes, in some general way, that the universe is organized by Flux mastery is not necessarily living a spiritually examined life. She is participating in a culture that uses religious language the way cultures use all their language — mostly habitually, occasionally seriously.

What most people believe, in the practical sense, is that effort should be rewarded, that it is not always, that this is unfair, and that complaining about it too loudly causes more problems than it solves. This belief system has no official name. It is roughly universal.
