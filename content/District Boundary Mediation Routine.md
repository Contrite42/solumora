# District Boundary Mediation Routine

**District Boundary Mediation Routine** is the practical process Greyveil uses to resolve service responsibility disputes where neighborhood, market, and relay-district boundaries overlap.

The routine exists because infrastructure burdens do not follow map lines, but funding and labor obligations do.

## Routine Structure

A standard mediation cycle includes:

- incident registration by affected parties
- temporary service assignment to prevent immediate collapse
- contribution negotiation between district representatives
- documented maintenance obligation split for follow-up work

This structure prioritizes continuity first, attribution second.

## Typical Dispute Classes

Most mediations involve:

- shared wells at district edges
- waste-haul route ownership
- road segment repair on mixed commercial/residential corridors
- storm-drain clearance affecting multiple wards

Without mediation, each side delays action waiting for the other to absorb cost.

## Participants and Authority

Participants usually include merchant-house delegates, relay administrators, and neighborhood coordinators.

Authority is procedural rather than sovereign: mediators can assign interim duties and record commitments, but enforcement depends on reputation, trade access pressure, and recurring relationship costs.

## Failure Conditions

Mediation fails when:

- parties reject temporary assignment obligations
- records are not carried into subsequent budget cycles
- emergency repairs repeatedly substitute for scheduled maintenance

These failures feed directly into recurring outages and dispatch pressure for [[Emergency Utility Work Crews]].

## System Role

Boundary mediation is one of the few mechanisms preventing local disputes from becoming citywide service cascades.

Its weakness is durability: agreements often solve this week while reproducing next month because underlying budget conflict remains unresolved.
