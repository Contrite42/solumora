_(Rift Monitoring Documentation, Classified Evidence)_

**Emberfall Probability Maps** are technical rift incursion forecasting documents produced by [[Emberfall]]'s dimensional research division as standard institutional output from their ongoing study of [[Anomalous Zones|anomalous zone]] behavior and [[Rift Incursions|rift formation]] patterns. The maps combine historical incursion data, ambient Flux density measurements, spatial geometry stress indicators, and predictive modeling to generate probability assessments for future breach events across the northern desert edge. The maps are normally published quarterly as routine research data with minimal institutional sensitivity. When one such map correctly predicted the [[The Thel Reach Incursion|Thel Reach incursion]] 12-18 days in advance and evidence emerged that [[The Council of Auralis]] received but ignored the warning, the maps became the documentary proof of institutional negligence that transformed a tragedy into a political crisis.

## Technical Methodology

[[Emberfall]] produces rift probability assessments through systematic monitoring protocols developed over forty years of dimensional research:

**Ambient Flux Density Mapping**: Research teams conduct monthly Flux measurement surveys across the northern desert edge, documenting baseline energy density at over 200 monitoring positions. Elevated density readings correlate with increased rift formation probability, though the relationship is statistical rather than deterministic (high density indicates higher risk, but doesn't guarantee breach occurrence).

**Spatial Geometry Stress Detection**: Specialized instruments measure microscopic distortions in physical space geometry — the earliest detectable precursors to dimensional boundary failures. The distortions are too subtle for human perception or standard Flux detection, but sensitive equipment can identify stress accumulation patterns that develop 15-30 days before threshold breach events.

**Historical Pattern Analysis**: Forty years of documented incursion events provide baseline data for pattern recognition. Breach events cluster spatially (certain locations experience repeat incidents) and temporally (incursion frequency correlates with Flux usage patterns in nearby population centers, suggesting dimensional boundaries weaken under sustained magical activity).

**Predictive Modeling**: Research analysts combine current monitoring data with historical patterns to generate probability estimates for specific locations over 30-day forward windows. The assessments are expressed as probability ranges (low/moderate/elevated/critical) rather than deterministic predictions, because rift formation involves chaotic threshold dynamics that resist precise forecasting.

The methodology produces approximately 65-70% accuracy for "elevated probability" assessments within stated timeline windows. False positive rate (elevated assessment, no incursion occurs) runs approximately 30-35%. The models significantly outperform random chance but remain imperfect — dimensional breach events involve enough unpredictable factors that some incidents occur without warning while some predicted events never manifest.

## The Thel Reach Map

The specific probability map that predicted [[The Thel Reach Incursion]] was produced on Day -21 (three weeks before the actual breach event) as part of [[Emberfall]]'s routine quarterly monitoring cycle. The map covered the northern desert edge sector including [[Thel Reach]], [[Emberfall]], [[Dren Outpost]], and fifteen smaller settlements.

The map flagged three locations with elevated probability assessments:
1. **[[Thel Reach]] eastern approach**: Elevated probability, 12-18 day timeline
2. **[[Dren Outpost]] northern perimeter**: Moderate probability, 20-25 day timeline  
3. **Unmapped desert position 47km northeast of [[Emberfall]]**: Elevated probability, 8-12 day timeline

All three assessments included supporting data: Flux density measurements, geometry stress readings, historical pattern analysis, and confidence intervals for the probability calculations.

The [[Thel Reach]] assessment specifically noted:
- Flux density readings 22% above baseline for that location
- Detectable geometry stress accumulation over previous 6 days of monitoring
- Historical pattern correlation with two previous incursions at the same position (11 years prior, 6 years prior)
- Probability confidence: 68% likelihood of threshold breach event within stated timeline

The assessment recommended: "Standard precautionary protocol — settlement notification, enhanced monitoring, evacuation preparation planning."

The map was distributed according to standard research publication procedures: archived in [[Emberfall]]'s institutional records, transmitted to [[Council Compliance Directorate]] for administrative logging, and included in quarterly research summary packages sent to collaborating institutions.

The map was also accidentally mis-routed to [[Thel Reach]] as part of a supply shipment documentation error (wrong data case loaded into cargo manifest). [[Merra Teld]] received it, recognized its significance, and filed immediate reports requesting guidance. No guidance came. On Day 0, the rift opened exactly where the map predicted.

## The Redaction

Two days after [[The Thel Reach Incursion]], [[Osten Vess]] — a records analyst at [[Council Orders Registry]] — noticed that [[Emberfall]]'s most recent quarterly research submission was missing seventeen pages from its appendix section. The missing pages corresponded exactly to the probability map section covering the [[Thel Reach]] assessment period.

[[Osten Vess|Osten]] submitted a routine administrative query requesting the complete document. Instead of receiving the missing pages, he received a notation that the appendix had been "redacted under Council executive discretion" and was no longer available to registry staff.

Redaction of research data is exceptionally rare — technical documents normally lack political sensitivity sufficient to warrant classification. [[Osten Vess|Osten]] flagged the discrepancy for supervisory review. His supervisor, [[Vessa Rolt]], quietly told him to drop the query because it had come from [[Drest]] personally through emergency authority channels.

[[Osten Vess|Osten]] dropped the official query. He did not stop investigating. Through careful archival research, he reconstructed the pattern: [[Emberfall]] probability maps had successfully predicted multiple historical incursion events, the data had always been published openly, and the [[Thel Reach]] map was the first instance where [[Council of Auralis|Council]] authority had intervened to classify the information after an incursion occurred.

The redaction made sense only if [[The Council of Auralis|Council]] leadership wanted to conceal that they had possessed advance warning. The timing — implemented two days after the incursion, after casualties were already being documented — suggested reactive damage control rather than proactive security classification.

[[Osten Vess|Osten]] made three copies of everything he'd found. One went into personal storage. One went into a dead-drop location he maintained for sensitive documentation. One he kept accessible for the moment when someone who could use the evidence might need it.

## The Cover-Up Fails

The redaction might have succeeded in isolating the evidence if [[Merra Teld]] hadn't received the mis-routed data case three weeks before the incursion. [[Merra Teld|Merra]] retained the original document. When [[Council Compliance Directorate]] investigators arrived at [[Thel Reach]] to document the incident, she provided them with her copy of the probability map along with her flag reports and queue receipts demonstrating that she had attempted to escalate the warning through official channels.

The investigator reviewing her documentation — a mid-level analyst named [[Tren Cross]] — recognized immediately that the map [[Merra Teld|Merra]] possessed was supposed to be classified. The timestamps proved she had received it before the incursion, which meant she had legitimate possession through institutional error rather than intelligence breach. The documentation protocol required him to confiscate the map and report the classification leak.

[[Tren Cross]] did not confiscate the map. He documented [[Merra Teld]]'s testimony carefully, noted that she possessed "institutional correspondence dated prior to incident event," and made no mention of classified material in his official report. Then he went back to [[Auralis]] and made very cautious inquiries about why probability maps had suddenly become classified intelligence.

Within 48 hours, [[Tren Cross]] was reassigned to administrative duties away from field investigation work. His supervisor received instructions that [[Thel Reach]]-related documentation was now under direct [[Council of Auralis|Council]] review and field investigators should route all materials through classified channels before including them in standard reports.

The message was clear: stop asking questions, stop documenting evidence that contradicts official narratives, comply with classification protocols that protect institutional leadership from accountability.

[[Tren Cross]] complied officially. He also made copies of his field notes before routing them to classified channels. And he made quiet contact with [[Osten Vess]], whom he knew professionally from cross-institutional documentation coordination work.

The cover-up was failing because procedural workers who believed in institutional integrity kept copies.

## Diplomatic Intelligence

When [[Terravelle]] learned that [[Auralis]] had classified rift monitoring data that should have been routine research publication, suspicion intensified dramatically. [[Terravelle]]'s intelligence assessment was straightforward: governments classify information when revelation would create political damage. [[The Council of Auralis|Auralis's Council]] classifying probability maps immediately after an incursion that killed [[Terravelle]] citizens suggested [[Auralis]] leadership was concealing something significant.

[[Terravelle]] requested formal access to [[Emberfall]]'s rift monitoring research as part of collaborative investigation into the [[The Thel Reach Incursion|incursion]]. [[Auralis]] refused, citing "military intelligence security protocols." [[Terravelle]] escalated the request through diplomatic channels, offering to provide equivalent access to [[Terravelle]]'s own anomalous zone research data in exchange for reciprocal transparency.

[[Auralis]] refused again. [[Drest]], speaking on behalf of [[The Council of Auralis|Council]] under emergency powers authority, stated that rift monitoring data constituted strategic military intelligence bearing on kingdom security, and that external access would compromise [[Auralis]]'s defensive capabilities during a period of elevated dimensional threat.

The refusal confirmed, in [[Terravelle]]'s assessment, that [[Auralis]] was deliberately concealing institutional negligence or worse. [[Eddan Voss|King Eddan]]'s advisors presented three possible explanations, each worse than the last:

1. **Negligence**: [[Auralis]] possessed warning data and failed to act due to bureaucratic incompetence, now attempting to hide institutional failure
2. **Research Priority**: [[Auralis]] deliberately allowed the incursion to proceed for scientific observation, treating border settlements as experimental subjects
3. **Manufactured Crisis**: [[Auralis]] engineered the incursion or allowed it to proceed specifically to create justification for emergency powers and military mobilization

[[Terravelle]] didn't know which explanation was correct. [[Auralis]]'s refusal to provide evidence suggested all three were plausible. The diplomatic breakdown was no longer about competing kingdom interests — it was about whether [[Auralis]] institutional leadership could be trusted when they claimed to act in good faith.

The probability maps became the symbol of that lost trust: technical documents that should have saved lives, instead weaponized as classified intelligence to protect political leadership from accountability.

## Current Status

The [[Emberfall]] probability maps remain classified as military intelligence by [[Council of Auralis|Council]] emergency authority. [[Emberfall]] researchers continue producing rift assessments, but the data now flows directly to military command rather than being published through research channels. The institutional practice of transparent dimensional research has been subordinated to war footing security protocols.

Multiple copies of the [[Thel Reach]] map exist outside official control:
- [[Merra Teld]]'s original mis-routed document (retained at [[Thel Reach]])
- [[Osten Vess]]'s archival reconstructions (secured in multiple dead-drop locations)
- [[Tren Cross]]'s field investigation notes (copied before classification routing)
- Unknown number of [[Emberfall]] researcher personal archives (some researchers maintained independent records before classification lockdown)

The evidence exists. The question is whether anyone positioned to do something with it can get access before the war makes accountability impossible to pursue through institutional mechanisms.

[[Osten Vess]] has been watching for someone who might be the right contact — someone directly harmed, with standing to pursue justice, desperate enough to go public despite personal risk. He's been monitoring [[Merra Teld]]'s testimony patterns. She keeps providing the same precise timeline with supporting timestamps. She has the evidence. She has the standing. She has nothing left to lose.

He's working on figuring out how to make contact without creating communication trails that implicate colleagues or expose restricted archive access. It's the kind of careful documentation that [[Osten Vess|Osten]] has spent fourteen years perfecting. This is just higher stakes than guild contract disputes.

The maps predicted disaster. The institutions ignored the warning. Now the maps themselves are evidence that institutional failure killed seventeen people and triggered a war.

Someone needs to make sure that evidence doesn't disappear into classified archives before justice has a chance to function.
