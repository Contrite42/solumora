[[Solumora]] is a vast supercontinent — the only known landmass in the world — home to nearly a quarter billion people. It is a world of striking balance, yet divided by nature's extremes.

The equator is dominated by [[Equatorial Desert|Desert Zakros]], an inhospitable wasteland that only those equipped with [[Grimoires]], sufficient [[Flux]] ability, or extreme preparation can survive crossing. The [[Kingdoms]] of Solumora flourish in two belts on either side: the northern region is ruled by [[Terravelle]], a realm of structure and resilience, while the south is governed by [[Auralis]], a land of ambition and magical advancement. Between them, [[Equatorial Desert|Desert Zakros]] acts as both barrier and crucible — shaping two cultures that developed in near-total isolation from one another and arrived at fundamentally different answers to the same world.

The equilibrium of Solumora is not just a balance of power. It is a constant negotiation with the world's harshest extremes.

## Economy

The economy of Solumora follows a pattern familiar to anyone who has tried to survive in it: the things you need to stay alive are within reach, and everything that makes life worth living will cost you dearly.

### Currency — [[Watts]]

The currency of Solumora is the **Watt**. [[Watts]] are not coins, notes, or physical objects. They are entries on a distributed ledger written into the ambient [[Flux]] field itself, verified by every transaction in the network simultaneously. There is no bank, no mint, no physical backing. No institution controls the ledger and no kingdom created it. Both [[Terravelle]] and [[Auralis]] run their formal economies on top of it, but neither owns it.

Transactions require the consent of both parties. One initiates, one confirms, and the ledger writes. The record is permanent and cannot be altered retroactively. This is what makes Watts work: the ledger cannot be cheated, because it lives in the Flux field distributed across Solumora with no central point of failure.

### The Birth Rune

A [[Birth Runes|birth rune]] is a personal [[Sigils|sigil]] that registers an individual within the ledger and serves as their financial identity. It is an address, not a container — the rune identifies who you are within the system, and the ledger maintains the record of what that identity holds. Birth runes are traditionally inscribed by the family within the first days of life, a low-[[Control Tier|tier]] sigil that any [[Flux Users|Formulist]] can manage.

Having a birth rune is common but not universal, and being without one carries no legal consequence in most contexts. It is simply how most people choose to participate in the Watt economy. Those without runes use barter, physical goods, favors, and informal debt — a parallel system that functions perfectly well and in some regions is the dominant one.

In [[Terravelle]], registration is common out of cultural habit — record-keeping fits the Terravellian preference for predictability. In [[Auralis]], participation in the ledger carries social and spiritual weight, and declining it is considered a statement whether or not it was intended as one.

### Price Structure

The Solumoran economy splits cleanly along one line: survival is cheap, comfort is not.

Basic food, rough clothing, common shelter, standard tools, and [[Common Grimoire|Common-tier]] [[Sigils|sigil]] work sit at prices within reach of almost anyone participating in the Watt economy. The floor is low by design and by long economic habit — neither kingdom has ever found instability at the bottom of society to be worth the savings.

The moment goods or services cross into quality-of-life territory the price climbs sharply and keeps climbing. A meal is cheap. A good meal is not. Shelter is affordable. Warm, warded, comfortable shelter is not. A [[Common Grimoire|Common]] [[Grimoires|grimoire]] is within reach of most working families. An [[Uncommon Grimoire|Uncommon]] one requires genuine saving. A [[Rare Grimoire|Rare]] text, if it surfaces at all, is the kind of purchase that marks a turning point in a household's financial life.

[[Flux Users|Channeler]]-tier [[Sigils|sigil]] work — anything at [[Rare Grimoire|Rare]] and above — is not really priced like a service. It is priced like access to something that most of the world will never be able to replicate, because that is exactly what it is.

## How People Move

Movement in Solumora is not one network. It is layered systems built for different risk and volume profiles.

Bulk goods in [[Terravelle]] move most efficiently through the [[Maren Freight Chain]], with [[Valdenmoor]] coordinating schedules and [[Ashford]] converting river volume into export flow. High-priority overland communication and compact critical cargo between border commerce and desert staging move through the [[Ashford-Halveth Courier Road]]. Seasonal expedition-linked heavy movement around the desert edge is handled by the [[Zakros Rim Caravan Loop]], where timing and local route knowledge matter more than nominal distance.

Maritime movement ties the coasts together on two different scales. The [[Hedun-Solhaven Coastal Run]] provides high-frequency west-coast circulation inside [[Auralis]]. The [[Outer Coast Packet Line]] carries slower long-haul mixed cargo and correspondence linking northern settlements near [[Wolfpoint]] to eastern trade infrastructure near [[Ashford]] and onward buyers.

Information usually arrives before volume, and systems are designed around that fact. When a route destabilizes, dispatchers reroute messages first, then cargo. Most institutions in both kingdoms survive disruption not by preventing breaks, but by preserving movement continuity across at least one channel.

## Religion and Belief

Solumora has no unified religion. What it has is [[Flux]] — real, permeating, accessible to anyone with the capacity to touch it — and five distinct cultural traditions that interpret what this means spiritually.

The northern kingdom of [[Terravelle]] is shaped by the [[Covenant of Measure]], a tradition emphasizing proportionality and ethical Flux use. It has no clergy and no formal theology, but its influence runs deep: the suspicion of display, the trust in demonstrated competence, the belief that excess reveals character flaws. A Terravellian city dweller lives this tradition without thinking about it as tradition.

The southern kingdom of [[Auralis]] centers on the [[Ascendant Path]], an institutional religion claiming that Flux mastery is the highest form of spiritual development. The Path is thoroughly embedded in Auralis's government, schools, and social hierarchy. Its priests (Luminants) are skilled casters who officiate at every significant life event. The Path offers a theology that makes Auralis's tier-based society feel like cosmic order.

Between and beyond these institutional traditions lie three other practices: [[The Quiet Work]] of the northern territories, which treats Flux as a precise language; [[The Returners]], who believe the ancient civilization persists in the ruins and waits for contact; and [[The Ancestral Current]], a folk tradition present everywhere but formalized nowhere, which holds that Flux presence lingers in places where practitioners worked.

Most people practice some blend of their region's dominant tradition and elements from the others. A Terravelle merchant honors Covenant contracts and leaves food for local spirits. An Auralis scholar speaks in Ascendant Path theological language while quietly maintaining Ancestral Current practices in their family's home. The traditions do not demand exclusivity, and boundaries blur in lived practice.

The exception is the Ascendant Path, which is explicit enough in its theology that orthodox Luminants do distinguish between Path-consistent and Path-inconsistent beliefs. This creates an undercurrent of tension — the Path's institutional power in Auralis contrasts with the Covenant's institutional invisibility in Terravelle, a difference that reflects deeper divergence in how the two kingdoms understand authority, truth, and what Flux is for.

## History

The full timeline of Solumora spans an unknown pre-modern period, a founding era approximately six hundred years ago, four centuries of isolation between the kingdoms, and two centuries of Contact-era development that has produced the current political situation.

The **ancient civilization** that built the ruins in Desert Zakros is not named in any surviving record. What they left behind — the ruins, the [[Watts]] ledger, the [[Ancient Ruins|anomalous Flux sites]] — is still active. What ended them is unknown. The leading theories are documented in [[The Ancient Civilization]] and [[The Desert Barrier]].

The **founding period** produced two kingdoms that developed independently across the [[The Isolation Period|Isolation Period]] — four centuries in which the [[The Grimoire Tradition|grimoire tradition]] emerged separately in both kingdoms (arriving at nearly identical systems), the [[The Persecution Era|Persecution Era]] burned through both kingdoms' communities, and the cultures diverged in ways documented in [[The Cultural Divergence]].

**Contact** began approximately two hundred years ago when Auralis developed reliable crossing technology. The relationship since then has included [[The Three Near-Wars]] and the slow accumulation of the current tensions described in [[The Fraying Peace]].

## Calendar and Time

**[[The Solumoran Calendar]]** provides the unified timekeeping system used across both kingdoms for coordinating harvests, planning expeditions, scheduling commerce, and marking historical events. The calendar is not arbitrary cultural convention — accurate shared timekeeping prevents trade failures, missed meetings, and logistical disasters when coordination spans hundreds of miles and weeks of travel time.

The system emerged from pre-Contact astronomical observations that both kingdoms independently developed and later discovered were functionally identical, which enabled immediate integration of time-based systems once Contact began.

### Basic Structure

**365 days** — One complete orbital cycle, divided into **12 months of 30 days each** (aligned with lunar cycle) plus **5 transition days** called Turndays at year-end.

**Months:** Colden (mid-winter), Thawmark (late winter), Firstgreen (early spring), Plantmoon (mid-spring), Brightward (late spring), Highdry (early summer), Harvestfirst (mid-summer), Peaklite (late summer), Harvestfall (early autumn), Coolward (mid-autumn), Darkmoon (late autumn), Frostfast (early winter).

**Weeks:** 6-day weeks with traditional work rhythms — Firstday through Longday working period, Marketday for trade coordination, Restday for rest and religious observance.

**Current year:** 623 FE (Foundation Era) — years counted from arbitrary origin point established during Contact negotiations as compromise between kingdoms' previous incompatible dating systems.

### Seasonal Patterns

Solumora's equatorial desert creates distinct climate zones:

**Equatorial region ([[Desert Zakros]]):** Consistent high heat year-round with minimal temperature variation. Dry and wet periods rather than traditional seasons. Crossing windows determined more by [[Rift Incursions|rift patterns]] than seasonal weather.

**Temperate zones ([[Terravelle]], [[Auralis]]):** Moderate seasonal variation. [[Terravelle]] experiences colder winters (river ice, snow) while [[Auralis]] remains milder. Spring planting in [[Maren Valley]] begins Firstgreen through Plantmoon. Summer harvest Harvestfall through Coolward. Maritime activity peaks Highdry through Peaklite (lower storm frequency).

**Northern extremes ([[Wolfpoint]]):** Harsh winters with extreme cold and heavy snow cause settlement isolation. Brief intense summers compress annual hunting, supply, and expedition activity into narrow window.

### Timekeeping Methods

**[[Timekeeping Technology]]** varies dramatically by class and wealth. Working-class populations rely on sundials (when available), shadow estimation, and natural indicators like meal times and work rhythms. Middle-class households own sundials and benefit from institutional water clocks maintained by [[Trade Guilds]] and [[Ascendant Path]] temples. Upper-class households possess expensive mechanical clocks (800-2000 [[Watts]]) or extremely rare personal watches (2000-5000 Watts).

[[Flux]]-assisted timekeeping methods exist (oscillation counters, time-bind [[Sigils]]) but remain expensive and increasingly unavailable as practitioners are conscripted for military applications.

Precise shared timekeeping infrastructure is currently deteriorating — clockmakers conscripted, institutional funding diverted to war preparation, cross-border calendar coordination declining. This degrades commerce coordination, maritime scheduling, and contract reliability.

## Seasonal Constraints and Work Cycles

The calendar structures time, but seasonality structures action. Every major system in Solumora — agriculture, shipping, expedition planning, labor markets, resource extraction — operates according to seasonal windows that compress activity, amplify resource competition, and create predictable points of systemic stress.

[[Terravelle]]'s agricultural rhythm dictates the year: spring planting (Firstgreen through Plantmoon) transitions to summer maintenance (Brightward through Harvestfirst), then harvest processing (Harvestfall through Darkmoon), followed by winter austerity (Colden through Thawmark) when storage infrastructure holds the population alive. This cycle creates waves of seasonal employment. Day laborers earn 15-25 [[Watts]] daily during spring preparation and autumn processing surges, but income collapses to 3-8 Watts during winter slack. Households depending on wage work must budget annual 12-month survival around income concentrated in four months — creating debt vulnerability that aligns with seasonal poverty patterns visible in all major settlements. Failure to complete the agricultural cycle at any point (poor spring planting, drought damage, failed storage) cascades into malnutrition windows six to eight weeks before the next cycle's output reaches edible yield.

[[Coastal and River Shipping Cycles]] compress further: the Maren River spring flood surge (Firstgreen-Plantmoon) enables 10-20x normal transport capacity — a window so productive that 60-70% of annual shipping revenue concentrates there. Summer low-water (Brightward-Harvestfirst) reduces the river to manageable but limited traffic. Autumn transition (Harvestfall-Coolward) brings unpredictable conditions. Winter ice (Colden-Thawmark) closes the river entirely. Regional adaptations differ: [[Terravelle]] optimizes spring flood use; [[Auralis]] distributes maritime shipping across the year with smaller-volume operations. The fundamental pressure remains: seasonal infrastructure can keep pace with current volume only by postponing maintenance, which reduces capacity and reliability the following season.

[[Zakros Crossing Windows and Failure Season]] operate at inverted seasonality: the desert's rift activity is highest autumn-through-winter (Harvestfall-Colden-Thawmark), making this the most dangerous crossing period. The primary window (Brightward-Harvestfirst, 12 weeks) carries 65-70% of annual crossing volume, with 5-10% documented casualty rates. Secondary windows (Plantmoon, 4-5 weeks) experience 12-15% casualties. Marginal windows compress danger-vs-logistics tradeoffs into tactical decisions: the fall-back route adds three days but reduces rift encounter probability from 30% to 15%. This seasonality forces military logistics planning months in advance — expeditions scheduled in low-rift windows or accepted casualty rates rise above institutional pain thresholds.

[[Urban Labor Surge and Slack Cycles]] show wage compression across [[Solhaven]], [[Valdenmoor]], and small settlements. Spring surge (Firstgreen-Brightward, 6-8 weeks) draws 15-25W daily unskilled wages. Summer maintenance (Brightward-Harvestfirst, 8-10 weeks) drops to 8-15W daily. Autumn crunch (Harvestfall-Coolward, 4-6 weeks) pushes 12-20W daily at exploitative conditions — unfinished work carries over into winter. Winter austerity (Colden-Thawmark, 8-12 weeks) drops wages to 3-8W daily or seasonal unemployment entirely. Geographic variation mirrors settlement size: [[Solhaven]] has enough institutional diversity to moderate the swing; [[Valdenmoor]] experiences extreme spring-autumn swings; small settlements endure near-subsistence winters. Skilled craftspeople (guild-protected) maintain more stable income. Day laborers and tenants absorb full volatility. For populations dependent on seasonal work, the calendar governs survival more directly than most realize — debt that appears manageable in spring becomes threatening by August when autumn's high wages are still two months away.

[[Resource Extraction Seasonal Work and Weather Constraints]] lock mining (Plantmoon-Coolward operating, 7 months; Colden-Plantmoon shutdown), logging (winter snow sledge vs. spring-autumn weather tolerance), and fishing (summer optimal, spring dangerous, autumn transition, winter isolation) into weather-driven cycles. Coastal fishing specifically peaks Highdry-Harvestfirst; spring (Firstgreen-Brightward) is high-casualty; autumn (Harvestfall) brings cyclone emergence; winter (Colden-Thawmark) creates operational isolation. Extraction labor concentrates seasonally: November-December recruitment for 20-25W daily peak season contracts drop to 5-8W winter maintenance work. Profitability pressure is severe: poor summers wipe out annual profit. Winter operations cost 150,000-350,000+ Watts in fixed overhead and zero revenue — all annual surplus must cover winter carrying costs plus profit.

The effect is systemic compression. Agriculture's harvest crunch collides with shipping's autumn transition and urban autumn employment surge. Spring surge recruitment for maritime and urban work competes for labor. Zakros expeditions concentrate in one 12-week window, creating cascading demand for guides, provisioners, and escort casters. Resource extraction labor recruitment concentrates when agricultural surplus is lowest and winter preparation is highest. Every sector experiences its own seasonal peak, but peaks overlap, triggering periodic shortages and wage spikes that spread across the entire economy.

Most important: seasonality is not abstract. Households do not smooth income across the year — they must survive the valleys. Every household with seasonal income experiences potential food insecurity, medical vulnerability, and debt accumulation in predictable months. Every institution with seasonal cash flow must maintain reserves or reduce services. Every route with seasonal capacity must absorb peak-season volume or accept queue delays that cascade into shipment failures. The calendar provides order. Seasonality provides constraint.

## People in Motion

Large structures in Solumora are held together by recurring people who move between systems rather than staying inside one institution. [[Orre Cavlt]] tracks policy pressure through cargo paperwork in [[Ashford]]. [[Selt Orvn]] and [[Vorn Teld]] translate desert risk into practical decisions at [[Halveth]]. [[Dess Tannor]] records disruption patterns in Solhaven's harbor economy while [[Keln Varost]] carries north-south diplomatic intent through the Wolfpoint channel.

These are not principal rulers, but they are often the first to notice that conditions have shifted. Their observations shape route choice, expedition behavior, procurement priorities, and who receives warning early enough to act.
