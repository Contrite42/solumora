**Jorren Pell** is a T1 harbor-clinic practitioner in [[Solhaven]]'s lower waterfront, age forty-four. He came from a freight crew background, entered temple infirmary training through the [[Ascendant Path]], and has practiced community medicine for sixteen years.

## Practice

Jorren runs a high-throughput clinic day built around short contacts and strict triage discipline.

His common case load:

- dock injuries (crush trauma, rope burns, contaminated cuts);
- respiratory stress from storage dust and smoke-heavy work zones;
- recurrent infection where workers return to shifts before recovery;
- post-incident monitoring after failed self-treatment.

He treats quickly, schedules aggressively, and sends difficult cases up-tier early when he can secure referral slots.

## District Role

In the harbor district, Jorren functions as both clinician and gatekeeper. He decides who can return to shift, who must rest, and who needs transfer to specialist care.

Because many workers are paid by attendance, his recommendations carry economic consequences. He has a reputation for being strict about "no return with open infection," which has reduced repeat severe cases in his block but created friction with warehouse supervisors.

## Economics

Jorren keeps pricing low to preserve clinic access:

- basic intake and treatment: 1-3 [[Watts]];
- follow-up bundle (three checks): 4 Watts;
- urgent evening contact: +2 Watts emergency fee.

He covers uncompensated care by maintaining a partial retainer arrangement with two dock associations and one temple charity fund.

## Constraints

His biggest constraints are not technical:

- too many patients for available follow-up time;
- referral queue delays for pathway and complex trauma cases;
- pressure from employers to clear workers early;
- low trust among transient workers who move before treatment cycles end.

He has lost patients to preventable complications caused by missed second and third visits, not by failed first treatment.

## Relationship to Flux Cases

Jorren is not a high-tier specialist, but he is good at identifying cases that look ordinary and recover abnormally. He keeps a standing escalation channel with a Crestward physician network and refers aggressively when signs point to pathway involvement.

He tells trainees that good low-tier medicine is mostly timing: "If you escalate two days early, people complain. If you escalate two days late, people die."
