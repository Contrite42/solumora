The Ancestral Current is a folk tradition present in rural communities across both [[Terravelle]] and [[Auralis]] — not institutionalized, not politically organized, and rarely discussed by the urban religious establishments of either kingdom because it does not demand recognition. It persists through family practice and community custom, not through clergy or theology.

## Core Belief

The Ancestral Current holds that a practitioner's [[Flux]] capacity does not vanish at death. It dissipates — slowly, over months or years — into the places where that practitioner spent their life. The concentration is greatest in the spaces of daily work: a craftsperson's workshop, a farmer's field boundary, a healer's treatment room, a caster's inscribing table.

This is not understood as the person persisting. The person is gone. What remains is more like warmth from a fire that has gone out — a presence that can be sensed, that occasionally resolves into something closer to impression or direction, but that is not responsive in the way a living person is responsive. Practitioners who are attuned to it describe it as feeling the texture of decisions made in a space, the accumulated orientation of work repeated over years.

Whether this is real Flux-sensing or cultural pattern-matching is a question the tradition does not ask. The question is not useful.

## How It Is Practiced

The Ancestral Current is practiced through maintenance rather than ceremony. You do not pray to your ancestors in the Current's framework; you maintain the spaces associated with them. You do not disturb a practitioner's work space in the first year after their death — this is the strongest taboo in the tradition, the period when the dissipation is believed to be most active and most disruptive. You include the tools of their craft in significant decisions made in that space, even if this is only a gesture.

In families with long practitioner lineages, specific objects carry accumulated Flux presence from multiple generations. These are treated with care proportional to their age and the tier of the practitioners who used them. They are not grimoires and not relics in a religious sense — they are tools that hold something, and you handle them accordingly.

## Relationship to the Major Traditions

In [[Terravelle]], the Ancestral Current exists alongside the [[Covenant of Measure]] without friction. The Covenant is an ethics of living; the Current is a practice around dying. They address different questions and do not compete.

In [[Auralis]], the relationship is more complicated. The [[Ascendant Path]]'s theology does not have a clear position on what happens to Flux capacity after death — the Path focuses on the advancement of the living practitioner, not on what persists afterward. Luminants address the Ancestral Current's persistence in rural Auralis communities with studied ambiguity: they do not condemn it, because the taboos around practitioners' work spaces have genuine overlap with things the Path already values, but they do not endorse it, because the tradition's implied metaphysics have not been formally harmonized with Path theology.

The Ancestral Current has no interest in being harmonized with Path theology. It is older than the Path by most estimates and exists in places the Path has never reached.

## The Edge Case

The tradition's most uncomfortable implication involves very high-tier practitioners. If Flux presence dissipates slowly, a T7 or T8 practitioner's home site would theoretically hold more significant presence for longer. Certain rural communities that have histories with high-tier practitioners treat those sites accordingly — with more caution, more maintenance, more care around the first-year taboo.

[[Wren]]'s presence in [[Halveth]] for thirty years has done something to the border road that experienced practitioners notice. Nobody frames it in Ancestral Current terms — Wren is not dead — but the folk intuition that long Flux work accumulates in a place is, in Halveth's case, accurate in ways that the Current's framework almost describes.
