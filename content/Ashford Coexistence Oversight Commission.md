# Ashford Coexistence Oversight Commission

The Ashford Coexistence Oversight Commission (ACOC) is the first formal cross-kingdom civilian oversight body created specifically to govern human-High Demon coexistence under post-cascade-risk conditions.

It was established 112 days after the first casualty under emergency extension provisions of the [[Ashford Emergency Ceasefire Framework]], after repeated legal disputes made ad hoc technical exceptions politically unstable.

## Purpose

The Commission exists to prevent two failure modes that became visible immediately after disclosure:
- tactical overreliance on emergency exceptions without durable legal structure,
- political reversion toward persecution-era identification and containment logic.

Its mandate is not diplomatic peace-making. Its mandate is procedural legitimacy: create enforceable rules for mixed-species technical governance while preserving civil rights and crisis response speed.

## Core Authorities

### 1. Oversight of Non-Human Technical Engagement
- Registers and audits all declared High Demon participation in public-risk interventions.
- Publishes scope, duration, and necessity rationale for each intervention.
- Requires post-action review packets within 10 days.

### 2. Anti-Persecution Safeguards
- Prohibits identity-screening programs based on rumored demonic traits.
- Requires evidentiary threshold and judicial review for any investigation invoking non-human threat claims.
- Automatically triggers external review if detention language mirrors historic [[The Identification Protocols]] patterns.

### 3. Crisis Governance Coordination
- Maintains liaison channel to the [[Joint Rift Monitoring Cell]] for imminent-breach exceptions.
- Can issue temporary compliance directives to civic bodies when technical risk and legal delay conflict.
- Must sunset emergency directives within 21 days unless ratified by both kingdoms' civic authorities.

### 4. Public Transparency Layer
- Operates public docket with redaction-limited records.
- Holds monthly open hearings in [[Ashford]] with rotating sessions in Auralis and Terravelle municipalities.
- Publishes quarterly trust and compliance indicators.

## Composition

The Commission uses fixed parity plus independent seats:
- 3 Auralis civic/legal delegates,
- 3 Terravelle civic/legal delegates,
- 2 technical seats (one logistics, one dimensional systems),
- 1 archival-historical safeguards seat,
- 1 independent ombud seat selected jointly by merchant-guild and municipal blocs.

No military command positions hold voting seats.

## Founding Constraints

The ACOC was negotiated under severe mistrust:
- Auralis hardline factions demanded containment-first policy.
- Terravelle civic blocs demanded explicit anti-persecution guardrails.
- Technical teams demanded faster exception processing than courts could provide.

The compromise architecture is intentionally awkward: strong reporting and audit burdens, narrow emergency powers, and distributed veto surfaces to prevent unilateral capture.

## Early Outcomes

In its first quarter, the Commission:
- blocked two proposed identity-screening ordinances as persecution-pattern replications,
- approved three emergency technical interventions with full public audit release,
- sanctioned one municipal office for unlawful rumor-based detention request,
- standardized crisis language replacing "threat class" terminology with risk-condition terminology.

## Structural Risk

The Commission's legitimacy depends on simultaneously satisfying two oppositional publics:
- those who believe any demon accommodation is capitulation,
- those who believe any oversight of demons is disguised persecution.

It can fail by being too weak to prevent abuse or too assertive to retain political buy-in.

## Strategic Significance

The ACOC marks the first durable governance shift after revelation: crisis cooperation has moved from improvised heroics into contested but institutionalized oversight.

If it holds, coexistence becomes policy.
If it collapses, both kingdoms retain all procedural pathways needed to reconstruct persecution mechanisms under new branding.
