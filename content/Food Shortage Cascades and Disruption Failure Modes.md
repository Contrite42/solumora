**Food Shortage Cascades and Disruption Failure Modes** documents what happens when food systems break — how shortages propagate through populations, what fails first, and how institutional response amplifies or mitigates the crisis. Food shortage is not shortage uniformly distributed; it is shortage concentrated among vulnerable populations, amplified by hoarding, and accelerated by psychological panic.

## Shortage Trigger Patterns

Food shortages emerge from distinct causes, each producing different cascade profiles.

### Supply-Side Disruption

**Harvest failure:** Poor weather, crop disease, labor shortage, or pest outbreak reduces grain yield below expected volume. Documented failure modes:

- Grain blight (fungal disease destroying standing crop): 15-30 FE saw major grain blight reducing [[Maren Valley]] yield 20-35% in affected regions. Result: [[Maren Valley]] regions facing 3-4 year recovery period as soil replenished. Food prices spiked 100-150% three years running. Working-class populations experienced famine conditions (documented malnutrition, elevated mortality in Valdenmoor 18-20 FE).

- Unexpected freezes during growing season: Harvestfall 619 FE early hard freeze destroyed most legume crop (35-40% loss). Protein-staple shortage elevated prices 80-100%, persisted through winter. Populations relying on legume-based protein faced malnutrition (grains alone insufficient protein).

- Labor shortage preventing harvest: Conscription year Harvestfirst-Coolward 623 FE: estimated 20-30% harvest labor unavailable due to military claims on working-age men. Grain left in fields (120,000 pounds estimated loss in [[Maren Valley]] alone) rotted or was damaged by weather. Smaller harvest at harvest-time (28-40% below historical volume) meant smaller food supply for year.

**Fish catch failure:** Spring storms preventing fishing (safety hazard); summer warm current shifts depleting usual fishing grounds; autumn cyclones extending through season preventing harvest. Coastal communities depending on fish as primary protein face acute shortage (no grain buffer, limited hunting alternatives).

**Transport disruption:** River flooding (Maren River cresting 50%+ above normal), road destruction from storms, or enemy action blocking supply routes creates bottleneck. Food produced in [[Maren Valley]] cannot reach [[Solhaven]] or [[Valdenmoor]] in time; stored grain spoils at source while cities face shortage. Documented delays of 20-40 days create visible shortage even if total supply adequate.

### Demand-Side Surge

**Military mobilization:** War conscription creates enclosed population (soldiers in camps, barracks, depots) with massive food demand. Military procurement diverts supply from civilian markets. Example: Harvestfirst 623 FE military camps near [[Valdenmoor]] (4000 personnel) created grain procurement surge requiring 500-800 pounds daily (military feeding rate 0.125-0.2 pounds per person daily; civilians ~0.15-0.2 pounds). Diversion of 400-500 pounds grain from civilian to military use creates 20-25% reduction in civilian market supply.

**Population influx:** Border refugees, displaced populations, or temporary migrations (seasonal workers, merchants, military movement) create surge demand. Historic example: refugee influx during Rift Incursion pressure (610-615 FE) brought 8000-12000 displaced persons to [[Solhaven]] over 18 months. Grain consumption spiked; market supply strained. Prices rose 40-60%. City implemented relief programs (institutional distribution to vulnerable populations) preventing full crisis.

### Institutional Extraction

**Requisition and taxation:** Government authority requisitioning food for military or strategic reasons. [[Terravelle]] increased grain requisition quota 2626-2630 FE (war preparation); farmers required to supply 15-20% of harvest to Crown (beyond normal tax). Effect: reduce civilian food supply 10-15% while raising prices (reduced available supply, increased prices due to shortage premium).

## Shortage Cascade Mechanics

Shortage does not distribute evenly; it concentrates through market and social mechanisms.

### Market Response: Price Surge and Hoarding

When supply shortage becomes apparent, prices rise rapidly. In [[Solhaven]] (autumn 619 FE grain shortage), prices rose 20% day-one, 50% by day-three, 120% by day-seven. 

**Hoarding:** Recognizing rising prices, households and merchants with capital accelerate purchases (buy forward), and merchants restrict sales (expecting further price rises). Result: temporary aggregate scarcity (more severe than actual supply shortage) as purchasing frontloads demand. Market response (individuals buying 2-3x normal quantities) sometimes creates artificial scarcity that worsens shortage psychology.

**Merchant behavior:** Small merchants buy aggressively, raising retail prices and profit margins. Wholesale merchants restrict supply to retail market (selling to institutional buyers at premium), reducing street-level availability. Effect: civilians cannot find goods at advertised prices; effective shortage worse than actual shortage.

### Access Concentration: Who Starves First

When shortage occurs:

**Day-laborers and working poor:** Earn income day-by-day or week-by-week with no ability to front-load purchases. Rise in price translates directly to reduced consumer access. A day laborer earning 10 Watts daily can purchase ~8 pounds grain at baseline price; at 2x baseline price, 4 pounds. Dietary staple drops 50%, creating immediate inadequacy.

**Renters and urban poor:** No home production buffer. Complete dependence on market access means shortage immediately impacts household. Compared to rural populations (might maintain kitchen gardens, poultry), urban poor have zero food production capacity.

**Dependent populations:** Young children, elderly, disabled. Dependents without independent income rely entirely on household purchasing power. If household purchasing collapses, dependents face first malnutrition effects (young children most vulnerable).

**Elite populations:** Absorb scarcity through wealth. Buy at high prices without concern. Shortage creates inconvenience (paying premium) not hardship.

### Institutional Response: Relief and Rationing

Settlements implement shortage response once crisis apparent.

**Distribution programs:** [[Basin Grain Allocation Board]] in [[Terravelle]] can release strategic reserves (~10-15% of annual consumption stored). [[Auralis]] less formalized reserves but merchant houses sometimes negotiate relief cooperation. Distribution systems typically allocate grain equitably to populations meeting need criteria (working poor, dependent families). Effectiveness varies: [[Solhaven]] documented relief programs reaching 20-30% of needy populations (unsystematic, insufficient); [[Valdenmoor]] more robust (reaching 50-60%).

**Rationing systems:** Most settlements do not implement formal rationing (considered extreme measure); shortage must be quite severe. Documented formal rationing occurred during Rift Incursion population displacement (610-615 FE) in [[Solhaven]] — grain rationed under guild coordination, limiting daily household consumption to 0.3 pounds per person (vs. normal 0.4-0.5). Rationing typically reduces consumer quantity 20-40%, stretching supply 25-50%.

**Price controls:** Some settlements attempt price-ceiling enforcement (prohibit retail markup beyond X%). Effectiveness limited: enforcement weak, merchants simply reduce supply (withdraw goods from market rather than sell at controlled prices). Documented attempts at price controls typically fail within 1-2 weeks as shortage worsens due to supply withdrawal.

## Population-Level Failure: Famine

When shortage cascades without institutional mitigation, populations face famine conditions.

### Malnutrition Effects

Onset of dietary inadequacy (consuming 20-30% below minimum nutrition) produces:

- **Week 1-2:** Hunger sensation, fatigue increase, subtle cognitive decline
- **Week 3-4:** Immune function decline (infection rates begin climbing), wound healing slow, muscle fatigue severe
- **Week 5+:** Severe weight loss (visible), organ function decline, child development damage (stunting)

Documented malnutrition in [[Solhaven]] winter 619-620 FE (grain shortage): childhood stunting (reduced height compared to controls) affected estimated 15-20% of children born in shortage year. Long-term impacts persist: stunted children show reduced cognitive development, lower adult earning capacity.

### Mortality

Death from malnutrition typically requires 8-12 weeks of severe dietary deficiency. First deaths occur among vulnerable populations (elderly, very young, immunocompromised). Historic example: Valdenmoor grain blight years (15-18 FE), documented mortality spike in Colden-Thawmark (winter) as malnutrition-weakened populations succumbed to secondary infection. Estimated 800-1200 excess deaths (beyond baseline) in [[Valdenmoor]] district during 3-year shortage period (population ~25,000).

### Social Breakdown

Extended famine creates social stress: theft increases dramatically (hungry populations steal food), internal conflict (hoarding accusations, family breakdown due to desperate choices), and institutional failure (authority loses legitimacy, governance collapses).

Documented in Rift Incursion refugee crisis: theft of food increased 200%+, violence in distribution lines, family separations (parents unable to feed children, abandonment documented). Institutional order required garrison support to maintain distribution system integrity.

## Disruption Vulnerability Assessment

Which populations and systems fail first under shortage?

**Most vulnerable:** Urban day-laborers and working poor (40-50% of urban population). Complete dependence on daily market access, no food storage, no agricultural safety net. Shortage transforms to malnutrition within 2-4 weeks for this population.

**Moderately vulnerable:** Rural agricultural workers (20-30% of population). Dependent on single harvest for annual food. Poor harvest year means year-long inadequacy. Somewhat less vulnerable than urban poor due to ability to preserve production (storage capacity exists) and potential for supplementary hunting/gathering.

**Less vulnerable:** Middle-class populations and property owners (15-25% of population). Access to stored grain, merchant-house reserves, ability to pay premium prices, institutional buffer (guild membership providing access to guild stores). Can absorb 40-60% shortage without reaching malnutrition.

**Minimal vulnerability:** Elite populations, government officials, military leadership (1-5% of population). Wealth and power access ensure food availability. Shortage inconvenience, not threat.

## Historical Example: Complete Cascade

Rift Incursion disruption 610-615 FE demonstrates complete food-shortage cascade:

1. **Trigger:** Refugee influx from rift-affected regions (8000-12000 persons over 18 months) created surge demand in [[Solhaven]].
2. **Market response:** Grain prices rose 50% year-one, 85% year-two. Merchants hoarded expecting further rises. Supply tightening accelerated shortage perception.
3. **Access collapse:** Working-class populations unable to afford normal quantities. Urban poverty food budget (previously adequate) covered only 60-70% of needs. Household dietary deficiency emerged within 3 months.
4. **Institutional response:** [[Basin Grain Allocation Board]] released 12% of reserves to relief distribution. [[Solhaven]] implemented formal rationing (0.3 pounds per person daily limit). Distribution programs reached estimated 35-40% of vulnerable populations.
5. **Population effects:** Despite institutional response, malnutrition documented in 20-25% of vulnerable households. Childhood stunting affected 600-900 children born during shortage period (estimated 12-15% of birth cohort). Three documented deaths (elderly, severely undernourished) in second shortage year.
6. **Recovery:** Food security returned by Harvestfirst 616 FE when new grain supply and refugee stabilization resolved original trigger. Population recovered within 12-18 months (with documented long-term impacts on stunted children and institutional trust damage).

## Contemporary Shortage Risk (623 FE)

Current wartime mobilization (623 FE) creates multiple shortage pressure points:

- **Military requisitions:** Crown requisition quotas reduce civilian grain supply 10-15%
- **Labor shortage:** Conscription reducing harvest labor capacity 20-30%, projected harvest 5-15% below normal
- **Supply disruption:** Border tensions disrupting [[Terravelle]]-[[Auralis]] grain trade (reduces [[Auralis]] supply 20-30%)
- **Demand surge:** Military mobilization camps requiring massive grain procurement

Integrated forecast: shortage probability high; severity likely 20-40% supply reduction in affected regions by winter 623-624 FE. Vulnerable populations at moderate-to-high famine risk if institutional response insufficient.
