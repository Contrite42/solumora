===WRITE: content/History/The Hunting Period.md===
The Hunting Period was a two-century span of decentralized persecution that began approximately four hundred years ago when a young [[High Demons|High Demon]] lost control of their spell in a populated market square. What followed was not an organized purge but a slow-burning community-level hunt driven by fear of something that looked human but could kill with capabilities no human should possess.

## The Triggering Incident

The incident occurred in what is now southern [[Terravelle]], in a market town whose name has been lost to the historical record. A young demon — likely less than fifty years old and new to [[Solumora]] — was attempting a simple Force application when their control failed catastrophically. The spell inverted into a massive downward compression centered on their position, killing a horse outright, crushing one person to death, and leaving a third with injuries that never fully healed.

The magical signature was distinctive enough that [[Flux Users|Scholars]] who investigated could determine it was a Force effect of extraordinary magnitude — far beyond what any human caster of apparent age could safely channel. The geometric pattern suggested tier capabilities that should have required decades of development, applied by someone who appeared to be barely past adolescence.

The young demon was found and killed within days. This was the worst possible outcome for every other High Demon on the continent.

## The Spread of Fear

What made the Hunting Period particularly dangerous was its decentralized nature. There was no royal decree, no formal bounty system, no coordinated authority directing the search. Instead, the knowledge that something that looked human could produce such effects spread through oral networks — traders, travelers, family connections between settlements.

Communities began turning on people who seemed too capable too young, or who could not account for their origins convincingly, or who simply carried the wrong kind of strangeness. The criteria were inconsistent, locally determined, and often had nothing to do with actual demonic presence. A human caster who had developed unusual efficiency in their discipline might find themselves under suspicion. A traveler with no clear family connections might be questioned aggressively. Anyone who demonstrated magical capability that seemed disproportionate to their apparent experience became a potential target.

The persecution was not constant or universal. It operated in waves, flaring up when rumors reached a community and dying down when no clear targets could be identified. Some regions experienced years of intensive suspicion. Others saw only occasional incidents. The pattern was unpredictable enough that neither humans nor demons could reliably anticipate where the danger would surface next.

## Impact on High Demons

[[Sorath]] and [[Fennick]], both already centuries old by this point, had established identities and some degree of social integration. Both withdrew completely. Sorath disappeared into the northern wilderness and remained there for decades. Fennick ceased his social activities and spent the period in careful isolation, moving only when absolutely necessary and avoiding all contact that might draw attention.

Sorath has described the waiting period as the most tedious of his existence — not because of physical hardship, but because of the enforced inactivity. For someone accustomed to wandering freely across the continent, the restriction to a single remote location was psychologically difficult in ways that physical danger would not have been.

Fennick found the situation "darkly interesting" from his safe distance, though he has never fully admitted how close that distance came to not being safe enough. His natural inclination toward social disruption had to be completely suppressed, and the discipline required was unlike anything his previous existence had demanded.

The three younger demons — [[Selvane]], [[Mave]], and [[Wren]] — all arrived after the Hunting Period had ended. None experienced it directly, and the older demons have been reluctant to discuss it in detail. This has left the younger generation without full understanding of how precarious their situation could become if human awareness of their presence were to resurface.

## Methods and Casualties

The hunting methods varied by community but typically involved some combination of magical testing, background investigation, and social pressure. A person under suspicion might be asked to demonstrate their capabilities under controlled conditions, questioned extensively about their personal history, or simply ostracized until they chose to leave the area.

The testing was often crude and dangerous. Communities with access to higher-tier casters might attempt to probe a suspect's magical signature directly. Others relied on observing behavior under stress or demanding demonstrations of capability that would reveal unusual power levels.

High Demons were not the only casualties. The period saw significant persecution of human casters who were unusually skilled, travelers with unclear origins, and anyone whose behavior or capabilities seemed anomalous. The total human death toll was likely higher than the demonic one, though exact numbers are impossible to determine given the decentralized nature of the persecution.

At least two High Demons were killed during this period, though the exact circumstances are not documented in surviving records. The deaths occurred early enough in the period to reinforce the fear driving the hunt, but not so frequently as to provide clear evidence of what was actually being hunted.

## The Gradual End

The Hunting Period did not end through resolution or official declaration. It exhausted itself. The trail went cold, new incidents stopped occurring, and without fresh fuel the fear gradually transformed from immediate concern to historical memory.

Several factors contributed to its decline. The surviving High Demons had learned to avoid detection completely, eliminating the incidents that had sustained community fear. The human casualties had created their own backlash — communities began to question whether the persecution was causing more harm than the original threat. The passage of time allowed the specific details of the triggering incident to blur into folk memory, reducing the clarity of what was being hunted.

The transition was gradual enough that no one could identify a clear endpoint. Communities simply stopped organizing searches. Travelers stopped being questioned as aggressively. The heightened vigilance faded back to normal levels of community caution.

## Historical Memory

Within a generation, the specific knowledge of what had been hunted became unclear. The period is remembered, where it is remembered at all, as a time of general suspicion and community tension rather than as a targeted campaign against a specific threat. Most historical accounts focus on the social dynamics — the breakdown of trust between communities, the persecution of travelers and outsiders — without understanding the original cause.

No surviving historical record makes the connection between the Hunting Period and dimensional incursion. The concept of High Demons as distinct from lower-order [[Flux Demons]] does not exist in current scholarship. The period is treated as an example of how fear can drive irrational community behavior, not as evidence of a genuine threat that was successfully suppressed.

This historical amnesia has been crucial to the current High Demon population's survival. The knowledge that enabled the original hunt has been lost, making a repeat significantly less likely even if new incidents were to occur.

## Legacy

The Hunting Period established patterns of caution that persist among High Demons today. The understanding that human communities can organize effective persecution, even without institutional coordination, has shaped how they approach integration and concealment. The knowledge that a single public incident can trigger continent-wide consequences has made them extremely careful about magical discipline and public exposure.

For the younger generation of High Demons, the period exists as cautionary history rather than lived experience. This has created some tension between older and younger demons about appropriate levels of caution and integration. The older demons' extreme care about exposure sometimes conflicts with the younger demons' desire for more active engagement with human society.

The period also demonstrates the effectiveness of decentralized response to threats that institutional authorities might not recognize or be able to address. The communities that participated in the hunt succeeded in suppressing High Demon activity for two centuries without any formal coordination or official support. This suggests that similar community-level responses could emerge again under the right conditions.

===WRITE: content/Places/Hedun.md===
Hedun is a coastal city on [[Auralis]]'s western shore, built where the Kess River meets the sea in a natural harbor that has supported maritime trade for centuries. With a population approaching two hundred thousand, it ranks as the kingdom's third-largest city after [[Solhaven]] and Vaelthorne. What makes Hedun distinctive is not its size or economic importance, but the particular quality of its social and political atmosphere — a combination of genuine intellectual vitality, managed chaos, and carefully orchestrated unpredictability that has been shaped by [[Fennick]]'s presence over the past forty years.

## Geography and Layout

The city spreads along both sides of the Kess River delta, connected by a series of bridges that have been rebuilt, reinforced, and expanded so many times that the oldest foundations support structures that bear no resemblance to their original design. The harbor district occupies the southern bank, where deeper water allows larger vessels to dock. The administrative and residential areas climb the northern hills in terraced neighborhoods that catch the ocean breeze and provide views across the water.

Unlike [[Solhaven]]'s dramatic vertical architecture, Hedun's elevation changes are gradual and organic. The city feels horizontal rather than stratified, with neighborhoods flowing into each other without sharp boundaries. This physical layout reflects the social reality — while [[Control Tier]] hierarchy exists here as it does throughout [[Auralis]], it operates more fluidly than in the capital.

The western districts, closest to the open ocean, house the city's fishing fleet and maritime industries. The eastern districts, inland along the river, focus on river trade and the processing of goods that flow down from the interior. The central districts, spanning both sides of the river, contain the markets, guildhalls, temples, and the particular concentration of taverns, meeting halls, and social spaces that give Hedun its reputation for lively public discourse.

## Economic Foundation

Hedun's economy rests on three pillars: maritime trade, fishing, and what the city's merchants euphemistically call "intellectual commerce" — the buying and selling of information, ideas, and influence that flows through the city's unusually active social networks.

The harbor handles a significant portion of [[Auralis]]'s western trade routes. Ships from the northern settlements bring timber, furs, and preserved goods. Vessels from the southern coastal cities carry manufactured items, textiles, and the occasional cargo of [[Ancient Ruins|ancient artifacts]] recovered from desert expeditions. The city's position makes it a natural stopping point for any maritime route that doesn't go directly to Solhaven.

The fishing industry is substantial and sophisticated. Hedun's fleet includes both small coastal vessels and larger deep-water ships capable of extended voyages. The city has developed preservation techniques that allow fresh fish to reach inland markets in good condition, creating trade relationships that extend well into [[Auralis]]'s interior.

The third pillar — intellectual commerce — is harder to quantify but equally important to the city's character. Hedun has become known as a place where information flows freely, where connections can be made between people who would never encounter each other elsewhere, and where the right conversation in the right tavern can be worth more than a formal business contract. This reputation is not accidental.

## Social Atmosphere

Hedun's social life operates with an energy and unpredictability that visitors from other [[Auralis]] cities find either exhilarating or exhausting. The city's taverns, meeting halls, and public spaces host a constant rotation of debates, performances, impromptu gatherings, and social events that seem to emerge spontaneously but often produce surprisingly concrete results.

Conversations in Hedun tend to be more direct than elsewhere in the kingdom. The [[Ascendant Path]]'s emphasis on hierarchy through [[Control Tier|tier]] advancement exists here but feels less rigid, less determining of social possibility. A T2 merchant with interesting ideas can find themselves in conversation with T5 [[Flux Users|Channelers]] in ways that would be unlikely in Solhaven. A T4 caster with useful connections can influence decisions that would normally be made at higher tier levels.

This social fluidity has practical consequences. Business partnerships form and dissolve more quickly than in other cities. Political alliances shift with each season's social calendar. Information spreads through the city's networks faster than through official channels, and often proves more accurate.

The city's residents have developed a particular skill at reading social dynamics — understanding who has influence with whom, which conversations are worth joining, and when a casual interaction might lead to significant opportunity. This creates a population that is socially sophisticated in ways that formal education does not typically produce.

## Fennick's Influence

[[Fennick]] arrived in Hedun forty-three years ago and has been shaping its social atmosphere ever since. His approach has been subtle, patient, and cumulative — not dramatic interventions but thousands of small adjustments to the city's social chemistry over decades of consistent presence.

His Chemical discipline, applied at the social level, creates conditions where inhibitions lower slightly, where strangers feel more inclined to trust each other, where conflicts that might escalate instead resolve into productive disagreement. He does not control outcomes directly. He adjusts the conditions under which outcomes emerge, nudging the city's social interactions toward greater honesty, more interesting combinations of people, and more frequent moments of genuine connection.

The effect is that Hedun has become a place where things happen. Conversations that would remain polite and superficial elsewhere develop into genuine exchanges of ideas. Business relationships that would be purely transactional elsewhere evolve into partnerships based on mutual interest and respect. Political discussions that would be carefully managed elsewhere become genuine debates about policy and principle.

Fennick moves through the city's social networks without maintaining a fixed identity. He appears at gatherings, contributes to conversations, facilitates connections between people who should know each other, and disappears before anyone thinks to ask too many questions about his background. His horns are covered when necessary and ignored when possible. The city has developed a collective understanding that some people are interesting enough to be worth not questioning too closely.

## Political Character

Hedun's relationship with [[The Council of Auralis]] is cordial but independent. The city sends representatives to Solhaven, participates in kingdom-wide policies, and contributes its share of taxes and resources. It also maintains a degree of local autonomy that other cities of similar size do not enjoy.

This independence is partly geographical — Hedun's distance from the capital makes direct oversight impractical — but mostly social. The city's networks of information and influence operate faster than official channels. Local decisions are often made and implemented before Solhaven's directives arrive. When conflicts arise between local and kingdom-wide interests, Hedun's representatives are skilled at finding compromises that satisfy both without requiring either side to abandon their principles.

The city's political culture emphasizes practical results over ideological purity. [[The Culmination Faction|Culmination]] and [[Expansion Faction]] politics exist here but feel less urgent, less determining of personal and professional relationships. A Culmination-aligned merchant and an Expansion-aligned ship captain can work together successfully if their business interests align, regardless of their theoretical disagreements about kingdom policy.

This pragmatic approach has made Hedun valuable to the council as a source of information about public opinion and as a testing ground for policies that might be controversial elsewhere. The city's social networks provide early warning about how kingdom-wide initiatives will be received, and its political flexibility allows for adjustments before policies are implemented more broadly.

## Cultural Life

Hedun has developed a distinctive cultural character that blends [[Auralis]]'s traditional emphasis on [[Flux]] mastery with a more experimental and inclusive approach to intellectual development. The city's libraries are extensive and well-used. Its temples host regular debates about [[Ascendant Path]] theology that would be considered inappropriate in more orthodox locations. Its guildhalls function as much as social centers as professional organizations.

The city is known throughout [[Auralis]] for its festivals, which occur with unusual frequency and tend to evolve organically rather than following traditional formats. A celebration that begins as a harvest festival might develop into a week-long series of debates about agricultural policy. A religious observance might expand to include performances, competitions, and impromptu markets that draw visitors from across the region.

Music and performance are particularly vibrant in Hedun. The city's taverns and meeting halls host regular entertainment that ranges from traditional ballads to experimental compositions that incorporate [[Flux]]-enhanced acoustics. Performers from across the kingdom come to Hedun to test new material, knowing that the city's audiences are both appreciative and honest in their responses.

## People in Motion

**Kess Valdorn** — Harbor master for the past twelve years. T3 Binding specialist who has revolutionized cargo handling through precisely applied force distribution. Currently negotiating with Solhaven merchants for expanded warehouse facilities while managing tensions between local fishing crews and visiting trade vessels.

**Mira Thorne** — Owner of the Crosswinds Tavern, the city's most influential social gathering place. T2 Heat user who keeps her establishment at perfect temperature year-round. Has facilitated more business partnerships and political alliances than most formal diplomats, though she would deny any deliberate role in such arrangements.

**Daven Kess** — River pilot who guides vessels up the Kess River to inland ports. T4 Water specialist with an intuitive understanding of current and weather patterns. Recently returned from a trading voyage to the northern settlements with reports of unusual [[Flux]] activity near the [[Equatorial Desert]] border.

**Sera Voss** — Visiting scholar from [[Wolfpoint]], currently staying in Hedun while researching maritime applications of [[Hypertext]] casting. T5 capabilities that she demonstrates sparingly, preferring to observe the city's social dynamics rather than participate directly in them.

**Jorik Helm** — Fishing fleet captain whose crews consistently return with larger catches than their competitors. T3 Detection specialist who claims his success comes from reading water conditions, though other captains suspect more sophisticated [[Flux]] applications than he admits to.
